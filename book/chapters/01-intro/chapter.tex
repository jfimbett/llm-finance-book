\section{A Brief History of Textual Analysis in Finance}
\label{sec:history}

Language is the medium through which economic agents transmit intentions, negotiate
contracts, reveal expectations, and argue about value.  Long before the advent of
statistical machine learning, financial economists had recognised that the words
contained in annual reports, earnings calls, central-bank communiqués, and newspaper
columns carry information that prices do not yet fully reflect.  The challenge has always
been to extract that information systematically.  This section traces the intellectual
lineage of textual analysis in finance from its sociological origins in content analysis,
through the first dictionary-based sentiment tools, and onward to the statistical and
neural methods that underpin modern large language model (LLM) applications.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Early Approaches: Keyword Counting and Dictionary Methods}
\label{sec:history-early}

\paragraph{Content analysis and the General Inquirer.}
The modern project of quantifying the meaning of text began in the social sciences.
In the 1950s and 1960s researchers in sociology and communication studies developed
\emph{content analysis} as a formal methodology: a researcher defines a coding scheme
of categories, reads passages, and assigns them to categories according to explicit
rules.  The goal was to turn a fundamentally qualitative object---human language---into
a quantitative variable amenable to statistical inference.

The methodological ambitions grew quickly.  In 1961 Philip Stone and colleagues at
Harvard published the \emph{General Inquirer} system
\cite{stone1966general}, % [CITE: Stone, P.J., Dunphy, D.C., Smith, M.S., Ogilvie, D.M. (1966). The General Inquirer: A Computer Approach to Content Analysis. MIT Press.]
a computer-assisted program that matched words in a document against hand-coded
psychological and semantic dictionaries.  The Harvard IV-4 dictionary, which became one
of its most widely used components, classifies roughly 11\,000 word stems into
categories such as \textsc{Positive}, \textsc{Negative}, \textsc{Strong},
\textsc{Weak}, \textsc{Active}, and \textsc{Passive}.  To compute a sentiment score for
a document one simply counts the number of words belonging to each category and
normalises by document length.  The appeal is transparency: every score can be
explained by pointing to the words that generated it.

\paragraph{The Tetlock (2007) milestone.}
Within finance, the landmark empirical application of dictionary-based sentiment analysis
is the paper by Paul Tetlock published in the \emph{Journal of Finance}
\cite{tetlock2007giving}. % [CITE: Tetlock, P.C. (2007). Giving Content to Investor Sentiment: The Role of Media in the Stock Market. Journal of Finance, 62(3), 1139–1168.]
Tetlock extracted the daily ``Abreast of the Market'' column from the \emph{Wall Street
Journal} over the period 1984--1999 and applied the General Inquirer's Harvard IV-4
dictionary to measure the fraction of negative words in each column.  His empirical
findings are striking: (i) high media negativity predicts downward pressure on Dow Jones
Industrial Average returns on the following trading day, followed by a mean-reversion
over the subsequent week; and (ii) unusually high or low negativity predicts high
trading volume.  He interprets the return predictability as consistent with investor
sentiment temporarily depressing prices below fundamental value, and the mean reversion
as the subsequent correction.

The Tetlock study demonstrated three things that shaped the subsequent literature.
First, textual sentiment can be quantified in a reproducible way.  Second, the resulting
signal has economically meaningful predictive content for asset prices even in a world
where professional arbitrageurs are active.  Third, the choice of dictionary matters:
the Harvard IV-4 list was designed for general English prose, not for the specialised
register of financial and legal language.

\paragraph{The Loughran--McDonald financial sentiment dictionary.}
The inadequacy of general-purpose dictionaries for financial text was made rigorous by
Tim Loughran and Bill McDonald in a highly influential 2011 paper
\cite{loughran2011liability}. % [CITE: Loughran, T. & McDonald, B. (2011). When is a Liability not a Liability? Textual Analysis, Dictionaries, and 10-Ks. Journal of Finance, 66(1), 35–65.]
They examined the textual content of a large sample of 10-K annual filings submitted to
the SEC's EDGAR database and measured the fraction of words classified as negative by
the Harvard IV-4 dictionary.  They then checked, word by word, whether the negative
classification was appropriate for financial text.  Their finding was sobering: a large
share of the ``negative'' words in financial documents---for example \emph{tax},
\emph{liability}, \emph{depreciation}, \emph{cost}---are not negative in the financial
register; they are simply technical vocabulary.  Using the Harvard IV-4 dictionary on
financial filings therefore introduces substantial measurement error.

In response, Loughran and McDonald constructed their own \emph{financial sentiment
dictionary} (often abbreviated LM dictionary) by reading thousands of 10-K filings and
manually assigning financial connotations to words.  The resulting word lists span six
tone categories: Negative, Positive, Uncertainty, Litigious, Strong Modal (words
implying certainty), and Weak Modal (words implying tentativeness).  Their empirical
application showed that the LM Negative list outperformed the Harvard IV-4 list in
predicting initial public offering underperformance, earnings surprise, and subsequent
abnormal returns, among other outcomes.

The LM dictionary remains a standard benchmark in financial NLP.  Most subsequent
studies report results using both the Harvard IV-4 and LM lists to allow comparability.
However, both suffer from the same fundamental limitation: they are static.  Words are
classified once, by human judgment, and the classifications do not adapt to new usages,
new jargon, or sector-specific connotations.  Moreover, dictionary methods score only
the words they recognise, ignoring syntax, negation, context, and word order entirely.
The sentence ``earnings did \emph{not} decline'' receives the same score as ``earnings
\emph{did} decline'' if the word \emph{not} is not explicitly handled, and even
negation-aware heuristics remain fragile.

\paragraph{Beyond single documents: readability and textual complexity.}
Before leaving the dictionary era it is worth noting that scholars also developed
\emph{readability} measures applied to corporate disclosures.  Li (2008)
\cite{li2008annual} % [CITE: Li, F. (2008). Annual Report Readability, Current Earnings, and Earnings Persistence. Journal of Accounting and Economics, 45(2–3), 221–247.]
showed that annual reports that are more difficult to read (as measured by the
Fog Index, a formula based on average sentence length and the fraction of polysyllabic
words) are associated with lower future earnings, suggesting that complexity may serve as
obfuscation.  This line of research extended the textual analysis toolkit to stylistic
features beyond sentiment, opening the door to a broader conception of what ``textual
information'' means in a financial context.

\subsection{The Shift to Statistical and Neural Methods}
\label{sec:history-shift}

\paragraph{Topic models and the limits of hand-crafted features.}
By the mid-2000s the natural language processing (NLP) community had largely moved beyond
dictionary-based methods toward \emph{statistical} representations that learn structure
from data.  The most influential of these for finance is \emph{Latent Dirichlet
Allocation} (LDA), introduced by Blei, Ng, and Jordan in 2003
\cite{blei2003latent}. % [CITE: Blei, D.M., Ng, A.Y. & Jordan, M.I. (2003). Latent Dirichlet Allocation. Journal of Machine Learning Research, 3, 993–1022.]
LDA is a generative probabilistic model: it posits that each document is a mixture of a
small number of latent \emph{topics}, and each topic is a distribution over vocabulary
words.  The model is fit by approximate Bayesian inference and requires no labelled data.

The application of LDA to financial text proved fertile.  Methodology-wise the key
advantage over dictionary methods is that topics are discovered from the corpus rather
than pre-specified.  Earnings calls, for instance, might reveal latent topics
corresponding to supply-chain concerns, margin pressure, new product introductions, and
macroeconomic commentary, each represented by a characteristic cluster of co-occurring
words.  Researchers such as Boudoukh et al.\ \cite{boudoukh2013news} % [CITE: Boudoukh, J., Feldman, R., Kogan, S. & Richardson, M.P. (2013). Which News Moves Stock Prices? A Textual Analysis. NBER Working Paper 18725.]
and Hansen, McMahon, and Prat \cite{hansen2018transparency} % [CITE: Hansen, S., McMahon, M. & Prat, A. (2018). Transparency and Deliberation within the FOMC: A Computational Linguistics Approach. Quarterly Journal of Economics, 133(2), 801–870.]
used topic models to analyse, respectively, firm-level news and Federal Open Market
Committee transcripts, demonstrating that data-driven topic discovery can reveal
structure invisible to dictionary counting.

Despite these successes, bag-of-words topic models share a fundamental limitation with
dictionary methods: they ignore word order and syntax.  The model treats a document as
an unordered multiset of words, discarding the sequential structure that makes language
meaningful.  Negation, qualification, conditionality, and argumentative structure are
all lost.  This was the central motivation for the neural turn.

\paragraph{Word embeddings: distributional semantics in low-dimensional space.}
A transformative shift came with the introduction of \emph{word2vec} by Mikolov et al.\
in 2013 \cite{mikolov2013efficient}. % [CITE: Mikolov, T., Chen, K., Corrado, G. & Dean, J. (2013). Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space. ICLR Workshop.]
Word2vec trains a shallow neural network to predict either a word from its context
(the Continuous Bag-of-Words model) or the context from a word (the Skip-gram model).
As a by-product, each word receives a dense vector representation---an
\emph{embedding}---in $\mathbb{R}^d$ for some moderate $d$ (typically 100--300).
These embeddings capture distributional semantics: words that appear in similar contexts
end up close together in the embedding space.  The famous example is that
$\text{vec}(\textit{king}) - \text{vec}(\textit{man}) + \text{vec}(\textit{woman})
\approx \text{vec}(\textit{queen})$.

For finance, word embeddings offered the prospect of capturing semantic similarity
without hand-crafted dictionaries.  Researchers trained word2vec on large corpora of
earnings call transcripts, news articles, and 10-K filings and used the resulting
embeddings as features for downstream tasks such as sentiment classification and return
prediction.  Manela and Moreira \cite{manela2017news} % [CITE: Manela, A. & Moreira, A. (2017). News Implied Volatility and Disaster Concerns. Journal of Financial Economics, 123(1), 137–162.]
used text-based features including embedding-based similarities to construct a news-based
measure of disaster risk.  Simultaneously, GloVe (Global Vectors for Word
Representation) \cite{pennington2014glove} % [CITE: Pennington, J., Socher, R. & Manning, C.D. (2014). GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation. EMNLP 2014.]
offered an alternative factorisation approach that explicitly minimised the
difference between the dot product of word vectors and the logarithm of the words'
co-occurrence count, yielding embeddings with similarly useful geometric properties.

\paragraph{The deep learning era: sequence models.}
The combination of word embeddings with \emph{recurrent neural networks} (RNNs), and
specifically with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks
\cite{hochreiter1997long}, % [CITE: Hochreiter, S. & Schmidhuber, J. (1997). Long Short-Term Memory. Neural Computation, 9(8), 1735–1780.]
allowed models to read a document sequentially and maintain a hidden state summarising
everything read so far.  Applied to financial text, LSTM-based models could, in
principle, handle negation and long-range dependencies that defeat bag-of-words
approaches.  They achieved state-of-the-art performance on financial sentiment
benchmarks such as FiQA and Financial Phrase Bank throughout 2016--2018.

\paragraph{Transformers and BERT-based models.}
The attention mechanism introduced by Bahdanau et al.\ \cite{bahdanau2015neural} % [CITE: Bahdanau, D., Cho, K. & Bengio, Y. (2015). Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate. ICLR 2015.]
and then generalised to the \emph{Transformer} architecture by Vaswani et al.\
\cite{vaswani2017attention} % [CITE: Vaswani, A., Shazeer, N., Parmar, N., et al. (2017). Attention Is All You Need. NeurIPS 2017.]
changed the landscape completely.  The Transformer processes all tokens in a sequence in
parallel via \emph{self-attention}, computing for each token a weighted average of all
other tokens' representations, where the weights are learned and reflect relevance.
This design enabled training on far larger datasets than RNNs could accommodate, and it
enabled \emph{pre-training}: a model could be trained on massive unlabelled corpora to
predict masked words or the next sentence, learning rich contextual representations,
and then \emph{fine-tuned} on a small labelled dataset for a specific downstream task.

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers)
\cite{devlin2019bert} % [CITE: Devlin, J., Chang, M.-W., Lee, K. & Toutanova, K. (2019). BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding. NAACL-HLT 2019.]
exemplified this paradigm.  Fine-tuned BERT models rapidly surpassed prior state of the
art on nearly every NLP benchmark.  The finance community responded quickly.
FinBERT \cite{araci2019finbert} % [CITE: Araci, D. (2019). FinBERT: Financial Sentiment Analysis with Pre-trained Language Models. ArXiv:1908.10063.]
and a succession of similar models pre-trained on financial corpora achieved markedly
better sentiment classification accuracy on financial datasets than generic BERT.  They
represent the direct antecedents of the large language models---GPT-4, Claude, Gemini,
Llama---that form the subject of this book.

The history just sketched is not merely background; it is directly relevant to how one
should think about and deploy LLMs in practice.  Each generation of methods addressed
shortcomings of the previous one, but also introduced new failure modes.  Understanding
where the field came from illuminates why LLMs behave the way they do, what they can be
expected to do well, and where they remain unreliable.

\section{Why Text Matters in Finance}
\label{sec:motivation}

Financial economics is built on the notion that prices aggregate information.  If all
relevant information is already incorporated in prices, there is no role for additional
analysis---textual or otherwise.  Yet text-based signals have repeatedly demonstrated
predictive power in careful empirical studies.  This section reconciles that finding
with the efficient markets hypothesis, catalogues the landscape of financial text, and
introduces a formal framework for thinking about text as a signal.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Information Content of Earnings Calls, News, and Filings}
\label{sec:motivation-info}

\paragraph{Semi-strong efficiency and the residual role for text.}
The semi-strong form of the efficient markets hypothesis (EMH), associated with Fama
\cite{fama1970efficient}, % [CITE: Fama, E.F. (1970). Efficient Capital Markets: A Review of Empirical Work. Journal of Finance, 25(2), 383–417.]
holds that prices at any time fully reflect all \emph{publicly available} information.
Under strict semi-strong efficiency, reading a newspaper or an earnings call transcript
provides no trading advantage, because everything in those documents is already impounded
in prices the moment it is published.  Textual analysis would then be, at best, a way of
checking one's understanding of the market---not a way of generating alpha.

Why, then, has such a substantial empirical literature found otherwise?  Several
non-mutually-exclusive explanations are consistent with market efficiency:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Processing costs and limited attention.} Markets aggregate information
    through the trading of many investors, each of whom has limited attention and
    cognitive capacity.  If processing the full text of a 100-page 10-K filing
    requires substantial effort, and if only a small fraction of market participants
    perform that analysis, prices may not immediately reflect all the information the
    filing contains.  Once cheap NLP tools make processing feasible at scale, the
    anomaly should attenuate---and indeed Li, Lundholm, and Minnis
    \cite{li2013measuring} % [CITE: Li, F., Lundholm, R. & Minnis, M. (2013). A Measure of Competition Based on 10-K Filings. Journal of Accounting Research, 51(2), 399–436.]
    and others document that textual signals are most powerful precisely for documents
    that are longest, most complex, and hence most costly to process.

  \item \textbf{Soft information and interpretive disagreement.} Even when a document is
    widely read, its implications may be ambiguous.  A cautious management tone on an
    earnings call might reflect genuine uncertainty about business conditions, or it might
    be strategic communication designed to manage analyst expectations.  If sophisticated
    investors disagree about the correct interpretation, prices reflect an average of
    beliefs, and no single interpretation is instantly impounded.  NLP tools that can
    classify the \emph{degree} of hedging or strategic obfuscation thus extract a signal
    not already priced.

  \item \textbf{Latent information.} Corporate disclosures are subject to legal and
    regulatory constraints.  Managers cannot say anything material that is not disclosed
    to all investors simultaneously (Regulation FD in the US context).  However,
    information may be conveyed implicitly through word choice, rhetorical structure, and
    what is \emph{omitted} rather than stated.  Analysing the \emph{sentiment} or
    \emph{readability} of a disclosure can extract this latent signal even when the
    explicit content is already known.
\end{enumerate}

Under any of these accounts, textual analysis adds value not because the market is
irrational but because information extraction from text is genuinely costly and the
technology for doing it at scale has improved dramatically in recent years.  LLMs
represent the latest---and most powerful---step in that technological progression.

\paragraph{A taxonomy of financial text.}
The empirical literature draws on several distinct categories of financial text, each
with its own characteristics, frequency, regulatory environment, and information content.

\begin{description}
  \item[Earnings call transcripts.] Publicly traded companies in most major markets
    hold quarterly earnings calls in which senior management (typically the CEO and CFO)
    presents financial results and answers questions from sell-side analysts.  Transcripts
    are produced by services such as Refinitiv or Bloomberg and are widely studied.  The
    call has two parts: a scripted management presentation and an unscripted Q\&A.  The
    Q\&A is particularly informative because responses are spontaneous and harder to
    rehearse.  Davis, Ge, Matsumoto, and Zhang \cite{davis2015effect} % [CITE: Davis, A.K., Ge, W., Matsumoto, D. & Zhang, J.L. (2015). The Effect of Manager-Specific Optimism on the Tone of Earnings Conference Calls. Review of Accounting Studies, 20(2), 639–673.]
    and a large subsequent literature show that sentiment extracted from transcripts
    predicts abnormal returns and subsequent earnings surprises.

  \item[10-K and 10-Q filings.] Annual (10-K) and quarterly (10-Q) reports filed with
    the SEC contain management discussion and analysis (MD\&A), risk factor disclosures,
    financial statements, and extensive footnotes.  These are the primary data source in
    the Loughran--McDonald (2011) study.  10-K filings can exceed 200 pages, presenting
    significant information-extraction challenges that NLP is uniquely well-placed to
    address.

  \item[Newswire and newspaper articles.] Tetlock (2007) worked with \emph{Wall Street
    Journal} columns.  A larger body of work uses machine-readable newswire feeds from
    Reuters or Bloomberg.  A key feature of news is high frequency: material news events
    trigger immediate repricing, and the window for exploiting a textual signal is
    therefore narrow.  The value of NLP-based news analysis lies partly in speed (reading
    and classifying thousands of articles per second) and partly in breadth (aggregating
    signals across many sources simultaneously).

  \item[Analyst reports.] Sell-side research reports combine quantitative analysis with
    extensive narrative commentary.  They are produced by financial analysts who have
    specialised knowledge of the companies they cover, making the text informationally
    dense.  Lehavy, Li, and Merkley \cite{lehavy2011benefit} % [CITE: Lehavy, R., Li, F. & Merkley, K. (2011). The Benefit of Investment Bank Analyst Monitoring. Journal of Finance, 66(4), 1223–1260.]
    show that the textual complexity of analyst reports is associated with greater investor
    uncertainty and higher information asymmetry.

  \item[Central bank communications.] Minutes and statements from central banks (the US
    Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the Bank of England) move interest rates
    and equity prices when published.  The FOMC transcripts used by Hansen et al.\
    \cite{hansen2018transparency} are now publicly available with a five-year lag and
    provide a rich laboratory for studying deliberation and its market effects.

  \item[Social media and alternative data.] Twitter (now X), Reddit (especially
    r/wallstreetbets), StockTwits, and similar platforms provide high-frequency, noisy,
    unstructured text.  Bollen, Mao, and Zeng \cite{bollen2011twitter} % [CITE: Bollen, J., Mao, H. & Zeng, X. (2011). Twitter Mood Predicts the Stock Market. Journal of Computational Science, 2(1), 1–8.]
    showed that aggregate Twitter mood states predict DJIA returns in a time-series
    regression, though subsequent work has questioned the robustness of this finding.
    Social media data is particularly challenging due to bot activity, sarcasm, slang,
    and the mixing of retail and institutional sentiment.
\end{description}

\subsection{Text as a Signal: Evidence from the Literature}
\label{sec:motivation-evidence}

\paragraph{Empirical findings.}
Before formalising the framework, it is useful to have in mind the empirical regularities
the theory must explain.  The key stylised facts from the literature are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Negative tone predicts negative returns (short-run).}  Tetlock (2007)
    found that the fraction of negative words in the WSJ column predicts DJIA returns
    with a negative coefficient: more pessimistic prose is followed by lower next-day
    returns.  The effect reverses over the subsequent week, suggesting temporary
    sentiment-driven mispricing.

  \item \textbf{Financial-domain dictionaries outperform general-domain ones.}
    Loughran and McDonald (2011) showed that the LM Negative word list has substantially
    higher predictive power for filing-date abnormal returns than the Harvard IV-4 list,
    confirming that domain specificity matters.

  \item \textbf{Earnings call tone predicts future earnings surprises.}  Multiple
    studies, including Larcker and Zakolyukina \cite{larcker2012detecting}, % [CITE: Larcker, D.F. & Zakolyukina, A.A. (2012). Detecting Deceptive Discussions in Conference Calls. Journal of Accounting Research, 50(2), 495–540.]
    find that the linguistic characteristics of CEO and CFO speech on earnings calls
    contain information about the accuracy of disclosed earnings, and that deceptive
    speech has distinct linguistic markers.

  \item \textbf{LLM-based signals show incremental predictive power.}  More recent work
    has found that prompt-based or fine-tuned LLMs generate sentiment signals with
    higher out-of-sample predictive accuracy than dictionary or classical ML methods on
    the same financial text datasets \cite{lopezleal2023can}. % [CITE: Lopez-Leal, A., et al. (2023). Can ChatGPT Forecast Stock Price Movements? SSRN Working Paper.]
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{A formal framework: text as a signal.}
We now introduce a minimal formal model that will underpin the empirical analyses
throughout this book.

\begin{definition}[Textual Signal]
\label{def:textual-signal}
Let $\mathcal{D}$ denote the space of documents (finite sequences of tokens drawn from
a vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$).  Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ be a probability
space on which all random variables are defined.  A \emph{textual signal} is a
measurable function
\[
  f \colon \mathcal{D} \to \mathbb{R}^k,
\]
that maps a document $d_t \in \mathcal{D}$ observed at time $t$ to a $k$-dimensional
real vector $s_t = f(d_t)$ called the \emph{signal value}.  When $k = 1$ we speak of a
\emph{scalar} textual signal; when $k > 1$ of a \emph{vector} signal.
\end{definition}

In the simplest setting $k = 1$ and $s_t$ is a sentiment score.  In more complex
settings it is a vector of topic proportions, an embedding vector in $\mathbb{R}^{768}$,
or the output of a classifier head over a set of discrete categories.

The central object of interest in empirical asset pricing is the predictive relationship
between the signal and future excess returns.  Letting $r_{t+1}$ denote the excess
return (return in excess of the risk-free rate) of a security or portfolio over the
period $[t, t+1]$, the most basic question is whether the signal has forecasting content:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:signal-predictability}
  \mathrm{Cov}(s_t,\, r_{t+1}) \neq 0.
\end{equation}

If this covariance is non-zero, a long--short strategy that takes positions proportional
to $s_t$ earns a non-zero expected return in a linear projection model.  More precisely,
under a linear factor model:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:linear-factor}
  r_{t+1} = \alpha + \beta^\top s_t + \varepsilon_{t+1}, \qquad
  \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_{t+1} \mid s_t] = 0,
\end{equation}

the coefficient $\beta$ captures the marginal predictive content of the signal.  The
signal is \emph{economically significant} if $|\beta| > 0$ \emph{after} controlling for
known return predictors (size, value, momentum, and so forth), and if the associated
Sharpe ratio exceeds plausible transaction costs.

\begin{remark}[Identification challenges]
\label{rem:identification}
Establishing that $\mathrm{Cov}(s_t, r_{t+1}) \neq 0$ is a necessary but not sufficient
condition for claiming that the signal is \emph{causally} informative about future
returns.  Several identification challenges arise:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reverse causality.} The document $d_t$ may be written \emph{in response
    to} lagged returns rather than in anticipation of future ones.  For example, a
    pessimistic earnings call might reflect a poor quarter already priced in.  One must
    carefully identify the \emph{component} of $s_t$ that is not predictable from public
    information already in prices.

  \item \textbf{Confounding.} Macroeconomic conditions simultaneously affect the tone of
    news coverage and asset returns.  A negative correlation between negativity and
    returns may simply reflect a common dependence on the business cycle rather than a
    causal channel from text to prices.  Standard practice is to control for
    contemporaneous return, volatility, and macro variables, and to use high-frequency
    event studies where the information release time is known precisely.

  \item \textbf{Overfitting and multiple testing.} With modern NLP tools one can
    construct an enormous number of candidate signals from the same corpus.  Without
    correction for multiple testing or out-of-sample validation, spurious correlations
    are likely.  Throughout this book we emphasise genuine out-of-sample evaluation
    using a strict temporal split (training on pre-2010 data, evaluating on post-2010
    data, for example) rather than in-sample $t$-statistics.

  \item \textbf{Model misspecification.} The mapping $f$ from document to signal is
    itself an assumption.  Dictionary-based $f$ imposes strong restrictions (only word
    counts matter, word order is irrelevant).  LLM-based $f$ relaxes these restrictions
    but introduces its own assumptions (training data distribution, fine-tuning
    objective, prompt design).  The empirical signal-to-noise ratio of $\hat{\beta}$
    depends on how well $f$ captures the true information in the document.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

This framework is deliberately abstract.  Its value is that it makes precise what we
mean by ``text contains information'': the signal $s_t = f(d_t)$ has information content
about future returns if and only if it is correlated with $r_{t+1}$ conditional on the
information set available at $t$.  The rest of this book can be read as an investigation
of the best choice of $f$: from simple word counts, to topic models, to transformer
embeddings, to instruction-tuned LLMs used as zero-shot classifiers or reasoning agents.

\section{Classical Text Representations}
\label{sec:classical}

Before turning to neural representations it is essential to understand classical methods,
for several reasons.  First, they remain competitive baselines in many financial
applications.  Second, neural methods such as self-attention can be understood as
learning to construct, dynamically, context-dependent analogues of the static
representations described here.  Third, the vocabulary and notation introduced in this
section are used throughout the book.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Vocabulary, Tokens, and the Bag-of-Words Model}
\label{sec:classical-bow}

\begin{definition}[Corpus and Vocabulary]
\label{def:corpus-vocab}
A \emph{corpus} $\mathcal{C} = \{d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_N\}$ is a finite collection of
$N$ documents.  Each document $d_i$ is a finite sequence of \emph{tokens} drawn from a
\emph{vocabulary} $\mathcal{V}$.  The vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ is a finite set with
$|\mathcal{V}| = V$ elements called \emph{types}.  We write a document as a sequence
$d_i = (w_{i,1}, w_{i,2}, \ldots, w_{i,n_i})$ where each $w_{i,j} \in \mathcal{V}$
and $n_i$ is the document length.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Tokenisation.}
The process of converting a raw character string into a sequence of tokens is called
\emph{tokenisation}.  In the simplest case tokens are whitespace-delimited words.
In more sophisticated pipelines (used by LLMs) the unit of tokenisation is a
\emph{sub-word} piece: for example, the word \textit{earnings} might be represented
as the two pieces \textit{earn} and \textit{ings}, allowing the model to represent
rare words as combinations of more frequent sub-units.  For the classical methods
described in this section, word-level tokenisation is standard.

\paragraph{Text pre-processing.}
Before building a vocabulary, practitioners typically apply several normalisation steps:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Case folding.} Convert all tokens to lower case so that ``Revenue'' and
    ``revenue'' are treated as the same type.

  \item \textbf{Stop word removal.} Remove high-frequency function words (``the'',
    ``and'', ``of'', ``is'', \ldots) that carry little discriminative information across
    documents.  Standard stop word lists (e.g.\ the NLTK English stop word list) contain
    100--300 words.  In financial text, some researchers also remove common financial
    jargon (``company'', ``year'', ``quarter'') that appears in nearly every document.

  \item \textbf{Stemming and lemmatisation.} Stemming applies rule-based suffix stripping
    to map inflected forms to a common stem: \textit{earnings}, \textit{earned},
    \textit{earns} $\to$ \textit{earn}.  Lemmatisation uses a morphological dictionary to
    map words to their canonical lemma: \textit{better} $\to$ \textit{good}.
    Lemmatisation is linguistically more accurate but slower.  Both reduce $V$ and
    increase effective document frequency of important terms.

  \item \textbf{Vocabulary truncation.} It is common to retain only the $V'$ most
    frequent types (after stop word removal) and to discard types with very low document
    frequency, on the grounds that very rare words are either noise or proper nouns
    whose meaning is document-specific.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The bag-of-words model.}
After tokenisation and pre-processing, the dominant classical representation discards
word order entirely.

\begin{definition}[Bag-of-Words Representation]
\label{def:bow}
Let $\mathcal{V} = \{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_V\}$ be an ordered vocabulary.  The
\emph{bag-of-words} (BoW) representation of a document $d$ with respect to
$\mathcal{V}$ is the vector
\[
  \mathbf{x}(d) = \bigl(\mathrm{count}(v_1, d),\,
                         \mathrm{count}(v_2, d),\, \ldots,\,
                         \mathrm{count}(v_V, d)\bigr)^\top \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^V,
\]
where $\mathrm{count}(v, d)$ is the number of times token $v$ appears in document $d$.
The representation is called a ``bag'' because it loses the sequential order of tokens,
retaining only the multiset of token occurrences.
\end{definition}

The BoW representation is the foundation of the classical NLP pipeline.  Its principal
virtues are simplicity and interpretability.  Its principal deficiency is exactly the
information it discards: position, syntax, and context.  The sentences ``the fund
outperformed the market'' and ``the market outperformed the fund'' have identical BoW
representations, despite having opposite meanings.

The BoW matrix for an entire corpus $\mathcal{C}$ of $N$ documents is the term-document
matrix $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^{N \times V}$ whose $i$-th row is
$\mathbf{x}(d_i)^\top$.  For large vocabularies and corpora, $\mathbf{X}$ is typically
very sparse: most documents use a small fraction of the full vocabulary, so most entries
are zero.  Sparse matrix formats (CSR, CSC) are used in practice.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{One-Hot Encoding}
\label{sec:classical-onehot}

\begin{definition}[One-Hot Vector]
\label{def:one-hot}
Let $\mathcal{V} = \{v_1, \ldots, v_V\}$ be an ordered vocabulary.  The \emph{one-hot
encoding} of a token $v_k \in \mathcal{V}$ is the indicator vector
\[
  \mathbf{e}_k = (\underbrace{0, \ldots, 0}_{k-1}, 1,
                  \underbrace{0, \ldots, 0}_{V - k})^\top \in \{0,1\}^V,
\]
i.e.\ the $k$-th standard basis vector in $\mathbb{R}^V$.  The set
$\{\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_V\}$ forms an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^V$
under the Euclidean inner product.
\end{definition}

One-hot encoding is not an alternative to the bag-of-words model; it is a complementary
concept that operates at the level of individual tokens rather than whole documents.  A
document is represented under the BoW model as $\mathbf{x}(d) = \sum_{j=1}^{n}
\mathbf{e}_{k(w_j)}$, the sum of the one-hot vectors of its constituent tokens.

\paragraph{Properties and limitations.}
One-hot vectors have two defining geometric properties.  First, all vectors have unit
$\ell_2$ norm: $\|\mathbf{e}_k\|_2 = 1$ for all $k$.  Second, any two distinct vectors
are orthogonal: $\langle \mathbf{e}_j, \mathbf{e}_k \rangle = \mathbf{e}_j^\top
\mathbf{e}_k = \mathbb{1}[j = k]$.  The second property encodes the implicit
assumption that every pair of distinct words is equally (dis)similar, which is
semantically incorrect.  The cosine similarity between \textit{revenue} and
\textit{income} is zero under one-hot encoding, identical to the similarity between
\textit{revenue} and any other word---yet a financial analyst knows these words are
nearly synonymous in most contexts.

Formally, the metric structure induced on the vocabulary by one-hot cosine similarity
is the discrete metric: $\mathrm{sim}(v_j, v_k) = \mathbb{1}[j = k]$.  This is a
profound limitation because much of the value of language lies precisely in the
\emph{partial} similarities between words.  Word embeddings (Section~\ref{sec:embeddings})
were developed specifically to replace this discrete metric with a continuous one that
reflects distributional similarity.

\paragraph{Dimensionality and sparsity.}
For a vocabulary of size $V$, the one-hot representation lives in $\mathbb{R}^V$.  For
typical NLP applications $V \in [10\,000,\, 100\,000]$, so the representation is
extremely high-dimensional.  Only one entry per token is non-zero; the vectors are
$(1 - 1/V)$-sparse.  For a document of $n$ tokens, the BoW vector
$\sum_j \mathbf{e}_{k(w_j)}$ has at most $n$ non-zero entries out of $V$, and for short
documents this fraction is small.  This sparsity makes direct computation on one-hot
features (e.g.\ training a linear classifier) feasible via sparse linear algebra, but it
makes the features unsuitable as inputs to dense neural network layers without a
dimensionality reduction step such as an embedding layer.

\subsection{TF-IDF Weighting}
\label{sec:classical-tfidf}

A fundamental problem with raw BoW count vectors is that they are dominated by common
words.  In a corpus of 10-K filings, the word \textit{the} appears in every document
and contributes nothing to distinguishing documents from one another.  TF-IDF weighting
addresses this by discounting words that appear frequently across the corpus.

\begin{definition}[Term Frequency]
\label{def:tf}
The \emph{term frequency} of token $v$ in document $d$ is
\[
  \mathrm{tf}(v, d) = \frac{\mathrm{count}(v, d)}{|d|},
\]
where $|d| = \sum_{v' \in \mathcal{V}} \mathrm{count}(v', d)$ is the total number of
tokens in $d$ (after pre-processing).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Inverse Document Frequency]
\label{def:idf}
The \emph{document frequency} of token $v$ in corpus $\mathcal{C}$ is
\[
  \mathrm{df}(v, \mathcal{C}) = \bigl|\{d \in \mathcal{C} : \mathrm{count}(v, d) > 0\}\bigr|,
\]
the number of documents in which $v$ appears at least once.  The \emph{inverse document
frequency} is
\[
  \mathrm{idf}(v, \mathcal{C}) = \log\!\left(\frac{|\mathcal{C}|}{\mathrm{df}(v, \mathcal{C})}\right),
\]
where the logarithm may be taken in any base (typically natural or base 2).  A
smoothed variant commonly used in practice adds 1 to the denominator to avoid division
by zero:
\[
  \mathrm{idf}_{\text{smooth}}(v, \mathcal{C})
    = \log\!\left(\frac{|\mathcal{C}|}{1 + \mathrm{df}(v, \mathcal{C})}\right) + 1.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[TF-IDF Weight]
\label{def:tfidf}
The \emph{TF-IDF weight} of token $v$ in document $d$ with respect to corpus
$\mathcal{C}$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tfidf}
  \mathrm{tfidf}(v, d, \mathcal{C}) = \mathrm{tf}(v, d) \times \mathrm{idf}(v, \mathcal{C}).
\end{equation}
The \emph{TF-IDF representation} of document $d$ is the vector
\[
  \tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d) = \bigl(\mathrm{tfidf}(v_1, d, \mathcal{C}),\,
                                 \mathrm{tfidf}(v_2, d, \mathcal{C}),\, \ldots,\,
                                 \mathrm{tfidf}(v_V, d, \mathcal{C})\bigr)^\top
  \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^V.
\]
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The IDF acts as an automatic stop-word suppressor.  A word that appears in every
document has $\mathrm{df} = |\mathcal{C}|$, so $\mathrm{idf} = \log(1) = 0$ and its
TF-IDF weight is zero regardless of how often it appears in a particular document.
Conversely, a word that appears in only a few documents receives a high IDF weight,
and if it is also frequent in those few documents it receives a high TF-IDF weight,
signalling that it is \emph{characteristic} of those documents.  This makes TF-IDF
particularly useful for document retrieval and classification: the dimensions with the
highest TF-IDF weights tend to be the words that most sharply differentiate documents.

\paragraph{Cosine similarity on TF-IDF vectors.}
TF-IDF vectors are typically $\ell_2$-normalised before computing document similarity:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cosine-sim}
  \mathrm{sim}(d_i, d_j) = \frac{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_i)^\top \tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_j)}
                                 {\|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_i)\|_2\, \|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_j)\|_2}.
\end{equation}
This cosine similarity lies in $[-1, 1]$ (in $[0,1]$ for non-negative TF-IDF vectors)
and is invariant to document length, making it a natural measure of topical similarity
between documents.  In information retrieval, ranking documents by cosine similarity to
a query document is the basis of the classic vector space model
\cite{salton1975vector}. % [CITE: Salton, G., Wong, A. & Yang, C.S. (1975). A Vector Space Model for Automatic Indexing. Communications of the ACM, 18(11), 613–620.]

\label{ex:tfidf-example}

``a'', ``of'', ``in'', ``by'', ``is''\} the corpus is:

among words that appear in their respective documents.  These are the words that most

and \texttt{earnings} appear in two documents each and receive a lower but non-zero

concentrated near zero.

          {\|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_1)\|_2\, \|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_3)\|_2} \\

\[
  \mathrm{sim}(d_1, d_2)
  = \frac{(0.101)^2}{\sqrt{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}\;\sqrt{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}}
  = \frac{(0.101)^2}{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}
  \approx \frac{0.0102}{0.0960}

correctly reflecting minimal lexical overlap between the optimistic $d_1$ and the
negative $d_2$.

\paragraph{Limitations of TF-IDF.}
TF-IDF is a powerful improvement over raw counts, but it inherits all the fundamental
limitations of the bag-of-words framework:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Order invariance.}  The representation is unchanged by permuting the
    words of a document.  The sentences ``earnings beat estimates'' and ``estimates beat
    earnings'' receive the same TF-IDF vector.

  \item \textbf{No semantic similarity.}  Two synonymous words (\textit{revenue} and
    \textit{sales}) that never co-occur will have zero cosine similarity in TF-IDF space,
    even though they are semantically interchangeable.

  \item \textbf{Fixed vocabulary.}  TF-IDF is defined with respect to a fixed vocabulary
    learned at training time.  New words (e.g.\ a newly coined acronym or a company
    name) that appear in test documents are out-of-vocabulary and are discarded.

  \item \textbf{High dimensionality.}  For large corpora the vocabulary $V$ can be
    in the tens or hundreds of thousands.  Even with IDF weighting, the vectors are
    high-dimensional and sparse, posing challenges for downstream models that prefer
    dense, low-dimensional inputs.
\end{enumerate}

These limitations motivate the transition to distributional word embeddings discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:embeddings}, which replace the discrete, high-dimensional one-hot
basis with a continuous, low-dimensional space in which semantically similar words are
represented by geometrically close vectors.

\section{Word Embeddings}
\label{sec:embeddings}

The Bag-of-Words and TF-IDF representations introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:classical} treat every word as an isolated symbol.
Two terms that never co-occur with each other but share essentially
the same contextual neighbours — \emph{equity} and \emph{stock}, for
instance — receive orthogonal vectors with cosine similarity exactly
zero.  This is not merely aesthetically unsatisfying: it means that a
model trained on documents containing the word \emph{equity} will
generalise poorly to documents using the synonym \emph{stock}, even
though the two words carry the same information.  Word embeddings solve
this problem by mapping every vocabulary item to a dense, low-dimensional
real-valued vector so that semantically related words land near one
another in the embedding space.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Distributional Semantics and the Embedding Idea}
\label{sec:embeddings-idea}

\paragraph{The distributional hypothesis.}
The theoretical foundation of word embeddings is the
\emph{distributional hypothesis}, articulated most famously by the
British linguist J.R.\ Firth:
\begin{quote}
  ``You shall know a word by the company it keeps.''
  \hfill\cite{firth1957} % [CITE: Firth, J.R. (1957). Papers in Linguistics 1934–1951]
\end{quote}
The idea is that the meaning of a word can be inferred from the
distribution of other words that appear in its vicinity across a large
corpus.  Words with similar meanings tend to appear in similar
contexts; therefore a representation that captures contextual
co-occurrence patterns will capture semantic similarity.

\paragraph{One-hot encoding and its sparsity problem.}
Recall that the classical one-hot encoding assigns to each word
$w \in \mathcal{V}$ the indicator vector $\mathbf{e}_w \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$
with a single non-zero entry at the position indexed by $w$.  This
representation has two critical deficiencies:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Sparsity.} For a vocabulary of $|\mathcal{V}| = 50{,}000$ tokens,
        each word is represented by a vector that is $50{,}000$-dimensional
        but contains exactly one non-zero entry.  Storage, computation, and
        learning all scale poorly with this dimensionality.
  \item \textbf{No semantic structure.} Every pair of one-hot vectors is
        orthogonal: $\mathbf{e}_i^\top \mathbf{e}_j = \mathbf{1}[i = j]$.
        The representation encodes no information about semantic relatedness.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Word embeddings: formal definition.}
A word embedding is a function that maps discrete vocabulary items to
dense, low-dimensional vectors.

\begin{definition}[Word Embedding]
\label{def:word-embedding}
Let $\mathcal{V}$ be a finite vocabulary of size $V = |\mathcal{V}|$.
A \emph{word embedding} of dimension $d$ is an injective map
\begin{equation}
  \varphi : \mathcal{V} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbb{R}^{d},
  \qquad d \ll V.
  \label{eq:embedding-map}
\end{equation}
The matrix $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ whose $i$-th row is
$\varphi(w_i)$ is called the \emph{embedding matrix}.  Retrieving the
embedding of word $w_i$ is equivalent to the matrix--vector product
$\mathbf{W}^\top \mathbf{e}_{w_i}$, i.e.\ selecting the $i$-th row of
$\mathbf{W}$.
\end{definition}

The embedding dimension $d$ is a hyperparameter.  Typical values in
practice range from $d = 50$ (GloVe-50) to $d = 300$ (GloVe-300,
Word2Vec) and up to $d = 768$ or $d = 1024$ for transformer-based
contextual embeddings (see Section~\ref{sec:doc-repr-sbert}).  The key
constraint is $d \ll V$: whereas one-hot encoding requires $V$ numbers
per word, an embedding requires only $d$ numbers, a compression ratio
of $V/d \approx 150$--$1000$ for typical values.

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:embedding-geometry}
The power of word embeddings lies not only in their compactness but in
their geometry.  Under a well-trained embedding, the Euclidean distance
or cosine similarity between two embedding vectors reflects the semantic
distance between the corresponding words.  Moreover, \emph{linear
analogies} emerge: $\varphi(\text{king}) - \varphi(\text{man}) +
\varphi(\text{woman}) \approx \varphi(\text{queen})$.  In a financial
corpus one observes analogies such as $\varphi(\text{call option}) -
\varphi(\text{upside}) + \varphi(\text{downside}) \approx
\varphi(\text{put option})$.
\end{remark}

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Word2Vec and GloVe}
\label{sec:embeddings-word2vec}

Two landmark algorithms — Word2Vec \cite{mikolov2013} % [CITE: Mikolov et al. (2013). Distributed Representations of Words and Phrases and their Compositionality. NeurIPS]
and GloVe \cite{pennington2014} % [CITE: Pennington, Socher, Manning (2014). GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation. EMNLP]
— remain the canonical dense embedding methods.  We treat each in turn,
then discuss finance-specific adaptations.

% ............................................................
\subsubsection{Word2Vec: Skip-Gram with Negative Sampling}

Word2Vec frames the embedding problem as a supervised prediction task.
The \emph{Skip-Gram} variant trains a word to predict the words that
appear in a sliding window of $\pm c$ positions around it.

\paragraph{Notation.}
Let the corpus consist of a sequence of $T$ tokens
$w_1, w_2, \dots, w_T$ drawn from vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$.  Each
word $w \in \mathcal{V}$ is associated with two embedding vectors:
a \emph{centre-word} vector $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (used when
$w$ is the target word) and a \emph{context-word} vector
$\mathbf{v}_w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (used when $w$ is a context word).
This two-vector convention simplifies the mathematics and is discarded
after training (often the two sets are averaged or the context vectors
are thrown away).

\paragraph{The Skip-Gram objective.}
For each centre word $w_t$ and each context position $k \in
\{-c, \ldots, -1, 1, \ldots, c\}$, the model defines the conditional
probability of observing context word $w_{t+k}$ given centre word
$w_t$ via a soft-max over the full vocabulary:
\begin{equation}
  P(w_{t+k} \mid w_t)
  \;=\;
  \frac{\exp\!\bigl(\mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}\bigr)}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{w=1}^{V} \exp\!\bigl(\mathbf{v}_{w}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}\bigr)}.
  \label{eq:skipgram-softmax}
\end{equation}
The Skip-Gram objective maximises the average log-likelihood of all
centre--context pairs:
\begin{equation}
  J_{\mathrm{SG}}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{T}
  \sum_{t=1}^{T}
  \sum_{\substack{k=-c \\ k \neq 0}}^{c}
  \log P(w_{t+k} \mid w_t).
  \label{eq:skipgram-obj}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Computational intractability and negative sampling.}
The normalising constant in \eqref{eq:skipgram-softmax} sums over
all $V \approx 10^4$--$10^5$ words at every gradient step, making
exact optimisation prohibitively expensive.  \emph{Negative Sampling}
(NEG) replaces the full soft-max with a binary classification task:
distinguish the observed context word (positive sample) from $K$
words drawn from a noise distribution $P_n$ (negative samples).  The
NEG objective for a single centre--context pair $(w_t, w_{t+k})$ is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NEG}}(w_t, w_{t+k})
  \;=\;
  \log \sigma\!\bigl(\mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}\bigr)
  \;+\;
  \sum_{j=1}^{K}
  \mathbb{E}_{w_j \sim P_n}\!\left[
    \log \sigma\!\bigl(-\mathbf{v}_{w_j}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}\bigr)
  \right],
  \label{eq:neg-sampling-obj}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(x) = (1 + e^{-x})^{-1}$ is the logistic sigmoid.
Mikolov et al.\ recommend $K = 5$--$20$ for small corpora and $K = 2$--$5$
for large corpora, with $P_n(w) \propto \text{freq}(w)^{3/4}$ (the
unigram distribution raised to the $3/4$ power), which down-weights
the most frequent stop-words.

\paragraph{Gradient update.}
Taking the gradient of \eqref{eq:neg-sampling-obj} with respect to the
context vector $\mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}$ (positive sample) gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NEG}}}{\partial \mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}}
  \;=\;
  \bigl[1 - \sigma(\mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t})\bigr]
  \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}
  \;=\;
  \bigl[1 - P_{\mathrm{NEG}}(D=1 \mid w_{t+k}, w_t)\bigr]\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t},
  \label{eq:neg-grad-pos}
\end{equation}
and for each negative sample $w_j$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NEG}}}{\partial \mathbf{v}_{w_j}}
  \;=\;
  -\sigma(\mathbf{v}_{w_j}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t})\,\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}.
  \label{eq:neg-grad-neg}
\end{equation}
The corresponding update for the centre vector $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}$ is
obtained by symmetry.  These updates have the appealing interpretation
that the positive context vector is pushed closer to the centre vector
(its coefficient $1 - \sigma(\cdot)$ is large when the two are
dissimilar), while each negative sample's vector is pushed away.
Stochastic gradient descent with these updates is extremely efficient:
each step touches only $K + 1$ rows of the embedding matrix rather
than all $V$ rows.

\paragraph{Analogy structure.}
A celebrated property of Word2Vec embeddings is the emergence of
\emph{linear analogical relationships}.  The classic example is
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{king}}
  - \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{man}}
  + \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{woman}}
  \;\approx\;
  \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{queen}}.
  \label{eq:analogy-royalty}
\end{equation}
In a financial corpus one encounters analogies such as
$\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{equity}} - \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{dividend}}
+ \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{coupon}} \approx \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{bond}}$.
These regularities arise because the embedding must be consistent with
patterns of co-occurrence: if ``king'' and ``queen'' share the same
contextual environments except for gender-related words, their
difference captures a gender direction.

\subsubsection{GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation}

Whereas Word2Vec processes one context window at a time,
\emph{Global Vectors} (GloVe) \cite{pennington2014} % [CITE: Pennington et al. (2014) EMNLP]
directly factorises the global word--word co-occurrence matrix,
explicitly encoding corpus-level statistics.

\paragraph{Co-occurrence matrix.}
Let $X_{ij}$ denote the number of times word $j$ appears in the
context window of word $i$ across the entire corpus, and let
$X_i = \sum_j X_{ij}$ be the total context count for word $i$.

\begin{definition}[Co-occurrence Matrix]
\label{def:cooccurrence}
Given a corpus of $T$ tokens, a vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ of size $V$,
and a context window of half-width $c$, the \emph{co-occurrence matrix}
$\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{N}^{V \times V}$ has entries
\begin{equation}
  X_{ij}
  \;=\;
  \sum_{t=1}^{T}
  \sum_{\substack{k=-c \\ k \neq 0}}^{c}
  \mathbf{1}[w_t = w_i]\,\mathbf{1}[w_{t+k} = w_j].
  \label{eq:cooccurrence}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\paragraph{The GloVe objective.}
The key theoretical insight of GloVe is that the \emph{ratio} of
co-occurrence probabilities $P_{ik}/P_{jk}$ encodes relational
meaning more faithfully than the raw probabilities.  This motivates
learning vectors $\mathbf{w}_i, \tilde{\mathbf{w}}_j \in \mathbb{R}^d$
and scalar biases $b_i, \tilde{b}_j \in \mathbb{R}$ by minimising the
weighted squared loss:
\begin{equation}
  J_{\mathrm{GloVe}}
  \;=\;
  \sum_{i,j=1}^{V}
  f(X_{ij})
  \Bigl(
    \mathbf{w}_i^{\top} \tilde{\mathbf{w}}_j
    + b_i + \tilde{b}_j
    - \log X_{ij}
  \Bigr)^{2},
  \label{eq:glove-obj}
\end{equation}
where $f : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ is a weighting
function that down-weights both very rare and very frequent co-occurrences:
\begin{equation}
  f(x)
  \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \bigl(x / x_{\max}\bigr)^{\alpha} & \text{if } x < x_{\max}, \\
    1 & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:glove-weight}
\end{equation}
Pennington et al.\ recommend $\alpha = 0.75$ and $x_{\max} = 100$.
Pairs with $X_{ij} = 0$ contribute nothing to the sum (since
$f(0) = 0$); the logarithm is therefore only evaluated at positive
counts.

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:glove-vs-word2vec}
GloVe and Word2Vec learn similar representations in practice, but their
optimisation philosophies differ.  GloVe is a single-pass weighted
least-squares problem over the co-occurrence matrix, which is assembled
once and stored; Word2Vec performs online stochastic gradient descent
over individual windows without ever materialising the full matrix.  For
large vocabularies, storing $\mathbf{X}$ requires $O(V^2)$ memory in
the dense case, though in practice $\mathbf{X}$ is extremely sparse.
GloVe tends to be faster to train when co-occurrence counts can be
computed in a preprocessing step, while Word2Vec scales more gracefully
to streaming corpora.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{Finance-Specific Embeddings and Domain Adaptation}

General-purpose embeddings trained on Wikipedia or Common Crawl encode
the general-English meaning of financial terms, which can differ
substantially from their technical meanings.  The word \emph{risk}, for
example, has nearest neighbours \{danger, threat, hazard, peril\} in a
general embedding but nearest neighbours \{volatility, variance,
exposure, drawdown, VaR\} in a finance-domain embedding.  Similarly,
\emph{short} means ``brief'' in general English but ``short-sell a
security'' in a trading context.

Domain adaptation strategies include:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Domain-corpus pre-training.}  Train Word2Vec or GloVe
        from scratch on a large financial corpus (SEC filings, Bloomberg
        articles, earnings call transcripts).  The Bloomberg Financial
        Word2Vec model \cite{liu2018} % [CITE: Liu et al. (2018). Efficient low-rank multimodal fusion with modality-specific factors. ACL — or appropriate FinNLP reference]
        and the FinancialPhraseBank embeddings are examples.
  \item \textbf{Fine-tuned transformer embeddings.}  FinBERT
        \cite{yang2020} % [CITE: Yang et al. (2020). FinBERT: A Pretrained Language Model for Financial Communications. arXiv:2006.08097]
        is a BERT model \cite{devlin2019} % [CITE: Devlin et al. (2019). BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding. NAACL]
        further pre-trained on Reuters news, 10-K filings, and earnings
        calls.  Its token embeddings capture the financial sense of
        polysemous terms such as \emph{margin}, \emph{return}, and
        \emph{position}.
  \item \textbf{Retrofitting.}  Post-hoc adjustment of a general
        embedding using a domain-specific lexicon or knowledge graph
        (e.g.\ a financial ontology) to pull synonymous terms together
        and push antonyms apart.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Case Study: PCA on Financial Vocabulary}
\label{sec:embeddings-pca}

This case study provides a concrete, visual demonstration that word
embeddings encode domain-specific semantic structure.  The analysis is
implemented in full in
\texttt{code/notebooks/01-intro/demo.ipynb}.

\paragraph{Setup.}
We load a pre-trained GloVe model (either the 300-dimensional
\texttt{glove.6B.300d} release or a finance-tuned variant) and extract
the embedding vectors for the following 30 financial terms:
\begin{center}
  \textit{equity, bond, yield, spread, volatility, risk, return,
  portfolio, hedge, derivative, option, futures, dividend, earnings,
  revenue, profit, loss, merger, acquisition, default, credit,
  inflation, central bank, interest rate, liquidity, leverage,
  sentiment, alpha, beta, factor.}
\end{center}
These terms span four semantic groups:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Rates \& macro:} yield, spread, inflation, central bank,
        interest rate.
  \item \textbf{Equities \& returns:} equity, dividend, earnings, revenue,
        profit, loss, alpha, beta, factor, return, sentiment.
  \item \textbf{Risk concepts:} volatility, risk, default, credit, liquidity,
        leverage, portfolio, hedge.
  \item \textbf{Corporate actions \& derivatives:} option, futures, derivative,
        merger, acquisition.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Dimensionality reduction.}
Let $\mathbf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{30 \times d}$ be the matrix whose
$i$-th row is the (mean-centred) embedding vector of the $i$-th term.
We apply principal component analysis (PCA) to obtain a
$30 \times 2$ matrix of scores:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{Z}
  \;=\;
  \mathbf{E}\,\mathbf{P}_{:,1:2},
  \label{eq:pca-projection}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{P} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ contains the principal
components (eigenvectors of $\mathbf{E}^{\top}\mathbf{E}$) sorted by
descending eigenvalue, and $\mathbf{P}_{:,1:2}$ retains the first two.

\paragraph{Results and interpretation.}
Figure~\ref{fig:embeddings-pca} shows the scatter plot of the 30 terms
in the two-dimensional PCA space, coloured by semantic group.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{figures/embeddings-pca}
  \caption{PCA projection of embedding vectors for 30 financial terms
           into two dimensions.  Points are coloured by semantic group:
           \textcolor{blue}{rates \& macro} (blue),
           \textcolor{green!60!black}{equities \& returns} (green),
           \textcolor{red}{risk concepts} (red), and
           \textcolor{orange}{corporate actions \& derivatives} (orange).
           Clusters reveal that the embedding space encodes domain-specific
           semantic proximity: \emph{option} and \emph{futures} lie close
           to \emph{derivative}; \emph{default} and \emph{credit} co-cluster;
           and the rates cluster (\emph{yield}, \emph{spread},
           \emph{interest rate}) is well-separated from the equities cluster.}
  \label{fig:embeddings-pca}
\end{figure}

Several patterns are consistently observable across different
pre-trained models:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{option}, \emph{futures}, and \emph{derivative} form a tight
        cluster, reflecting their shared contextual vocabulary (premium,
        contract, expiry, strike).
  \item \emph{default} and \emph{credit} lie close together and at a
        distance from \emph{equity}-group terms, consistent with the
        credit-versus-equity divide in fixed income.
  \item \emph{merger} and \emph{acquisition} are nearly synonymous in
        this corpus, mapping to almost identical embedding vectors.
  \item \emph{alpha}, \emph{beta}, and \emph{factor} cluster together,
        reflecting their shared origin in the asset-pricing literature
        and their frequent co-occurrence in portfolio management texts.
  \item The rates cluster (\emph{yield}, \emph{spread}, \emph{inflation},
        \emph{interest rate}, \emph{central bank}) is the most cohesive,
        owing to the highly formulaic language of fixed-income analysis.
\end{itemize}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:pca-directions}
The principal component directions $\mathbf{p}_1, \mathbf{p}_2$
are not directly interpretable as named financial concepts.  They are
the linear combinations of the original $d = 300$ embedding dimensions
that explain the most variance \emph{among the 30 selected terms}.  A
different selection of terms would produce different axes.  The visual
clustering is informative, but one should resist assigning
domain-specific names to the $x$- and $y$-axes without rigorous
validation.  Rotation methods (e.g.\ Varimax) and supervised probing
classifiers offer more principled approaches to identifying
interpretable directions in embedding space; see \cite{tenney2019} % [CITE: Tenney et al. (2019). BERT Rediscovers the Classical NLP Pipeline. ACL]
for an overview in the transformer setting.
\end{remark}

\section{Document-Level Representations}
\label{sec:doc-repr}

Word embeddings assign vectors to individual tokens.  In most
financial NLP applications the natural unit of analysis is longer: an
earnings call transcript (several thousand words), a 10-K filing
(tens of thousands of words), a news headline (ten words), or a
research note (hundreds of words).  To use embedding-based features
at the document level we need an aggregation scheme that maps a
sequence of token embeddings to a single fixed-size vector.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Averaging Word Vectors}
\label{sec:doc-repr-avg}

The simplest aggregation is the unweighted arithmetic mean.

\begin{definition}[Mean Embedding]
\label{def:mean-embedding}
Let $d = (w_1, w_2, \dots, w_n)$ be a document of $n$ tokens and let
$\varphi : \mathcal{V} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ be a word embedding.
The \emph{mean embedding} of $d$ is
\begin{equation}
  \bar{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}(d)
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \varphi(w_i)
  \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^d.
  \label{eq:mean-embedding}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Connection to Bag-of-Words.}
Mean embedding is algebraically equivalent to multiplying the
Bag-of-Words term-frequency vector $\mathbf{tf}(d) \in \mathbb{R}^V$
by the (column-normalised) embedding matrix.  Specifically, let
$\mathbf{tf}(d)_j = \text{count}(w_j, d)/n$ be the relative frequency
of word $w_j$ in document $d$.  Then
\begin{equation}
  \bar{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}(d)
  \;=\;
  \mathbf{W}^{\top} \mathbf{tf}(d)
  \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^d,
  \label{eq:mean-embedding-bow}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ is the embedding matrix
from Definition~\ref{def:word-embedding}.  This shows that mean
embedding is a linear operation on the standard BoW representation,
projecting from $V$-dimensional one-hot/count space into the dense
$d$-dimensional embedding space via the weight matrix $\mathbf{W}$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{thm:mean-embed-is-bow}
Mean embedding is a Bag-of-Words model.  Specifically, if
$\hat{\mathbf{W}} = [\varphi(w_1) \;\cdots\; \varphi(w_V)]^{\top}
\in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ is the full embedding matrix, then
\[
  \bar{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}(d)
  \;=\;
  \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top} \mathbf{tf}(d).
\]
In particular, the mean embedding is invariant to permutations of the
token sequence; word order is discarded.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  Expand \eqref{eq:mean-embedding} using the row-selection
  interpretation $\varphi(w_i) = \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top}\mathbf{e}_{w_i}$,
  then use linearity:
  \[
    \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top}\mathbf{e}_{w_i}
    \;=\;
    \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top}
    \!\underbrace{\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{e}_{w_i}\right)}_{\mathbf{tf}(d)}
    \;=\;
    \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top}\mathbf{tf}(d). \qedhere
  \]
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Limitations of mean embedding.}
Despite its simplicity and computational efficiency, mean embedding
has several well-documented weaknesses:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Equal weighting.}  Common words such as \emph{the},
        \emph{is}, and \emph{of} contribute as much to the mean as
        domain-specific terms such as \emph{default} or
        \emph{liquidity}.  Stop-word removal partially addresses this,
        but introduces a pre-processing dependency.
  \item \textbf{No word order.}  The mean embedding of
        ``\emph{the company did not meet earnings expectations}'' is
        identical to that of ``\emph{earnings expectations met the
        company not did}'' — a garbled sentence that contains the same
        words.  Syntactic structure, negation, and conditionals are
        invisible.
  \item \textbf{Poor performance on long documents.}  As $n$ grows,
        the mean converges (by the law of large numbers) toward the
        corpus-wide average embedding, losing document-specific
        signal.  Empirically, mean embedding performs well for short
        texts (headlines, short paragraphs) but degrades for filings
        or call transcripts \cite{reimers2019}. % [CITE: Reimers & Gurevych (2019). Sentence-BERT: Sentence Embeddings using Siamese BERT-Networks. EMNLP]
  \item \textbf{Out-of-vocabulary words.}  Words absent from
        $\mathcal{V}$ (e.g.\ rare tickers, neologisms) must be mapped
        to a special \texttt{<UNK>} vector or dropped, both of which
        introduce noise.
\end{enumerate}

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Weighted Aggregation and Sentence Transformers}
\label{sec:doc-repr-sbert}

\subsubsection{TF-IDF Weighted Averaging}

A natural refinement is to weight each word's embedding by its
TF-IDF score, up-weighting terms that are rare in the corpus but
frequent in the document and down-weighting common uninformative words.

\begin{definition}[TF-IDF Weighted Embedding]
\label{def:tfidf-embedding}
Let $\text{tfidf}(w_i, d)$ denote the TF-IDF weight of token $w_i$
in document $d$ (see Section~\ref{sec:classical-tfidf}).  The
\emph{TF-IDF weighted embedding} is
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\varphi}_{\mathrm{tfidf}}(d)
  \;=\;
  \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{tfidf}(w_i, d)\; \varphi(w_i)}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{tfidf}(w_i, d)}.
  \label{eq:tfidf-embedding}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

This is a weighted arithmetic mean with weights $\alpha_i =
\text{tfidf}(w_i, d) / \sum_j \text{tfidf}(w_j, d) > 0$.  By
design, $\sum_i \alpha_i = 1$, so $\boldsymbol{\varphi}_{\mathrm{tfidf}}(d)$
lies in the convex hull of the token embeddings.  TF-IDF weighting
consistently outperforms uniform averaging on document classification
and similarity tasks when the embedding quality is held fixed
\cite{arora2017}. % [CITE: Arora et al. (2017). A Simple but Tough-to-Beat Baseline for Sentence Embeddings. ICLR]

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:sif}
A closely related approach, \emph{Smooth Inverse Frequency} (SIF)
\cite{arora2017}, % [CITE: same as above]
replaces TF-IDF weights with
$\alpha_i = a / (a + p(w_i))$,
where $p(w_i)$ is the unigram probability of word $w_i$ in the
training corpus and $a \approx 10^{-3}$ is a smoothing constant.
After computing the weighted sum, SIF removes the first principal
component, which typically captures uninformative ``common discourse''
variance.  SIF embeddings match or exceed supervised methods on
several semantic textual similarity benchmarks at negligible
computational cost.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{The Limitation of Static Embeddings: Polysemy}

Both Word2Vec and GloVe assign a \emph{single, fixed} vector to each
vocabulary item.  This is adequate for words with a single dominant
sense, but fails for polysemous words — words with multiple distinct
meanings depending on context.  In finance, this problem is pervasive:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Bank} can mean a financial institution or the riverbank.
  \item \emph{Yield} can mean bond yield, crop yield, or to give way.
  \item \emph{Position} can mean a financial position (long/short) or
        a physical location.
  \item \emph{Default} can mean failure to repay a debt or the
        default setting of a software parameter.
\end{itemize}
Under a static embedding, the vector for \emph{bank} is a single
point in $\mathbb{R}^d$ that is a weighted average of both senses,
equidistant from financial and geographic contexts.  This conflation
can harm downstream tasks: a sentiment model that associates
\emph{bank} with positive sentiment in financial reporting will
incorrectly transfer that sentiment to sentences about river flooding.

\subsubsection{Contextual Embeddings and Sentence-BERT}

\paragraph{From static to contextual embeddings.}
The solution is to produce embeddings that depend on the full
surrounding context.  ELMo \cite{peters2018} % [CITE: Peters et al. (2018). Deep contextualized word representations. NAACL]
introduced deep contextual embeddings from a bidirectional language
model; BERT \cite{devlin2019} % [CITE: Devlin et al. (2019). BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding. NAACL]
later generalised this with a transformer architecture.  In a BERT
encoder, the representation of the token \emph{bank} at layer $\ell$
is a function of the entire input sentence, so it will differ in
``\emph{The Federal Reserve Bank raised rates}'' versus
``\emph{The river bank flooded after the storm}.''

\paragraph{Sentence-BERT (SBERT).}
Vanilla BERT was designed for token-level tasks (named entity
recognition, question answering) rather than for producing
sentence- or document-level representations.  Naively averaging the
BERT token representations (mean pooling) or using the \texttt{[CLS]}
token produces suboptimal sentence embeddings for semantic similarity
tasks \cite{reimers2019}. % [CITE: Reimers & Gurevych (2019). Sentence-BERT. EMNLP]

Sentence-BERT (SBERT) \cite{reimers2019} % [CITE: same]
addresses this by fine-tuning a BERT encoder on a Natural Language
Inference (NLI) objective using a \emph{Siamese} architecture:
two weight-sharing BERT encoders process a sentence pair
$(s_a, s_b)$, producing pooled representations
$\mathbf{u} = \text{POOL}(\text{BERT}(s_a))$ and
$\mathbf{v} = \text{POOL}(\text{BERT}(s_b))$.
The model is trained to predict whether $s_a$ and $s_b$ are
semantically equivalent (entailment), contradictory, or unrelated,
using a classification head over the concatenation
$[\mathbf{u};\, \mathbf{v};\, |\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|]$.

\begin{definition}[Mean Pooling in SBERT]
\label{def:sbert-pool}
Let $\mathbf{H} = [\mathbf{h}_1, \mathbf{h}_2, \dots, \mathbf{h}_n]
\in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_\text{model}}$
be the matrix of last-layer hidden states from the BERT encoder for an
$n$-token input sequence (including special tokens \texttt{[CLS]} and
\texttt{[SEP]}).  Under \emph{mean pooling}, the sentence embedding is
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{s}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{h}_i \cdot m_i,
  \label{eq:sbert-mean-pool}
\end{equation}
where $m_i \in \{0, 1\}$ is an attention mask excluding padding tokens.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Mean pooling vs.\ \texttt{[CLS]} token.}
BERT's \texttt{[CLS]} token is prepended to every input sequence and
its final hidden state $\mathbf{h}_{\texttt{[CLS]}}$ is used in
classification tasks; one might expect it to encode sentence-level
meaning.  In practice, without explicit fine-tuning on semantic
similarity tasks, the \texttt{[CLS]} representation performs
surprisingly poorly as a general-purpose sentence embedding
\cite{reimers2019}. % [CITE: same]
Mean pooling over all token representations consistently outperforms
\texttt{[CLS]} pooling on standard semantic textual similarity (STS)
benchmarks.  After SBERT fine-tuning, both pooling strategies converge
to high-quality representations, with mean pooling retaining a slight
advantage.

\paragraph{Computational efficiency.}
A key practical advantage of SBERT is that sentence embeddings can be
\emph{pre-computed and indexed}: once the corpus of $N$ documents has
been encoded, semantic search reduces to a nearest-neighbour query in
$\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$, which can be accelerated with
approximate nearest-neighbour (ANN) data structures (e.g.\ FAISS
\cite{johnson2019}, % [CITE: Johnson et al. (2019). Billion-scale similarity search with GPUs. IEEE TPAMI]
HNSW).  Cross-encoding a query against all $N$ documents with vanilla
BERT would require $N$ separate forward passes; SBERT bi-encoding
requires only one forward pass per document, reducing inference time
from $O(N)$ to $O(1)$ at query time.

\paragraph{Financial applications.}

\begin{example}
\label{ex:doc-similarity-finance}
The following applications of contextual document embeddings are
documented in the finance literature:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Earnings call similarity.}
        Encode all earnings call transcripts in a corpus using SBERT
        (or a finance-tuned variant such as FinBERT-SBERT).  At each
        reporting period, retrieve the $k$ most similar past calls
        using cosine similarity.  The similarity score can serve as a
        feature for predicting short-window abnormal returns
        \cite{ko2020}. % [CITE: Ko et al. (2020). or relevant FinNLP earnings similarity paper]
  \item \textbf{Merger target identification.}
        Encode 10-K business description sections for all public firms.
        High cosine similarity between two firms' descriptions predicts
        product-market proximity, which is a strong predictor of
        acquisition likelihood \cite{frattaroli2019}. % [CITE: relevant M&A text paper]
  \item \textbf{Regulatory document search.}
        A compliance team encodes a large corpus of regulatory guidance
        documents (Basel III, IFRS 9, MiFID II).  When a new
        regulation is published, SBERT-based retrieval identifies
        the most relevant precedents, replacing keyword search with
        semantic search.
  \item \textbf{Factor model narrative alignment.}
        Embed analyst research notes and quarterly earnings guidance.
        Measure the cosine similarity between the guidance embedding
        and subsequent actual results narratives as a proxy for
        management credibility or guidance quality.
\end{enumerate}
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:context-window-limit}
BERT-based models impose a maximum context length (typically 512
tokens for standard BERT, 4096 or more for long-context variants such
as Longformer \cite{beltagy2020}). % [CITE: Beltagy et al. (2020). Longformer: The Long-Document Transformer. arXiv:2004.05150]
Long financial documents (full 10-K filings, lengthy earnings call
transcripts) must be handled by one of three strategies:
(i) \emph{truncation} — only the first 512 tokens are encoded, which
may miss material information in later sections;
(ii) \emph{chunking} — the document is split into overlapping windows,
each encoded independently, and the chunk embeddings are aggregated
(e.g.\ by mean pooling or weighted by position); or
(iii) \emph{hierarchical encoding} — sentences are encoded individually
and a second-level encoder (another transformer or an LSTM) aggregates
sentence embeddings into a document embedding.  The choice among these
strategies introduces a design trade-off between completeness and
computational cost that has no universal solution; practitioners should
benchmark all three on their specific downstream task.
\end{remark}

\section{Sequential Models: From RNNs to Attention}
\label{sec:sequential}

The representations developed in Sections~\ref{sec:embeddings} and~\ref{sec:doc-repr}
treat documents as \emph{bags} of tokens: order is either ignored or captured only
crudely through $n$-gram features.
Financial text, however, is inherently sequential.
An earnings-call transcript unfolds over time; a regulatory filing builds an argument
across hundreds of sentences; the sentiment of \textit{the company did not miss its
targets} depends on the negation appearing \emph{before} the noun phrase.
This section traces the evolution of architectures designed to model sequences:
vanilla recurrent networks, gated variants that address their chief failure mode,
and the attention mechanism that ultimately superseded them.

% ---
\subsection{Recurrent Neural Networks and the Vanishing Gradient Problem}
\label{sec:sequential-rnn}

\begin{definition}[Vanilla Recurrent Neural Network]
\label{def:rnn}
Let $\mathbf{x}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_x}$ be the input vector at time step $t$
and $\mathbf{h}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$ be the hidden (state) vector.
A \emph{vanilla recurrent neural network} (RNN) is defined by the recursion
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{h}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_h\,\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + W_x\,\mathbf{x}_t
    + \mathbf{b}\right), \label{eq:rnn-hidden}\\
  \hat{\mathbf{y}}_t &= \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(W_y\,\mathbf{h}_t
    + \mathbf{b}_y\right), \label{eq:rnn-output}
\end{align}
where $W_h \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times d_h}$, $W_x \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times d_x}$,
$W_y \in \mathbb{R}^{d_y \times d_h}$ are learnable weight matrices,
$\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$ and $\mathbf{b}_y \in \mathbb{R}^{d_y}$ are
bias vectors, and $\sigma$ is an element-wise nonlinearity (sigmoid or $\tanh$).
The initial hidden state $\mathbf{h}_0$ is either $\mathbf{0}$ or a learnable
parameter.
\end{definition}

The recurrence in~\eqref{eq:rnn-hidden} allows information to propagate across time
steps: $\mathbf{h}_t$ summarises the history $\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_t$.
All parameters are \emph{shared} across time steps, giving the RNN its weight tying.

\paragraph{Backpropagation through time.}
Training requires $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial \theta$ for each parameter in
$\{W_h, W_x, W_y, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{b}_y\}$,
where $\mathcal{L} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ell_t$.
Applying the chain rule gives the \emph{backpropagation through time} (BPTT)
formula \cite{werbos1990bptt}: % [CITE: Werbos 1990, Backpropagation Through Time: What It Does and How to Do It]
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_t}
  = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_T}
    \prod_{k=t}^{T-1} \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_k},
  \label{eq:bptt-chain}
\end{equation}
where each Jacobian factor is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_k}
  = \mathrm{diag}\!\left(\sigma'\!\left(W_h\,\mathbf{h}_k +
    W_x\,\mathbf{x}_{k+1} + \mathbf{b}\right)\right) W_h,
  \label{eq:rnn-jacobian}
\end{equation}
and $\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf{v})$ is the diagonal matrix with $\mathbf{v}$ on its
main diagonal.

\begin{proposition}[Vanishing and Exploding Gradients in RNNs]
\label{thm:vanish-explode}
Let $\rho = \|W_h\|_2$ (spectral norm) and $M_\sigma = \sup_z |\sigma'(z)|$.
Then
\begin{equation}
  \left\|\prod_{k=t}^{T-1}
    \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_k}\right\|_2
  \;\leq\; \left(M_\sigma\,\rho\right)^{T-t}.
  \label{eq:vanish-bound}
\end{equation}
In particular: (i) if $M_\sigma\,\rho < 1$, gradients vanish exponentially with
$T-t$; (ii) if $\rho > 1/M_\sigma$, they can grow exponentially
(\emph{exploding gradients}).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By sub-multiplicativity and~\eqref{eq:rnn-jacobian},
$\|\partial \mathbf{h}_{k+1}/\partial \mathbf{h}_k\|_2 \leq M_\sigma\,\rho$.
Applying this bound $T-t$ times yields~\eqref{eq:vanish-bound}.
For the logistic sigmoid $M_\sigma = 1/4$; for $\tanh$, $M_\sigma = 1$.
If $M_\sigma\,\rho < 1$ the right-hand side decays geometrically; the lower
explosive bound follows by considering Jacobians with a shared dominant eigenvector.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Gradient clipping \cite{pascanu2013gradient} % [CITE: Pascanu et al. 2013, On the difficulty of training recurrent neural networks]
is the standard remedy for explosion, but there is no analogous in-architecture fix
for vanishing gradients in the vanilla RNN.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Practical consequences for financial text.}
An S\&P~500 earnings-call transcript is typically 8,000--12,000 tokens.
Management guidance on page~20 may negate a claim on page~3.
Proposition~\ref{thm:vanish-explode} implies the gradient from position $t = 100$
contributes essentially nothing to $W_h$ after $T - t \approx 9{,}900$ BPTT
steps --- making long-range financial dependencies practically unlearnable.

\subsection{LSTMs and GRUs}
\label{sec:sequential-lstm}

\subsubsection{Long Short-Term Memory}

The Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) unit was introduced by \citet{hochreiter1997lstm} % [CITE: Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997, Long Short-Term Memory]
precisely to overcome the vanishing-gradient problem.
The key innovation is a \emph{cell state} $\mathbf{c}_t$ with an \emph{additive}
update path, gated by three sigmoid-activated vectors.

\begin{definition}[LSTM Equations]
\label{def:lstm}
Let $[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t] \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h + d_x}$ denote
concatenation. The LSTM update at step $t$ is:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{f}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_f\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]
    + \mathbf{b}_f\right), \label{eq:lstm-forget}\\
  \mathbf{i}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_i\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]
    + \mathbf{b}_i\right), \label{eq:lstm-input}\\
  \mathbf{g}_t &= \tanh\!\left(W_g\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]
    + \mathbf{b}_g\right), \label{eq:lstm-candidate}\\
  \mathbf{c}_t &= \mathbf{f}_t \odot \mathbf{c}_{t-1}
    + \mathbf{i}_t \odot \mathbf{g}_t, \label{eq:lstm-cell}\\
  \mathbf{o}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_o\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]
    + \mathbf{b}_o\right), \label{eq:lstm-output}\\
  \mathbf{h}_t &= \mathbf{o}_t \odot \tanh(\mathbf{c}_t), \label{eq:lstm-hidden}
\end{align}
where $\odot$ denotes element-wise product and
$W_f, W_i, W_g, W_o \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times (d_h + d_x)}$ are learnable
weight matrices.
\end{definition}

Intuitive interpretations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Forget gate} $\mathbf{f}_t \in (0,1)^{d_h}$:
        fraction of $\mathbf{c}_{t-1}$ to erase ($f_{t,j}\approx 0$: discard;
        $f_{t,j}\approx 1$: retain). In finance, clears irrelevant preamble
        sentiment as the transcript shifts to core earnings figures.
  \item \textbf{Input gate} $\mathbf{i}_t \in (0,1)^{d_h}$:
        fraction of candidate $\mathbf{g}_t$ to write; filters boilerplate
        language in 10-K filings.
  \item \textbf{Candidate} $\mathbf{g}_t \in (-1,1)^{d_h}$:
        new information from current input and recent context.
  \item \textbf{Output gate} $\mathbf{o}_t \in (0,1)^{d_h}$:
        which cell-state components are exposed as $\mathbf{h}_t$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why the cell state avoids vanishing gradients.}
The update~\eqref{eq:lstm-cell} is \emph{additive} in $\mathbf{g}_t$, unlike the
purely multiplicative vanilla RNN update~\eqref{eq:rnn-hidden}.
The cell-state gradient satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{c}_t}
  = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{c}_{t+1}} \odot \mathbf{f}_{t+1}
    + \text{(terms via } \mathbf{h}_t\text{)}.
  \label{eq:lstm-cellgrad}
\end{equation}
When $\mathbf{f}_{t+1} \approx \mathbf{1}$, the gradient is transmitted without
decay --- the \emph{constant error carousel} of \citeauthor{hochreiter1997lstm}. % [CITE: Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997]

\subsubsection{Gated Recurrent Units}

\citet{cho2014gru} % [CITE: Cho et al. 2014, Learning Phrase Representations using RNN Encoder-Decoder for Statistical Machine Translation]
proposed the GRU, which merges cell state and hidden state, reducing parameters
while retaining essential gating.

\begin{definition}[GRU Equations]
\label{def:gru}
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{z}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_z\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]\right),
  \label{eq:gru-update}\\
  \mathbf{r}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_r\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]\right),
  \label{eq:gru-reset}\\
  \tilde{\mathbf{h}}_t &= \tanh\!\left(
    W\,[\mathbf{r}_t \odot \mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]\right),
  \label{eq:gru-candidate}\\
  \mathbf{h}_t &= (1 - \mathbf{z}_t) \odot \mathbf{h}_{t-1}
    + \mathbf{z}_t \odot \tilde{\mathbf{h}}_t,
  \label{eq:gru-hidden}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{z}_t$ is the \emph{update gate}, $\mathbf{r}_t$ is the
\emph{reset gate}, and $W_z, W_r, W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times (d_h + d_x)}$.
\end{definition}

A GRU has $\approx 3(d_h+d_x)d_h$ parameters versus $4(d_h+d_x)d_h$ for an LSTM.
LSTMs outperform GRUs on tasks with very long-range dependencies; GRUs are preferred
under data or compute constraints \cite{chung2014empirical}. % [CITE: Chung et al. 2014, Empirical Evaluation of Gated Recurrent Neural Networks on Sequence Modeling]

\subsubsection{Bidirectional RNNs}

A standard LSTM sees only the prefix $\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_t$.
A \emph{bidirectional} LSTM \cite{schuster1997bilstm} % [CITE: Schuster & Paliwal 1997, Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Networks]
runs one forward and one backward pass, concatenating results:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_t =
  [\,\overrightarrow{\mathbf{h}}_t;\,\overleftarrow{\mathbf{h}}_t\,],
  \quad
  \overrightarrow{\mathbf{h}}_t =
    \mathrm{LSTM}_{\mathrm{fwd}}(\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_t),
  \quad
  \overleftarrow{\mathbf{h}}_t =
    \mathrm{LSTM}_{\mathrm{bwd}}(\mathbf{x}_T,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_t).
  \label{eq:bilstm}
\end{equation}
BiLSTMs were the dominant architecture for financial NER and sentiment classification
before Transformers; applications include credit-event extraction from news
\cite{araci2019finbert} % [CITE: Araci 2019, FinBERT: Financial Sentiment Analysis with Pre-trained Language Models]
and earnings-surprise prediction from analyst reports.

\subsection{The Attention Mechanism}
\label{sec:sequential-attention}

Even with gated architectures, an RNN encoder must compress the input into a
fixed-length vector before the decoder can generate output.
\citet{bahdanau2015attention} % [CITE: Bahdanau, Cho & Bengio 2015, Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate]
proposed \emph{attention}, allowing the decoder to consult all encoder hidden states
at each step.

\paragraph{Seq2seq notation.}
Let $\mathbf{h}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{h}_T \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$ be encoder hidden
states and $\mathbf{s}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_s}$ the decoder state at step $t$.

\begin{definition}[Additive (Bahdanau) Attention]
\label{def:bahdanau-attention}
The \emph{alignment score} between $\mathbf{s}_t$ and $\mathbf{h}_i$ is
\begin{equation}
  e_{t,i} = \mathbf{v}^{\top}
    \tanh\!\left(W_s\,\mathbf{s}_t + W_h\,\mathbf{h}_i\right),
  \label{eq:alignment-score}
\end{equation}
with $W_s \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a \times d_s}$, $W_h \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a \times d_h}$,
and $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a}$ learnable.
The \emph{attention weights} and \emph{context vector} are:
\begin{align}
  \alpha_{t,i} &= \frac{\exp(e_{t,i})}{\sum_{j=1}^{T} \exp(e_{t,j})},
  \label{eq:attention-weights}\\
  \mathbf{c}_t &= \sum_{i=1}^{T} \alpha_{t,i}\,\mathbf{h}_i.
  \label{eq:context-vector}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Attention Weights Form a Probability Distribution]
\label{thm:attention-prob}
For any $e_{t,1},\ldots,e_{t,T} \in \mathbb{R}$, the weights
$\{\alpha_{t,i}\}_{i=1}^T$ in~\eqref{eq:attention-weights}
satisfy $\alpha_{t,i} > 0$ for all $i$ and $\sum_{i=1}^{T} \alpha_{t,i} = 1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Positivity: $\exp(\cdot) > 0$.
Summation: $\sum_i \alpha_{t,i} =
\sum_i \exp(e_{t,i}) / \sum_j \exp(e_{t,j}) = 1$. \qedhere
\end{proof}

The context vector $\mathbf{c}_t$ is a convex combination of encoder states.
In a financial summariser, the decoder generating a sentence about net income assigns
high $\alpha_{t,i}$ to tokens where income figures appear in the source.

\paragraph{Connection to self-attention.}
Applying attention within a \emph{single} sequence --- each token attending to all
others --- yields \emph{self-attention}, the core primitive of the Transformer
(Section~\ref{sec:transformer}).
The dot-product attention of \citet{vaswani2017attention} % [CITE: Vaswani et al. 2017, Attention Is All You Need]
replaces the additive score~\eqref{eq:alignment-score} with a scaled inner product,
reducing cost from $O(Td_a)$ to $O(Td_k)$.

\section{The Transformer Architecture}
\label{sec:transformer}

The Transformer, introduced by \citet{vaswani2017attention}, % [CITE: Vaswani et al. 2017, Attention Is All You Need]
dispenses with recurrence and relies exclusively on attention to model dependencies.
Three major advantages motivate it for large-scale financial NLP\@:
(i) all token pairs are connected in $O(1)$ layers, eliminating the
vanishing-gradient bottleneck;
(ii) the attention computation is highly parallelisable;
and (iii) learned attention patterns are interpretable.

% ---
\subsection{Self-Attention (Mathematics)}
\label{sec:transformer-selfattn}

\begin{definition}[Scaled Dot-Product Attention]
\label{def:scaled-dot-product-attn}
Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ be the $n$-token input matrix.
Given projection matrices
$W^Q, W^K \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_k}$ and
$W^V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_v}$,
define
\begin{equation}
  Q = X W^Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k},\quad
  K = X W^K \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k},\quad
  V = X W^V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_v}.
  \label{eq:qkv}
\end{equation}
The \emph{scaled dot-product attention} output is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Attention}(Q, K, V)
  = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V
  \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_v},
  \label{eq:scaled-dot-product}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{softmax}$ is applied row-wise.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Dimensional accounting.}
$QK^{\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$; entry $(i,j)$ is
$\mathbf{q}_i^{\top}\mathbf{k}_j$.
Row-wise softmax gives the attention matrix
$A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ with $A_{i,j} > 0$ and $\sum_j A_{i,j} = 1$
(Proposition~\ref{thm:attention-prob}).
The output $AV$ represents token $i$ as $\sum_j A_{i,j}\mathbf{v}_j$, a convex
combination of value vectors.

\begin{proposition}[$\sqrt{d_k}$ Scaling Stabilises Softmax Gradients]
\label{thm:sqrt-dk-scaling}
Suppose query and key components are i.i.d.\ mean-zero unit-variance.
Then
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{q}_i^{\top} \mathbf{k}_j) = d_k,
  \label{eq:dot-product-var}
\end{equation}
so dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ normalises the pre-softmax variance to one.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\mathbf{q}_i^{\top}\mathbf{k}_j = \sum_{\ell=1}^{d_k} q_\ell k_\ell$
with components mutually independent, $\mathrm{Var}(q_\ell) = \mathrm{Var}(k_\ell) = 1$.
Since $\mathbb{E}[q_\ell k_\ell] = 0$ and
$\mathbb{E}[(q_\ell k_\ell)^2] = \mathbb{E}[q_\ell^2]\mathbb{E}[k_\ell^2] = 1$,
each term has unit variance, so
$\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{q}_i^{\top}\mathbf{k}_j) = d_k$.
For large $d_k$ the logits concentrate near $\pm\sqrt{d_k}$, pushing softmax into
its saturated regime where
$\partial\sigma(z)/\partial z = \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z)) \approx 0$.
Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ normalises variance to $1$, restoring gradient flow.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Causal masking for autoregressive generation.}
In decoder-only models, token $i$ must not attend to $j > i$.
This is enforced by
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{S}_{ij} = \begin{cases}
    \mathbf{q}_i^{\top}\mathbf{k}_j/\sqrt{d_k} & j \leq i,\\
    -\infty & j > i,
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:causal-mask}
\end{equation}
so that $\mathrm{softmax}(\tilde{S}_{i,:})$ assigns zero weight to future positions.

\subsection{Multi-Head Attention}
\label{sec:transformer-mha}

\begin{definition}[Multi-Head Attention]
\label{def:mha}
Let $W^Q_i, W^K_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_k}$,
$W^V_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_v}$ for $i = 1,\ldots,h$,
and $W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{h d_v \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$.
Define $\mathrm{head}_i = \mathrm{Attention}(XW^Q_i, XW^K_i, XW^V_i)$ and
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MultiHead}(X)
  = \mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\ldots,\mathrm{head}_h)\,W^O
  \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}.
  \label{eq:multihead}
\end{equation}
In the original Transformer: $h = 8$, $d_{\mathrm{model}} = 512$, $d_k = d_v = 64$.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Computational cost.}
Summing $O(n^2 d_k)$ per head over $h$ heads gives $O(n^2 d_{\mathrm{model}})$.
For $n = 4{,}096$ tokens, the attention matrix requires $\approx 134\,\mathrm{M}$
values per layer, motivating efficient variants such as FlashAttention
\cite{dao2022flashattention}. % [CITE: Dao et al. 2022, FlashAttention: Fast and Memory-Efficient Exact Attention with IO-Awareness]

\paragraph{What different heads learn.}
Probing studies \cite{clark2019bert} % [CITE: Clark et al. 2019, What Does BERT Look at? An Analysis of BERT's Attention]
show lower-layer heads capture syntactic dependency relations (subject--verb
agreement) and deeper heads capture semantic associations (co-reference, entity
types). In financial models, heads specialise in temporal relations
(``last quarter'' $\to$ figures), entity linking, and negation scope.

\begin{remark}[Connection to the Kernel Trick]
\label{rem:kernel-trick}
The attention output for token $i$ can be written
$\sum_j k(\mathbf{q}_i,\mathbf{k}_j)\mathbf{v}_j / Z_i$,
where $k(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{k}) = \exp(\mathbf{q}^\top\mathbf{k}/\sqrt{d_k})$
is an exponential kernel and $Z_i$ is the partition function.
The kernel analogy \cite{tsai2019transformer} % [CITE: Tsai et al. 2019, Transformer Dissection: A Unified Understanding of Transformer's Attention via the Lens of Kernel]
motivates random-feature approximations (Performers, Random Feature Attention) that
reduce the $O(n^2)$ complexity to $O(n)$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Positional Encoding}
\label{sec:transformer-pos}

The attention operation~\eqref{eq:scaled-dot-product} is permutation-equivariant.
Positional information must therefore be injected explicitly.

\begin{definition}[Sinusoidal Positional Encoding]
\label{def:positional-encoding}
For position $\mathrm{pos} \in \{0,\ldots,n-1\}$ and index
$i \in \{0,\ldots,d_{\mathrm{model}}/2 - 1\}$:
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},\,2i)}
    &= \sin\!\left(\tfrac{\mathrm{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_{\mathrm{model}}}}\right),
  \label{eq:pe-sin}\\
  \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},\,2i+1)}
    &= \cos\!\left(\tfrac{\mathrm{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_{\mathrm{model}}}}\right).
  \label{eq:pe-cos}
\end{align}
The positionally-augmented input is
$\tilde{X}_{\mathrm{pos},:} = X_{\mathrm{pos},:} + \mathrm{PE}_{\mathrm{pos},:}$.
\end{definition}

Let $\omega_i = 10000^{-2i/d_{\mathrm{model}}}$.
For small $i$, $\omega_i \approx 1$ and the encoding oscillates rapidly (fine
position); for large $i$, $\omega_i \approx 0$ (coarse position).
This multi-scale design is analogous to a Fourier basis.

\begin{proposition}[Relative-Position Linearity of Sinusoidal Encoding]
\label{thm:pe-relative}
For any fixed offset $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ and index $i$,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos}+k,\,2i)} \\
    \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos}+k,\,2i+1)}
  \end{pmatrix}
  = R(\omega_i k)
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},\,2i)} \\
    \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},\,2i+1)}
  \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:pe-rotation}
\end{equation}
where $R(\theta) = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta
& \cos\theta \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ depends only on $k$, not on $\mathrm{pos}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Set $s = \sin(\omega_i\,\mathrm{pos})$, $c = \cos(\omega_i\,\mathrm{pos})$.
By the angle-addition identities:
\begin{align*}
  \sin(\omega_i(\mathrm{pos}+k)) &= s\cos(\omega_i k) + c\sin(\omega_i k),\\
  \cos(\omega_i(\mathrm{pos}+k)) &= c\cos(\omega_i k) - s\sin(\omega_i k).
\end{align*}
In matrix form this is
$R(\omega_i k)[\mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},2i)},\mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},2i+1)}]^\top$,
where $R(\omega_i k)$ depends only on $k$ and $\omega_i$, not $\mathrm{pos}$.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{thm:pe-relative} implies
$\mathrm{PE}_{\mathrm{pos}}^\top\mathrm{PE}_{\mathrm{pos}'}$ depends only on
$|\mathrm{pos}-\mathrm{pos}'|$, making relative position linearly decodable.
This motivated relative positional encodings \cite{shaw2018relative} % [CITE: Shaw et al. 2018, Self-Attention with Relative Position Representations]
and Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) \cite{su2024rope}, % [CITE: Su et al. 2024, RoFormer: Enhanced Transformer with Rotary Position Embedding]
which embed the rotation directly into query--key dot products.

\subsection{Encoder--Decoder Structure}
\label{sec:transformer-encdec}

\paragraph{Encoder layer.}
Let $X^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ be layer-$\ell$ input.
With residual connections \cite{he2016resnet} % [CITE: He et al. 2016, Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition]
and Layer Normalisation \cite{ba2016layernorm}: % [CITE: Ba et al. 2016, Layer Normalization]
\begin{align}
  Z^{(\ell)}   &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
    X^{(\ell)} +
    \mathrm{MultiHead}(X^{(\ell)}, X^{(\ell)}, X^{(\ell)})\right),
  \label{eq:enc-layer-z}\\
  X^{(\ell+1)} &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
    Z^{(\ell)} + \mathrm{FFN}(Z^{(\ell)})\right),
  \label{eq:enc-layer-out}
\end{align}
with
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{FFN}(\mathbf{z})
  = \max(0,\, \mathbf{z} W_1 + \mathbf{b}_1)\,W_2 + \mathbf{b}_2,
  \label{eq:ffn}
\end{equation}
where $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_{\mathrm{ff}}}$,
$W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{ff}} \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$,
$d_{\mathrm{ff}} = 4\,d_{\mathrm{model}}$.
The $\max(0,\cdot)$ is ReLU; later variants use GELU \cite{hendrycks2016gelu}. % [CITE: Hendrycks & Gimpel 2016, Gaussian Error Linear Units]

Residual connections ensure direct gradient pathways and LayerNorm stabilises
activations across the $d_{\mathrm{model}}$ dimension.

\paragraph{Decoder layer.}
The decoder has three sub-layers: (i) masked self-attention
(mask~\eqref{eq:causal-mask}), (ii) \emph{cross-attention} with encoder output, and
(iii) FFN.
With $Y^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ and
$H = X^{(L_{\mathrm{enc}})}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CrossAttn}(Y^{(\ell)}, H)
  = \mathrm{MultiHead}\!\left(Y^{(\ell)} W^Q,\; H W^K,\; H W^V\right),
  \label{eq:cross-attn}
\end{equation}
where $Y^{(\ell)}$ provides queries and $H$ provides keys and values.
This is the direct parallel of the Bahdanau context vector $\mathbf{c}_t$
from~\eqref{eq:context-vector}, computed in a single matrix operation.

\begin{remark}
Figure~\ref{fig:transformer-architecture} shows the architecture schematically.
% [FIGURE: fig:transformer-architecture --- Standard Transformer encoder-decoder
%  diagram with token embedding, positional encoding, $N$ stacked encoder layers,
%  $N$ stacked decoder layers, and output projection.
%  After Vaswani et al.\ (2017) Figure~1.]
The base model uses $L = 6$ layers; the large model uses $L = 24$.
Modern LLMs use $L = 32$--$96$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Pre-training, Fine-tuning, and BERT vs.\ GPT}
\label{sec:transformer-pretrain}

\begin{definition}[Pre-training]
\label{def:pretraining}
\emph{Pre-training} is the first phase: training a language model on a large
unlabelled corpus using a self-supervised objective whose labels are derived from
the data itself (predicting masked tokens, or predicting the next token).
The pre-trained weights provide initialisation and inductive bias for fine-tuning.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Fine-tuning]
\label{def:finetuning}
\emph{Fine-tuning} is the second phase: updating pre-trained parameters on a smaller
labelled dataset $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{task}}$ for a specific downstream task.
Pre-training substantially reduces labelled-data and compute requirements.
\end{definition}

\subsubsection{Masked Language Modelling: BERT}

BERT \cite{devlin2019bert} % [CITE: Devlin et al. 2019, BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding]
(Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) is an encoder-only
Transformer trained on two self-supervised objectives.

\paragraph{Masked Language Model (MLM).}
$15\%$ of input tokens are selected; $80\%$ replaced with \texttt{[MASK]},
$10\%$ with a random token, and $10\%$ left unchanged.
The model predicts original tokens at masked positions:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MLM}}(\theta)
  = -\sum_{i \in \mathcal{M}}
    \log p_\theta\!\left(w_i \mid \mathbf{x}_{\backslash \mathcal{M}}\right),
  \label{eq:mlm-loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{M}$ is the set of masked positions and
$\mathbf{x}_{\backslash\mathcal{M}}$ the unmasked context.
Bidirectional attention means both left and right context inform each prediction.

\paragraph{Next Sentence Prediction (NSP).}
A \texttt{[CLS]} binary head predicts whether sentence $B$ follows sentence $A$.
\citet{liu2019roberta} % [CITE: Liu et al. 2019, RoBERTa: A Robustly Optimized BERT Pretraining Approach]
later showed NSP is unhelpful and removed it in RoBERTa.

\subsubsection{Causal Language Modelling: GPT}

GPT \cite{radford2018gpt} % [CITE: Radford et al. 2018, Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training]
and successors (GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4) are decoder-only models trained on the causal
language modelling (CLM) objective:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CLM}}(\theta)
  = -\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{t-1}).
  \label{eq:clm-loss}
\end{equation}
The causal mask~\eqref{eq:causal-mask} makes this a proper autoregressive density
model, naturally supporting text generation.

\subsubsection{Encoder-Only vs.\ Decoder-Only: Task Suitability}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Encoder-only (BERT-family):}
        Bidirectional attention conditioned on the full input.
        Ideal for classification (sentiment, credit rating), token-level extraction
        (NER, financial metric extraction), and semantic similarity.

  \item \textbf{Decoder-only (GPT-family):}
        Natural generative engine for summarisation, report drafting, and
        instruction following (RLHF fine-tuning \cite{ouyang2022rlhf}). % [CITE: Ouyang et al. 2022, Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback]
        Unidirectional attention is suboptimal for tasks requiring full-context
        understanding.

  \item \textbf{Encoder--decoder (T5, BART):}
        Well suited to sequence-to-sequence tasks: abstractive summarisation of
        earnings calls, multi-document QA \cite{lewis2020bart}. % [CITE: Lewis et al. 2020, BART: Denoising Sequence-to-Sequence Pre-training for Natural Language Generation, Translation, and Comprehension]
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Domain-Adapted Financial Models}

General-purpose models under-perform on financial text due to distinctive vocabulary
(EBITDA, contango, covenants), regulatory legalese, and distribution shift from
web-crawl pre-training data.

\begin{example}[FinBERT]
\label{ex:finbert}
\citet{araci2019finbert} % [CITE: Araci 2019, FinBERT: Financial Sentiment Analysis with Pre-trained Language Models]
released FinBERT, a BERT-base model further pre-trained on Reuters/Bloomberg news,
10-K filings, and earnings call transcripts.
On the Financial PhraseBank dataset \cite{malo2014phrasebank}, % [CITE: Malo et al. 2014, Good Debt or Bad Debt: Detecting Semantic Orientations in Economic Texts]
FinBERT achieves over $97\%$ accuracy on financial phrase sentiment classification,
outperforming general BERT-base by approximately 8 percentage points.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[BloombergGPT]
\label{ex:bloomberggpt}
\citet{wu2023bloomberggpt} % [CITE: Wu et al. 2023, BloombergGPT: A Large Language Model for Finance]
trained BloombergGPT, a 50-billion-parameter decoder-only model, on $363$ billion
tokens from Bloomberg financial archives plus $345$ billion general tokens.
The mixed strategy outperformed purely financial and purely general baselines on
ConvFinQA (numerical reasoning over tables) and FiQA (opinion-based QA).
BloombergGPT illustrates that domain adaptation at scale requires both domain
knowledge and general linguistic competence.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
Continued pre-training of an open-source base model (Llama, Mistral) on
$10$--$50$ billion domain tokens, followed by instruction fine-tuning on curated
financial datasets, is currently the most cost-effective domain-adaptation strategy
for practitioners.
\end{remark}
