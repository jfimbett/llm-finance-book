01

Recap: the two formulas you need

How two ranked lists become one, and how a stack of news articles becomes a single risk score.
Formula 1 · merging two search engines

Reciprocal rank fusion: trust what both retrievers agree on

Hybrid search runs two retrievers — a "dense" semantic one and a "sparse" keyword one. Each returns its own ranked list. RRF blends them: a document scores high when it sits near the top of both lists, not just one.

\[ \mathrm{RRF}(d) = \sum_{r \in R} \frac{1}{\kappa + \mathrm{rank}_r(d)}, \qquad \kappa = 60. \] \(R = \{\text{dense}, \text{sparse}\}\); a document missing from a list gets rank \(\infty\) (that term contributes \(0\)). The constant \(\kappa\) damps the influence of the very top ranks so no single list can dominate.
the move No training, no learned weights — just reciprocal ranks added together. It rewards cross-signal agreement for free.
Formula 2 · text becomes a calibrated risk

The Adverse Media Index: one number for "how risky is this subject?"

After retrieval, the agent reads each article and rates it on relevance, source credibility, and how recent it is — then squashes the total into a probability between 0 and 1.

\[ \mathrm{AMI}(s) = \sigma\!\left(\alpha + \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_i\, c_i\, t_i\, \mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{e}_i\right), \qquad \sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}. \] Per document \(i\): relevance \(r_i\), credibility \(c_i\), recency \(t_i\), and content score \(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{e}_i\); \(\alpha\) is the baseline intercept (the prior on a random subject). The factors multiply, so any weak component drags the whole term toward zero.
the bigger picture A continuous score, not a binary flag — so risk can be compared across subjects, tracked over time, and fed into a quantitative customer-risk model.
Where the two scores live

RRF and AMI sit at different stages of the same pipeline

RRF fuses the retriever's two ranked lists; the AMI is computed by the output formatter from the reader's per-document evidence scores.

StageProduces
Hybrid retrieverdense rank, sparse rank per doc
→ RRFfused ranking → top-\(k\) passages
Reader / ranker\(r_i, c_i, t_i, \mathbf{e}_i\) per doc
→ AMIscalar risk \(\in [0,1]\)
why continuous A binary low/high label cannot be compared across subjects, tracked over time, or fed into a quantitative customer-risk model.

Case study: the same two scores in Python

Notebook 11 — deterministic, no network. Confirm your hand calculations, then stress-test them.
Case study · Part 1

RRF fusion in Python

From code/notebooks/11-regtech-compliance-aml/demo.ipynb — hybrid retrieval fusion, deterministic and offline.

import math
KAPPA = 60
docs        = ['court filing','news article','press release','blog post','registry record']
dense_rank  = [1, 3, 2, 5, 4]
sparse_rank = [2, 1, 4, 3, 5]

def rrf(ranks):
    return sum(1.0 / (KAPPA + r) for r in ranks)

fused = {d: rrf([dr, sr]) for d, dr, sr in zip(docs, dense_rank, sparse_rank)}
for d, s in sorted(fused.items(), key=lambda kv: -kv[1]):
    print(f'{d:16s} RRF={s:.5f}')
Case study · Part 2

AMI scoring in Python

The calibrated Adverse Media Index, same notebook — a sigmoid over the multiplicative per-article terms.

import math
def sigmoid(x): return 1.0 / (1.0 + math.exp(-x))
alpha = -2.0
# (relevance, credibility, recency, content_score) per retrieved article
articles = [(0.9,0.8,1.0,2.0), (0.6,0.5,0.7,1.2), (0.3,0.9,0.4,0.5)]
ami = sigmoid(alpha + sum(r*c*t*e for (r,c,t,e) in articles))
print(f'AMI = {ami:.3f}  (probability of genuine AML risk in [0,1])')

Discussion: governance clinic

When is a compliance LLM "high-risk", and how do you keep a tired reviewer genuinely in the loop?
Discussion · Part 1

High-risk or not? Place each system on the EU AI Act tier

For each system, decide the EU AI Act tier (Annex III) and justify it. The dividing line is the degree of automation — does the model assist, or does it decide?

  1. An LLM that summarises a news article for a human analyst, no automated decision.
  2. An LLM that auto-declines onboarding when \(\mathrm{AMI} > 0.85\).
  3. An LLM that files a SAR with FinCEN with no human review.
frame your answer around Degree of automation, individual impact, GDPR Art. 22 review rights, and where a human genuinely outperforms the model.
Discussion · Part 2

Your reviewers rubber-stamp 95% of alerts — fix it

Your bank's reviewers clear 95% of alerts in under 30 seconds — classic automation bias. Propose two concrete changes to the queue or interface that would restore substantive review.

Levers to pull
  • Task decomposition — break the decision into checkable sub-judgments
  • Uncertainty communication — surface the model's confidence, not just its verdict
  • Override feedback — close the loop so disagreement teaches the system
  • Queue ordering — front-load the genuinely hard cases
the catch A human who clears an alert in 30 seconds is not reviewing it. Good HITL design gives the human a task they genuinely do better than the model.

Solution · Problem 1

RRF by hand: consistency beats a single-signal #1

Task A. \(\mathrm{RRF}(d) = \frac{1}{60+\text{dense}} + \frac{1}{60+\text{sparse}}\):

PassageDenseSparseRRF
court filing12\(\tfrac{1}{61}+\tfrac{1}{62}=0.03252\)
news article31\(\tfrac{1}{63}+\tfrac{1}{61}=0.03227\)
press release24\(\tfrac{1}{62}+\tfrac{1}{64}=0.03175\)
blog post53\(\tfrac{1}{65}+\tfrac{1}{63}=0.03126\)
registry record45\(\tfrac{1}{64}+\tfrac{1}{65}=0.03101\)
Task B & C Order: court filing ≻ news article ≻ press release ≻ blog ≻ registry. News article is #1 by sparse but only 3rd by dense; court filing is #1 dense and #2 sparse — its consistency across both signals lifts it above the single-signal winner. That is exactly the robustness property RRF buys.
Solution · Problem 2

AMI by hand: one weak factor sinks the whole term

Task A. Contributions \(r_i c_i t_i (\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{e}_i)\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{Art. 1} &= 0.9 \times 0.8 \times 1.0 \times 2.0 = 1.440 \\ \text{Art. 2} &= 0.6 \times 0.5 \times 0.7 \times 1.2 = 0.252 \\ \text{Art. 3} &= 0.3 \times 0.9 \times 0.4 \times 0.5 = 0.054 \end{aligned} \] Task B. Sum \(= 1.746\); add \(\alpha = -2.0 \Rightarrow -0.254\): \[ \mathrm{AMI}(s) = \sigma(-0.254) = \frac{1}{1+e^{0.254}} \approx \mathbf{0.437}. \]
Task C — why the most credible article contributes least
Despite the highest credibility (\(c_3 = 0.9\)), Article 3 is crushed by low relevance (0.3) and stale recency (0.4) — the factors multiply, so any single weak factor drags the whole term down. Credibility cannot rescue a doc that is barely about the subject or years old.
Wrap-up

Key takeaways — and where Lecture 12 goes

Problem 1 — RRF
  • Fusion rewards cross-signal agreement — a doc strong on both retrievers beats a single-signal #1
  • \(\kappa = 60\) compresses rank differences; no learned weighting needed
Problem 2 — AMI
  • Multiplicative factors mean one weak component (relevance, recency) dominates — credibility alone cannot save a stale, off-target doc
  • \(\alpha\) sets the prior; \(\mathbf{w}\) encodes offence severity and must be calibrated on enforcement outcomes
Discussion
  • Degree of automation decides the Annex III tier — assist vs. decide / file
  • Good HITL gives the human a task they do better than the model

Appendix — stretch problems

Two optional extensions: entity-resolution thresholds and AMI temporal decay.
Appendix · stretch problem 1

Entity resolution: how high should the match threshold sit?

A sanctions screener outputs a calibrated match probability \(P(\hat y = 1 \mid x_1, x_2)\). You onboard 1,000,000 customers/year; the prevalence of true sanctions hits is \(\approx 1\) in 50,000.

  1. At a threshold giving recall \(0.999\) and FPR \(0.02\), how many false positives must analysts review per year? (Hint: \(\approx 0.02 \times 10^6\).)
  2. Lowering the threshold raises recall toward \(1.0\) but inflates FPR. Argue, using the cost asymmetry, why sanctions screening still favours the higher recall despite the review burden.
  3. How would a two-stage (ANN blocking → cross-encoder) pipeline change the operating point versus a single fuzzy-match stage?
Appendix · stretch problem 2

AMI temporal decay: how fast should old coverage fade?

Make recency explicit: the recency weight is \(t_i = e^{-\lambda (T - T_i)}\) with \(T - T_i\) in days.

  1. For \(\lambda = 0.01\)/day, compute \(t_i\) for an article 30, 180, and 365 days old. Confirm \(t \to 0\) slowly.
  2. Re-run Problem 2's Article 1, now dated 365 days ago instead of today. Recompute its contribution and the resulting AMI.
  3. A subject was acquitted 4 years ago but old coverage still surfaces. Argue for an aggressive (large \(\lambda\)) vs. conservative (small \(\lambda\)) decay, and the fairness implications of each.
the fairness stakes Decay choice is not just engineering — it decides how long a person carries the weight of stale or overturned allegations.
