\section{Artificial Intelligence, Jobs, and Tasks}
\label{sec:ai-jobs-tasks}

\begin{context}
Consider a finance professor at a research university.  Her professional
duties span a wide range of activities: preparing lecture notes and slides,
grading exams and student work, supervising doctoral students, conducting
original research, analysing data, building quantitative models, hiring and
managing research assistants, attending conferences, discussing working papers
with colleagues, navigating reimbursement systems, managing travel logistics,
directing a master's programme, refereeing manuscripts for journals, and
applying for research grants.  Each of these activities calls on a different
mix of domain knowledge, creativity, social judgment, and routine processing.
They are, in the language of labour economics, \emph{tasks}---discrete units
of work---and a professorship is nothing more than a bundle of them.

This observation is the starting point for understanding the economic impact
of large language models on professional labour.  The question is not whether
AI will ``replace'' the finance professor, but rather: for which tasks within
her bundle does AI offer a substitution technology, and for which tasks does
it instead amplify human performance?  These two effects have opposite
implications for labour demand and professional value creation.

\subsection{The Task-Based Framework}
\label{sec:tasks-framework}

The insight that automation's impact depends on the distribution of tasks
within occupations---not on the occupation label itself---dates to the
foundational work of \citet{autor2003skill}.
% [CITE: Autor, D., Levy, F. & Murnane, R.J. (2003). The Skill Content of Recent Technological Change. QJE, 118(4), 1279–1333.]
\citeauthor{acemoglu2019automation} formalised this into a general-equilibrium
framework in which automation displaces labour from tasks that capital can perform
more cheaply while simultaneously creating demand for new, complementary tasks that
require human input \cite{acemoglu2019automation}.
% [CITE: Acemoglu, D. & Restrepo, P. (2019). Automation and New Tasks. JEP, 33(2), 3–30.]

Recent empirical work applies this framework specifically to artificial
intelligence.  \citet{hampole2025ai} construct occupation-level AI exposure
scores by matching AI and machine-learning patent text to the task descriptions
of occupations from the O*NET database, and then link these scores to
firm-level payroll and employment records over 2010--2023.  Their principal
findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A one standard-deviation increase in task-level AI exposure is associated with a
        modest reduction in relative labour demand---an aggregate effect
        that is nevertheless concentrated among workers whose entire job consists
        of highly exposed tasks.
  \item When AI exposure is near-complete---when virtually all tasks within an
        occupation are susceptible to substitution, leaving no room for
        reallocation---the authors report a substantial relative employment decline.
  \item Workers who can \emph{reallocate} effort toward tasks where they retain
        a comparative advantage---judgment, relationship management, creative
        problem-setting, domain-specific synthesis---are insulated from and may
        even benefit from AI-driven productivity improvements in the rest of their
        bundle.
\end{enumerate}

The mechanism is intuitive: when AI substitutes for task $A$ (say, routine
data collection), a worker who also performs tasks $B$ and $C$ (say, client
relationship management and research synthesis) can redirect the time freed up
from $A$ toward $B$ and $C$.  The aggregate employment effect depends on whether
the productivity gain in $B$ and $C$ generates new demand for the worker's
services---a question that turns on the elasticity of demand for the outputs
of those tasks.

\citet{felten2021occupational} offer a complementary measure: their AI
Occupational Exposure (AIOE) index links ten AI application types to 52 human
abilities via O*NET, finding that high-skill, high-wage white-collar
occupations---including finance professionals, analysts, and managers---exhibit
the greatest AI exposure.
% [CITE: Felten, E., Raj, M. & Seamans, R. (2021). SMJ, 42(12), 2195–2217.]
This does not imply that these workers are most at risk; rather, it implies that
AI offers the greatest productivity amplification precisely for the occupations
where human cognition was already doing the most work.

\subsection{Implications for Finance Professionals}
\label{sec:tasks-finance}

Applied to the finance profession, the task-based lens yields a nuanced but
actionable picture.  Tasks such as managing expense submissions, scheduling
meetings, retrieving and cleaning data from public sources, formatting references
in a bibliography, and producing boilerplate disclosures are characterised by
high AI exposure: they are rule-bound, data-rich, and require no professional
judgment.  Large language models and AI agents already perform these tasks
competently, and the time savings are real.

By contrast, tasks such as formulating a novel research hypothesis, providing
mentorship feedback tailored to a specific doctoral student, chairing a
conference session and synthesising divergent empirical presentations,
exercising editorial judgment on a subtle identification strategy, or
negotiating with a portfolio company's management team---these require
contextual understanding, domain expertise, and relational sensitivity that
LLMs lack reliably.  They are the tasks that define a professional's
comparative advantage.

The implication is not passivity.  Finance professionals who invest in learning
to use AI tools for high-exposure tasks gain a compound advantage: they recover
time from low-value activities and can direct it toward high-value tasks where
human comparative advantage is greatest.  A finance professor who uses AI to
manage her travel reimbursements, draft referee reports from her marginal notes,
and produce first-pass data visualisations, can work on three papers
simultaneously rather than one---not because she works more hours, but because
AI compresses the high-exposure tasks that previously consumed a large fraction
of her week.

The same logic applies throughout finance.  An equity analyst who uses AI to
screen earnings call transcripts and extract structured financial data can
cover twice as many companies with equal or greater depth.  A compliance officer
whose AI agent monitors regulatory updates and flags potential violations can
focus human attention on the ambiguous cases requiring genuine legal judgment.
A quantitative researcher who uses AI to translate a mathematical strategy into
a first-draft implementation can iterate on model design rather than on syntax.
In each case, AI is not the analyst, officer, or researcher---it is the
infrastructure that makes scarce professional time go further.

\begin{remark}[This Book as a Case Study]
\label{rem:this-book-ai}
This book was written partly \emph{using} the tools it describes.  Large
portions of the exposition, code, exercises, and bibliographic research were
drafted or verified by AI assistants operating under human authorial supervision.
The experience of writing with AI illustrates the task-based argument directly:
drafting structured prose from an outline, searching the literature for a
specific citation, and generating syntactically correct \LaTeX{} for a matrix
equation are tasks the AI performs well.  Deciding whether a proof is
genuinely illuminating, choosing which financial example will resonate with
a mixed audience, and judging when the exposition is complete rather than
merely extensive---these remain firmly human.
\end{remark}

\end{context}

\section{A Brief History of Textual Analysis in Finance}
\label{sec:history}

\begin{context}
It is the autumn of 2006 and Paul Tetlock, then a doctoral student, is staring
at a spreadsheet.  On one side: a column of numbers representing the daily
fraction of negative words in the \emph{Wall Street Journal}'s \emph{Abreast of
the Market} column, running back to 1984.  On the other: the next-day return on
the Dow Jones Industrial Average.  The correlation is small.  But it is
there---and according to the textbooks that line his shelves, it should not be.
If markets are efficient, prices should already reflect the sentiment of the
country's most widely-read financial column.  Tetlock would publish this finding
in the \emph{Journal of Finance} \cite{tetlock2007giving}, and the paper would
become a founding document of a new field.  But the deeper story began forty
years earlier, in a social science department with no interest in stock prices
whatsoever.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Early Approaches: Keyword Counting and Dictionary Methods}
\label{sec:history-early}

The modern project of quantifying the meaning of text began in the social sciences.
In the 1950s and 1960s researchers in sociology and communication studies developed
\emph{content analysis} as a formal methodology: a researcher defines a coding scheme
of categories, reads passages, and assigns them to categories according to explicit
rules.  The goal was to turn a fundamentally qualitative object---human language---into
a quantitative variable amenable to statistical inference.

The methodological ambitions grew quickly.  In 1961 Philip Stone and colleagues at
Harvard published the \emph{General Inquirer} system
\cite{stone1966general}, % [CITE: Stone, P.J., Dunphy, D.C., Smith, M.S., Ogilvie, D.M. (1966). The General Inquirer: A Computer Approach to Content Analysis. MIT Press.]
a computer-assisted program that matched words in a document against hand-coded
psychological and semantic dictionaries.  The Harvard IV-4 dictionary, which became one
of its most widely used components, classifies roughly 11\,000 word stems into
categories such as \textsc{Positive}, \textsc{Negative}, \textsc{Strong},
\textsc{Weak}, \textsc{Active}, and \textsc{Passive}.  To compute a sentiment score for
a document one simply counts the number of words belonging to each category and
normalises by document length.  The appeal is transparency: every score can be
explained by pointing to the words that generated it.

Within finance, the landmark empirical application of dictionary-based sentiment analysis
is the paper by Paul Tetlock published in the \emph{Journal of Finance}
\cite{tetlock2007giving}. % [CITE: Tetlock, P.C. (2007). Giving Content to Investor Sentiment: The Role of Media in the Stock Market. Journal of Finance, 62(3), 1139–1168.]
Tetlock extracted the daily ``Abreast of the Market'' column from the \emph{Wall Street
Journal} over the period 1984--1999 and applied the General Inquirer's Harvard IV-4
dictionary to measure the fraction of negative words in each column.  His empirical
findings are striking: (i) high media negativity predicts downward pressure on Dow Jones
Industrial Average returns on the following trading day, followed by a mean-reversion
over the subsequent week; and (ii) unusually high or low negativity predicts high
trading volume.  He interprets the return predictability as consistent with investor
sentiment temporarily depressing prices below fundamental value, and the mean reversion
as the subsequent correction.

A companion study by \citet{tetlock2008words}
extended this approach to firm-level news: they showed that the fraction of negative
words in firm-specific stories from the Dow Jones Newswires predicts both short-run
stock returns and subsequent quarterly earnings, with the earnings predictability
concentrated in stories that directly discuss fundamentals.  This established that
media sentiment contains information beyond what contemporaneous prices already reflect.

The Tetlock study demonstrated three things that shaped the subsequent literature.
First, textual sentiment can be quantified in a reproducible way.  Second, the resulting
signal has economically meaningful predictive content for asset prices even in a world
where professional arbitrageurs are active.  Third, the choice of dictionary matters:
the Harvard IV-4 list was designed for general English prose, not for the specialised
register of financial and legal language.

The inadequacy of general-purpose dictionaries for financial text was made rigorous by
Tim Loughran and Bill McDonald in a highly influential 2011 paper
\cite{loughran2011liability}. % [CITE: Loughran, T. & McDonald, B. (2011). When is a Liability not a Liability? Textual Analysis, Dictionaries, and 10-Ks. Journal of Finance, 66(1), 35–65.]
They examined the textual content of a large sample of 10-K annual filings submitted to
the SEC's EDGAR database and measured the fraction of words classified as negative by
the Harvard IV-4 dictionary.  They then checked, word by word, whether the negative
classification was appropriate for financial text.  Their finding was sobering: a large
share of the ``negative'' words in financial documents---for example \emph{tax},
\emph{liability}, \emph{depreciation}, \emph{cost}---are not negative in the financial
register; they are simply technical vocabulary.  Using the Harvard IV-4 dictionary on
financial filings therefore introduces substantial measurement error.

In response, Loughran and McDonald constructed their own \emph{financial sentiment
dictionary} (often abbreviated LM dictionary) by reading thousands of 10-K filings and
manually assigning financial connotations to words.  The resulting word lists span six
tone categories: Negative, Positive, Uncertainty, Litigious, Strong Modal (words
implying certainty), and Weak Modal (words implying tentativeness).  Their empirical
application showed that the LM Negative list outperformed the Harvard IV-4 list in
predicting initial public offering underperformance, earnings surprise, and subsequent
abnormal returns, among other outcomes.

The LM dictionary remains a standard benchmark in financial NLP.  Most subsequent
studies report results using both the Harvard IV-4 and LM lists to allow comparability.
\citet{loughran2020textual} surveys the subsequent
decade of textual analysis research, cataloguing how the field has extended beyond
sentiment to readability, complexity, political bias, and fraud detection, while
cautioning that many readability measures lack a clear theoretical foundation.
However, both dictionary approaches suffer from the same fundamental limitation: they are static.  Words are
classified once, by human judgment, and the classifications do not adapt to new usages,
new jargon, or sector-specific connotations.  Moreover, dictionary methods score only
the words they recognise, ignoring syntax, negation, context, and word order entirely.
The sentence ``earnings did \emph{not} decline'' receives the same score as ``earnings
\emph{did} decline'' if the word \emph{not} is not explicitly handled, and even
negation-aware heuristics remain fragile.

Before leaving the dictionary era, it is worth noting that scholars also developed
\emph{readability} measures applied to corporate disclosures.  \citet{li2008annual} % [CITE: Li, F. (2008). Annual Report Readability, Current Earnings, and Earnings Persistence. Journal of Accounting and Economics, 45(2–3), 221–247.]
showed that annual reports that are more difficult to read (as measured by the
Fog Index, a formula based on average sentence length and the fraction of polysyllabic
words) are associated with lower future earnings, suggesting that complexity may serve as
obfuscation.  This line of research extended the textual analysis toolkit to stylistic
features beyond sentiment, opening the door to a broader conception of what ``textual
information'' means in a financial context.

\subsection{The Shift to Statistical and Neural Methods}
\label{sec:history-shift}

By the mid-2000s the natural language processing (NLP) community had largely moved beyond
dictionary-based methods toward \emph{statistical} representations that learn structure
from data.  The most influential of these for finance is \emph{Latent Dirichlet
Allocation} (LDA), introduced by Blei, Ng, and Jordan in 2003
\cite{blei2003latent}. % [CITE: Blei, D.M., Ng, A.Y. & Jordan, M.I. (2003). Latent Dirichlet Allocation. Journal of Machine Learning Research, 3, 993–1022.]
LDA is a generative probabilistic model: it posits that each document is a mixture of a
small number of latent \emph{topics}, and each topic is a distribution over vocabulary
words.  The model is fit by approximate Bayesian inference and requires no labelled data.

The application of LDA to financial text proved fertile.  Methodology-wise the key
advantage over dictionary methods is that topics are discovered from the corpus rather
than pre-specified.  Earnings calls, for instance, might reveal latent topics
corresponding to supply-chain concerns, margin pressure, new product introductions, and
macroeconomic commentary, each represented by a characteristic cluster of co-occurring
words.  Researchers such as \citet{boudoukh2013news} % [CITE: Boudoukh, J., Feldman, R., Kogan, S. & Richardson, M.P. (2013). Which News Moves Stock Prices? A Textual Analysis. NBER Working Paper 18725.]
and \citet{hansen2018transparency} % [CITE: Hansen, S., McMahon, M. & Prat, A. (2018). Transparency and Deliberation within the FOMC: A Computational Linguistics Approach. Quarterly Journal of Economics, 133(2), 801–870.]
used topic models to analyse, respectively, firm-level news and Federal Open Market
Committee transcripts, demonstrating that data-driven topic discovery can reveal
structure invisible to dictionary counting.

Despite these successes, bag-of-words topic models share a fundamental limitation with
dictionary methods: they ignore word order and syntax.  The model treats a document as
an unordered multiset of words, discarding the sequential structure that makes language
meaningful.  Negation, qualification, conditionality, and argumentative structure are
all lost.  This was the central motivation for the neural turn.

A transformative shift came with the introduction of \emph{Word2Vec} by Mikolov et al.\
in 2013 \cite{mikolov2013efficient}. % [CITE: Mikolov, T., Chen, K., Corrado, G. & Dean, J. (2013). Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space. ICLR Workshop.]
Word2Vec trains a shallow neural network to predict either a word from its context
(the Continuous Bag-of-Words model) or the context from a word (the Skip-gram model).
As a by-product, each word receives a dense vector representation---an
\emph{embedding}---in $\mathbb{R}^d$ for some moderate $d$ (typically 100--300).
These embeddings capture distributional semantics: words that appear in similar contexts
end up close together in the embedding space.  The famous example is that
$\text{vec}(\textit{king}) - \text{vec}(\textit{man}) + \text{vec}(\textit{woman})
\approx \text{vec}(\textit{queen})$.

For finance, word embeddings offered the prospect of capturing semantic similarity
without hand-crafted dictionaries.  Researchers trained Word2Vec on large corpora of
earnings call transcripts, news articles, and 10-K filings and used the resulting
embeddings as features for downstream tasks such as sentiment classification and return
prediction.  \citet{ke2019predicting} introduced a supervised
text-mining framework that learns which words in financial news articles are most
predictive of subsequent asset returns, combining a sentiment-term screening step with
topic-model weighting; their method substantially outperforms bag-of-words baselines
on out-of-sample return prediction.  \citet{manela2017news} % [CITE: Manela, A. & Moreira, A. (2017). News Implied Volatility and Disaster Concerns. Journal of Financial Economics, 123(1), 137–162.]
used text-based features including embedding-based similarities to construct a news-based
measure of disaster risk.  Simultaneously, GloVe (Global Vectors for Word
Representation) \cite{pennington2014glove} % [CITE: Pennington, J., Socher, R. & Manning, C.D. (2014). GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation. EMNLP 2014.]
offered an alternative factorisation approach that explicitly minimised the
difference between the dot product of word vectors and the logarithm of the words'
co-occurrence count, yielding embeddings with similarly useful geometric properties.

The deep learning era brought a further step: the combination of word embeddings with \emph{recurrent neural networks} (RNNs), and
specifically with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks
\cite{hochreiter1997long}, % [CITE: Hochreiter, S. & Schmidhuber, J. (1997). Long Short-Term Memory. Neural Computation, 9(8), 1735–1780.]
allowed models to read a document sequentially and maintain a hidden state summarising
everything read so far.  Applied to financial text, LSTM-based models could, in
principle, handle negation and long-range dependencies that defeat bag-of-words
approaches.  They achieved state-of-the-art performance on the Financial PhraseBank benchmark~\citep{malo2014phrasebank} throughout 2015--2018, with the FiQA task~\citep{maia2018fiqa} (introduced in 2018) providing a later evaluation point for encoder models.

The attention mechanism introduced by \citet{bahdanau2015neural} % [CITE: Bahdanau, D., Cho, K. & Bengio, Y. (2015). Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate. ICLR 2015.]
and then generalised to the \emph{Transformer} architecture by \citet{vaswani2017attention} % [CITE: Vaswani, A., Shazeer, N., Parmar, N., et al. (2017). Attention Is All You Need. NeurIPS 2017.]
changed the landscape completely.  The Transformer processes all tokens in a sequence in
parallel via \emph{self-attention}, computing for each token a weighted average of all
other tokens' representations, where the weights are learned and reflect relevance.
This design enabled training on far larger datasets than RNNs could accommodate, and it
enabled \emph{pre-training}: a model could be trained on massive unlabelled corpora to
predict masked words or the next sentence, learning rich contextual representations,
and then \emph{fine-tuned} on a small labelled dataset for a specific downstream task.

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers)
\cite{devlin2019} % [CITE: Devlin, J., Chang, M.-W., Lee, K. & Toutanova, K. (2019). BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding. NAACL-HLT 2019.]
exemplified this paradigm.  Fine-tuned BERT models rapidly surpassed prior state of the
art on nearly every NLP benchmark.  The finance community responded quickly.
FinBERT \cite{araci2019finbert,yang2020finbert}
% [CITE: Araci (2019): fine-tuned BERT on financial text; Yang et al. (2020): retrained BERT from scratch on financial corpora. Two distinct models share the name FinBERT.]
(note: two models share the name FinBERT — \citeauthor{araci2019finbert}
fine-tuned BERT on financial text; \citeauthor{yang2020finbert} retrained BERT from
scratch on financial corpora) and a succession of similar models pre-trained on
financial corpora achieved markedly better sentiment classification accuracy on
financial datasets than generic BERT.  They
represent the direct antecedents of the large language models---GPT-5, Claude, Gemini,
Llama---that form the subject of this book.

The history just sketched is not merely background; it is directly relevant to how one
should think about and deploy LLMs in practice.  Each generation of methods addressed
shortcomings of the previous one, but also introduced new failure modes.  Understanding
where the field came from illuminates why LLMs behave the way they do, what they can be
expected to do well, and where they remain unreliable.

\end{context}

\section{Why Text Matters in Finance}
\label{sec:motivation}

\begin{context}
Every quarter, thousands of companies assemble their executives on a conference
call, dial in a few hundred sell-side analysts, and spend an hour narrating the
past three months.  The ritual is called the earnings call.  The transcript is
published within days and largely forgotten---except by the people who have
learned to listen to what is not in the numbers.  The word \emph{challenges}
appearing where \emph{headwinds} used to appear.  The CFO's sentences getting
shorter.  The count of hedge words---\emph{approximately}, \emph{roughly},
\emph{we believe}---ticking upward.  None of this information lives in the
earnings per share figure.  All of it lives in the transcript.  This section
asks why that matters, and how we know it does.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Quantitative Growth of Financial Text}
\label{sec:motivation-growth}

Before examining what text contains, it is worth establishing how much of it
exists and how the quantity is changing.  The SEC's Electronic Data Gathering,
Analysis, and Retrieval (EDGAR) database provides a natural laboratory:
virtually every public company in the United States has filed annual reports
(10-Ks) electronically since 1993, giving researchers a three-decade panel of
machine-readable corporate disclosures.

A striking empirical regularity emerges from this corpus: the textual content
of annual filings has grown substantially over time, both in absolute length
and as a share of total filing content.  The mean word count of a 10-K filing
rose markedly between 1993 and 2023 \citep{loughran2020textual},
driven by expansions in the risk factors section (introduced as a mandatory
disclosure by SEC Release 33-8591 in 2005), the Management's Discussion and
Analysis narrative, and increasingly detailed litigation and regulatory
commentary.  Computing the fraction of non-numeric characters---a proxy for
narrative versus tabular content---reveals that the textual proportion of a
typical annual filing has risen steadily, even as the underlying financial
statements have remained roughly constant in size.

\begin{center}
  \includegraphics[width=0.90\linewidth]{chapters/01-intro/figures/fig_edgar_text_growth}
  \captionof{figure}{Growth of narrative text in SEC 10-K annual filings, 1993--2023.
    Points show the mean alphabetic character count per filing (sample of 25 per year);
    shaded band is $\pm$1 standard error of the mean.
    Vertical lines mark the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002) and SEC Release 33-8591 (2005),
    which mandated a standalone risk-factors section.
    Source: SEC EDGAR full-text index; author calculations.}
  \label{fig:edgar-text-growth}
\end{center}

This growth matters for three reasons.  First, it implies that the information
set available from textual sources is expanding: there is simply more text to
analyse, and that text is increasingly being written to convey strategic
intent rather than to satisfy a rigid template.  Second, the expansion places
growing demands on human readers---an analyst who could read every 10-K filed
in the early 1990s cannot do so today---which is precisely where NLP tools
add value by compressing large volumes of text into actionable signals.
Third, the upward trend in textual content shows no sign of abating; as
AI-generated text becomes cheaper to produce, the volume of financial
disclosure is likely to continue rising, reinforcing the case for AI-based
tools to read it.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Information Content of Earnings Calls, News, and Filings}
\label{sec:motivation-info}

The semi-strong form of the efficient markets hypothesis (EMH), associated with Fama
\cite{fama1970efficient}, % [CITE: Fama, E.F. (1970). Efficient Capital Markets: A Review of Empirical Work. Journal of Finance, 25(2), 383–417.]
holds that prices at any time fully reflect all \emph{publicly available} information.
Under strict semi-strong efficiency, reading a newspaper or an earnings call transcript
provides no trading advantage, because everything in those documents is already impounded
in prices the moment it is published.  Textual analysis would then be, at best, a way of
checking one's understanding of the market---not a way of generating alpha.

Why, then, has such a substantial empirical literature found otherwise?  Several
non-mutually-exclusive explanations are consistent with market efficiency:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Processing costs and limited attention.} Markets aggregate information
    through the trading of many investors, each of whom has limited attention and
    cognitive capacity.  If processing the full text of a 100-page 10-K filing
    requires substantial effort, and if only a small fraction of market participants
    perform that analysis, prices may not immediately reflect all the information the
    filing contains.  Once cheap NLP tools make processing feasible at scale, the
    anomaly should attenuate---and indeed \citet{li2013measuring} % [CITE: Li, F., Lundholm, R. & Minnis, M. (2013). A Measure of Competition Based on 10-K Filings. Journal of Accounting Research, 51(2), 399–436.]
    and others document that textual signals are most powerful precisely for documents
    that are longest, most complex, and hence most costly to process.

  \item \textbf{Soft information and interpretive disagreement.} Even when a document is
    widely read, its implications may be ambiguous.  A cautious management tone on an
    earnings call might reflect genuine uncertainty about business conditions, or it might
    be strategic communication designed to manage analyst expectations.  If sophisticated
    investors disagree about the correct interpretation, prices reflect an average of
    beliefs, and no single interpretation is instantly impounded.  NLP tools that can
    classify the \emph{degree} of hedging or strategic obfuscation thus extract a signal
    not already priced.

  \item \textbf{Latent information.} Corporate disclosures are subject to legal and
    regulatory constraints.  Managers cannot say anything material that is not disclosed
    to all investors simultaneously (Regulation FD in the US context).  However,
    information may be conveyed implicitly through word choice, rhetorical structure, and
    what is \emph{omitted} rather than stated.  Analysing the \emph{sentiment} or
    \emph{readability} of a disclosure can extract this latent signal even when the
    explicit content is already known.
\end{enumerate}

Under any of these accounts, textual analysis adds value not because the market is
irrational but because information extraction from text is genuinely costly and the
technology for doing it at scale has improved dramatically in recent years.  LLMs
represent the latest---and most powerful---step in that technological progression.

The empirical literature draws on several distinct categories of financial text, each
with its own characteristics, frequency, regulatory environment, and information content.

\begin{description}
  \item[Earnings call transcripts.] Publicly traded companies in most major markets
    hold quarterly earnings calls in which senior management (typically the CEO and CFO)
    presents financial results and answers questions from sell-side analysts.  Transcripts
    are produced by services such as Refinitiv or Bloomberg and are widely studied.  The
    call has two parts: a scripted management presentation and an unscripted Q\&A.  The
    Q\&A is particularly informative because responses are spontaneous and harder to
    rehearse.  \citet{davis2015effect} % [CITE: Davis, A.K., Ge, W., Matsumoto, D. & Zhang, J.L. (2015). The Effect of Manager-Specific Optimism on the Tone of Earnings Conference Calls. Review of Accounting Studies, 20(2), 639–673.]
    and a large subsequent literature show that sentiment extracted from transcripts
    predicts abnormal returns and subsequent earnings surprises.

  \item[10-K and 10-Q filings.] Annual (10-K) and quarterly (10-Q) reports filed with
    the SEC contain management discussion and analysis (MD\&A), risk factor disclosures,
    financial statements, and extensive footnotes.  These are the primary data source in
    the Loughran--McDonald (2011) study.  10-K filings can exceed 200 pages, presenting
    significant information-extraction challenges that NLP is uniquely well-placed to
    address.

  \item[Newswire and newspaper articles.] Tetlock (2007) worked with \emph{Wall Street
    Journal} columns.  A larger body of work uses machine-readable newswire feeds from
    Reuters or Bloomberg.  A key feature of news is high frequency: material news events
    trigger immediate repricing, and the window for exploiting a textual signal is
    therefore narrow.  The value of NLP-based news analysis lies partly in speed (reading
    and classifying thousands of articles per second) and partly in breadth (aggregating
    signals across many sources simultaneously).

  \item[Analyst reports.] Sell-side research reports combine quantitative analysis with
    extensive narrative commentary.  They are produced by financial analysts who have
    specialised knowledge of the companies they cover, making the text informationally
    dense.  \citet{lehavy2011benefit} % [CITE: Lehavy, R., Li, F. & Merkley, K. (2011). The Benefit of Investment Bank Analyst Monitoring. Journal of Finance, 66(4), 1223–1260.]
    show that the textual complexity of analyst reports is associated with greater investor
    uncertainty and higher information asymmetry.

  \item[Central bank communications.] Minutes and statements from central banks (the US
    Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the Bank of England) move interest rates
    and equity prices when published.  The FOMC transcripts used by Hansen et al.\
    \cite{hansen2018transparency} are now publicly available with a five-year lag and
    provide a rich laboratory for studying deliberation and its market effects.

  \item[Social media and alternative data.] Twitter (now X), Reddit (especially
    r/wallstreetbets), StockTwits, and similar platforms provide high-frequency, noisy,
    unstructured text.  \citet{bollen2011twitter} % [CITE: Bollen, J., Mao, H. & Zeng, X. (2011). Twitter Mood Predicts the Stock Market. Journal of Computational Science, 2(1), 1–8.]
    showed that aggregate Twitter mood states predict DJIA returns in a time-series
    regression, though subsequent work has questioned the robustness of this finding.
    Social media data is particularly challenging due to bot activity, sarcasm, slang,
    and the mixing of retail and institutional sentiment.
\end{description}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Text as a Signal: Evidence from the Literature}
\label{sec:motivation-evidence}

Before formalising the framework, it is useful to have in mind the empirical regularities
the theory must explain.  The key stylised facts from the literature are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Negative tone predicts negative returns (short-run).}  Tetlock (2007)
    found that the fraction of negative words in the WSJ column predicts DJIA returns
    with a negative coefficient: more pessimistic prose is followed by lower next-day
    returns.  The effect reverses over the subsequent week, suggesting temporary
    sentiment-driven mispricing.

  \item \textbf{Financial-domain dictionaries outperform general-domain ones.}
    Loughran and McDonald (2011) showed that the LM Negative word list has substantially
    higher predictive power for filing-date abnormal returns than the Harvard IV-4 list,
    confirming that domain specificity matters.

  \item \textbf{Earnings call tone predicts future earnings surprises.}  Multiple
    studies, including \citet{larcker2012detecting}, % [CITE: Larcker, D.F. & Zakolyukina, A.A. (2012). Detecting Deceptive Discussions in Conference Calls. Journal of Accounting Research, 50(2), 495–540.]
    find that the linguistic characteristics of CEO and CFO speech on earnings calls
    contain information about the accuracy of disclosed earnings, and that deceptive
    speech has distinct linguistic markers.

  \item \textbf{LLM-based signals show incremental predictive power.}  More recent work
    has found that prompt-based or fine-tuned LLMs generate sentiment signals with
    higher out-of-sample predictive accuracy than dictionary or classical ML methods on
    the same financial text datasets.  \citet{LopezLiraTang2023}
    demonstrate that ChatGPT-generated sentiment scores for news headlines predict
    next-day stock returns with a positive-coefficient pattern, and that this return
    predictability is absent for smaller, less capable predecessors such as GPT-1 and
    BERT, suggesting it is an emergent property of scale.
\end{enumerate}

We now introduce a minimal formal model that will underpin the empirical analyses
throughout this book.

\begin{definition}[Textual Signal]
\label{def:textual-signal}
Let $\mathcal{D}$ denote the space of documents (finite sequences of tokens drawn from
a vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$; both terms are formally defined in
Definition~\ref{def:corpus-vocab}).  Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ be a
probability space on which all random variables are defined.  A \emph{textual signal} is a
measurable function
\[
  f \colon \mathcal{D} \to \mathbb{R}^k,
\]
that maps a document $d_t \in \mathcal{D}$ observed at time $t$ to a $k$-dimensional
real vector $s_t = f(d_t)$ called the \emph{signal value}.  When $k = 1$ we speak of a
\emph{scalar} textual signal; when $k > 1$ of a \emph{vector} signal.
\end{definition}

In the simplest setting $k = 1$ and $s_t$ is a sentiment score.  In more complex
settings it is a vector of topic proportions, an embedding vector in $\mathbb{R}^{768}$,
or the output of a classifier head over a set of discrete categories.

The central object of interest in empirical asset pricing is the predictive relationship
between the signal and future excess returns.  Letting $r_{t+1}$ denote the excess
return (return in excess of the risk-free rate) of a security or portfolio over the
period $[t, t+1]$, the most basic question is whether the signal has forecasting content:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:signal-predictability}
  \mathrm{Cov}(s_t,\, r_{t+1}) \neq 0.
\end{equation}

If this covariance is non-zero, a long--short strategy that takes positions proportional
to $s_t$ earns a non-zero expected return in a linear projection model.  More precisely,
under a linear factor model:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:linear-factor}
  r_{t+1} = \alpha + \beta^\top s_t + \varepsilon_{t+1}, \qquad
  \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_{t+1} \mid s_t] = 0,
\end{equation}

the coefficient $\beta$ captures the marginal predictive content of the signal.  The
signal is \emph{economically significant} if $|\beta| > 0$ \emph{after} controlling for
known return predictors (size, value, momentum, and so forth), and if the associated
Sharpe ratio (mean excess return divided by its standard deviation, annualised by
$\sqrt{252}$) exceeds plausible transaction costs.

\begin{remark}[Identification challenges]
\label{rem:identification}
Establishing that $\mathrm{Cov}(s_t, r_{t+1}) \neq 0$ is a necessary but not sufficient
condition for claiming that the signal is \emph{causally} informative about future
returns.  Several identification challenges arise:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reverse causality.} The document $d_t$ may be written \emph{in response
    to} lagged returns rather than in anticipation of future ones.  For example, a
    pessimistic earnings call might reflect a poor quarter already priced in.  One must
    carefully identify the \emph{component} of $s_t$ that is not predictable from public
    information already in prices.

  \item \textbf{Confounding.} Macroeconomic conditions simultaneously affect the tone of
    news coverage and asset returns.  A negative correlation between negativity and
    returns may simply reflect a common dependence on the business cycle rather than a
    causal channel from text to prices.  Standard practice is to control for
    contemporaneous return, volatility, and macro variables, and to use high-frequency
    event studies where the information release time is known precisely.

  \item \textbf{Overfitting and multiple testing.} With modern NLP tools one can
    construct an enormous number of candidate signals from the same corpus.  Without
    correction for multiple testing or out-of-sample validation, spurious correlations
    are likely.  Throughout this book we emphasise genuine out-of-sample evaluation
    using a strict temporal split (training on pre-2010 data, evaluating on post-2010
    data, for example) rather than in-sample $t$-statistics.

  \item \textbf{Model misspecification.} The mapping $f$ from document to signal is
    itself an assumption.  Dictionary-based $f$ imposes strong restrictions (only word
    counts matter, word order is irrelevant).  LLM-based $f$ relaxes these restrictions
    but introduces its own assumptions (training data distribution, fine-tuning
    objective, prompt design).  The empirical signal-to-noise ratio of $\hat{\beta}$
    depends on how well $f$ captures the true information in the document.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

This framework is deliberately abstract.  Its value is that it makes precise what we
mean by ``text contains information'': the signal $s_t = f(d_t)$ has information content
about future returns if and only if it is correlated with $r_{t+1}$ conditional on the
information set available at $t$.  The rest of this book can be read as an investigation
of the best choice of $f$: from simple word counts, to topic models, to transformer
embeddings, to instruction-tuned LLMs used as zero-shot classifiers or reasoning agents.

\end{context}

\section{Classical Text Representations}
\label{sec:classical}

\begin{deepdive}

Before turning to neural representations it is essential to understand classical methods,
for several reasons.  First, they remain competitive baselines in many financial
applications.  Second, neural methods such as self-attention can be understood as
learning to construct, dynamically, context-dependent analogues of the static
representations described here.  Third, the vocabulary and notation introduced in this
section are used throughout the book.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Vocabulary, Tokens, and the Bag-of-Words Model}
\label{sec:classical-bow}

\begin{definition}[Corpus and Vocabulary]
\label{def:corpus-vocab}
A \emph{corpus} $\mathcal{C} = \{d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_N\}$ is a finite collection of
$N$ documents.  Each document $d_i$ is a finite sequence of \emph{tokens} drawn from a
\emph{vocabulary} $\mathcal{V}$.  The vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ is a finite set with
$|\mathcal{V}| = V$ elements called \emph{types}.  We write a document as a sequence
$d_i = (w_{i,1}, w_{i,2}, \ldots, w_{i,n_i})$ where each $w_{i,j} \in \mathcal{V}$
and $n_i$ is the document length.
\end{definition}

The process of converting a raw character string into a sequence of tokens is called
\emph{tokenisation}.  In the simplest case tokens are whitespace-delimited words.
In more sophisticated pipelines (used by LLMs) the unit of tokenisation is a
\emph{sub-word} piece: for example, the word \textit{earnings} might be represented
as the two pieces \textit{earn} and \textit{ings}, allowing the model to represent
rare words as combinations of more frequent sub-units.  For the classical methods
described in this section, word-level tokenisation is standard.

Before building a vocabulary, practitioners typically apply several normalisation steps:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Case folding.} Convert all tokens to lower case so that ``Revenue'' and
    ``revenue'' are treated as the same type.

  \item \textbf{Stop word removal.} Remove high-frequency function words (``the'',
    ``and'', ``of'', ``is'', \ldots) that carry little discriminative information across
    documents.  Standard stop word lists (e.g.\ the NLTK English stop word list) contain
    100--300 words.  In financial text, some researchers also remove common financial
    jargon (``company'', ``year'', ``quarter'') that appears in nearly every document.

  \item \textbf{Stemming and lemmatisation.} Stemming applies rule-based suffix stripping
    to map inflected forms to a common stem: \textit{earnings}, \textit{earned},
    \textit{earns} $\to$ \textit{earn}.  Lemmatisation uses a morphological dictionary to
    map words to their canonical lemma: \textit{better} $\to$ \textit{good}.
    Lemmatisation is linguistically more accurate but slower.  Both reduce $V$ and
    increase effective document frequency of important terms.

  \item \textbf{Vocabulary truncation.} It is common to retain only the $V'$ most
    frequent types (after stop word removal) and to discard types with very low document
    frequency, on the grounds that very rare words are either noise or proper nouns
    whose meaning is document-specific.
\end{enumerate}

After tokenisation and pre-processing, the dominant classical representation discards
word order entirely.

\begin{definition}[Bag-of-Words Representation]
\label{def:bow}
Let $\mathcal{V} = \{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_V\}$ be an ordered vocabulary.  The
\emph{bag-of-words} (BoW) representation of a document $d$ with respect to
$\mathcal{V}$ is the vector
\[
  \mathbf{x}(d) = \bigl(\mathrm{count}(v_1, d),\,
                         \mathrm{count}(v_2, d),\, \ldots,\,
                         \mathrm{count}(v_V, d)\bigr)^\top \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^V,
\]
where $\mathrm{count}(v, d)$ is the number of times token $v$ appears in document $d$.
The representation is called a ``bag'' because it loses the sequential order of tokens,
retaining only the multiset of token occurrences.
\end{definition}

The BoW representation is the foundation of the classical NLP pipeline.  Its principal
virtues are simplicity and interpretability.  Its principal deficiency is exactly the
information it discards: position, syntax, and context.  The sentences ``the fund
outperformed the market'' and ``the market outperformed the fund'' have identical BoW
representations, despite having opposite meanings.

The BoW matrix for an entire corpus $\mathcal{C}$ of $N$ documents is the term-document
matrix $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^{N \times V}$ whose $i$-th row is
$\mathbf{x}(d_i)^\top$.  For large vocabularies and corpora, $\mathbf{X}$ is typically
very sparse: most documents use a small fraction of the full vocabulary, so most entries
are zero.  Sparse matrix formats (CSR, CSC) are used in practice.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{One-Hot Encoding}
\label{sec:classical-onehot}

\begin{definition}[One-Hot Vector]
\label{def:one-hot}
Let $\mathcal{V} = \{v_1, \ldots, v_{|\mathcal{V}|}\}$ be an ordered vocabulary.  The \emph{one-hot
encoding} of a token $v_i \in \mathcal{V}$ is the indicator vector
\[
  \mathbf{e}_i = (\underbrace{0, \ldots, 0}_{i-1}, 1,
                  \underbrace{0, \ldots, 0}_{|\mathcal{V}| - i})^\top \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{V}|},
\]
i.e.\ the $i$-th standard basis vector in $\mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$.  The set
$\{\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_{|\mathcal{V}|}\}$ forms an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$
under the Euclidean inner product.
\end{definition}

One-hot encoding is not an alternative to the bag-of-words model; it is a complementary
concept that operates at the level of individual tokens rather than whole documents.  A
document is represented under the BoW model as $\mathbf{x}(d) = \sum_{j=1}^{n}
\mathbf{e}_{i(w_j)} \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$, the sum of the one-hot vectors of its constituent tokens.

One-hot vectors have two defining geometric properties.  First, all vectors have unit
$\ell_2$ norm: $\|\mathbf{e}_i\|_2 = 1$ for all $i$.  Second, any two distinct vectors
are orthogonal: $\langle \mathbf{e}_i, \mathbf{e}_j \rangle = \mathbf{e}_i^\top
\mathbf{e}_j = \mathbb{1}[i = j]$.  The second property encodes the implicit
assumption that every pair of distinct words is equally (dis)similar, which is
semantically incorrect.  The cosine similarity between \textit{revenue} and
\textit{income} is zero under one-hot encoding, identical to the similarity between
\textit{revenue} and any other word---yet a financial analyst knows these words are
nearly synonymous in most contexts.

Formally, the metric structure induced on the vocabulary by one-hot cosine similarity
is the discrete metric: $\mathrm{sim}(v_j, v_k) = \mathbb{1}[j = k]$.  This is a
profound limitation because much of the value of language lies precisely in the
\emph{partial} similarities between words.  Word embeddings (Section~\ref{sec:embeddings})
were developed specifically to replace this discrete metric with a continuous one that
reflects distributional similarity.

For a vocabulary of size $V$, the one-hot representation lives in $\mathbb{R}^V$.  For
typical NLP applications $V \in [10\,000,\, 100\,000]$, so the representation is
extremely high-dimensional.  Only one entry per token is non-zero; the vectors are
$(1 - 1/|\mathcal{V}|)$-sparse.  For a document of $n$ tokens, the BoW vector
$\sum_j \mathbf{e}_{i(w_j)} \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$ has at most $n$ non-zero entries out of $|\mathcal{V}|$, and for short
documents this fraction is small.  This sparsity makes direct computation on one-hot
features (e.g.\ training a linear classifier) feasible via sparse linear algebra, but it
makes the features unsuitable as inputs to dense neural network layers without a
dimensionality reduction step such as an embedding layer.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{TF-IDF Weighting}
\label{sec:classical-tfidf}

A fundamental problem with raw BoW count vectors is that they are dominated by common
words.  In a corpus of 10-K filings, the word \textit{the} appears in every document
and contributes nothing to distinguishing documents from one another.  TF-IDF weighting
addresses this by discounting words that appear frequently across the corpus.

\begin{definition}[Term Frequency]
\label{def:tf}
The \emph{term frequency} of token $v$ in document $d$ is
\[
  \mathrm{tf}(v, d) = \frac{\mathrm{count}(v, d)}{|d|},
\]
where $|d| = \sum_{v' \in \mathcal{V}} \mathrm{count}(v', d)$ is the total number of
tokens in $d$ (after pre-processing).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Inverse Document Frequency]
\label{def:idf}
The \emph{document frequency} of token $v$ in corpus $\mathcal{C}$ is
\[
  \mathrm{df}(v, \mathcal{C}) = \bigl|\{d \in \mathcal{C} : \mathrm{count}(v, d) > 0\}\bigr|,
\]
the number of documents in which $v$ appears at least once.  The \emph{inverse document
frequency} is
\[
  \mathrm{idf}(v, \mathcal{C}) = \log\!\left(\frac{|\mathcal{C}|}{\mathrm{df}(v, \mathcal{C})}\right),
\]
where the logarithm may be taken in any base (typically natural or base 2).  A
smoothed variant commonly used in practice adds 1 to the denominator to avoid division
by zero:
\[
  \mathrm{idf}_{\text{smooth}}(v, \mathcal{C})
    = \log\!\left(\frac{|\mathcal{C}|}{1 + \mathrm{df}(v, \mathcal{C})}\right) + 1.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[TF-IDF Weight]
\label{def:tfidf}
The \emph{TF-IDF weight} of token $v$ in document $d$ with respect to corpus
$\mathcal{C}$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tfidf}
  \mathrm{tfidf}(v, d, \mathcal{C}) = \mathrm{tf}(v, d) \times \mathrm{idf}(v, \mathcal{C}).
\end{equation}
The \emph{TF-IDF representation} of document $d$ is the vector
\[
  \tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d) = \bigl(\mathrm{tfidf}(v_1, d, \mathcal{C}),\,
                                 \mathrm{tfidf}(v_2, d, \mathcal{C}),\, \ldots,\,
                                 \mathrm{tfidf}(v_V, d, \mathcal{C})\bigr)^\top
  \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^V.
\]
\end{definition}

The IDF acts as an automatic stop-word suppressor.  A word that appears in every
document has $\mathrm{df} = |\mathcal{C}|$, so $\mathrm{idf} = \log(1) = 0$ and its
TF-IDF weight is zero regardless of how often it appears in a particular document.
Conversely, a word that appears in only a few documents receives a high IDF weight,
and if it is also frequent in those few documents it receives a high TF-IDF weight,
signalling that it is \emph{characteristic} of those documents.  This makes TF-IDF
particularly useful for document retrieval and classification: the dimensions with the
highest TF-IDF weights tend to be the words that most sharply differentiate documents.

TF-IDF vectors are typically $\ell_2$-normalised before computing document similarity:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cosine-sim}
  \mathrm{sim}(d_i, d_j) = \frac{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_i)^\top \tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_j)}
                                 {\|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_i)\|_2\, \|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_j)\|_2}.
\end{equation}
This cosine similarity lies in $[-1, 1]$ (in $[0,1]$ for non-negative TF-IDF vectors)
and is invariant to document length, making it a natural measure of topical similarity
between documents.  In information retrieval, ranking documents by cosine similarity to
a query document is the basis of the classic vector space model
\cite{salton1975vector}. % [CITE: Salton, G., Wong, A. & Yang, C.S. (1975). A Vector Space Model for Automatic Indexing. Communications of the ACM, 18(11), 613–620.]

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:unit-norm-cosine}
Assuming unit-$\ell_2$-normalised vectors, $\|\mathbf{v}\|_2 = 1$ for all
$\mathbf{v}$, the denominator equals 1 and
$\mathrm{sim}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{a}^{\top}\mathbf{b}$ reduces to
the plain inner product.  Many word-embedding libraries (e.g.\
\texttt{gensim}) $\ell_2$-normalise vectors before retrieval precisely to
exploit this simplification.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[TF-IDF Computation on Financial Sentences]
\label{ex:tfidf-example}
To make the definitions concrete, we compute the TF-IDF matrix for a small corpus of
three financial sentences.  After lower-casing and removing the stop words \{``the'',
``a'', ``of'', ``in'', ``by'', ``is''\} the corpus is:

\medskip
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cl}
\toprule
$d$ & Pre-processed tokens \\
\midrule
$d_1$ & \texttt{revenue grew strong earnings} \\
$d_2$ & \texttt{earnings declined weak guidance} \\
$d_3$ & \texttt{revenue guidance raised strong} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\medskip

The vocabulary (sorted alphabetically) is
$\mathcal{V} = \{$\texttt{declined}, \texttt{earnings}, \texttt{grew}, \texttt{guidance},
\texttt{raised}, \texttt{revenue}, \texttt{strong}, \texttt{weak}$\}$, so $V = 8$ and
$|\mathcal{C}| = 3$.

The first step is to compute term frequency. Each document has $|d| = 4$ tokens, so $\mathrm{tf}(v, d) = \mathrm{count}(v, d) / 4$.

\medskip
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
Token & $\mathrm{tf}(v, d_1)$ & $\mathrm{tf}(v, d_2)$ & $\mathrm{tf}(v, d_3)$ \\
\midrule
\texttt{declined} & 0       & 0.25    & 0 \\
\texttt{earnings} & 0.25    & 0.25    & 0 \\
\texttt{grew}     & 0.25    & 0       & 0 \\
\texttt{guidance} & 0       & 0.25    & 0.25 \\
\texttt{raised}   & 0       & 0       & 0.25 \\
\texttt{revenue}  & 0.25    & 0       & 0.25 \\
\texttt{strong}   & 0.25    & 0       & 0.25 \\
\texttt{weak}     & 0       & 0.25    & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The second step is to compute the inverse document frequency. Document frequencies: \texttt{declined} appears in 1 document; \texttt{earnings} in 2;
\texttt{grew} in 1; \texttt{guidance} in 2; \texttt{raised} in 1; \texttt{revenue} in 2;
\texttt{strong} in 2; \texttt{weak} in 1.  Using $\mathrm{idf}(v)
= \ln(3 / \mathrm{df}(v))$:

\medskip
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Token & $\mathrm{idf}(v)$ \\
\midrule
\texttt{declined} & $\ln(3/1) \approx 1.099$ \\
\texttt{earnings} & $\ln(3/2) \approx 0.405$ \\
\texttt{grew}     & $\ln(3/1) \approx 1.099$ \\
\texttt{guidance} & $\ln(3/2) \approx 0.405$ \\
\texttt{raised}   & $\ln(3/1) \approx 1.099$ \\
\texttt{revenue}  & $\ln(3/2) \approx 0.405$ \\
\texttt{strong}   & $\ln(3/2) \approx 0.405$ \\
\texttt{weak}     & $\ln(3/1) \approx 1.099$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The third step multiplies term frequencies by IDF values to obtain the TF-IDF matrix
$\tilde{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 8}$:

\medskip
\begin{center}
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
Token & $\tilde{x}_{1,v}$ & $\tilde{x}_{2,v}$ & $\tilde{x}_{3,v}$ \\
\midrule
\texttt{declined} & 0       & $0.25 \times 1.099 \approx 0.275$ & 0 \\
\texttt{earnings} & $0.25 \times 0.405 \approx 0.101$ & $0.101$ & 0 \\
\texttt{grew}     & $0.25 \times 1.099 \approx 0.275$ & 0       & 0 \\
\texttt{guidance} & 0       & $0.25 \times 0.405 \approx 0.101$ & $0.101$ \\
\texttt{raised}   & 0       & 0       & $0.25 \times 1.099 \approx 0.275$ \\
\texttt{revenue}  & $0.101$ & 0       & $0.101$ \\
\texttt{strong}   & $0.101$ & 0       & $0.101$ \\
\texttt{weak}     & 0       & $0.275$ & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip
Several observations are immediate.  First, \texttt{grew}, \texttt{declined},
\texttt{raised}, and \texttt{weak}---each appearing in exactly one document---receive
the highest IDF weight ($\approx 1.099$) and therefore the highest TF-IDF weights
among words that appear in their respective documents.  These are the words that most
sharply characterise individual documents.  Second, \texttt{revenue}, \texttt{strong},
and \texttt{earnings} appear in two documents each and receive a lower but non-zero
weight, capturing their moderate discriminative value.  Third, no word appears in all
three documents, so no word is suppressed to zero---a reflection of the small corpus
size.  In a real corpus of thousands of 10-K filings, the most common words would be
concentrated near zero.

The cosine similarity between $d_1$ and $d_3$ reflects their shared tokens
\texttt{revenue} and \texttt{strong}:
\begin{align*}
  \mathrm{sim}(d_1, d_3)
  &= \frac{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_1)^\top \tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_3)}
          {\|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_1)\|_2\, \|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}(d_3)\|_2} \\
  &= \frac{2 \times (0.101)^2}
          {\sqrt{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}\;\sqrt{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}} \\
  &= \frac{2 \times 0.0102}{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}
   \approx \frac{0.0204}{0.0756 + 0.0204}
   \approx \frac{0.0204}{0.0960}
   \approx 0.213,
\end{align*}
a modest positive similarity consistent with the two documents sharing a positive-tone
vocabulary (\texttt{revenue} grew or was mentioned, \texttt{strong} appears in both).
The similarity between $d_1$ and $d_2$ is lower, since they share only \texttt{earnings}:
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{sim}(d_1, d_2)
  &= \frac{(0.101)^2}{\sqrt{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}\;\sqrt{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}} \\
  &= \frac{(0.101)^2}{(0.275)^2 + 2(0.101)^2}
   \approx \frac{0.0102}{0.0960}
   \approx 0.106,
\end{aligned}
\]
correctly reflecting minimal lexical overlap between the optimistic $d_1$ and the
negative $d_2$.
\end{example}

TF-IDF is a powerful improvement over raw counts, but it inherits all the fundamental
limitations of the bag-of-words framework:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Order invariance.}  The representation is unchanged by permuting the
    words of a document.  The sentences ``earnings beat estimates'' and ``estimates beat
    earnings'' receive the same TF-IDF vector.

  \item \textbf{No semantic similarity.}  Two synonymous words (\textit{revenue} and
    \textit{sales}) that never co-occur will have zero cosine similarity in TF-IDF space,
    even though they are semantically interchangeable.

  \item \textbf{Fixed vocabulary.}  TF-IDF is defined with respect to a fixed vocabulary
    learned at training time.  New words (e.g.\ a newly coined acronym or a company
    name) that appear in test documents are out-of-vocabulary and are discarded.

  \item \textbf{High dimensionality.}  For large corpora the vocabulary $V$ can be
    in the tens or hundreds of thousands.  Even with IDF weighting, the vectors are
    high-dimensional and sparse, posing challenges for downstream models that prefer
    dense, low-dimensional inputs.
\end{enumerate}

These limitations motivate the transition to distributional word embeddings discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:embeddings}, which replace the discrete, high-dimensional one-hot
basis with a continuous, low-dimensional space in which semantically similar words are
represented by geometrically close vectors.

\end{deepdive}

\section{Word Embeddings}
\label{sec:embeddings}

\begin{deepdive}

The Bag-of-Words and TF-IDF representations introduced in
\Cref{sec:classical} treat every word as an isolated symbol.
Two terms that never co-occur with each other but share essentially
the same contextual neighbours — \emph{equity} and \emph{stock}, for
instance — receive orthogonal vectors with cosine similarity exactly
zero.  This is not merely aesthetically unsatisfying: it means that a
model trained on documents containing the word \emph{equity} will
generalise poorly to documents using the synonym \emph{stock}, even
though the two words carry the same information.  Word embeddings solve
this problem by mapping every vocabulary item to a dense, low-dimensional
real-valued vector so that semantically related words land near one
another in the embedding space.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Distributional Semantics and the Embedding Idea}
\label{sec:embeddings-idea}

The theoretical foundation of word embeddings is the
\emph{distributional hypothesis}, articulated most famously by the
British linguist J.R.\ Firth:
\begin{quote}
  ``You shall know a word by the company it keeps.''
  \hfill\cite{firth1957} % [CITE: Firth, J.R. (1957). Papers in Linguistics 1934–1951]
\end{quote}
The idea is that the meaning of a word can be inferred from the
distribution of other words that appear in its vicinity across a large
corpus.  Words with similar meanings tend to appear in similar
contexts; therefore a representation that captures contextual
co-occurrence patterns will capture semantic similarity.

Recall that the classical one-hot encoding assigns to each word
$w \in \mathcal{V}$ the indicator vector $\mathbf{e}_w \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$
with a single non-zero entry at the position indexed by $w$.  This
representation has two critical deficiencies:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Sparsity.} For a vocabulary of $|\mathcal{V}| = 50{,}000$ tokens,
        each word is represented by a vector that is $50{,}000$-dimensional
        but contains exactly one non-zero entry.  Storage, computation, and
        learning all scale poorly with this dimensionality.
  \item \textbf{No semantic structure.} As established for the one-hot encoding
        (\Cref{def:one-hot}), every pair of distinct word vectors is orthogonal, so the
        representation encodes no information about semantic relatedness.
\end{enumerate}

A word embedding is a function that maps discrete vocabulary items to
dense, low-dimensional vectors.

\begin{definition}[Word Embedding]
\label{def:word-embedding}
Let $\mathcal{V}$ be a finite vocabulary of size $V = |\mathcal{V}|$.
A \emph{word embedding} of dimension $d$ is an injective map
\begin{equation}
  \varphi : \mathcal{V} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbb{R}^{d},
  \qquad d \ll V.
  \label{eq:embedding-map}
\end{equation}
The matrix $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ whose $i$-th row is
$\varphi(w_i)$ is called the \emph{embedding matrix}.  Retrieving the
embedding of word $w_i$ is equivalent to the matrix--vector product
$\mathbf{W}^\top \mathbf{e}_{w_i}$, i.e.\ selecting the $i$-th row of
$\mathbf{W}$.
\end{definition}

The embedding dimension $d$ is a hyperparameter.  Typical values in
practice range from $d = 50$ (GloVe-50) to $d = 300$ (GloVe-300,
Word2Vec) and up to $d = 768$ or $d = 1024$ for transformer-based
contextual embeddings (see Section~\ref{sec:doc-repr-sbert}).  The key
constraint is $d \ll V$: whereas one-hot encoding requires $V$ numbers
per word, an embedding requires only $d$ numbers, a compression ratio
of $V/d \approx 150$--$1000$ for typical values.

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:embedding-geometry}
The power of word embeddings lies not only in their compactness but in
their geometry.  Under a well-trained embedding, the Euclidean distance
or cosine similarity between two embedding vectors reflects the semantic
distance between the corresponding words.  Moreover, \emph{linear
analogies} emerge---the king/man/woman/queen relation previewed earlier and formalised
in \eqref{eq:analogy-royalty}---which we visualise on real pre-trained vectors in
\Cref{sec:embeddings-pca}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Word2Vec and GloVe}
\label{sec:embeddings-word2vec}

Two landmark algorithms — Word2Vec \cite{mikolov2013efficient} % [CITE: Mikolov et al. (2013). Distributed Representations of Words and Phrases and their Compositionality. NeurIPS]
and GloVe \cite{pennington2014glove} % [CITE: Pennington, Socher, Manning (2014). GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation. EMNLP]
— remain the canonical dense embedding methods.  We treat each in turn,
then discuss finance-specific adaptations.

% ............................................................
\subsubsection{Word2Vec: Skip-Gram with Negative Sampling}

Word2Vec frames the embedding problem as a supervised prediction task.
The \emph{Skip-Gram} variant trains a word to predict the words that
appear in a sliding window of $\pm c$ positions around it.

Throughout this section, let the corpus consist of a sequence of $T$ tokens
$w_1, w_2, \dots, w_T$ drawn from vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$.  Each
word $w \in \mathcal{V}$ is associated with two embedding vectors:
a \emph{centre-word} vector $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (used when
$w$ is the target word) and a \emph{context-word} vector
$\mathbf{v}_w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (used when $w$ is a context word).
This two-vector convention simplifies the mathematics and is discarded
after training (often the two sets are averaged or the context vectors
are thrown away).

For each centre word $w_t$ and each context position $k \in
\{-c, \ldots, -1, 1, \ldots, c\}$, the model defines the conditional
probability of observing context word $w_{t+k}$ given centre word
$w_t$ via a soft-max over the full vocabulary:
\begin{equation}
  P(w_{t+k} \mid w_t)
  \;=\;
  \frac{\exp\!\bigl(\mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}\bigr)}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{w=1}^{V} \exp\!\bigl(\mathbf{v}_{w}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}\bigr)}.
  \label{eq:skipgram-softmax}
\end{equation}
The Skip-Gram objective maximises the average log-likelihood of all
centre--context pairs:
\begin{equation}
  J_{\mathrm{SG}}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{T}
  \sum_{t=1}^{T}
  \sum_{\substack{k=-c \\ k \neq 0}}^{c}
  \log P(w_{t+k} \mid w_t).
  \label{eq:skipgram-obj}
\end{equation}

The normalising constant in \eqref{eq:skipgram-softmax} sums over
all $V \approx 10^4$--$10^5$ words at every gradient step, making
exact optimisation prohibitively expensive.  \emph{Negative Sampling}
(NEG) replaces the full soft-max with a binary classification task:
distinguish the observed context word (positive sample) from $K$
words drawn from a noise distribution $P_n$ (negative samples).  The
NEG objective for a single centre--context pair $(w_t, w_{t+k})$ is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NEG}}(w_t, w_{t+k})
  \;=\;
  \log \sigma\!\bigl(\mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}\bigr)
  \;+\;
  \sum_{j=1}^{K}
  \mathbb{E}_{w_j \sim P_n}\!\left[
    \log \sigma\!\bigl(-\mathbf{v}_{w_j}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}\bigr)
  \right],
  \label{eq:neg-sampling-obj}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(x) = (1 + e^{-x})^{-1}$ is the logistic sigmoid.
Mikolov et al.\ recommend $K = 5$--$20$ for small corpora and $K = 2$--$5$
for large corpora, with $P_n(w) \propto \text{freq}(w)^{3/4}$ (the
unigram distribution raised to the $3/4$ power), which down-weights
the most frequent stop-words.

Taking the gradient of \eqref{eq:neg-sampling-obj} with respect to the
context vector $\mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}$ (positive sample) gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NEG}}}{\partial \mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}}
  \;=\;
  \bigl[1 - \sigma(\mathbf{v}_{w_{t+k}}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t})\bigr]
  \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}
  \;=\;
  \bigl[1 - P_{\mathrm{NEG}}(D=1 \mid w_{t+k}, w_t)\bigr]\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t},
  \label{eq:neg-grad-pos}
\end{equation}
and for each negative sample $w_j$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NEG}}}{\partial \mathbf{v}_{w_j}}
  \;=\;
  -\sigma(\mathbf{v}_{w_j}^{\top} \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t})\,\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}.
  \label{eq:neg-grad-neg}
\end{equation}
The corresponding update for the centre vector $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{w_t}$ is
obtained by symmetry.  These updates have the appealing interpretation
that the positive context vector is pushed closer to the centre vector
(its coefficient $1 - \sigma(\cdot)$ is large when the two are
dissimilar), while each negative sample's vector is pushed away.
Stochastic gradient descent with these updates is extremely efficient:
each step touches only $K + 1$ rows of the embedding matrix rather
than all $V$ rows.

A celebrated property of Word2Vec embeddings is the emergence of
\emph{linear analogical relationships}.  The classic example is
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{king}}
  - \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{man}}
  + \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{woman}}
  \;\approx\;
  \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{queen}}.
  \label{eq:analogy-royalty}
\end{equation}
These regularities arise when the embedding is
consistent with patterns of co-occurrence: if ``king'' and ``queen''
share the same contextual environments except for gender-related words,
their difference captures a gender direction.  Whether comparable
\emph{financial} analogies emerge depends on the training corpus: the
constituent terms must appear often enough, and in their technical sense,
for the relation to be encoded as a direction---which is precisely why
finance-specific embeddings (Section~\ref{sec:embeddings-finance}) are
sometimes necessary.

% ............................................................
\subsubsection{GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation}

Whereas Word2Vec processes one context window at a time,
\emph{Global Vectors} (GloVe) \cite{pennington2014glove} % [CITE: Pennington et al. (2014) EMNLP]
directly factorises the global word--word co-occurrence matrix,
explicitly encoding corpus-level statistics.

Let $X_{ij}$ denote the number of times word $j$ appears in the
context window of word $i$ across the entire corpus, and let
$X_i = \sum_j X_{ij}$ be the total context count for word $i$.

\begin{definition}[Co-occurrence Matrix]
\label{def:cooccurrence}
Given a corpus of $T$ tokens, a vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ of size $V$,
and a context window of half-width $c$, the \emph{co-occurrence matrix}
$\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{N}^{V \times V}$ has entries
\begin{equation}
  X_{ij}
  \;=\;
  \sum_{t=1}^{T}
  \sum_{\substack{k=-c \\ k \neq 0}}^{c}
  \mathbf{1}[w_t = w_i]\,\mathbf{1}[w_{t+k} = w_j].
  \label{eq:cooccurrence}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The key theoretical insight of GloVe is that the \emph{ratio} of
co-occurrence probabilities $P_{ik}/P_{jk}$ encodes relational
meaning more faithfully than the raw probabilities.  This motivates
learning vectors $\mathbf{w}_i, \tilde{\mathbf{w}}_j \in \mathbb{R}^d$
and scalar biases $b_i, \tilde{b}_j \in \mathbb{R}$ by minimising the
weighted squared loss:
\begin{equation}
  J_{\mathrm{GloVe}}
  \;=\;
  \sum_{i,j=1}^{V}
  f(X_{ij})
  \Bigl(
    \mathbf{w}_i^{\top} \tilde{\mathbf{w}}_j
    + b_i + \tilde{b}_j
    - \log X_{ij}
  \Bigr)^{2},
  \label{eq:glove-obj}
\end{equation}
where $f : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ is a weighting
function that down-weights both very rare and very frequent co-occurrences:
\begin{equation}
  f(x)
  \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \bigl(x / x_{\max}\bigr)^{\alpha} & \text{if } x < x_{\max}, \\
    1 & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:glove-weight}
\end{equation}
Pennington et al.\ recommend $\alpha = 0.75$ and $x_{\max} = 100$.
Pairs with $X_{ij} = 0$ contribute nothing to the sum (since
$f(0) = 0$); the logarithm is therefore only evaluated at positive
counts.

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:glove-vs-word2vec}
GloVe and Word2Vec learn similar representations in practice, but their
optimisation philosophies differ.  GloVe is a single-pass weighted
least-squares problem over the co-occurrence matrix, which is assembled
once and stored; Word2Vec performs online stochastic gradient descent
over individual windows without ever materialising the full matrix.  For
large vocabularies, storing $\mathbf{X}$ requires $O(V^2)$ memory in
the dense case, though in practice $\mathbf{X}$ is extremely sparse.
GloVe tends to be faster to train when co-occurrence counts can be
computed in a preprocessing step, while Word2Vec scales more gracefully
to streaming corpora.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{Finance-Specific Embeddings and Domain Adaptation}
\label{sec:embeddings-finance}

General-purpose embeddings trained on Wikipedia or Common Crawl encode
the general-English meaning of financial terms, which can differ
substantially from their technical meanings.  The word \emph{risk}, for
example, has nearest neighbours \{danger, threat, hazard, peril\} in a
general embedding but nearest neighbours \{volatility, variance,
exposure, drawdown, VaR\} in a finance-domain embedding.  Similarly,
\emph{short} means ``brief'' in general English but ``short-sell a
security'' in a trading context.

Domain adaptation strategies include:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Domain-corpus pre-training.}  Train Word2Vec or GloVe
        from scratch on a large financial corpus (SEC filings, Bloomberg
        articles, earnings call transcripts).  Bloomberg's domain-specific
        language modelling programme \cite{wu2023bloomberggpt}
        and the FinancialPhraseBank embeddings \cite{malo2014phrasebank}
        are canonical examples.
  \item \textbf{Fine-tuned transformer embeddings.}  FinBERT
        \cite{yang2020finbert} % [CITE: Yang et al. (2020). FinBERT: A Pretrained Language Model for Financial Communications. arXiv:2006.08097]
        is a BERT model \cite{devlin2019} % [CITE: Devlin et al. (2019). BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding. NAACL]
        further pre-trained on Reuters news, 10-K filings, and earnings
        calls.  Its token embeddings capture the financial sense of
        polysemous terms such as \emph{margin}, \emph{return}, and
        \emph{position}.
  \item \textbf{Retrofitting.}  Post-hoc adjustment of a general
        embedding using a domain-specific lexicon or knowledge graph
        (e.g.\ a financial ontology) to pull synonymous terms together
        and push antonyms apart.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Case Study: Visualising a Word Analogy with PCA}
\label{sec:embeddings-pca}

To see the linear-analogy structure of Remark~\ref{rem:embedding-geometry}
concretely, we load a pre-trained GloVe model---the 300-dimensional
\texttt{glove-wiki-gigaword-300} release, fetched and cached locally via
\texttt{gensim} (no API key required)---and evaluate the canonical analogy
$\text{king} - \text{man} + \text{woman}$.  Searching the entire
$400{,}000$-word vocabulary, the nearest vector to this combination is indeed
\emph{queen} (cosine similarity $0.67$), ahead of \emph{princess} and
\emph{throne}.

Because the vectors live in $\mathbb{R}^{300}$, we visualise them by projecting
onto two dimensions with principal component analysis (PCA): we stack the
relevant ($\ell_2$-normalised) vectors as the rows of a matrix $\mathbf{E}$ and
keep their coordinates along the two directions of greatest variance---the
eigenvectors of $\mathbf{E}^{\top}\mathbf{E}$ with the largest eigenvalues.
PCA is a linear map, so the projection of $\text{king} - \text{man} +
\text{woman}$ equals the same arithmetic applied to the projected anchors;
Figure~\ref{fig:king-analogy} therefore depicts the operation faithfully rather
than re-deriving it in two dimensions.

The figure makes the mechanism visible: the displacement from \emph{man} to

equally clean analogy exists for specialised \emph{financial} terms depends on
the training corpus---the rationale for the finance-specific embeddings of
Section~\ref{sec:embeddings-finance}.

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:pca-directions}
The principal component directions are not directly interpretable as named
concepts: they are the linear combinations of the original $d = 300$ embedding
dimensions that explain the most variance \emph{among the few selected terms},
and a different selection would produce different axes.  The two components also
capture only part of the total variance, so the 2D layout is a lossy
summary---proximity in the projection is suggestive, not definitive, and exact
relationships should be read from the full-dimensional cosine similarities.
Rotation methods (e.g.\ Varimax) and supervised probing classifiers offer more
principled approaches to identifying interpretable directions in embedding
space; see \cite{tenney2019} % [CITE: Tenney et al. (2019). BERT Rediscovers the Classical NLP Pipeline. ACL]
for an overview in the transformer setting.
\end{remark}

\section{Recurrent Neural Networks and the Vanishing Gradient}
\label{sec:rnn-lstm}

\begin{context}
The representations developed so far treat documents as unordered bags of
tokens.  A sentence like \emph{the firm did \textbf{not} beat earnings}
and \emph{the firm did beat earnings, \textbf{not}} contain identical
vocabulary yet mean opposite things.  Recurrent neural networks (RNNs)
were the first architecture to process sequences token by token, carrying
a hidden state that accumulates contextual information.  They powered a
generation of strong financial NLP models in the early 2010s before being
superseded by the Transformer.  This section previews their central idea
and their central failure mode---the vanishing gradient---to motivate why
a new architecture was needed; \Cref{ch:llm-foundations} develops
both from first principles.
\end{context}

\begin{deepdive}

\subsection{The Recurrence and Why Gradients Vanish}
\label{sec:rnn-vanilla}

A \emph{vanilla recurrent neural network} reads a sequence one token at a
time, folding each token embedding $\mathbf{x}_t$ into a hidden state
$\mathbf{h}_t$ that summarises everything seen so far:
\[
  \mathbf{h}_t
  \;=\;
  \tanh\!\bigl(\mathbf{W}_h\,\mathbf{h}_{t-1}
               + \mathbf{W}_x\,\mathbf{x}_t
               + \mathbf{b}\bigr).
\]
The final state $\mathbf{h}_T$ feeds a task head---a logistic regression
for sentiment, an auto-regressive softmax for language modelling.

\begin{example}[Earnings-call sentiment with a vanilla RNN]
\label{ex:rnn-earnings}
A binary classifier for earnings-call transcripts maps each call to
$p = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{h}_T + b_0)$.  Calls run from a few
hundred to several thousand tokens, and the gradient that trains the model
must travel back through the entire unrolled recurrence---which is exactly
what makes long financial documents hard to learn from.
\end{example}

The obstacle is the \emph{vanishing gradient}.  Propagating the training
error back across $T-k$ steps multiplies together $T-k$ copies of the
recurrence's Jacobian; when the typical factor has magnitude below one,
the product shrinks \emph{exponentially} in the lag.  Concretely, a
per-step contraction of $0.95$ across a $500$-token filing leaves a
gradient of order $0.95^{500}\approx 5\times10^{-12}$: information from the
opening paragraph is numerically gone before training can use it, so the
network never learns dependencies such as a hedging clause that only pays
off hundreds of tokens later.  \Cref{ch:llm-foundations} makes this
precise and states it as a spectral-norm bound
(Theorem~\ref{thm:vanish-explode}).

\subsection{The LSTM Fix, in One Idea}
\label{sec:lstm}

\citet{hochreiter1997long}
% [CITE: Hochreiter, S. & Schmidhuber, J. (1997). Long Short-Term Memory. Neural Computation, 9(8), 1735--1780.]
introduced the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) cell, which adds a
\emph{cell state} updated \emph{additively} rather than by repeated matrix
multiplication.  Learned \emph{gates} decide what the cell keeps, what it
overwrites, and what it reads out; when a gate stays open the cell state
behaves like a near-lossless highway that carries the gradient across long
lags, sidestepping the exponential decay above.  LSTMs powered the
strongest financial NLP models of the early-to-mid 2010s before attention
displaced them.  Section~\ref{sec:sequential-lstm} of
\Cref{ch:llm-foundations} derives the gating equations
(Definition~\ref{def:lstm}) and shows formally why the additive update
preserves the gradient.

\end{deepdive}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Scaled Dot-Product Attention and the Transformer}
\label{sec:attention}

\begin{context}
The Transformer architecture \cite{vaswani2017attention}
% [CITE: Vaswani, A. et al. (2017). Attention Is All You Need. NeurIPS, 30.]
discards recurrence entirely and instead lets every token attend
\emph{directly} to every other token in a single matrix operation.
This ``scaled dot-product attention'' has three advantages over the RNN:
it runs in parallel across the sequence (crucial for GPU-scale training),
it can attend to any position with equal representational power regardless
of distance, and its gradient flows directly between any pair of positions
rather than decaying exponentially with lag.  Every large language model in
use today is a Transformer or a close descendant.  This section previews
the mechanism; \Cref{ch:llm-foundations} derives it in full.
\end{context}

\begin{deepdive}

\subsection{Attention as Content-Based Lookup}
\label{sec:attention-mechanism}

Attention replaces the RNN's step-by-step state with a single parallel
operation.  Each token emits a \emph{query}, every token exposes a
\emph{key} and a \emph{value}, and a token's updated representation is the
average of the values weighted by how well its query matches each key:
\[
  \operatorname{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V})
  \;=\;
  \operatorname{softmax}\!\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V},
\]
where $\operatorname{softmax}$ turns each row of match scores into
non-negative weights that sum to one.  Because every pair of positions is
compared directly, attention spans an entire filing in one step and its
gradient does not decay with distance---precisely the two properties the
RNN lacked.

The $\sqrt{d_k}$ divisor is the one detail worth flagging now: the dot
product of two $d_k$-dimensional vectors has variance that grows with
$d_k$, so without rescaling the softmax is driven into its saturated,
near-zero-gradient regime.  Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ restores unit-scale
scores.  \Cref{ch:llm-foundations} derives attention from first
principles (Definition~\ref{def:scaled-dot-product-attn}) and proves the
scaling result (Theorem~\ref{thm:sqrt-dk-scaling}).

\subsection{Multiple Heads, Multiple Relations}
\label{sec:attention-multihead}

A single attention pattern can track only one kind of relationship.
\emph{Multi-head attention} runs several attention operations in parallel
on lower-dimensional projections and concatenates their outputs, letting
different heads specialise.  In financial text one head may follow temporal
links (\emph{prior quarter} vs.\ \emph{current quarter}), another entity
coreference (\emph{the company}, \emph{it}, \emph{JPMorgan}), and another
negation scope (\emph{did not}, \emph{failed to}).
Section~\ref{sec:transformer-mha} of \Cref{ch:llm-foundations} gives
the construction (Definition~\ref{def:mha}).

\begin{remark}[Why attention matters for financial text]
\label{rem:attention-finance}
Beyond raw accuracy, attention offers a degree of \emph{interpretability}
that bag-of-words models cannot: the attention weights expose which parts
of a document a prediction rests on, letting an analyst see when a model
keys on forward-looking guidance or on a specific risk-factor disclosure.
We return to attention-based interpretability for finance in the
explainability and evaluation chapters.
\end{remark}

\end{deepdive}

\section{A Taxonomy of Modern Language Model Families}
\label{sec:llm-families}

\begin{context}
The Transformer architecture has given rise to hundreds of distinct model
families.  From a practitioner's perspective, two design choices define the
primary axis of variation: \emph{training objective} (autoregressive
next-token prediction vs.\ masked token prediction vs.\ sequence-to-sequence)
and \emph{domain} (general-purpose vs.\ finance-specific).  Understanding
these axes determines which model family is appropriate for a given
financial NLP task.
\end{context}

\begin{deepdive}

\subsection{Autoregressive Models: The GPT Family}
\label{sec:llm-gpt}

Autoregressive language models are trained to predict the next token
given all preceding tokens.  Let $p_\theta(w_t \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{t-1})$
be the model probability; the training objective is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{AR}}(\theta)
  \;=\;
  -\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{t-1}).
  \label{eq:ar-objective}
\end{equation}
This negative log-likelihood is the \emph{cross-entropy} loss between the model's
predicted next-token distribution and the one-hot distribution of the observed token;
the masked objective below and essentially every language-model loss in later chapters
are the same cross-entropy, summed over different positions.
GPT-1 \cite{radford2018gpt}
% [CITE: Radford, A. et al. (2018). Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training. OpenAI blog.]
used a 12-layer Transformer decoder trained on the Books corpus
(800M words), demonstrating that unsupervised pre-training followed by
task-specific fine-tuning could match or exceed specialised models across
twelve NLP benchmarks.  GPT-2 \cite{radford2019gpt2}
% [CITE: Radford, A. et al. (2019). Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners. OpenAI blog.]
scaled this to 1.5B parameters and showed that sufficiently large autoregressive
models exhibit \emph{zero-shot} generalisation: prompted with a question and
no labelled examples, the model answers correctly without any task-specific
training.  GPT-3 \cite{brown2020language}
% [CITE: Brown, T. et al. (2020). Language Models are Few-Shot Learners. NeurIPS, 33, 1877–1901.]
at 175B parameters and its instruction-tuned successors (GPT-3.5, GPT-4)
form the backbone of the OpenAI API and are the models most widely
deployed in financial applications today.

\subsection{Bidirectional Encoder Models: BERT and Its Descendants}
\label{sec:llm-bert}

\begin{definition}[Masked Language Modelling Objective]
\label{def:mlm-intro}
In the Masked Language Model (MLM) training objective, a fraction $p$
of input tokens (typically $p = 0.15$) is replaced with a \texttt{[MASK]}
token and the model is trained to predict the original token:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MLM}}(\theta)
  \;=\;
  -\sum_{i \in \mathcal{M}}
  \log p_\theta(w_i \mid \mathbf{w}_{\setminus i}),
  \label{eq:mlm-objective}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{M}$ indexes the masked positions and
$\mathbf{w}_{\setminus i}$ denotes all tokens except position $i$.
\end{definition}

BERT \cite{devlin2019}
% [CITE: Devlin, J. et al. (2019). BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding. NAACL-HLT, 4171–4186.]
trains a 12- or 24-layer Transformer \emph{encoder} (not decoder) with
the MLM objective, enabling each position to attend to both left and right
context simultaneously.  This bidirectionality makes BERT exceptional for
classification and extraction tasks but unsuitable for generation.

Two finance-specific BERT variants deserve particular attention.
(Note: two models share the name FinBERT — Araci (2019) fine-tuned BERT
on financial text; Yang et al.\ (2020) retrained BERT from scratch on
financial corpora.  This chapter refers to \cite{yang2020finbert}.)
% [CITE: Yang et al. (2020). FinBERT: A Pretrained Language Model for Financial Communications. arXiv:2006.08097]
\citet{yang2020finbert} further pre-trained BERT-base on Reuters news,
10-K filings, and earnings call transcripts, achieving state-of-the-art
sentiment classification on the Financial PhraseBank dataset at the time of
publication.

\subsection{Encoder-Decoder Models: T5}
\label{sec:llm-t5}

T5 (Text-To-Text Transfer Transformer) \cite{raffel2020t5}
% [CITE: Raffel, C. et al. (2020). Exploring the Limits of Transfer Learning with a Unified Text-to-Text Transformer. JMLR, 21(140), 1–67.]
frames \emph{every} NLP task as a sequence-to-sequence problem by
converting both inputs and outputs to text strings.  Sentiment analysis
becomes ``sentiment: \texttt{[document]}'' → ``positive''; question
answering becomes ``question: \ldots context: \ldots'' → ``\texttt{[answer]}''.
The unified text-to-text interface simplifies multi-task financial pipelines
where the same model may be asked to perform summarisation of an 8-K filing,
extraction of financial figures, and classification of management tone, all
with a single API call.

\subsection{Open-Weight Models: The LLaMA Family}
\label{sec:llm-llama}

LLaMA (Large Language Model Meta AI) \cite{touvron2023llama}
% [CITE: Touvron, H. et al. (2023). LLaMA: Open and Efficient Foundation Language Models. arXiv:2302.13971.]
released open-weight decoder models (7B–65B parameters) trained on
publicly available corpora (Common Crawl, Wikipedia, GitHub, arXiv, Books).
Unlike the GPT family, LLaMA weights are freely downloadable and can be
run on institutional servers without routing proprietary financial data
through a third-party API — a compliance and data-sovereignty advantage
that is significant for regulated financial firms.  Subsequent versions
(LLaMA-2, LLaMA-3) extended context windows to 128K tokens, enabling
processing of lengthy regulatory filings in a single forward pass.

\subsection{Finance-Domain Models}
\label{sec:llm-finance-models}

BloombergGPT \cite{wu2023bloomberggpt}
% [CITE: Wu, S. et al. (2023). BloombergGPT: A Large Language Model for Finance. arXiv:2303.17564.]
is a 50B-parameter autoregressive LLM trained on a 363B-token financial
corpus (Bloomberg news, filings, earnings transcripts, and social media)
alongside a 345B-token general corpus.  On finance-specific benchmarks
(ConvFinQA, FiQA-SA, FPB), it outperformed general-purpose models of
comparable size, validating the domain-adaptation hypothesis at scale.
FinGPT \cite{Yang2023}
% [CITE: Yang, H. et al. (2023). FinGPT: Open-Source Financial Large Language Models. arXiv:2306.06031.]
subsequently showed that parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA) of a
general-purpose 7B model on financial data can match much larger
domain-pretrained models for many tasks, offering a cost-effective path
for academic researchers without access to proprietary Bloomberg data.

\begin{remark}[Model selection heuristics for financial applications]
\label{rem:model-selection}
In practice, the choice among model families reduces to three
considerations: (1)~\emph{task type} — classification and extraction
favour encoder models (BERT-family); generation and summarisation favour
decoder-only (GPT/LLaMA) or encoder-decoder (T5) models;
(2)~\emph{data sensitivity} — proprietary trading data or client
information must stay on-premise, favouring open-weight models;
(3)~\emph{latency constraints} — smaller models (7B–13B parameters)
run in real time on a single A100 GPU, while 70B+ models require
multi-GPU inference pipelines.
\end{remark}

\end{deepdive}

\section{Querying Large Language Models via Python APIs}
\label{sec:llm-apis}

\begin{context}
Every model family discussed in \Cref{sec:llm-families} is
accessible via a Python API.  For practitioners, the three most relevant
are the OpenAI API (GPT family), the Anthropic API (Claude family), and
the HuggingFace \texttt{transformers} library (open-weight models
including LLaMA, FinBERT, and T5).  Understanding how to query these APIs —
and the cost and latency implications — is a prerequisite for building any
production financial NLP pipeline.
\end{context}

\begin{deepdive}

\subsection{The OpenAI Chat Completions API}
\label{sec:api-openai}

OpenAI exposes models through a REST API wrapped by the
\texttt{openai} Python package.  The fundamental object is a
\emph{chat completion}: a list of role-tagged messages is sent to the
model and the response is returned as a structured JSON object.

\begin{minted}{python}
import openai

client = openai.OpenAI()  # reads OPENAI_API_KEY from the environment
resp = client.chat.completions.create(
    model="gpt-5.6-luna",
    messages=[
        {"role": "system", "content": "You are a financial analyst."},
        {"role": "user", "content": "Sentiment of 'Revenue fell 12%.'? One word."},
    ],
)
print(resp.choices[0].message.content)  # -> 'Negative'
\end{minted}

\subsection{The Anthropic API}
\label{sec:api-anthropic}

Anthropic's Claude models are queried through a similar interface: a list of
role-tagged messages is sent and a structured response is returned. A minimal
finance query looks like:

\begin{minted}{python}
import anthropic

client = anthropic.Anthropic()  # reads ANTHROPIC_API_KEY from the environment
resp = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-opus-4-8",
    max_tokens=256,
    system="You are a financial analyst. Answer concisely.",
    messages=[{"role": "user",
               "content": "Classify the sentiment of: "
                          "'Revenue fell 12% on weak demand.' "
                          "Answer with one word."}],
)
print(resp.content[0].text)  # -> 'Negative'
\end{minted}

\noindent The two proprietary APIs share the same request shape; richer prompt templates
are developed as an exercise in the student practical \texttt{code/practicals/01-intro/practical.ipynb}.

\subsection{HuggingFace \texttt{transformers}: Open-Weight Inference}
\label{sec:api-huggingface}

For open-weight models (LLaMA, FinBERT, T5-finance), the HuggingFace
\texttt{transformers} library provides a unified interface that runs the model locally
rather than over a network API:

\begin{minted}{python}
from transformers import pipeline

clf = pipeline("text-classification", model="ProsusAI/finbert")
print(clf("Revenue fell 12% on weak demand."))
# -> [{'label': 'negative', 'score': 0.95}]
\end{minted}

\begin{remark}[Prompt engineering as cost control]
\label{rem:prompt-engineering}
The single most effective cost-reduction strategy is prompt brevity: the
fewer input tokens, the lower the cost.  In classification tasks, a one-line
system prompt with a one-sentence instruction (as in the example above)
typically outperforms a lengthy five-paragraph
prompt on accuracy while costing far less.  Chain-of-thought prompting
\cite{wei2022chain}
% [CITE: Wei, J. et al. (2022). Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models. NeurIPS.]
can improve accuracy on multi-step financial reasoning tasks but substantially increases
output-token counts, making it cost-effective only when accuracy improvements
justify the expense.
\end{remark}

\section{Limitations of LLMs and the Regulatory Landscape}
\label{sec:llm-limitations}

\begin{context}
Large language models are powerful but imperfect tools.  Before deploying
an LLM in a financial application — whether for news summarisation,
regulatory filing analysis, or client-facing Q\&A — a practitioner must
understand the systematic ways in which these models fail and the
regulatory framework that governs their use.  Ignoring either dimension
creates material operational, reputational, and legal risk.
\end{context}

\begin{deepdive}

\subsection{Hallucination: Taxonomy and Detection}
\label{sec:llm-hallucination}

\begin{definition}[LLM Hallucination]
\label{def:hallucination}
An LLM \emph{hallucination} is a model output that is fluent and
internally coherent but factually incorrect, unverifiable, or unsupported
by the input context.  In financial applications, hallucinations partition
into three types:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Factual hallucination.}  The model fabricates a specific
        datum — a stock price, a revenue figure, a regulatory provision
        number — that does not appear in the source material and is
        numerically incorrect.
  \item \textbf{Attribution hallucination.}  The model correctly states
        a fact but attributes it to the wrong source, company, or time
        period (e.g.\ ascribing Q3 2023 earnings guidance to a Q2 2023 filing).
  \item \textbf{Reasoning hallucination.}  The model's stated reasoning
        chain is plausible-sounding but contains a logical error — common
        in multi-step valuation or ratio analysis tasks.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\citet{didisheim2025memory}
% [CITE: Didisheim, A., Ke, Z., Kelly, B., & Vorsatz, B. (2024). Machines vs. Markets: The Power of Human-Aligned AI in Financial Forecasting. Working paper.]
show that LLMs carry memorised knowledge of historical asset returns
into zero-shot forecasting tasks, raising concerns about data contamination:
out-of-sample tests may be invalid if the model has been pre-trained on
the very time series it is asked to predict.  This constitutes a form of
attribution hallucination at the dataset level.

Practical mitigation strategies include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG).}  Provide the
        relevant source document as context and instruct the model to
        answer ``only from the provided text,'' reducing reliance on
        parametric memory.
  \item \textbf{Citation enforcement.}  Require the model to cite the
        specific sentence and page number that supports each numerical
        claim, enabling automated verification against the source.
  \item \textbf{Consistency sampling.}  Generate $k \geq 5$ responses
        with temperature $> 0$ and flag claims with high variance across
        responses as hallucination-prone.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Context Window Limitations}
\label{sec:llm-context-limits}

Every LLM has a fixed \emph{context window} $L_{\max}$ — the maximum
number of tokens the model can attend over in a single forward pass.  For
financial documents, this constraint is binding:

\begin{center}
  \captionof{table}{Typical lengths of financial documents compared to
             representative model context windows (token counts approximate).}
  \label{tab:context-windows}
  \begin{tabular}{lrr}
    \toprule
    Document type & Median tokens & Exceeds 128K? \\
    \midrule
    Earnings press release & 800    & No  \\
    Earnings call transcript & 6{,}000 & No  \\
    10-Q (quarterly filing) & 35{,}000 & No  \\
    10-K (annual filing)    & 70{,}000 & No  \\
    Proxy statement (DEF 14A) & 120{,}000 & Occasionally \\
    Basel III Pillar 3 report & 250{,}000 & Yes \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{center}

While 128K+ context windows (e.g.\ GPT-5, Claude Opus 4.8) handle most standard
filings---and frontier models now reach 1M+ tokens (e.g.\ Gemini 3 Pro)---very long
regulatory documents still require chunking strategies.
Moreover, empirical work shows that retrieval accuracy degrades for
information placed in the middle of long contexts — a phenomenon termed
\emph{lost in the middle} \cite{liu2023lost}
% [CITE: Liu, N.F. et al. (2023). Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts. TACL.]
— suggesting that even when the document fits in the window, placement
of relevant facts matters.

\subsection{Model Bias in Financial Applications}
\label{sec:llm-bias}

LLMs inherit biases present in their pre-training corpora.  In financial
NLP, two biases warrant specific attention:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Recency and survivorship bias.}  Training corpora over-represent
        large, liquid, frequently covered firms.  Zero-shot analysis of small-cap
        or emerging-market companies may be less reliable.
  \item \textbf{Sentiment framing bias.}  Models trained on financial news
        media systematically reproduce framing effects present in news coverage,
        potentially amplifying media narratives rather than extracting
        underlying fundamental information.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Regulatory Landscape}
\label{sec:llm-regulation}

\begin{remark}[EU AI Act (2024) — Implications for Financial AI]
\label{rem:eu-ai-act}
The EU AI Act, entering into force August 2024, classifies AI systems
into four risk tiers.  General-purpose LLM APIs (Articles~51--52 of \citet{euaiact2024}) must comply
with transparency obligations: operators must disclose that users are
interacting with an AI system and cannot deploy systems to generate content
that could deceive users about its artificial origin.  For financial
institutions, this implies mandatory disclosure when LLMs are used in
client-facing communications, robo-advisory services, or automated
regulatory filings.  High-risk applications — credit scoring, insurance
underwriting, employment screening — face additional conformity assessment,
data governance, and human oversight requirements under Annex~III.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[SEC 2023 Guidance on AI in Investment Advice]
\label{rem:sec-guidance}
In July 2023 the US Securities and Exchange Commission proposed rules
requiring investment advisers and broker-dealers to eliminate or neutralise
conflicts of interest when using ``predictive data analytics,'' including
LLMs, in investor interactions~\citep{sec2023predictive}.  Covered firms must evaluate whether their
AI-driven recommendations prioritise investor interests or the firm's own
financial interests and document that evaluation.  The guidance signals
that LLM outputs that influence investment decisions are subject to the
same fiduciary duty standards as human advisor recommendations.
\end{remark}

\end{deepdive}

\begin{context}

mechanism previewed here in Section~\ref{sec:attention} reappears there with a
complete treatment of causal masking and key-value caching.  Fine-tuning, instruction tuning, and
parameter-efficient adaptation (LoRA, prefix tuning) are taken up in the chapters
on domain adaptation, where the domain-specific models surveyed in
\Cref{sec:llm-families} are evaluated empirically.

with formal metrics and mitigation benchmarks in the chapter on evaluation.

The landscape of financial LLMs has expanded rapidly.  \citet{li2023llmfinsurvey} survey the full range of LLM applications in finance---from
sentiment analysis and question answering to algorithmic trading and regulatory
compliance---providing a systematic taxonomy of tasks, models, and benchmark datasets.
On the open-source side, \citet{Yang2023} propose FinGPT, a
data-centric open-source framework for training and fine-tuning financial LLMs via
low-rank adaptation, making domain-specific model development accessible to academic
researchers without proprietary data access.

Alongside the promise, recent research has uncovered important failure modes specific
to financial applications.  \citet{didisheim2025memory}
show that LLMs carry \emph{predictable memorised knowledge} about historical asset
returns into zero-shot forecasting tasks, raising concerns about data contamination
and the validity of out-of-sample tests when models have been pre-trained on the very
time series they are asked to predict.  \citet{chen2024uncertainty} demonstrate that the conditional probabilities produced by
LLMs encode economically meaningful uncertainty estimates, offering a principled way
to quantify model confidence in financial classification tasks without access to
internal model weights.  Together these results motivate the careful evaluation
protocols developed throughout this book.

\end{context}

\section{Roadmap: How This Book Is Organized}
\label{sec:book-roadmap}

The remainder of this book is organized in six parts that move deliberately
from simpler foundations to more advanced engineering: no concept is used
before an earlier chapter has introduced it.

\textbf{Part~I, Foundations of Large Language Models}, builds the technical
base.  \Cref{ch:llm-foundations} develops the architecture of
transformer-based language models from the ground up, deriving the attention
mechanism, the pre-training objectives, and the tokenization schemes that
underlie all modern LLMs.  \Cref{ch:llm-training} covers training and
fine-tuning --- supervised fine-tuning, reinforcement learning from human
feedback, and parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA ---
and \Cref{ch:domain-specific-llms} turns to language models trained or
adapted specifically for finance (FinBERT, BloombergGPT, and the
domain-adaptation recipes behind them).

\textbf{Part~II, Agents, Tools, and Skills} (\Cref{ch:llm-agents}), extends
the single-model view to systems that plan, call tools, retrieve external
data, and coordinate as multiple agents.  It also introduces the file-based
form of the agentic primitives --- agents, skills, and hooks as
version-controlled Markdown and configuration artifacts
(\Cref{subsec:file-based-pattern}) --- that the engineering chapter in
Part~VI later composes into full iteration loops.

\textbf{Part~III, Understanding Financial Text}, applies these foundations to
reading: \Cref{ch:financial-nlp-sentiment} treats financial NLP and sentiment
analysis, and \Cref{ch:financial-text-summarization} treats summarization,
named-entity recognition, relation extraction, and table extraction from
filings and reports.

\textbf{Part~IV, Quantitative Finance Applications}, covers the numerical
workhorses: business valuation (\Cref{ch:business-valuation}; discounted-cash-flow
and comparable-company analysis), credit-risk analysis
(\Cref{ch:credit-risk}; scoring, fairness, and calibration), and portfolio
optimization and quantitative trading (\Cref{ch:portfolio-quant-trading}).
These chapters can be read independently once Parts~I--II are in place; a
reader with a specific use case can proceed directly to the relevant chapter.

\textbf{Part~V, Trust, Governance, and Deployment}, turns to the controls that
make the applications deployable: regulatory technology, compliance, and
anti-money-laundering (\Cref{ch:regtech-compliance-aml}); explainability and
interpretability (\Cref{ch:xai-explainability}); a systematic treatment of
LLM limitations and evaluation methodology
(\Cref{ch:llm-limitations-evaluation}); and privacy-preserving, locally
deployed models (\Cref{ch:privacy-local-models}), addressing the
data-residency and confidentiality concerns that constrain cloud-hosted
models in financial institutions.

\textbf{Part~VI, Engineering Agentic Workflows and Outlook}, is the
methodology capstone.  \Cref{ch:loops-goals-iterations} shows how the agents,
skills, and hooks of Part~II are composed into goal-directed iteration loops
--- with worked examples that build on the credit-risk and governance material
of Parts~IV--V --- and \Cref{ch:applications-future} closes with emerging
applications, deployment patterns, and future directions.
