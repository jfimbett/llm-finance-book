01

Data for LLMs

Where financial language comes from — and why a general tokenizer taxes it.
The raw material is scarce

Financial text is a tiny slice of the web

A model trained on "the internet" mostly reads casual prose. Financial contracts barely register — so the model is fluent at chit-chat and weak on 10-Ks.

the bigger picture Financial text is roughly 0.5% of a typical filtered web crawl. A model that has seen the distribution of the web will be fluent in casual prose and weak on financial contracts.
EDGAR
10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, proxy filings; full regulatory English; public from 1996 to present.
Earnings-call transcripts
Spoken-register financial language, Q&A with management; via commercial vendors or scraping.
Two more sources — and the bill

Bloomberg and alternative data round out the corpus

Bloomberg news
Licensed; high-density, entity-linked financial news with multi-decade coverage.
Alternative data
Satellite-imagery metadata, credit-card transaction summaries, job-posting feeds, shipping manifests.
consequence Low financial representation ⇒ high tokenizer fertility on financial text ⇒ longer sequences ⇒ higher cost and lower accuracy.
Why finance words cost more

The tokenizer shreds financial vocabulary into pieces

A tokenizer chops text into chunks the model reads. Common English words stay whole; finance jargon it has rarely seen gets shattered into fragments — and you pay per fragment.

  • Fertility ≈ 1.0 means efficient coverage; fertility > 1.5 means heavy fragmentation of domain vocabulary.
  • CUSIP, EBITDA, ISDA may split into 3–5 sub-tokens, each consuming an attention slot.
  • Training a domain-specific tokenizer alongside domain weights reduces fertility and shortens effective sequences.
Text typeFertility (GPT-2 BPE)Effect
Standard English prose1.10–1.20Efficient
Financial regulatory text1.40–1.60Fragmented
Byte-pair encoding (BPE) iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent symbol pair until the target vocabulary size is reached. Fertility of tokenizer \(\tau\) on corpus \(C\): \[ F(\tau, C) = \frac{\text{tokens produced by } \tau \text{ on } C}{\text{whitespace-separated words in } C}. \]
Cleaning before training

Raw web crawl is about 90% noise

Before any learning happens, a pipeline throws away most of what it scraped. Four stages turn garbage into a usable corpus.

  1. Language identification — a fastText classifier labels each document; non-target languages discarded.
  2. Heuristic filters — punctuation rate, symbol-to-word ratio, minimum token count, repeated n-gram detection.
  3. Perplexity filtering — a small KenLM reference model scores each document; high-perplexity outliers removed.
  4. MinHash deduplication + PII scrubbing — near-duplicate removal by Jaccard similarity; emails, phone numbers, SSNs stripped.
The most important filter

Why removing duplicates matters most

If the same text appears a thousand times, the model memorizes it instead of learning from it. In finance this is acute — one press release is mirrored by hundreds of portals.

why deduplication matters most Duplicated training data causes memorisation, not generalisation. Financial press releases are reproduced verbatim by hundreds of portals.
With \(S(d)\) the set of \(k\)-gram shingles of document \(d\), the Jaccard similarity is \[ J(A, B) = \frac{|S(A) \cap S(B)|}{|S(A) \cup S(B)|}. \] Documents with \(J > 0.8\) are near-duplicates; all but one copy is removed.

Pre-training and scaling

What the model learns from raw text — and the rule for how big to build it.
What "training" actually optimizes

Three ways to teach a model to read

Pre-training is a fill-in-the-blank game. The kind of blank you ask the model to fill decides what it becomes good at.

ObjectiveThe blankBest suited for
CLM (causal LM)Predict the next tokenGeneration, chat, decoder-only (GPT)
MLM (masked LM)Fill masked words from both sidesClassification, NER, encoder-only (BERT, FinBERT)
Span corruption (T5)Reconstruct masked spans from sentinelsSeq2seq, QA, summarisation
Finance rule of thumb
Classification / extraction → MLM encoder (FinBERT).   Open-ended analysis / generation → CLM decoder.
CLM uses causal (lower-triangular) attention masking: \[ -\sum_t \log P(x_t \mid x_{<t}). \] MLM attends bidirectionally to all unmasked tokens, over the masked set \(\mathcal{M}\): \[ -\sum_{t\in\mathcal{M}} \log P(x_t \mid x_{\setminus\mathcal{M}}). \] Span corruption replaces runs of consecutive tokens with a single sentinel <extra_id_k> and trains the decoder to produce all spans in order.
How big should the model be?

The scaling question: more parameters or more data?

You have a fixed compute budget. Spend it on a bigger brain or on more reading? Hoffmann et al. (2022) measured the trade-off precisely.

  • \(N\) = trainable parameters (model size)
  • \(D\) = training tokens (how much it reads)
  • Compute scales as \(C \approx 6ND\) — the budget that ties them together
The empirical loss of an autoregressive LM is fit as \[ L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}}, \] with \(E\) the irreducible entropy floor and \(\alpha,\beta>0\) governing each term's decay. The compute constraint is \[ C \approx 6ND \] (factor 6: 2 for the forward pass, 4 for the backward pass and parameter update — standard mixed-precision accounting). The question: given fixed \(C\), what is the optimal split \((N^*, D^*)\)?
The answer that reshaped the field

Roughly 20 tokens of data per parameter

The compute-optimal recipe: for every parameter, feed it about twenty tokens. By that ruler, the famous giant models were built wrong.

Minimising \(L(N,D)\) subject to \(C = 6ND\) gives \[ N^*(C) \propto C^{\beta/(\alpha+\beta)}, \qquad D^*(C) \propto C^{\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)}. \] With Chinchilla estimates \(\alpha \approx 0.34,\ \beta \approx 0.28\): \[ \frac{D^*}{N^*} \approx 20. \]

Fine-tuning

Adapting a base model to finance — cheaply — and aligning it to what humans want.
A menu of adaptation

Four ways to bend a model toward your task

You don't always retrain. The choice runs from "just ask it" to "rebuild every weight," trading data and compute for accuracy.

The hidden cost of specializing

Why fine-tuning can make a model worse

Teach a model only finance and it can forget how to be a good generalist — a real problem when the same assistant must also answer everyday client questions.

the adaptation tax Full fine-tuning on a narrow domain can degrade performance on adjacent tasks — a real risk when a deployed finance LLM must also handle general client queries. The art of adaptation is buying in-domain accuracy without surrendering the breadth that made the base model useful.
The cheap-adaptation trick

LoRA: train 0.12% of the weights, get most of the benefit

Instead of moving all seven billion weights, LoRA freezes them and learns a tiny pair of side-matrices. The update is so small it fits in a fraction of the memory.

≈ 8.4M
trainable params (7B LLaMA, r=8)
0.12%
of the full model
For a pre-trained weight matrix \(W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times k}\), LoRA sets \[ W = W_0 + BA, \quad B \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times r},\; A \in \mathbb{R}^{r\times k},\; r \ll \min(d,k). \] \(W_0\) is frozen; only \(B\) and \(A\) train. Trainable parameters per layer: \(r(d+k)\).

Worked example — 7B LLaMA, \(r=8\), all 4 projections, 32 layers: \[ 32 \times 4 \times 8 \times (4096 + 4096) = 8{,}388{,}608 \approx 8.4\text{M}, \qquad \frac{8.4\text{M}}{7{,}000\text{M}} \approx 0.12\%. \]
What you save

LoRA vs. full fine-tuning: 90 minutes against 40 hours

The payoff is wall-clock time and money — and after training you merge the side-matrices back in, so serving the model costs nothing extra.

From capable to compliant

A trained model doesn't know what humans prefer

Predicting the next word makes a model fluent, not trustworthy. Alignment teaches it the difference between an answer that sounds right and one that is accurate and compliant.

why alignment matters in finance A base model predicts the next token; it does not know what humans prefer. RLHF aligns behaviour to human judgement — in finance, the gap between a "plausible" answer and an "accurate, compliant" one is exactly what alignment must close.
Stage 1 — SFT
Train the base model on high-quality human-written demonstrations. Produces an initial policy \(\pi_{\text{SFT}}\).
Stage 2 — Reward model
Annotators rank pairs of responses \((y_w \succ y_l)\) for the same prompt \(x\); a reward model learns the ranking.
The reward model \(r_\theta\) is fit on ranked pairs with \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}} = -\mathbb{E}\!\left[\log \sigma\!\left(r_\theta(x,y_w) - r_\theta(x,y_l)\right)\right]. \]
RLHF, the final stage

Stage 3: chase the reward, but don't drift too far

The model now tunes itself to score high on the reward model — while a leash keeps it from wandering away from the sensible behaviour it started with.

limitations for finance Reward models can be gamed; PPO is unstable; human annotation is expensive. A single annotator disagreement on "plausible vs. accurate" in a financial context can propagate to thousands of training examples.
Stage 3 optimises the policy \(\pi_\theta\) against the reward \(r_\phi\), penalised for drifting from the SFT reference \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\): \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{RL}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\!\left[r_\phi(x,y) - \beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\pi_\theta(\cdot|x) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot|x)\right)\right]. \] \(\beta\) controls how far the policy can drift from the reference.
A simpler alignment

DPO: align directly, skip the reward model

Direct Preference Optimisation throws out the separate reward model and the unstable RL loop. It learns straight from the same "this answer beats that one" pairs.

  • No separate reward model to train or maintain
  • Same preference data as RLHF; simpler single-stage training loop
  • Empirically competitive with RLHF on alignment benchmarks
  • \(\beta\) still controls the implicit KL penalty vs. \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\)
Interpretation
DPO increases the log-probability of the preferred response \(y_w\) relative to \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\) while decreasing that of \(y_l\) — a direct implementation of the preference signal.
DPO re-parameterises the reward in terms of policy log-ratios and optimises \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)}\!\left[ \log \sigma\!\left( \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)} \right)\right]. \]

BloombergGPT uses ALiBi, biasing attention scores by query-key distance: \[ \text{score}(q_i, k_j) = \frac{q_i^\top k_j}{\sqrt{d_k}} - m\,(i - j), \] where \(m\) is a head-specific slope. ALiBi extrapolates more gracefully to sequences longer than those seen in training than learned absolute positional embeddings.
How do we grade a model?

Perplexity and Macro-F1: fluency vs. fairness

Two different questions: how surprised is the model by real text (perplexity), and how well does it classify every category — including the rare, important ones (Macro-F1)?

Perplexity
Language-modelling fluency. Three limits: not a task metric; gameable by spreading probability mass; needs token-level probabilities.
Macro-F1
Treats all classes equally regardless of frequency — right for imbalanced financial datasets where rare event classes matter as much as common ones.
Perplexity from per-token cross-entropy \(H\): \[ \text{PPL} = 2^{H}, \qquad H = -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log_2 p(x_t \mid x_{<t}). \] Macro-F1 averages per-class F1 over \(K\) classes: \[ \text{Macro-F1} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{2\,P_k R_k}{P_k + R_k}. \]
Summaries and the finance scoreboard

ROUGE for summaries — and where LLMs still fail

ROUGE checks how much of a reference summary the model recovered. The FinBen benchmark then stress-tests finance LLMs broadly — and finds a revealing weak spot.

FinBen benchmark
42 datasets, 24 tasks, 21 models. LLMs excel at extraction/classification but struggle on forecasting and decision tasks — exactly what practitioners want most.
\[ \text{ROUGE-N} = \frac{\sum_{\text{ref}}\sum_{g \in \text{ngrams(ref},N)} \text{count}_\text{match}(g)} {\sum_{\text{ref}}\sum_{g \in \text{ngrams(ref},N)} \text{count}(g)}. \]
Breaking the model on purpose

Four ways a finance LLM fails under red-teaming

Adversarial testing surfaces recurring, finance-specific failure modes — the kind that turn a confident answer into a compliance incident.

  1. Factual hallucination — plausible but incorrect price forecasts or earnings figures
  2. Temporal hallucination — cites outdated regulations as current (e.g., pre-2018 MiFID II rules)
  3. Regulatory compliance failure — gives what looks like personalised investment advice without required licensing disclosures
  4. Herd-bias amplification — echoes analyst consensus while suppressing dissenting signals
empirical illustration · Lopez-Lira & Tang (2023) A long-short strategy on ChatGPT sentiment scores reached a Sharpe ratio > 6 in 2021, declining to ≈ 1 by late 2024 as the signal became known and arbitraged away. Any published alpha source degrades once it is public.
05

Safety and governance

The biases baked into the data, the lies the model tells, and the rulebook around both.
The data has a worldview

Four biases baked into financial training data

A model inherits the blind spots of what it read. In finance, four recurring biases systematically tilt its picture of the world.

survivorship bias Corpora overrepresent successful firms: public companies file EDGAR; bankrupt or never-public firms do not. The model learns a rosier world than practitioners inhabit.
Temporal bias
Models trained on historical data are deployed in structurally different regimes (post-pandemic, rising rates, de-globalisation). Past test performance may not persist.
Geographic bias
English-language, US-centric text dominates open web corpora. Non-US conventions, currencies, and legal structures are underrepresented.
Analyst consensus bias
Commentary echoes dominant sell-side views; contrarian information is rarer in the training distribution and systematically under-weighted.
When the model makes things up

Two kinds of hallucination, four layered defenses

Models invent facts and misquote rules. No single fix works — production systems stack several defenses with an audit trail at every step.

Two hallucination types
  • Factual — fabricated prices, dates, figures in no training document
  • Regulatory — rules that are outdated, misquoted, or jurisdiction-mismatched
  1. RAG — ground outputs in retrieved, verified passages
  2. Confidence calibration — flag or reject responses below a calibrated threshold
  3. Output verification — a checker flags claims inconsistent with a knowledge base
  4. Human-in-the-loop — route low-confidence / high-stakes outputs to expert review
no single mitigation is sufficient Production deployments should layer at least two mitigations, with audit trails at every step.
The rulebook

Three regimes that govern finance AI

Deploying an LLM in finance means answering to regulators. Three frameworks set the bar for explainability, validation, and oversight.

MiFID II (Art. 37)
Research must be independent. LLM contributions need demonstrable suitability and explainability. Attention-weight visualisations are not sufficient — they show co-occurrence, not causal reasoning.
SR 11-7 (Fed)
Model-risk management with tiered validation. High-risk models (credit, capital, trading) need independent validation, assumption documentation, periodic monitoring.
EU AI Act (Aug 2026)
Conformity assessments, technical docs, human oversight, EU-database registration for high-risk financial AI (credit scoring, insurance pricing, investment decisions at scale).
Operationalizing the rules

Classify the risk, then control it

Governance starts by labelling each use case by how much damage a bad output could do — then attaching controls proportional to that risk.

TierExamplesControls required
LowInternal Q&A, document searchStandard access controls, logging
MediumEarnings summarisation, first-pass compliance screeningAudit trails, human review sample
HighCredit decisioning, trade recommendation, regulatory filingFull SR 11-7 validation, EU AI Act registration
Four-component governance framework
1. Risk classification (Low/Medium/High by autonomy and impact) · 2. Audit trails (prompts, retrieved context, outputs, actions — immutable) · 3. Monitoring (drift and accuracy-degradation alerts) · 4. Incident response (documented rapid mitigation).

Appendix · scaling the hardware

Three ways to split a model across machines

Models too big for one chip are sliced across many. What you partition — the batch, the weights, or the layers — defines the parallelism strategy.

TypeWhat is partitionedKey mechanism
Data parallelismBatch (each device holds full model)Ring-AllReduce gradient aggregation
Tensor / model parallelismWeight matrices column-wiseAll-gather / all-reduce intra-layer
Pipeline parallelismLayer groups to successive devicesMicro-batches; bubble fraction
Tensor parallelism splits \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times k} \to [W_1|W_2|\cdots]\). Pipeline bubble fraction \(\approx \tfrac{p-1}{m+p-1}\) for \(p\) stages and \(m\) micro-batches. ZeRO (Zero Redundancy Optimizer): Stage 1 partitions optimiser states; Stage 2 also gradients; Stage 3 also model weights — near-linear memory reduction with device count.
Appendix · doing more with less memory

Lower precision, recomputed activations, fewer wasted moves

Three engineering tricks shrink the memory bill so far bigger models fit on the same chips.

Mixed precision (bfloat16)
Weights in 16-bit with an fp32 master copy for the optimiser; halves memory and HBM bandwidth. Loss scaling prevents gradient underflow.
Gradient checkpointing
Discard intermediate activations and recompute them in the backward pass — trade one extra forward pass for much less memory.
Loss scaling: multiply the loss by \(s\) before the backward pass, divide gradients by \(s\) to prevent underflow. Activation memory falls from \[ O(L) \xrightarrow{\text{checkpoint every } \sqrt{L}} O(\sqrt{L}). \]
Appendix · the attention kernel

FlashAttention: exact attention, far less memory traffic

Attention's bottleneck is shuffling a huge matrix to and from memory. FlashAttention keeps the work in fast on-chip memory — same answer, far fewer round-trips.

Combined effect
bfloat16 + checkpointing + FlashAttention enable training models 4–8× larger on the same hardware versus naive full-precision training.
FlashAttention tiles \(QK^\top\) into SRAM-resident blocks, avoiding writing the full \(N\times N\) attention matrix to HBM. It reduces HBM reads/writes from \(O(N^2)\) to subquadratic in practice — exact attention, no approximation.
Appendix · other PEFT methods

Prefix tuning and adapters — and why LoRA usually wins

LoRA is not the only cheap-adaptation method. Two alternatives steer the model without retraining it — but each carries a catch LoRA avoids.

Prefix tuning
Learnable prefix vectors prepended to \(K\) and \(V\) at every layer; only the prefixes train. Effective for generation; steers output without touching weights.
Adapters
A small bottleneck module inserted after each sub-layer. Adds inference latency unless fused.
why LoRA wins in practice Zero inference overhead (merge \(W_0 + BA\) before serving); multiple adapters swappable per task; QLoRA extends it to 4-bit quantised base models.
Prefix tuning prepends \(P_K, P_V\) of length \(l\): \[ K' = \begin{bmatrix} P_K \\ K \end{bmatrix}, \qquad V' = \begin{bmatrix} P_V \\ V \end{bmatrix}, \] and only \(P_K, P_V\) are trained. An adapter is a bottleneck residual update \[ h \leftarrow h + W_{\text{up}}\,\text{ReLU}(W_{\text{down}}\,h), \quad W_{\text{down}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times m},\; W_{\text{up}} \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times d},\; m \ll d. \]
