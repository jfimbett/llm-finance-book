Recap: the four formulas you need today

Calibration error, the temporal split, the Sharpe ratio, and the cost drag — surface ideas first, derivations one click away.
Recap · does its confidence mean anything?

Calibration error: when "90% sure" should be 90% right

Sort predictions into confidence buckets. In each bucket, compare the model's stated confidence to how often it was actually right. The gap is its dishonesty.

  • ECE — the average gap, weighted by how many predictions fall in each bucket
  • MCE — the worst single bucket's gap
the bigger picture A model can be a great ranker and still a terrible thermometer. Calibration measures the thermometer.
Bin the predictions into \(M\) confidence bins \(B_m\). With \(\text{acc}(B_m)\) the empirical accuracy and \(\overline{p}(B_m)\) the mean predicted probability in bin \(m\), \[ \text{ECE} = \sum_{m=1}^{M}\frac{|B_m|}{n}\bigl|\text{acc}(B_m)-\overline{p}(B_m)\bigr|, \qquad \text{MCE} = \max_m \bigl|\text{acc}(B_m)-\overline{p}(B_m)\bigr|. \] ECE is the size-weighted average gap; MCE is the worst-case gap.
Recap · did it cheat, and does the trade survive?

The temporal split, the Sharpe ratio, and the cost drag

Test only on the future the model never saw, then judge the strategy by reward-per-unit-risk after paying to trade.

  • Temporal split with a gap — leave a buffer \(\Delta\) so no label straddles the train/test boundary
  • Sharpe ratio — excess return divided by volatility
  • Net return — gross return minus what trading actually costs
Temporal split with gap \(\Delta \geq h\) (the prediction horizon): \[ \text{train}: t \leq T_1-\Delta,\quad \text{valid}: T_1\leq t \leq T_2-\Delta,\quad \text{test}: t \geq T_2. \] Sharpe and net return: \[ \text{SR} = \frac{\overline{R}_p-R_f}{\sigma_p},\qquad R^{\text{net}}_p = R^{\text{gross}}_p - 2\tau(\text{half-spread}+\text{impact}). \]
recall Temperature scaling — dividing the logits by \(T>1\) before softmax — cures overconfidence and preserves ranking, so AUROC is unchanged.

Per-bin gaps: \(0.03,\ 0.05,\ 0.15,\ 0.24\). Weights \(|B_m|/n\): \(0.10,\ 0.20,\ 0.30,\ 0.40\). \[ \text{ECE} = 0.10(0.03)+0.20(0.05)+0.30(0.15)+0.40(0.24) \] \[ = 0.003 + 0.010 + 0.045 + 0.096 = \mathbf{0.154}. \] \[ \text{MCE} = \max\{0.03,0.05,0.15,0.24\} = \mathbf{0.24}. \]
interpretation Both ECE and MCE peak in the top bin. A desk acting only on \(>0.75\) signals operates in the model's worst region: stated 90% confidence is really 66% accuracy. Recalibrate — or abstain — before trusting high-confidence calls.

Problem 2: design a contamination audit

If the model was trained on the future you are testing it on, the "edge" is just memory. How to design the test so it can't cheat.
Problem 2 · the setup

An LLM that may have already read the answers

A team evaluates an LLM (training cutoff Dec 2023) on predicting 1-month stock direction from earnings calls. Prediction horizon \(h = 21\) trading days.

Tasks
  1. Give concrete cutoffs \(T_1, T_2\) and gap \(\Delta\) for a temporal split that is safe against this model. Justify \(\Delta\).
  2. The team measures accuracy on original (62%) vs. anonymised (54%) transcripts. Quantify the leakage and state what it implies about the true edge.
  3. Name two further controls and what each detects.
why this matters An LLM can "predict" a 2022 stock move because it memorised the news, not because it reasoned. Contamination audits separate skill from recall.
Problem 2 · solution

Eight points of the "edge" were just memory

(1) Safe split
All test data must post-date the cutoff plus a publication-lag margin. Take \(T_2 = \) Apr 2024 (Dec 2023 cutoff \(+\geq 3\) months); \(T_1\) in early 2023 for validation. Set \(\Delta = 1\) month so no label window straddles the boundary.
(2) Leakage
\(62\% - 54\% = \mathbf{8}\) pp of accuracy was entity-level memorisation. The residual 54% still beats 50%, but the apparent edge shrank by more than half — likely economically negligible after costs.
The gap must cover both the prediction horizon and the publication lag of the label: \[ \Delta \geq h + \text{lag} \approx 21\text{d} + \text{a few weeks} \;\Rightarrow\; \Delta = 1\ \text{month}. \] This guarantees no label window can straddle the train/test boundary, blocking look-ahead leakage.
(3) two further controls N-gram dedup (13-grams, MinHash LSH) detects direct memorisation of document text. Date-blinding ablation — a large drop when temporal markers are removed reveals pattern-retrieval, not reasoning.

Problem 3: gross vs. net Sharpe

A strategy that looks attractive on paper — until you charge it for the cost of trading every single day.
Problem 3 · the setup

A 0.80-Sharpe strategy that rebalances daily

A daily news-sentiment LLM strategy reports, before costs, a 12% annual excess return at 15% annual volatility — and it turns over half its book every day.

\[ \overline{R}^{\text{gross}}_p - R_f = 12\%\ \text{annual},\qquad \sigma_p = 15\%\ \text{annual}. \] Rebalances daily with turnover \(\tau = 0.5\)/day; half-spread \(= 2\) bps, market impact \(= 8\) bps.
Tasks
  1. Compute the gross annual Sharpe ratio.
  2. Daily round-trip cost \(= 2\tau(\text{half-spread}+\text{impact})\). Annualise over 252 trading days.
  3. Compute the net Sharpe (subtract annual cost from excess return; \(\sigma_p\) unchanged). Is the strategy still worth trading?
Problem 3 · solution

+0.80 gross becomes −0.88 net

0.80
gross Sharpe
25.2%
annual cost drag
−0.88
net Sharpe
(1) Gross Sharpe: \(\text{SR}_{\text{gross}} = 12/15 = \mathbf{0.80}\).
(2) Cost: daily round-trip \(= 2(0.5)(2+8)\ \text{bps} = 10\) bps/day. Over 252 days: \(10\ \text{bps}\times 252 = 2520\ \text{bps} = \mathbf{25.2\%}\)/year.
(3) Net: \[ \overline{R}^{\text{net}}_p - R_f = 12\% - 25.2\% = -13.2\%,\qquad \text{SR}_{\text{net}} = \frac{-13.2}{15} = \mathbf{-0.88}. \]
verdict A respectable-looking gross Sharpe of 0.80 turns deeply negative once daily-rebalancing costs are charged. The "edge" was an artefact of ignoring transaction costs. Lower turnover or longer holding periods are mandatory before this signal is tradeable.

Case study · the goal

Recalibrate without re-training — and prove it

Reproduce the credit-classifier reliability diagram, compute its ECE, then apply temperature scaling and watch ECE fall while AUROC stays unchanged.

Reference code

What you will observe
  1. High-confidence bins sit below the diagonal — overconfidence
  2. Fitting \(T>1\) spreads the softmax: bins move toward the diagonal, ECE drops
  3. The ROC curve (and AUROC) is unchanged — scaling is rank-preserving. Calibration \(\neq\) discrimination
Case study · the code (1/2)

Computing ECE from bin statistics

A size-weighted average of the confidence-minus-accuracy gaps — five bins, a few lines of NumPy.

import numpy as np

# Fixed bin stats from the credit example (ex:ch13-ece-credit).
conf  = np.array([0.08, 0.31, 0.50, 0.71, 0.88])  # mean predicted prob
acc   = np.array([0.11, 0.28, 0.47, 0.55, 0.62])  # empirical accuracy
count = np.array([120, 180, 200, 300, 200])

def ece(conf, acc, count):
    w = count / count.sum()
    return float(np.sum(w * np.abs(conf - acc)))

print("ECE  =", round(ece(conf, acc, count), 3))   # -> 0.115
print("MCE  =", round(float(np.max(np.abs(conf - acc))), 3))
Case study · the code (2/2)

Temperature scaling in five lines

Divide the logits by \(T>1\) before softmax. It spreads the probabilities toward the middle, taming overconfidence — and because it's monotone, it never reorders the predictions.

# Temperature scaling on raw logits z: p = softmax(z / T).
# T > 1 spreads the distribution and reduces overconfidence;
# because it is monotone in z, the ranking (AUROC) is preserved.
def temperature_scale(logits, T):
    z = logits / T
    z = z - z.max(axis=1, keepdims=True)        # numerical stability
    e = np.exp(z)
    return e / e.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True)
why it can't hurt ranking Dividing every logit by the same \(T\) is monotone, so the order of scores — and therefore the ROC curve — is untouched. Only the spacing changes.

Discussion · the committee's call

Three red flags, one sign-off decision

An LLM credit model shows ECE \(=0.115\), 8 pp of its accuracy attributable to entity memorisation, and a news-overlay sleeve with gross Sharpe 0.80 / net Sharpe \(-0.88\). Deploy, fix, or reject?

Debate, framed around
  • Calibration — deploy with recalibration + conformal sets, or not?
  • Contamination — is the residual 54% edge real or an artefact?

takeaway LLM outputs are inputs to a decision process, not end products. The committee's job is the surrounding evaluation discipline.

Appendix — stretch problems

Two optional extensions: the breadth–depth law of active management, and walk-forward backtesting with a purge period.
Stretch · breadth vs. depth

Information ratio: many small bets beat one big one

A long-short LLM signal covers \(N = 100\) independent monthly bets with information coefficient \(\text{IC} = 0.04\). How good is that — and how would you make it better?

Tasks
  1. Estimate the Information Ratio via \(\text{IR} \approx \text{IC}\sqrt{N}\).
  2. Is this "strong" (IR \(>0.5\)) or "exceptional" (IR \(>1.0\))?
  3. To reach IR \(=0.8\) at the same IC, how many bets \(N\)? What practical cost does increasing \(N\) introduce?
\[ \text{IR} \approx 0.04\sqrt{100} = 0.40 \quad(\text{below "strong"}). \] For IR \(=0.8\): \(\sqrt{N} = 0.8/0.04 = 20 \Rightarrow N = 400\) bets/year. More bets means higher turnover \(\Rightarrow\) higher transaction costs, which can erase the gain (see Problem 3).
Stretch · honest backtesting

Walk-forward with a purge period

You backtest a monthly-rebalanced LLM signal (\(h = 1\) month) over 2019–2024. The trick is leaving a purge gap so training never peeks across the evaluation boundary.

Tasks
  1. Sketch the train/eval windows for the first three walk-forward iterations with purge \(\Delta_{\text{purge}} = 1\) month.
  2. Why must \(\Delta_{\text{purge}} \geq h\)? What leaks if it is zero?
  3. For full LLM fine-tuning (expensive to re-estimate), what is the cost of widening \(h\) to amortise re-training?
Iteration \(k\): train \(t \leq kh - \Delta_{\text{purge}}\), eval \(kh \leq t \leq (k{+}1)h-1\). With \(\Delta_{\text{purge}} < h\), a label window overlapping the boundary appears in both sets \(\Rightarrow\) look-ahead leakage. Widening \(h\) amortises re-fitting cost but evaluates an increasingly stale model late in each window.
