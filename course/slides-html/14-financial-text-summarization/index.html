01

The information-extraction problem in finance

Why the cost of reading — not the absence of facts — is the binding constraint.
The scale problem

Markets don't lack information — they lack time to read it

Tens of thousands of filings, transcripts, and analyst notes are released daily. The bottleneck is the cost of reading, interpreting, and routing — not the lack of facts.

Who hits the wall
  • A PM cannot read every 10-K from the 6,000+ firms in the Russell 3000
  • A compliance officer cannot scan every contract in a merger data room
Three coupled IE sub-tasks
  1. Entity recognition — who/what the text refers to
  2. Fact / relation extraction — how entities relate, what numbers they carry
  3. Summarization — condense long documents, faithfully
the dependency chain NER quality caps relation-extraction accuracy; faithful summarization requires accurate entity and fact extraction underneath. Get the bottom of the stack wrong and everything above it inherits the error.
Know your raw material

Structured, semi-structured, or unstructured — and why it dictates the tool

Before you pick a model, ask how much schema the document already carries. That answer decides whether you query a database or unleash full language understanding.

TierExamplesStrategy
StructuredXBRL filings, tick data, options chainsDatabase query
Semi-structured10-K / 10-Q, transcripts, proxiesTables + NLP
UnstructuredNews, social media, analyst notes, contractsFull NLU
A document is structured if every element can be queried programmatically via a published schema (no natural language needed); semi-structured if it mixes schema-conformant sections with free-form narrative; unstructured if its content lives in natural language with no imposed schema.
practitioner rule Start with structured sources (low noise, low cost). But unstructured text holds the forward-looking signal — guidance, qualitative risk, interpretation — absent from the tables. The best pipelines combine both.
What you're hunting for

The four entity types financial text is built from

  • Organisations — most frequent, most ambiguous. "Apple" (firm vs. fruit), "Morgan" (J.P. Morgan vs. Morgan Stanley). Resolution = entity linking to LEI / CUSIP / EDGAR CIK.
  • Temporal expressions — "next quarter", "FY2024", "within 30 days of the trigger". Normalise fiscal + calendar terms to dates.
  • Monetary figures & metrics — $ amounts, %, EPS, EBITDA margin, D/E. Resolve units (M vs. B), parenthesised negatives, and which entity/period each figure describes.
  • Legal / contractual — covenants in loan agreements and bond indentures that can trigger default; classify and extract threshold values.
Earnings-call excerpt → extracted records
"In Q3 2023, Microsoft reported revenue of $56.5 billion, up 13% YoY. CEO Satya Nadella noted Azure grew 29%…"
ORG Microsoft (CIK 789019) · PERSON Nadella/CEO · DATE Q3'23 → 2023-07-01..09-30 · MONEY $56.5B (total rev) · METRIC +13% rev, +29% Azure
Two jobs, not one

Extraction fills a form; summarization writes a paragraph

They look similar but are fundamentally different tasks — with different outputs, different models, and different ways to fail.

Information Extraction (IE)
Schema-driven. Structure specified information into a predefined schema. Output = typed records (entities, attributes, relations). Models are discriminative: classify whether a span fills a slot.

"What happened to whom, when, by how much?" → populates databases.

Summarization
Content-driven. Produce a shorter coherent text capturing the salient content. Output = a natural-language string. Models are generative; "salient" is learned from reference summaries.

"What should I know without reading it all?" → serves readers.

why the distinction matters Different evaluation criteria and failure modes. A quant trading earnings surprises needs IE; an investment committee reviewing a note needs summarization; a covenant monitor needs IE then a human-readable summary.
02

Named-entity recognition in finance

Labelling the firms, figures, and clauses — the foundational layer everything else rests on.
The foundational task

Why four entity classes aren't enough for finance

NER labels each word with its entity type. The general-purpose schema everyone starts from was never designed for the language of filings.

the bigger picture The standard CoNLL-2003 schema — Person, Location, Organisation, Misc — is inadequate for finance. Documents add specialised types: financial instruments, economic indicators, legal clauses, corporate roles.
Three finance-specific annotation challenges
  1. Context-dependent type — "Apple" = org (portfolio) vs. product (supply chain)
  2. Nested entities — "Goldman Sachs Investment Banking Division" = org + unit
  3. Implicit entities — "the issuer" refers to a firm named earlier
The data that trains the models

  • EDGAR-Corpus — 10-K/10-Q from 1993–2020. Models pretrained on it systematically beat general-domain models on financial NER.
How a model learns to tag

Teaching a transformer to label every word

You bolt a small classification layer onto a pretrained language model and ask it, for each word, "which entity type is this?" — then train it on annotated examples.

Token classification on a pretrained encoder. For tokens \(\mathbf{x}=(x_1,\dots,x_T)\) with contextual states \(\mathbf{h}_i \in \R^d\): \[ P(y_i \mid \mathbf{x}) = \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i + \mathbf{b}), \quad \mathbf{W}\in\R^{C\times d}. \] Labels follow BIO: \(\{B\text{-}e, I\text{-}e, O\}\). Train end-to-end on token cross-entropy \(\mathcal{L}=-\sum_t \log p(\hat y_t = y_t)\).
Encoder choice matters
FinBERT > vanilla BERT; SEC-BERT (pretrained only on EDGAR) is state-of-the-art on SEC-domain NER — it has seen "Regulation S-K Item 1A" thousands of times.
  • Segmentation — docs exceed 512 tokens → sliding windows (≥64-token overlap) or Longformer
  • Label imbalance — O is 85–95% of tokens → focal / weighted loss
  • Post-processing — a CRF + Viterbi layer enforces valid BIO transitions
A concrete recipe

Fine-tuning SEC-BERT on FINER-139, end to end

A production-grade recipe that runs in about 20 minutes on a single GPU — and beats the baseline exactly where it's hardest.

The steps
  1. Download FINER-139 splits (~900 annotated training sentences)
  2. Initialise from SEC-BERT checkpoint (MLM-pretrained on millions of SEC sentences)
  3. Linear head of dimension \(C = 2\times 139 + 1 = 279\) (BIO for 139 types + O)
  4. AdamW (\(\beta_1{=}0.9,\beta_2{=}0.999\), wd 0.01), linear warmup 10%, peak LR \(2\times10^{-5}\), 10 epochs
where domain pretraining pays off ~20 min on one A100; macro-F\(_1\) substantially exceeds the FinBERT baseline on rare types (<50 training examples) — exactly the long tail where general models fall apart.
From entities to facts

Relation extraction: how the entities connect

An entity list is just a vocabulary. The meaning is in the links between them — who acquired whom, who runs what, what collateralises which loan.

the bigger picture Microsoft acquired Activision; Buffett is CEO of Berkshire; a loan is collateralised by specific assets. These relations feed knowledge graphs, earnings attribution, and corporate-hierarchy databases.
Joint entity-and-relation models (e.g. DyGIE++) score ordered span pairs: \[ s(e_i, r, e_j) = f\bigl(\mathbf{h}_{e_i}, \mathbf{h}_{e_j}, \mathbf{r}\bigr), \] with span reps \(\mathbf{h}_{e}\) (mean/endpoint of encoder states), a learned relation embedding \(\mathbf{r}\in\R^{d_r}\), and a bilinear/FFN scorer \(f\); predict \(r \in \mathcal{R}\cup\{\text{None}\}\).
the hard case Cross-sentence relations are far harder than within-sentence ones. Document-level models (DocRED-style) that encode the full context win — taxonomy: Subsidiary-Of, Acquired-By, Employed-At, Board-Member-Of, Issued-By, Secured-By, Reported-Value.
03

Financial document summarization

Condensing 100-page filings into a paragraph — without inventing the numbers.
The core tradeoff

Copy sentences, or write new ones? The faithfulness tradeoff

Every summarizer sits on one of two sides: it either reuses the author's exact words, or it writes fresh prose. The choice trades safety against fluency.

Extractive
Select a subset of sentences/spans verbatim from the source. Faithfulness is guaranteed by construction — every sentence was written by the original author.
Abstractive
Generate new text that paraphrases and synthesises. More concise and fluent — but susceptible to hallucination: plausible-sounding but factually wrong statements.
why hallucination is consequential in finance Summarising "revenue of $57B" when the truth was $56.5B is small in % but can be material misinformation in a decision. SOTA abstractive models hallucinate factual errors in 25–30% of CNN/DailyMail summaries; untuned financial models fare no better.

Recommendation: prefer extractive when faithfulness is paramount (esp. numbers); use abstractive when fluency dominates. A hybrid (extract, then rephrase) is a useful middle ground.

The flagship document

How you summarize a 100,000-word 10-K

A typical 10-K runs 50,000–150,000 words: business, risk factors, legal proceedings, MD&A, financial statements, notes. You cannot feed it to a model whole.

why the MD&A is the prize The MD&A is the densest section for an equity investor — management's interpretation and forward guidance. Its tone, readability, and length carry measurable signal about future earnings and returns (Loughran–McDonald). Tone-preservation must be an explicit constraint in any faithful prompt.
Three-stage pipeline
  1. Section extraction & routing — parse the TOC, route each section to a sub-model (table extractor vs. text summariser)
  2. Chunk-level summarization — 512-tok chunks (64 overlap) for BERT; whole MD&A in one pass for 8K–128K decoders
  3. Hierarchical aggregation — second-pass or retrieval-based merge of chunk summaries into a document summary
A prompt-engineering move

One sentence in the prompt that cuts number errors

The simplest defence against a model inventing numbers is to tell it, explicitly, not to. "Quote all numbers exactly as stated" is a real, measurable technique.

You are a senior equity analyst. Summarise the following earnings
release in no more than 150 words, covering: (1) revenue and EPS
vs. consensus, (2) the single most important forward guidance
statement, and (3) one key risk mentioned by management.
Quote all numbers exactly as stated.

[EARNINGS RELEASE TEXT]
illustrative finding (varies by corpus/model) The constrained prompt cut number errors by ~40% vs. an unconstrained paraphrase prompt; ~8% of cases still showed paraphrase errors on nuanced guidance — which is exactly why we add factual-consistency checks later.
When documents won't fit

Analyst notes, transcripts, and the length problem

  • Analyst-note condensation — reports run 10–40 pp with a predictable macro-structure (thesis in para 1, target price standardised). Template-aware parsing exploits this.
  • Change / delta summarization — a PM wants what changed, not what they already know — requires report history + reasoning over diffs.
  • Earnings transcripts — 15,000–30,000 words; uneven density (prepared remarks dense; Q&A mixes signal + boilerplate). ECTSum: 2,425 transcript–summary pairs, 3–6 abstractive bullets each.
  • S-1 filings — a tech S-1 can exceed 300,000 words.
the common thread No single context window fits these documents, so the architecture — not just the prompt — must handle length.
Three ways to beat the context window

Strategies for documents that don't fit in one pass

When a document is longer than any model can read at once, you have three engineering options — each with a different cost-and-coverage profile.

  • Two-stage hierarchical: each section → 200–400 words, then a second-pass model over the concatenated section summaries.
  • Retrieval-augmented summarization: dense-retrieve the top-\(k\) passages for a query, summarise only those (summarization as RAG).
  • Long-context models: Longformer; 128K–1M-token models (e.g. Gemini 1.5 Pro) process an entire S-1 in one pass — cost-bound.
In plain words
Either chop the document and summarise the summaries, search for the relevant passages and ignore the rest, or buy a model big enough to swallow it whole.
04

Table and numerical data extraction

The numbers live in grids — and getting them right is a different problem from reading prose.
The free, accurate shortcut

When the filing already tagged its own numbers: XBRL

For U.S. filings, the most reliable way to extract a financial figure involves no language model at all — the filer already machine-tagged it.

the bigger picture A table is a 2-D grid where each cell's meaning depends on its row header (what measure) and column header (what period/entity). Reliable table extraction underpins database population and consistency checking.
Inline XBRL — mandated for U.S. domestic filers since 2020
Every figure is tagged with a U.S.-GAAP concept (e.g. us-gaap:Revenues), a context (entity + period), and units — machine-readable and browser-readable. The EDGAR XBRL API returns a figure with full provenance for a (CIK, concept, year) query. Accuracy is guaranteed by the filer and its auditors — no NLP.
coverage gap Non-GAAP measures (adjusted EBITDA, organic growth, book-to-bill) and many footnotes are not XBRL-tagged. Combine XBRL for core figures with LLM/NLP extraction for the surrounding narrative.
When there's no XBRL

Reading tables the hard way — and why generation beats retrieval

International filings, private docs, and pre-XBRL legacy have no tags. The tables must be parsed from raw HTML/PDF — merged cells, multi-row headers, embedded footnotes, non-standard layouts.

FinQA benchmarks multi-step numerical reasoning over tables:
  • Baseline cell-retrieval models: ~50% exact-match.
  • Fine-tuned models that generate a Python-like calc program: >70%.
why generation wins Financial table questions chain several operations (subtract prior year, divide, express as %). A model that emits an explicit calculation program externalises that reasoning and routes the arithmetic to a deterministic interpreter.
The table-parsing pipeline

Four stages from pixels to GAAP concepts

When you must parse a table yourself, it breaks into four sequential jobs — and the last principle is the one that saves you from wrong numbers.

Four-stage LLM pipeline
  1. Layout parsing (LayoutLM / PDF parser) → cells with row/col coordinates
  2. Header disambiguation → LLM labels each cell with (row-hdr, col-hdr)
  3. Value normalisation → units, parenthesised negatives, % vs. absolute
  4. Schema mapping → map to GAAP taxonomy concepts
arithmetic reliability — the ReAct principle LLMs are unreliable calculators. Route all arithmetic to a code interpreter: the LLM decides what to compute; Python actually computes it. This separation dramatically cuts numerical errors.
A quality gate that catches lies

Do the numbers in the prose match the numbers in the tables?

The same revenue figure shows up in the tables, the press release, and the MD&A. Consistency checking verifies they agree — catching both extraction errors and genuine, possibly material, disclosure inconsistencies.

Three-step procedure
  1. Multi-source extraction — pull every occurrence of each metric (revenue, op. income, EPS) from tables, press release, MD&A, footnotes
  2. Normalisation — common unit & sign; "$56.5B" → $56,500M; all sources should agree
  3. Cross-reference — flag any pair disagreeing beyond tolerance (e.g. 0.1% for rounding); report with source attribution
it works both ways "Net income grew 25% YoY" can be verified against extracted current/prior figures — a powerful quality gate for both document pipelines and LLM-generated summaries, where hallucinated numbers are a known failure mode.
05

Evaluation and benchmarks

How you actually know a summary is good — and why overlap scores aren't the answer.
The default metric

ROUGE: how much of the reference did you reproduce?

The most widely used summarization metric asks a simple question: of the words in the human-written reference summary, how many did the machine's version also use? A recall-oriented overlap score.

ROUGE-\(N\) = \(n\)-gram recall of candidate \(c\) against references \(\mathcal{R}\) (\(\mathcal{G}_N(s)\) = \(n\)-grams of \(s\)): \[ \text{ROUGE-}N=\frac{\sum_{s\in\mathcal{R}}\sum_{g\in\mathcal{G}_N(s)} \mathrm{Count}_{\text{match}}(g,c)} {\sum_{s\in\mathcal{R}}\sum_{g\in\mathcal{G}_N(s)}\mathrm{Count}(g)}. \] ROUGE-L uses the longest common subsequence instead of exact \(n\)-grams.
limitation: lexical, not semantic "net income declined 12%" → "profit fell 12%" deserves full credit but scores zero ROUGE-1 on the substituted words. Conversely, copying irrelevant source sentences can score high ROUGE without being informative. Treat ROUGE as a coarse recall proxy, not a quality verdict.
A semantic upgrade

BERTScore: matching meaning, not just words

BERTScore fixes ROUGE's blind spot: a candidate word counts as a match when its meaning aligns with a reference word — even if the surface spelling is completely different.

Cosine similarity of contextual embeddings for candidate token \(\hat r_j\) and reference token \(r_i\): \[ \mathrm{sim}(r_i,\hat r_j)= \frac{\mathbf{h}_{r_i}^\top \mathbf{h}_{\hat r_j}} {\|\mathbf{h}_{r_i}\|\,\|\mathbf{h}_{\hat r_j}\|}. \] Precision/recall/F\(_1\) via greedy token matching in both directions. With a financial encoder (FinBERT), it captures domain-specific similarity better.
but neither one measures truth A summary inventing "revenue grew 15%" (true: 13%) can still score high if the surrounding language matches the reference. And outputs can be inconsistent across runs — reproducibility itself is a QA concern. Similarity metrics rank fluency and coverage, not facts.
Measuring truth directly

Factual consistency and the human gold standard

If overlap scores can't tell you whether a summary is true, you need metrics that check entailment against the source — and, ultimately, expert humans.

Factual-consistency metrics
FactCC trains an NLI classifier (entailment / contradiction / neutral) to predict whether each candidate claim is entailed by the source. For finance, strengthen it by grounding numerical claims against extracted XBRL: any claim that cannot be verified against structured data is flagged.
Human evaluation — by domain professionals
A crowdworker cannot judge GAAP vs. non-GAAP revenue. Assess four dimensions:
  • Factual accuracy (most critical; mark each claim verified / unverifiable / incorrect)
  • Completeness — all material disclosures covered?
  • Conciseness — no redundancy?
  • Fluency / readability
Report agreement (Cohen's \(\kappa\) / Krippendorff's \(\alpha\)): typically low on completeness (analysts value different facts), high on factual accuracy (errors are objective) \(\Rightarrow\) accuracy is the tractable automatic target.

Wrap-up

Five takeaways — and where Practical 14 goes

  1. Financial text is heterogeneous — structured (query) to unstructured (full NLU). Production pipelines combine both, anchored on structured sources.
  2. Financial NER needs domain data & models — general NER is inadequate for the 139-class taxonomy. Pretrain on EDGAR, fine-tune on FINER-139.
  3. Abstractive summarization carries hallucination risk — hallucinated numbers can be misinformation. Pair with factual-consistency checking against structured data.
  4. Tables & numerical consistency are underappreciated — XBRL is free and accurate; LLMs supplement for non-GAAP & legacy; route arithmetic to code.
  5. Match the metric to the stakes — ROUGE/BERTScore measure similarity, not facts. Add factual-consistency checks and expert human evaluation.

Appendix · annotation

Why financial annotation needs its own guidelines

General-domain NER guidelines simply do not anticipate the ambiguities that fill financial documents.

  • Surface-string ambiguity — "Apple" as organisation (portfolio) vs. product (supply-chain discussion): the same string, different type.
  • Nested entities — "Goldman Sachs Investment Banking Division" = org (Goldman Sachs) + business unit, simultaneously.
  • Implicit entities — "the issuer" in a bond prospectus refers to a previously named company without restating it.
the implication These motivate domain-specific annotation guidelines and adjudication processes — not just relabelled CoNLL guidelines.
Appendix · relation extraction

Why cross-sentence relations are the hard case

  • Within-sentence RE — both entities co-occur → local context suffices.
  • Cross-sentence RE — entities in different sentences → requires document-level encoding and coreference resolution.
  • Benchmarks — DocRED and its financial adaptations; document-level models that encode the whole context (not sentence-by-sentence) achieve the gains.
why it matters in finance Ownership chains and "the issuer"-style implicit references routinely span paragraphs in prospectuses and 10-Ks.
Appendix · long documents

Retrieval-augmented summarization for 300k-word filings

For documents that exceed any context window — an S-1 over 300,000 words — turn summarization into retrieval-augmented generation.

The recipe
  1. Index the document's passages with a dense retriever
  2. For a query ("What are the principal risk factors?"), retrieve the top-\(k\) passages
  3. Summarise only the retrieved passages
tradeoff vs. hierarchical summarization RAG is query-targeted and cheap but may miss globally salient content not matching the query; hierarchical two-stage covers everything but costs a full pass over the document.
