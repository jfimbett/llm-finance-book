Recap · formula 1 of 2

Domain-adaptive pre-training: keep teaching the model, on your text

Take a general model that already speaks English, then keep training it on a pile of financial documents so it picks up the vocabulary and register of the domain. That is DAPT.

Starting from general weights \(\theta_0\), continue pre-training on a domain corpus \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{domain}}\): \[ \theta^\star = \arg\min_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{PT}}(\theta;\mathcal{D}_{\text{domain}}), \qquad \mathcal{L}_{\text{PT}} \in \{\text{MLM},\ \text{CLM}\}, \] run at a reduced learning rate (\(\sim 10\times\) smaller) to limit catastrophic forgetting of general language.
rule of thumb Encoders need 1–5B domain tokens; the first \(\sim\)1B buys most of the gain — classic diminishing returns.
Recap · formula 2 of 2

Effective cost: what one correct answer really costs you

A cheap model that is often wrong is not actually cheap. Send the hard cases to a human reviewer, and the right metric is cost per correct output — not cost per query.

\[ C_{\text{eff}}(m,\rho) = \frac{c_m + \rho\,c_h}{p_m + \rho(1-p_m)}, \] where \(c_m\) = model cost/query, \(c_h\) = human-review cost, \(p_m\) = model correctness, and \(\rho\) = fraction routed to review. Constraint: \(p_m + \rho(1-p_m) \ge p^\star\).
How to read it
The top is what you pay per query — the model always, the human only on the routed fraction \(\rho\). The bottom is the accuracy you achieve. Routing more to humans raises both; the optimisation finds the smallest \(\rho\) that still clears your target \(p^\star\).

Guided problems

Two pencil-and-paper exercises: budget a training run, then price a human-in-the-loop pipeline.
Problem 1 · the brief

How much financial text should you train on?

You will domain-adapt a BERT-base encoder (110M parameters) for SEC-filing named-entity recognition, then fine-tune. Three data sources are on the table:

Data on hand
  • SEC EDGAR — \(\sim\)50B tokens, open
  • Reuters TRC2 — \(\sim\)2B, licensed
  • General replay (the Pile) — effectively unlimited
the real question More data feels safer, but training is a budget. Where is the point past which extra EDGAR tokens stop earning their compute?
Problem 1 · your tasks

Four decisions to defend

  1. A — Budget. Given diminishing returns, what domain-token budget do you target, and why not just use all 50B?
  2. B — Forgetting. You worry about catastrophic forgetting. Propose a replay ratio (general:domain) and justify it.
  3. C — Learning rate. Should the DAPT learning rate sit above or below the original pre-training rate? By roughly how much, and what failure does this prevent?
  4. D — TAPT. Would task-adaptive pre-training help here? What additional data would it require?

Work it through before flipping to the solution — the reasoning matters more than the numbers.

Problem 2 · the brief

The cheapest way to hit 95% accuracy

A FinBERT classifier is cheap but imperfect. A human reviewer is expensive but reliable. Mix them — and find the mix that meets your accuracy target for the least money.

\(\$\)0.0002
model cost / query \(c_m\)
0.88
model accuracy \(p_m\)
\(\$\)2.00
human review / query \(c_h\)
0.95
target accuracy \(p^\star\)

Assume human review fixes every error it sees.

Problem 2 · your tasks

Solve for the routing fraction \(\rho\)

  1. A. Write achieved accuracy as a function of \(\rho\) and find the minimum \(\rho\) that meets \(p^\star\).
  2. B. Evaluate \(C_{\text{eff}}\) at that \(\rho\).
  3. C. Compare against routing everything to the human (\(\rho=1\)). What is the cost ratio?
  4. D. Qualitatively: if DAPT lifted \(p_m\) to 0.95, what happens to the required \(\rho\) and to \(C_{\text{eff}}\)?
hint Achieved accuracy \(= p_m + \rho(1-p_m)\), because review recovers the \((1-p_m)\) errors on the routed fraction.

Setup: two pipelines, four sentences

from transformers import pipeline

# Domain-specific encoder (Araci FinBERT) vs. general sentiment
finbert = pipeline("text-classification", model="ProsusAI/finbert")
general = pipeline("sentiment-analysis")  # default SST-2 model

SENTENCES = [
    "The firm took on additional liability to fund the acquisition.",
    "We are cautiously optimistic but flag meaningful headwinds.",
    "Leverage increased to 3.2x following the debt issuance.",
    "Unprecedented demand drove revenue above guidance.",
]

Two pipelines; four sentences chosen so general-English polarity is misleading. The comparison loop is next.

The comparison loop

for s in SENTENCES:
    f = finbert(s)[0]
    g = general(s)[0]
    print(f"{s[:48]:48s} | FinBERT={f['label']:8s} "
          f"| General={g['label']}")

Run it, then fill in the table — where do the two models disagree, and who is right for a financial reader?

Solutions

Worked answers — read after you have committed to your own.
Solution · Problem 1

Sizing the DAPT corpus

A — Budget
Target \(\sim\)3–5B EDGAR tokens. Most of the gain is in the first \(\sim\)1B; beyond \(\sim\)5B the marginal downstream improvement for a 110M encoder is small — not worth the compute or the added forgetting risk.
B — Replay
Mix in \(\sim\)5–10% general (Pile) tokens: enough to preserve broad language ability, small enough that the domain still dominates the update.
C — Learning rate
Below the original PT rate, by \(\sim 10\times\). A large LR would overwrite the general representations DAPT is meant to reuse — that is catastrophic forgetting.
D — TAPT
Yes: a further continued-PT pass on the unlabelled text of the NER dataset (same distribution as the labelled set), before supervised fine-tuning. Needs task-distribution unlabelled data — which here we have.
Solution · Problem 2

The optimal routing fraction

The hybrid pipeline hits 95% accuracy at roughly 1.6× lower cost than sending everything to a human — and improving the model pays off mostly through the review bill it eliminates.

A — Required \(\rho\). Achieved accuracy \(= p_m + \rho(1-p_m) = 0.88 + 0.12\rho\). Set \(\ge 0.95\): \[ 0.88 + 0.12\rho \ge 0.95 \;\Rightarrow\; \rho \ge \tfrac{0.07}{0.12} \approx \mathbf{0.583}. \] B — \(C_{\text{eff}}\) at \(\rho=0.583\): \[ C_{\text{eff}} = \frac{0.0002 + 0.583\cdot 2.00}{0.88 + 0.583\cdot 0.12} = \frac{1.1662}{0.95} \approx \mathbf{\$1.23}\ \text{per correct output.} \] C — All-human (\(\rho=1\)): \(C_{\text{eff}} = (0.0002+2.00)/1.0 \approx \$2.00\). Hybrid is \(\approx\)1.6× cheaper at the same 95% target. D — If DAPT lifts \(p_m\) to 0.95: required \(\rho = 0\) (target already met), so \(C_{\text{eff}} \approx c_m/p_m = 0.0002/0.95 \approx \$0.0002\) — four orders of magnitude cheaper.
the punchline Investing in the model collapses the human-review bill. The model's correctness, not its sticker price, drives total cost.

Stretch problem 1

The 0.97 F1 that should make you suspicious

A vendor claims their 70B model scores 0.97 F1 on Financial PhraseBank — above any published FinBERT.

  1. Why is this number not immediately trustworthy?
  2. Design a contamination-controlled evaluation: what temporal property must the test set satisfy relative to the model's knowledge cutoff?
  3. PhraseBank predates almost every frontier model. What kind of held-out set would you build instead, and from what source?
key idea A benchmark in the pre-training corpus measures memorisation, not generalisation. Require test data that postdates the model's cutoff.
Stretch problem 2

Grounded-and-small vs. big-and-ungrounded

A 7B decoder + EDGAR retrieval (RAG) vs. a 50B ungrounded decoder, both asked to "extract the FY revenue from this 10-K".

  1. On which axis does RAG win even if the 50B model is slightly more accurate ungrounded? (Hint: SR 11-7.)
  2. Sketch why grounding reduces numerical hallucination specifically.
  3. Give one task where the 50B model would still be preferable, and say why retrieval does not help there.
expected RAG wins on traceability and cost; grounding pins figures to retrieved text; the big model still wins on open-ended reasoning no single passage supports.
