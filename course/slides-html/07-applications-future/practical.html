Recap · choosing a pattern

Three deployment patterns, three different jobs

Before any modelling, decide how the model meets the task. Each pattern wins in a different situation.

Zero-shot
General reasoning, no training data. Fast to stand up; weakest on consistency.
RAG
Updatable documents, an audit trail, citable sources. The default when answers must be grounded.
Fine-tuning
Structured, consistent output. Worth it when format and reliability matter more than flexibility.
the heuristic No data + reasoning → zero-shot. Documents + auditability → RAG. Structured output + consistency → fine-tune.
Recap · combining retrievers

Reciprocal Rank Fusion: blend two rankings without tuning weights

A dense (semantic) retriever and a sparse (keyword/BM25) retriever each rank your passages. RRF merges them using only each passage's rank — no fragile score calibration.

For query \(q\) and passage \(d_i\), with ranks from each retriever: \[ s_{\text{RRF}}(q, d_i) = \frac{1}{k + r_{\text{dense}}(d_i)} + \frac{1}{k + r_{\text{sparse}}(d_i)}, \qquad k = 60. \] A passage that ranks high in either list earns a large reciprocal term; the constant \(k\) damps the outsized influence of the very top ranks.
why it matters Keyword search nails exact figures ("capex 2023"); semantic search catches paraphrases. RRF lets you keep both strengths with one line of arithmetic.
Recap · what "fair" means

Three definitions of fairness — and they can't all hold

"Fair" is not one thing. There are at least three precise, reasonable definitions — and when groups default at different rates, no model can satisfy all three at once.

Demographic parity
Same approval rate for every group.
Equalized odds
Same error rates (TPR, FPR) across groups.
Calibration
A score of \(p\) means a true \(p\) chance — within every group.
Demographic parity: \[ \Pr(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=a) = \Pr(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=b). \] Equalized odds: \[ \Pr(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=y, A=a) = \Pr(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=y, A=b). \] Calibration within group: \[ \Pr(Y=1 \mid \hat{P}=p, A=a) = p. \]
impossibility · Chouldechova (2017) When base rates differ across groups, these three criteria cannot be simultaneously satisfied by any non-trivial classifier. Fairness becomes a policy choice, not a checkbox.

Solutions · Problem 2

The fairness audit, worked

Part A — demographic parity
A \(= (15+300)/500 = 63\%\); B \(= (30+400)/600 \approx 71.7\%\). Not satisfied.
Part B — equalized odds
TPR: A \(= 85/100 = 85\%\), B \(= 70/100 = 70\%\). FPR: A \(= 100/400 = 25\%\), B \(= 100/500 = 20\%\). Not satisfied (both differ).
Part C — impossibility
Base rates differ (20% vs 16.7%). Calibration within group forces different approval rates, which breaks demographic parity. Mutually incompatible — Chouldechova's theorem.
Part D — which to enforce
Under ECOA, equalized odds is most defensible: it equalises error rates, so creditworthy borrowers across groups face equal false-denial rates.
Solutions · Problem 3

RRF scores and the winning passage

With \(s_{\text{RRF}} = 1/(60 + r_{\text{dense}}) + 1/(60 + r_{\text{sparse}})\): \[ d_1: 1/61 + 1/64 \approx 0.03202, \quad d_2: 1/62 + 1/61 \approx 0.03252, \] \[ d_3: 1/63 + 1/65 \approx 0.03125, \quad d_4: 1/64 + 1/62 \approx 0.03176, \] \[ d_5: 1/65 + 1/63 \approx 0.03125. \]
Part B — ranking
\(d_2 > d_1 > d_4 > d_3 = d_5\). \(d_2\) ranks first — top in sparse, 2nd in dense.
Part C
"Capex for FY2023" is a specific numeric query — BM25 wins on exact term matching; dense may surface related-but-wrong passages. Hybrid keeps BM25's exactness and dense's semantic reach.
Part D
At \(k=0\), rank 1 scores 1 and rank 2 scores 0.5 — a huge gap. As \(k\to\infty\) all scores flatten to equality. \(k=60\) is moderate smoothing: it keeps rank information without letting the top rank dominate.

Hallucination protocol — key points

Detection
Cross-validate each numerical claim against the structured XBRL database, not just free-text passages.
Severity
Low = unverifiable figure in a summary. High = hallucinated figure in a recommendation that reached a client.
Escalation
High severity → immediate review, potential reversal, compliance notification, and root-cause analysis before the system resumes.
Wrap-up · the whole course in one slide

Seven principles to carry out the door

  1. Match the tool to the task — not every problem needs a frontier model
  2. Architecture is compliance — feature store, append-only decision DB, versioned API, monitoring are regulatory requirements
  3. Retrieval beats memorisation for any factual claim that must be auditable
  4. Fairness is a policy choice — the impossibility theorem is non-negotiable; pick explicitly
  1. Chain-of-thought is a trace, not a proof — stated reasoning may not be the actual computation
  2. Measure everything — retrieval quality, faithfulness, fairness, drift; what isn't measured can't be managed
  3. The tools are new; the standards are not — document honestly, validate independently, escalate, retire models when limits prove material

Appendix · stretch

Effective-cost routing: when is a human reviewer worth it?

A small FinLLM answers \(p_m = 80\%\) of filing-QA queries correctly at \(c_m = \$0.01\) each. Low-confidence queries (fraction \(\rho\)) go to a human at \(c_h = \$5.00\) each, who fixes all errors.

\[ C_{\text{eff}}(\rho) = \frac{c_m + \rho\,c_h}{p_m + \rho(1-p_m)}. \] The numerator is the average spend per query; the denominator is the fraction ultimately correct.
Part A
Evaluate \(C_{\text{eff}}\) at \(\rho = 0\) and \(\rho = 0.20\). Which is cheaper per correct output?
Part B
Compliance requires effective correctness \(p_m + \rho(1-p_m) \ge 0.95\). Find the minimum \(\rho^\star\).
Part C
At \(\rho^\star\), what is \(C_{\text{eff}}\)? Discuss cost vs. the regulatory correctness floor.
hint Solve \(0.80 + \rho(0.20) \ge 0.95\) for Part B, then substitute back.
