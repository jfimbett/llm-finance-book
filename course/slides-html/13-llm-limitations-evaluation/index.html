01

Calibration and overconfidence

When stated confidence drifts away from actual accuracy — and how to measure the gap.
The core question

Does "80% confident" actually mean "right 80% of the time"?

A model's expressed confidence — a stated probability, a hedging phrase, a fluent rationale — can diverge sharply from its empirical accuracy. Calibration is whether the two line up.

the bigger picture In finance, confidence is something people act on. A credit officer who triages cases by the model's stated certainty needs that certainty to be honest, or effort flows to exactly the wrong files.
Five fragilities of LLMs in finance (this lecture)
  1. Calibration — systematic overconfidence
  2. Temporal leakage — memorising the future
  3. Stock prediction — statistical vs. economic value
  4. Evaluation — beyond classification accuracy
  5. Hallucination — fabricated facts with no warning signal
Two numbers for one idea

ECE measures the average gap; MCE catches the worst region

Perfect calibration means that among all the times the model says "p," exactly a fraction p turn out right. We summarise how far reality strays from that promise with two scores.

ECE — Expected Calibration Error
A weighted-average gap between confidence and accuracy across all confidence levels.
MCE — Maximum Calibration Error
The single worst confidence band — the catastrophe an average can hide.
Perfect calibration: among predictions with confidence \(p\), a fraction \(p\) are correct, \[ \Pr(Y = 1 \mid \hat p = p) = p \quad\text{for all } p \in [0,1]. \] Bin predictions into \(M\) equal-width intervals \(B_1,\dots,B_M\) (typically \(M=10\)). Plot mean confidence \(\overline p(B_m)\) vs. accuracy \(\mathrm{acc}(B_m)\) — the reliability diagram — and aggregate the gaps: \[ \mathrm{ECE} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \frac{|B_m|}{n}\,\bigl|\,\mathrm{acc}(B_m) - \overline p(B_m)\,\bigr|, \qquad \mathrm{MCE} = \max_m \bigl|\,\mathrm{acc}(B_m) - \overline p(B_m)\,\bigr|. \]
why MCE matters A region where 90–100% confidence predictions are only 40% accurate is unacceptable in high-stakes finance, even when the average looks fine.
Worked example

An LLM credit-risk classifier with an 11.5% calibration error

reading the number An ECE of 11.5% is substantial. The top band — 80–100% confidence — is right only 62% of the time. A credit officer triaging by stated confidence would chase the least reliable cases first.
Where overconfidence comes from

Three reinforcing reasons LLMs sound surer than they are

  1. RLHF-induced overconfidence. Human raters prefer confident, fluent answers over hedged ones — even when hedging is more appropriate. The reward signal pushes toward confident expression regardless of knowledge.
  2. Token- vs. claim-level confidence. Token probabilities compound across a multi-token claim. A model calibrated per token can be wildly miscalibrated per claim.
  3. Distributional shift. Financial data is non-stationary. A model trained on pre-2022 text stays confident on topics that have structurally changed — "it does not know that it does not know."
Documented in finance
Chen, Green, Gulen & Zhou (2024): LLM return forecasts over-extrapolate recent trends and show optimistic bias — a human-like extrapolation/over-optimism that persists after prompt engineering.
Getting and fixing the numbers

How to read confidence — and three ways to recalibrate it

First you need a confidence score: log-probs from the API; or, for text-only access, self-consistency (run \(k\) samples, take the fraction that agree) or just asking the model.

the catch with asking Wang et al. (2023) show verbalised confidence is unstable — 70% under one prompt, 95% under another for the same question.
  • Temperature scaling: \(\operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf z / T)\), one scalar \(T\) fit by NLL. \(T>1\) spreads the probabilities (cures overconfidence); it preserves ranking, so AUROC is unchanged.
  • Isotonic regression: a non-parametric monotone map; flexible but needs a larger calibration set.
  • Platt scaling: a logistic fit on the raw scores; robust on small sets.
When you must not guess

Output a set, not a point — and re-check it every quarter

When the model is uncertain, the honest move is to hand back a set of possible answers with a coverage guarantee, rather than one confident label.

Conformal prediction emits a set \(\mathcal C(\mathbf x)\) — e.g. \(\{\text{default}, \text{non-default}\}\) when uncertain — carrying the guarantee \[ \Pr\bigl(Y \in \mathcal C(\mathbf x)\bigr) \ge 1-\alpha. \]
operational rule Calibration is distribution-specific. Re-estimate it at least quarterly — it is part of model monitoring under SR 11-7.
02

Temporal leakage and look-ahead bias

The most underappreciated failure mode: the model may have already read the answer.
The silent advantage

Did the model forecast the future — or just remember it?

the bigger picture Evaluating an LLM is not just asking questions and scoring answers. The model may have seen the answers — or the test instances themselves — during pretraining. If benchmark and training corpus draw on the same news, filings, and forums, measured "performance" may be memorisation, not generalisation.

Canonical trap: "Given this Q2-2021 earnings call, did the stock rise next month?" A model with a Jan-2022 cutoff has already read the news, the analyst reactions, and the social commentary on how it turned out.

Three leakage channels (Ziemke et al., 2024)
  1. Direct memorisation — the exact or near-verbatim document was seen.
  2. Outcome narratives — "how the trade played out" retrospectives.
  3. Imbalanced corpora — textual survivorship bias toward extreme outcomes that attracted commentary.
This is measured, not speculated

The stakes are empirical: leakage has been quantified

memorisation is measurable Lopez-Lira, Tang & Zhu (2025): LLMs have memorised the exact numerical values of key economic and financial variables inside their training window — making it impossible to distinguish genuine out-of-sample forecasting from recall.

Two confirmations of the magnitude:

  • Rahimikia & Drinkall (2024) pre-train year-specific LLMs (2007–2023). Properly time-bounded models outperform much larger general-purpose models on trading — so off-the-shelf benchmarks overstate true skill.
  • Gao, Jiang & Yan (2025) introduce the Lookahead Propensity (LAP); a positive LAP–accuracy correlation on stock-return and capex tasks confirms apparent skill is substantially lookahead bias.

Practitioner taxonomy: Noguer i Alonso (2024) catalogues look-ahead sources — training cutoffs, entity embeddings, backtest design — and their mitigations.

The first two defences

Use only what was knowable then, and hide who it was about

Point-in-time (PIT) data is what was actually available at a historical moment — not later-restated values (think CRSP/Compustat snapshots).

  1. Hard temporal cutoffs — evaluate \(\ge 3\) months past the confirmed knowledge cutoff (a publication-lag safety margin).
  2. Document-level timestamps — verified publication dates; exclude uncertain provenance.
  3. Contextual anonymisation — replace company names, tickers, and executives with synthetic identifiers.
why anonymise "Will Apple report revenue above \(\$\)100B in Q4 2022?" is a recall question, not a forecast. Replace it with "Company X, a consumer electronics firm with ~\(\$\)380B revenue" to force reasoning from context.
Designing the split

Random splits leak the future; split on time with a gap

For temporal data, train, validation, and test must be ordered in time and separated by a gap \(\Delta\) wide enough that no label window straddles the boundary.

\[ \mathcal D_{\text{train}} = \{(x_t,y_t): t \le T_1 - \Delta\}, \quad \mathcal D_{\text{valid}} = \{(x_t,y_t): T_1 \le t \le T_2 - \Delta\}, \quad \mathcal D_{\text{test}} = \{(x_t,y_t): t \ge T_2\}. \] Require \(\Delta \ge\) prediction horizon \(h\) + publication-lag buffer.
  • N-gram dedup — hash 13-gram windows (MinHash LSH) against the training corpus; drop high-overlap instances.
  • Factual probing — if the model answers facts a non-leaked model could not, suspect contamination.
  • Date-blinding ablation — a big accuracy drop when temporal markers are removed signals pattern retrieval, not reasoning.
Audit in action

A contamination audit that shrank a real edge

500 earnings calls (2022–23), an LLM with a Dec-2023 cutoff, predicting EPS beat/miss. Cleaning the data, step by step, exposes how much "skill" was memory.

  1. N-gram check: 34/500 transcripts have \(\ge 3\) matching 13-grams in Common Crawl → flagged and removed.
  2. Anonymise the remaining 466 (names, tickers, executives).
  3. Compare: accuracy falls 62% → 54% on anonymisation, so ≈ 8 pp was entity-level memorisation.
conclusion The residual 54% still beats the 50% baseline — but the apparent edge has shrunk substantially. Contamination auditing is not cosmetic: it changes conclusions.
03

Stock movement prediction: a cautionary tale

Why "51–65% accurate" is a much smaller claim than it sounds.
The theoretical ceiling

Market efficiency caps what public text can be worth

the bigger picture If LLMs digest news, sentiment, and calls faster than humans, perhaps they find price-relevant signals first. But market efficiency sets a hard limit on how much edge publicly available text can contain.

Under the semi-strong efficient-markets hypothesis (Fama, 1970), prices already reflect all public information. An LLM trained on public text is reconstructing information already traded on — not extracting novel alpha.

Grossman–Stiglitz, the loophole
Markets cannot be perfectly efficient — some mispricing must remain to pay informed traders. The open question: can LLMs reach that residual edge before it is arbitraged away? (Where genuine value might remain: see appendix.)
What the data actually shows

Directional accuracy lands a few points above a coin flip

Across studies, the consistent pattern is daily/weekly directional accuracy only a little above the 50% chance level (Xu et al., 2024).

  • Lopez-Lira & Tang (2023): ChatGPT headline sentiment yields significant out-of-sample directional accuracy that scales with model complexity — but the window was unusually volatile and returns concentrated in some news categories.
  • Vidal (2024): four leading LLMs on 30-day S&P 500 direction — accuracy only marginally above chance, below the economic threshold after costs.
above 62%? check for these artefacts (Zhao et al., 2025) Insufficient train–eval gap · no anonymisation · overlapping windows · gross accuracy without transaction costs. After controls, the residual is 51–54% — statistically positive at best, economically negligible after costs.

CFA Institute: durable alpha is unlikely in liquid, well-covered markets; value is possible in small-cap, EM, or private-information synthesis.

Sophistication is not skill

When a simple model beats the LLM

"More sophisticated" does not mean more accurate. On many price-prediction tasks, decades-old time-series models match or beat LLMs.

ARIMA(-GARCH) — Box & Jenkins (1970)
  • Principled time-series structure (AR, differencing, MA + variance clustering)
  • Few parameters, fast to refit
  • No contaminable training corpus
LSTM — Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997)
  • Learns longer dependencies
  • On pure price-sequence prediction, often matches or beats LLMs (Zhao et al., 2025)
Three conditions that favour simpler models
  1. Text irrelevance — the target is driven by price/volume dynamics.
  2. Short history — too little labelled data to justify fine-tuning.
  3. Regime changes — ARIMA re-estimates cheaply; re-fine-tuning an LLM is an order of magnitude more expensive.
methodological lesson A random-walk baseline is not enough — benchmark against the best classical alternative on the same information set.
04

Evaluation beyond classification accuracy

Judge the model in the language of capital: returns, risk, and alpha.
Accuracy is the wrong yardstick

53% can be worthless and 51% can be gold

the bigger picture 53% directional accuracy can be worthless after costs; 51% can be valuable if the confident calls land on the big moves. Accuracy is necessary, not sufficient. Financial evaluation must speak in returns, risk, and alpha.
Sharpe ratio (risk-adjusted return): \[ \mathrm{SR} = \frac{\overline R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}, \qquad \text{annualise } \times\sqrt{252}\ \text{(daily)},\ \sqrt{12}\ \text{(monthly)}. \] Information ratio (active management): \(\mathrm{IR} = \alpha/\mathrm{TE} \approx \mathrm{IC}\cdot\sqrt N\), where IC = signal quality (rank correlation of predicted vs. realised) and \(N\) = independent bets per year. IR \(>0.5\) is strong, \(>1.0\) exceptional.
Three blind spots of accuracy

Why economic metrics beat a hit-rate

  • Accuracy ignores magnitude. A 51% model that is right on the big moves and wrong on the small ones can dominate a 53% model that nails noise and misses the moves that matter.
  • Accuracy ignores costs. A directional hit-rate says nothing about the turnover, spread, and impact needed to monetise the signal.
  • Accuracy ignores risk. Two strategies with identical returns but different volatility and drawdown are not equivalent capital allocations.
the reframing Report performance in the language of capital — returns, risk-adjusted returns, and alpha — not classification scores.
The hardest test

Is the edge real skill, or just repackaged risk premia?

The economic question is not "does the strategy make money?" but "does it make money beyond known risk premia?" To answer, strip out market, size, and value exposure and see what's left.

\[ r_{p,t} = \alpha + \beta_{\mathrm{MKT}}r_{\mathrm{MKT},t} + \beta_{\mathrm{SMB}}r_{\mathrm{SMB},t} + \beta_{\mathrm{HML}}r_{\mathrm{HML},t} + \varepsilon_t. \] The intercept \(\alpha\) is the return unexplained by market, size, and value exposure — the genuine skill component.
the sobering finding Many LLM strategies produce \(\alpha\) indistinguishable from zero: their apparent returns are repackaged factor exposure, not new information.
Validating without cheating

Walk forward through time — never shuffle it

Standard \(k\)-fold cross-validation shuffles the data, leaking the future into the training folds. For finance, the validation itself must respect the arrow of time.

Walk-forward
Train up to \(t\), evaluate \([t, t{+}h]\), advance; aggregate the out-of-sample windows.
Expanding-window (anchored)
Fix the start, grow the window, re-estimate each step — standard in financial ML (Lopez de Prado, 2018).
Both need a purge period \(\Delta_{\text{purge}} \ge h\) between train and eval, so no observation appears in both through overlapping label windows. At iteration \(k\): \[ \mathcal D_{\text{train}}^{(k)} = \{t \le kh - \Delta_{\text{purge}}\}, \qquad \mathcal D_{\text{eval}}^{(k)} = \{kh \le t \le (k{+}1)h - 1\}. \]
cost trade-off Full LLM fine-tuning is expensive to re-estimate, so practitioners widen \(h\) — at the price of evaluating a staler model late in each window.
Paper vs. practice

Trading-grade evaluation: costs, capacity, drawdown

A strategy can win on paper and fail in practice. A complete evaluation prices in the frictions that erase paper edges.

Transaction costs (turnover \(\tau\)): \[ c_{\text{round-trip}} = 2\tau\,(\text{half-spread} + \text{market impact}). \] US large-cap half-spreads 1–3 bps; impact 5–20 bps; daily news rebalancing can hit 10–50 bps/day, erasing any edge.
Capacity (Almgren–Chriss): temporary impact \(\approx \eta\,(q/V)\); profitable at \(\$\)10M may be unprofitable at \(\$\)1B.
Max drawdown: \(\mathrm{MDD} = \max_{t_1<t_2}\bigl(\frac{P_{t_1}-P_{t_2}}{P_{t_1}}\bigr)\); Calmar = annual return / MDD.
A complete report should state
Gross & net Sharpe · IR vs. benchmark · MDD & Calmar · turnover & capacity · factor decomposition · \(\alpha\) significance with robust SEs and a multiple-testing correction.
05

Hallucination in financial contexts

Fluent, confident, factually wrong — and invisible to a reader who lacks the answer.
The silent failure mode

Hallucination gives no warning signal

the bigger picture Hallucination — fluent, confident, factually wrong — is undetectable by a reader who lacks the correct answer. A junior analyst handed a hallucinated analysis gets no warning: the prose reads exactly as plausibly as accurate content.

Four dangerous categories in finance:

  1. Fabricated citations — plausible journals, SSRN IDs, or regulatory sections that do not exist (Bang et al., 2023). Serious liability in compliance filings.
  2. Incorrect numerical facts — revenue, EPS, EBITDA, spreads. Kang et al. (2023): GPT-4 class models err on a non-trivial fraction, with no lexical signal separating right from wrong.
  3. Phantom companies/executives — plausible but non-existent entities; a material risk in due diligence and KYC.
  4. Anachronistic facts — stale rates, ratings, or leadership stated confidently (Ji et al., 2023); no awareness of obsolescence.
The first line of defence

Grounding: anchor the model to verified sources

The fix is to reduce reliance on the model's parametric memory and tie answers to external sources you can check.

  • RAG (Lewis et al., 2020) — retrieve verified docs into context. Effectiveness hinges on retrieval quality; topically relevant but factually tangential context still yields hallucination.
  • Retrieval verification — FActScore (Min et al., 2023) decomposes output into atomic facts and checks each against a source (EDGAR, Bloomberg, a regulatory DB).
  • Tool use / function calling (Schick et al., 2023) — fetch live data; the model becomes a reasoning engine, not a knowledge store.
  • Self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022; Manakul et al., 2023) — facts stable across samples are likelier correct; variable facts are flagged for review.
grounding reduces but does not eliminate RAG fails on erroneous docs; tools return stale data; self-consistency can be consistently wrong if the error is strong in parametric memory. High-stakes claims still need human-in-the-loop review.

Wrap-up

Five fragilities, one disciplined stance

  1. Calibration — LLMs overconfident; ECE can exceed 10%. Recalibrate (temperature/isotonic/Platt); use conformal sets for high stakes.
  2. Temporal leakage — in/near-cutoff evaluation inflates results. Hard splits, purge, anonymisation, n-gram dedup. Audits cut accuracy by ≥8 pp.
  3. Stock prediction — 51–65%, the upper end from artefacts. After controls, residual alpha is economically modest; ARIMA/LSTM often win.
  1. Economic evaluation — Sharpe, IR, factor alpha; costs, capacity, drawdown. Accuracy alone is not enough.
  2. Hallucination — fabricated citations/numbers/entities, no lexical tell. Grounding helps; human review remains necessary.
the disciplined stance
Limitations do not preclude LLMs in finance — they demand treating outputs as inputs to a decision process, not end products.

Appendix · binning detail

Adaptive binning, and when to prefer MCE

  • Bin-boundary sensitivity. Equal-width ECE is sensitive to boundaries. Adaptive binning — equal mass per bin — is more reliable when confidence scores cluster near 0 or 1, as they do for overconfident models (Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana, 2005).
  • When to prefer MCE. In high-stakes regimes a single catastrophically miscalibrated region (e.g. 90–100% confidence at 40% accuracy) is unacceptable even with a good average ECE. Report both.
Surfacing overconfidence in practice
EPS to two decimals with no interval; definitive credit calls near the boundary; cherry-picked market rationales; "in/not in compliance" that hides jurisdictional ambiguity.
Appendix · the optimistic case

Where genuine LLM value might remain

Market efficiency limits the edge in public text, but residual value can survive in three places:

  • Synthesis at scale — diffuse signals across thousands of low-attention documents.
  • Novel reasoning chains — cross-domain links a factor model misses.
  • Private/proprietary text — not in training, not in prices (raises regulatory questions).
the defensible uses These are exactly the cases the lecture flags — small-cap, emerging-market, or private-information synthesis, where public-text efficiency bites least.
Appendix · the fundamental law of active management

Signal quality times breadth equals skill

\[ \mathrm{IR} \approx \mathrm{IC}\cdot\sqrt N. \]
  • IC (Information Coefficient): cross-sectional rank correlation between predicted and realised returns — signal quality.
  • N: number of independent bets per year — breadth, driven by securities covered × prediction frequency.
Why this matters for LLMs
It separates the two value sources: a weak signal (low IC) can still produce a respectable IR if breadth \(N\) is large (many names, high frequency) — but breadth multiplies turnover, and turnover multiplies transaction costs. The decomposition makes the cost/breadth tension explicit.
