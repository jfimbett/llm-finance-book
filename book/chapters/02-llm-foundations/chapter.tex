\section{Document-Level Representations}
\label{sec:doc-repr}

\begin{deepdive}

Word embeddings assign vectors to individual tokens.  In most
financial NLP applications the natural unit of analysis is longer: an
earnings call transcript (several thousand words), a 10-K filing
(tens of thousands of words), a news headline (ten words), or a
research note (hundreds of words).  To use embedding-based features
at the document level we need an aggregation scheme that maps a
sequence of token embeddings to a single fixed-size vector.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Averaging Word Vectors}
\label{sec:doc-repr-avg}

The simplest aggregation is the unweighted arithmetic mean.

\begin{definition}[Mean Embedding]
\label{def:mean-embedding}
Let $d = (w_1, w_2, \dots, w_n)$ be a document of $n$ tokens and let
$\varphi : \mathcal{V} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ be a word embedding.
The \emph{mean embedding} of $d$ is
\begin{equation}
  \bar{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}(d)
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \varphi(w_i)
  \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^d.
  \label{eq:mean-embedding}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Mean embedding is algebraically equivalent to multiplying the
Bag-of-Words term-frequency vector $\mathbf{tf}(d) \in \mathbb{R}^V$
by the (column-normalised) embedding matrix.  Specifically, let
$\mathbf{tf}(d)_j = \text{count}(w_j, d)/n$ be the relative frequency
of word $w_j$ in document $d$.  Then
\begin{equation}
  \bar{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}(d)
  \;=\;
  \mathbf{W}^{\top} \mathbf{tf}(d)
  \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^d,
  \label{eq:mean-embedding-bow}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ is the embedding matrix
from Definition~\ref{def:word-embedding}.  This shows that mean
embedding is a linear operation on the standard BoW representation,
projecting from $V$-dimensional one-hot/count space into the dense
$d$-dimensional embedding space via the weight matrix $\mathbf{W}$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{thm:mean-embed-is-bow}
Mean embedding is a Bag-of-Words model.  Specifically, if
$\hat{\mathbf{W}} = [\varphi(w_1) \;\cdots\; \varphi(w_V)]^{\top}
\in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ is the full embedding matrix, then
\[
  \bar{\boldsymbol{\varphi}}(d)
  \;=\;
  \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top} \mathbf{tf}(d).
\]
In particular, the mean embedding is invariant to permutations of the
token sequence; word order is discarded.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  Expand \eqref{eq:mean-embedding} using the row-selection
  interpretation $\varphi(w_i) = \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top}\mathbf{e}_{w_i}$,
  then use linearity:
  \[
    \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top}\mathbf{e}_{w_i}
    \;=\;
    \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top}
    \!\underbrace{\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{e}_{w_i}\right)}_{\mathbf{tf}(d)}
    \;=\;
    \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\top}\mathbf{tf}(d). \qedhere
  \]
\end{proof}

Despite its simplicity and computational efficiency, mean embedding
has several well-documented weaknesses:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Common words such as \emph{the}, \emph{is}, and \emph{of}
        contribute as much to the mean as domain-specific terms such as
        \emph{default} or \emph{liquidity} (equal weighting).
        Stop-word removal partially addresses this, but introduces a
        pre-processing dependency.
  \item The mean embedding of ``\emph{the company did not meet earnings
        expectations}'' is identical to that of ``\emph{earnings
        expectations met the company not did}'' --- a garbled sentence
        containing the same words.  Syntactic structure, negation, and
        conditionals are invisible (no word order).
  \item As $n$ grows, the mean converges (by the law of large numbers)
        toward the corpus-wide average embedding, losing
        document-specific signal.  Mean embedding performs well for
        short texts but degrades for filings or call transcripts
        \cite{reimers2019}. % [CITE: Reimers & Gurevych (2019). Sentence-BERT: Sentence Embeddings using Siamese BERT-Networks. EMNLP]
  \item Words absent from $\mathcal{V}$ (e.g.\ rare tickers,
        neologisms) must be mapped to a special \texttt{<UNK>} vector
        or dropped, both of which introduce noise (out-of-vocabulary
        words).
\end{enumerate}

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Weighted Aggregation and Sentence Transformers}
\label{sec:doc-repr-sbert}

\subsubsection{TF-IDF Weighted Averaging}

A natural refinement is to weight each word's embedding by its
TF-IDF score, up-weighting terms that are rare in the corpus but
frequent in the document and down-weighting common uninformative words.

\begin{definition}[TF-IDF Weighted Embedding]
\label{def:tfidf-embedding}
Let $\text{tfidf}(w_i, d)$ denote the TF-IDF weight of token $w_i$
in document $d$ (see Section~\ref{sec:classical-tfidf}).  The
\emph{TF-IDF weighted embedding} is
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\varphi}_{\mathrm{tfidf}}(d)
  \;=\;
  \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{tfidf}(w_i, d)\; \varphi(w_i)}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{tfidf}(w_i, d)}.
  \label{eq:tfidf-embedding}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

This is a weighted arithmetic mean with weights $\alpha_i =
\text{tfidf}(w_i, d) / \sum_j \text{tfidf}(w_j, d) > 0$.  By
design, $\sum_i \alpha_i = 1$, so $\boldsymbol{\varphi}_{\mathrm{tfidf}}(d)$
lies in the convex hull of the token embeddings.  TF-IDF weighting
consistently outperforms uniform averaging on document classification
and similarity tasks when the embedding quality is held fixed
\cite{arora2017}. % [CITE: Arora et al. (2017). A Simple but Tough-to-Beat Baseline for Sentence Embeddings. ICLR]

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:sif}
A closely related approach, \emph{Smooth Inverse Frequency} (SIF)
\cite{arora2017}, % [CITE: same as above]
replaces TF-IDF weights with
$\alpha_i = a / (a + p(w_i))$,
where $p(w_i)$ is the unigram probability of word $w_i$ in the
training corpus and $a \approx 10^{-3}$ is a smoothing constant.
After computing the weighted sum, SIF removes the first principal
component, which typically captures uninformative ``common discourse''
variance.  SIF embeddings match or exceed supervised methods on
several semantic textual similarity benchmarks at negligible
computational cost.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{The Limitation of Static Embeddings: Polysemy}

Both Word2Vec and GloVe assign a \emph{single, fixed} vector to each
vocabulary item.  This is adequate for words with a single dominant
sense, but fails for polysemous words — words with multiple distinct
meanings depending on context.  In finance, this problem is pervasive:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Bank} can mean a financial institution or the riverbank.
  \item \emph{Yield} can mean bond yield, crop yield, or to give way.
  \item \emph{Position} can mean a financial position (long/short) or
        a physical location.
  \item \emph{Default} can mean failure to repay a debt or the
        default setting of a software parameter.
\end{itemize}
Under a static embedding, the vector for \emph{bank} is a single
point in $\mathbb{R}^d$ that is a weighted average of both senses,
equidistant from financial and geographic contexts.  This conflation
can harm downstream tasks: a sentiment model that associates
\emph{bank} with positive sentiment in financial reporting will
incorrectly transfer that sentiment to sentences about river flooding.

\subsubsection{Contextual Embeddings and Sentence-BERT}

The solution to polysemy is to produce embeddings that depend on the full
surrounding context.  ELMo \cite{peters2018} % [CITE: Peters et al. (2018). Deep contextualized word representations. NAACL]
introduced deep contextual embeddings from a bidirectional language
model; BERT \cite{devlin2019} % [CITE: Devlin et al. (2019). BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding. NAACL]
later generalised this with a transformer architecture.  In a BERT
encoder, the representation of the token \emph{bank} at layer $\ell$
is a function of the entire input sentence, so it will differ in
``\emph{The Federal Reserve Bank raised rates}'' versus
``\emph{The river bank flooded after the storm}.''

Sentence-BERT (SBERT) addresses the gap between contextual word representations and sentence-level embeddings. Vanilla BERT was designed for token-level tasks (named entity
recognition, question answering) rather than for producing
sentence- or document-level representations.  Naively averaging the
BERT token representations (mean pooling) or using the \texttt{[CLS]}
token produces suboptimal sentence embeddings for semantic similarity
tasks \cite{reimers2019}. % [CITE: Reimers & Gurevych (2019). Sentence-BERT. EMNLP]

Sentence-BERT (SBERT) \cite{reimers2019} % [CITE: same]
addresses this by fine-tuning a BERT encoder on a Natural Language
Inference (NLI) objective using a \emph{Siamese} architecture:
two weight-sharing BERT encoders process a sentence pair
$(s_a, s_b)$, producing pooled representations
$\mathbf{u} = \text{POOL}(\text{BERT}(s_a))$ and
$\mathbf{v} = \text{POOL}(\text{BERT}(s_b))$.
The model is trained to predict whether $s_a$ and $s_b$ are
semantically equivalent (entailment), contradictory, or unrelated,
using a classification head over the concatenation
$[\mathbf{u};\, \mathbf{v};\, |\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|]$.

\begin{definition}[Mean Pooling in SBERT]
\label{def:sbert-pool}
Let $\mathbf{H} = [\mathbf{h}_1, \mathbf{h}_2, \dots, \mathbf{h}_n]
\in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_\text{model}}$
be the matrix of last-layer hidden states from the BERT encoder for an
$n$-token input sequence (including special tokens \texttt{[CLS]} and
\texttt{[SEP]}).  Under \emph{mean pooling}, the sentence embedding is
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{s}
  \;=\;
  \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{h}_i \cdot m_i}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{n} m_j},
  \label{eq:sbert-mean-pool}
\end{equation}
where $m_i \in \{0, 1\}$ is an attention mask that equals 1 for real tokens
and 0 for padding tokens.  Dividing by $\sum_{j} m_j$ (the number of
non-padding tokens) rather than $n$ (the full sequence length) ensures that the
embedding magnitude does not decrease with the amount of padding.
\end{definition}

BERT's \texttt{[CLS]} token is prepended to every input sequence and
its final hidden state $\mathbf{h}_{\texttt{[CLS]}}$ is used in
classification tasks; one might expect it to encode sentence-level
meaning.  In practice, without explicit fine-tuning on semantic
similarity tasks, the \texttt{[CLS]} representation performs
surprisingly poorly as a general-purpose sentence embedding
\cite{reimers2019}. % [CITE: same]
Mean pooling over all token representations consistently outperforms
\texttt{[CLS]} pooling on standard semantic textual similarity (STS)
benchmarks.  After SBERT fine-tuning, both pooling strategies converge
to high-quality representations, with mean pooling retaining a slight
advantage.

A key practical advantage of SBERT is that sentence embeddings can be
\emph{pre-computed and indexed}: once the corpus of $N$ documents has
been encoded, semantic search reduces to a nearest-neighbour query in
$\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$, which can be accelerated with
approximate nearest-neighbour (ANN) data structures (e.g.\ FAISS
\cite{johnson2019billion}, % [CITE: Johnson et al. (2019). Billion-scale similarity search with GPUs. IEEE TPAMI]
HNSW).  Cross-encoding a query against all $N$ documents with vanilla
BERT would require $N$ separate forward passes; SBERT bi-encoding
requires only one forward pass per document, reducing inference time
from $O(N)$ to $O(1)$ at query time.


\begin{example}
\label{ex:doc-similarity-finance}
The following applications of contextual document embeddings are
documented in the finance literature:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Encode all earnings call transcripts in a corpus using SBERT
        (or a finance-tuned variant such as FinBERT-SBERT).  At each
        reporting period, retrieve the $k$ most similar past calls by
        cosine similarity; the score serves as a feature for predicting
        short-window abnormal returns \cite{ko2020}. % [CITE: Ko et al. (2020). or relevant FinNLP earnings similarity paper]
  \item Encode 10-K business description sections for all public firms.
        High cosine similarity between two firms' descriptions predicts
        product-market proximity, a strong predictor of acquisition
        likelihood \cite{frattaroli2019}. % [CITE: relevant M&A text paper]
  \item Encode a corpus of regulatory guidance documents (Basel III,
        IFRS 9, MiFID II).  When a new regulation is published,
        SBERT-based retrieval identifies the most relevant precedents,
        replacing keyword search with semantic search.
  \item Embed analyst research notes and quarterly earnings guidance;
        measure cosine similarity between the guidance embedding and
        subsequent results narratives as a proxy for management
        credibility or guidance quality.
\end{enumerate}
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:context-window-limit}
BERT-based models impose a maximum context length (typically 512
tokens for standard BERT, 4096 or more for long-context variants such
as Longformer \cite{beltagy2020}). % [CITE: Beltagy et al. (2020). Longformer: The Long-Document Transformer. arXiv:2004.05150]
Long financial documents (full 10-K filings, lengthy earnings call
transcripts) must be handled by one of three strategies:
(i) \emph{truncation} — only the first 512 tokens are encoded, which
may miss material information in later sections;
(ii) \emph{chunking} — the document is split into overlapping windows,
each encoded independently, and the chunk embeddings are aggregated
(e.g.\ by mean pooling or weighted by position); or
(iii) \emph{hierarchical encoding} — sentences are encoded individually
and a second-level encoder (another transformer or an LSTM) aggregates
sentence embeddings into a document embedding.  The choice among these
strategies introduces a design trade-off between completeness and
computational cost that has no universal solution; practitioners should
benchmark all three on their specific downstream task.
\end{remark}

\end{deepdive}

\section{Sequential Models: From RNNs to Attention}
\label{sec:sequential}

\begin{deepdive}

The representations developed in Sections~\ref{sec:embeddings} and~\ref{sec:doc-repr}
treat documents as \emph{bags} of tokens: order is either ignored or captured only
crudely through $n$-gram features.
Financial text, however, is inherently sequential.
An earnings-call transcript unfolds over time; a regulatory filing builds an argument
across hundreds of sentences; the sentiment of \textit{the company did not miss its
targets} depends on the negation appearing \emph{before} the noun phrase.
This section traces the evolution of architectures designed to model sequences:
vanilla recurrent networks, gated variants that address their chief failure mode,
and the attention mechanism that ultimately superseded them.

% ---
\subsection{Recurrent Neural Networks and the Vanishing Gradient Problem}
\label{sec:sequential-rnn}

\begin{definition}[Vanilla Recurrent Neural Network]
\label{def:rnn}
Let $\mathbf{x}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_x}$ be the input vector at time step $t$
and $\mathbf{h}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$ be the hidden (state) vector.
A \emph{vanilla recurrent neural network} (RNN) is defined by the recursion
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{h}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_h\,\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + W_x\,\mathbf{x}_t
    + \mathbf{b}\right), \label{eq:rnn-hidden}\\
  \hat{\mathbf{y}}_t &= \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(W_y\,\mathbf{h}_t
    + \mathbf{b}_y\right), \label{eq:rnn-output}
\end{align}
where $W_h \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times d_h}$, $W_x \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times d_x}$,
$W_y \in \mathbb{R}^{d_y \times d_h}$ are learnable weight matrices,
$\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$ and $\mathbf{b}_y \in \mathbb{R}^{d_y}$ are
bias vectors, and $\sigma$ is an element-wise nonlinearity (sigmoid or $\tanh$).
The initial hidden state $\mathbf{h}_0$ is either $\mathbf{0}$ or a learnable
parameter.
\end{definition}

The recurrence in~\eqref{eq:rnn-hidden} allows information to propagate across time
steps: $\mathbf{h}_t$ summarises the history $\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_t$.
All parameters are \emph{shared} across time steps, giving the RNN its weight tying.

Training requires $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial \theta$ for each parameter in
$\{W_h, W_x, W_y, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{b}_y\}$,
where $\mathcal{L} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ell_t$.
Applying the chain rule gives the \emph{backpropagation through time} (BPTT)
formula \cite{werbos1990bptt}: % [CITE: Werbos 1990, Backpropagation Through Time: What It Does and How to Do It]
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_t}
  = \sum_{s=t}^{T}
    \frac{\partial \ell_s}{\partial \mathbf{h}_s}
    \prod_{k=t}^{s-1} \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_k},
  \label{eq:bptt-chain}
\end{equation}
where the empty product $\prod_{k=t}^{t-1}(\cdot) = \mathbf{I}$ by convention, and each Jacobian factor is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_k}
  = \mathrm{diag}\!\left(\sigma'\!\left(W_h\,\mathbf{h}_k +
    W_x\,\mathbf{x}_{k+1} + \mathbf{b}\right)\right) W_h,
  \label{eq:rnn-jacobian}
\end{equation}
and $\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf{v})$ is the diagonal matrix with $\mathbf{v}$ on its
main diagonal.

\begin{proposition}[Vanishing and Exploding Gradients in RNNs]
\label{thm:vanish-explode}
Let $\rho = \|W_h\|_2$ (spectral norm) and $M_\sigma = \sup_z |\sigma'(z)|$.
Then
\begin{equation}
  \left\|\prod_{k=t}^{T-1}
    \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_k}\right\|_2
  \;\leq\; \left(M_\sigma\,\rho\right)^{T-t}.
  \label{eq:vanish-bound}
\end{equation}
In particular: (i) if $M_\sigma\,\rho < 1$, gradients vanish exponentially with
$T-t$; (ii) if $\rho > 1/M_\sigma$, they can grow exponentially
(\emph{exploding gradients}).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By sub-multiplicativity and~\eqref{eq:rnn-jacobian},
$\|\partial \mathbf{h}_{k+1}/\partial \mathbf{h}_k\|_2 \leq M_\sigma\,\rho$.
Applying this bound $T-t$ times yields~\eqref{eq:vanish-bound}.
For the logistic sigmoid $M_\sigma = 1/4$; for $\tanh$, $M_\sigma = 1$.
If $M_\sigma\,\rho < 1$ the right-hand side decays geometrically; the lower
explosive bound follows by considering Jacobians with a shared dominant eigenvector.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Gradient clipping \cite{pascanu2013gradient} % [CITE: Pascanu et al. 2013, On the difficulty of training recurrent neural networks]
is the standard remedy for explosion, but there is no analogous in-architecture fix
for vanishing gradients in the vanilla RNN.
\end{remark}

These theoretical constraints have concrete consequences for financial text. An S\&P~500 earnings-call transcript is typically 8,000--12,000 tokens.
Management guidance on page~20 may negate a claim on page~3.
Proposition~\ref{thm:vanish-explode} implies the gradient from position $t = 100$
contributes essentially nothing to $W_h$ after $T - t \approx 9{,}900$ BPTT
steps --- making long-range financial dependencies practically unlearnable.

% ---
\subsection{LSTMs and GRUs}
\label{sec:sequential-lstm}

\subsubsection{Long Short-Term Memory}

The Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) unit was introduced by \citet{hochreiter1997long} % [CITE: Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997, Long Short-Term Memory]
precisely to overcome the vanishing-gradient problem.
The key innovation is a \emph{cell state} $\mathbf{c}_t$ with an \emph{additive}
update path, gated by three sigmoid-activated vectors.

\begin{definition}[LSTM Equations]
\label{def:lstm}
Let $[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t] \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h + d_x}$ denote
concatenation. The LSTM update at step $t$ is:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{f}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_f\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]
    + \mathbf{b}_f\right), \label{eq:lstm-forget}\\
  \mathbf{i}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_i\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]
    + \mathbf{b}_i\right), \label{eq:lstm-input}\\
  \mathbf{g}_t &= \tanh\!\left(W_g\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]
    + \mathbf{b}_g\right), \label{eq:lstm-candidate}\\
  \mathbf{c}_t &= \mathbf{f}_t \odot \mathbf{c}_{t-1}
    + \mathbf{i}_t \odot \mathbf{g}_t, \label{eq:lstm-cell}\\
  \mathbf{o}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_o\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]
    + \mathbf{b}_o\right), \label{eq:lstm-output}\\
  \mathbf{h}_t &= \mathbf{o}_t \odot \tanh(\mathbf{c}_t), \label{eq:lstm-hidden}
\end{align}
where $\odot$ denotes element-wise product and
$W_f, W_i, W_g, W_o \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times (d_h + d_x)}$ are learnable
weight matrices.
\end{definition}

Intuitive interpretations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The forget gate $\mathbf{f}_t \in (0,1)^{d_h}$ controls what
        fraction of $\mathbf{c}_{t-1}$ to erase ($f_{t,j}\approx 0$:
        discard; $f_{t,j}\approx 1$: retain).  In finance, it clears
        irrelevant preamble sentiment as the transcript shifts to core
        earnings figures.
  \item The input gate $\mathbf{i}_t \in (0,1)^{d_h}$ controls what
        fraction of the candidate $\mathbf{g}_t$ to write, filtering
        boilerplate language in 10-K filings.
  \item The candidate $\mathbf{g}_t \in (-1,1)^{d_h}$ carries new
        information from the current input and recent context.
  \item The output gate $\mathbf{o}_t \in (0,1)^{d_h}$ selects which
        cell-state components are exposed as $\mathbf{h}_t$.
\end{itemize}

The reason the LSTM cell state avoids vanishing gradients lies in the additive structure of its update. The update~\eqref{eq:lstm-cell} is \emph{additive} in $\mathbf{g}_t$, unlike the
purely multiplicative vanilla RNN update~\eqref{eq:rnn-hidden}.
The cell-state gradient satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{c}_t}
  = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{c}_{t+1}} \odot \mathbf{f}_{t+1}
    + \text{(terms via } \mathbf{h}_t\text{)}.
  \label{eq:lstm-cellgrad}
\end{equation}
When $\mathbf{f}_{t+1} \approx \mathbf{1}$, the gradient is transmitted without
decay --- the \emph{constant error carousel} of \citeauthor{hochreiter1997long} % [CITE: Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997]
--- mitigating, though not eliminating, vanishing gradients: when forget gates
saturate toward $\mathbf{0}$, exponential decay along the cell-state path
can still occur over very long sequences \citep{bengio1994learning}.

\subsubsection{Gated Recurrent Units}

\citet{cho2014learning} % [CITE: Cho et al. 2014, Learning Phrase Representations using RNN Encoder-Decoder for Statistical Machine Translation]
proposed the GRU, which merges cell state and hidden state, reducing parameters
while retaining essential gating; \citet{chung2014empirical} subsequently provided
an empirical evaluation comparing GRUs to LSTMs across sequence modelling tasks.

\begin{definition}[GRU Equations]
\label{def:gru}
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{z}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_z\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]\right),
  \label{eq:gru-update}\\
  \mathbf{r}_t &= \sigma\!\left(W_r\,[\mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]\right),
  \label{eq:gru-reset}\\
  \tilde{\mathbf{h}}_t &= \tanh\!\left(
    W\,[\mathbf{r}_t \odot \mathbf{h}_{t-1},\,\mathbf{x}_t]\right),
  \label{eq:gru-candidate}\\
  \mathbf{h}_t &= (1 - \mathbf{z}_t) \odot \mathbf{h}_{t-1}
    + \mathbf{z}_t \odot \tilde{\mathbf{h}}_t,
  \label{eq:gru-hidden}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{z}_t$ is the \emph{update gate}, $\mathbf{r}_t$ is the
\emph{reset gate}, and $W_z, W_r, W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times (d_h + d_x)}$.
\end{definition}

A GRU has $\approx 3(d_h+d_x)d_h$ parameters versus $4(d_h+d_x)d_h$ for an LSTM.
LSTMs outperform GRUs on tasks with very long-range dependencies; GRUs are preferred
under data or compute constraints \cite{chung2014empirical}. % [CITE: Chung et al. 2014, Empirical Evaluation of Gated Recurrent Neural Networks on Sequence Modeling]

\subsubsection{Bidirectional RNNs}

A standard LSTM sees only the prefix $\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_t$.
A \emph{bidirectional} LSTM \cite{schuster1997bilstm} % [CITE: Schuster & Paliwal 1997, Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Networks]
runs one forward and one backward pass, concatenating results:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_t =
  [\,\overrightarrow{\mathbf{h}}_t;\,\overleftarrow{\mathbf{h}}_t\,],
  \quad
  \overrightarrow{\mathbf{h}}_t =
    \mathrm{LSTM}_{\mathrm{fwd}}(\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_t),
  \quad
  \overleftarrow{\mathbf{h}}_t =
    \mathrm{LSTM}_{\mathrm{bwd}}(\mathbf{x}_T,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_t).
  \label{eq:bilstm}
\end{equation}
BiLSTMs were the dominant architecture for financial NER and sentiment classification
before Transformers; applications include credit-event extraction from news
\cite{araci2019finbert} % [CITE: Araci 2019, FinBERT: Financial Sentiment Analysis with Pre-trained Language Models]
and earnings-surprise prediction from analyst reports.

\subsection{The Attention Mechanism}
\label{sec:sequential-attention}

Even with gated architectures, an RNN encoder must compress the input into a
fixed-length vector before the decoder can generate output.
\citet{bahdanau2015neural} % [CITE: Bahdanau, Cho & Bengio 2015, Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate]
proposed \emph{attention}, allowing the decoder to consult all encoder hidden states
at each step.

Let $\mathbf{h}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{h}_T \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$ be encoder hidden
states and $\mathbf{s}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_s}$ the decoder state at step $t$.

\begin{definition}[Additive (Bahdanau) Attention]
\label{def:bahdanau-attention}
The \emph{alignment score} between $\mathbf{s}_t$ and $\mathbf{h}_i$ is
\begin{equation}
  e_{t,i} = \mathbf{v}^{\top}
    \tanh\!\left(W_s\,\mathbf{s}_t + W_h\,\mathbf{h}_i\right),
  \label{eq:alignment-score}
\end{equation}
with $W_s \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a \times d_s}$, $W_h \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a \times d_h}$,
and $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a}$ learnable.
The \emph{attention weights} and \emph{context vector} are:
\begin{align}
  \alpha_{t,i} &= \frac{\exp(e_{t,i})}{\sum_{j=1}^{T} \exp(e_{t,j})},
  \label{eq:attention-weights}\\
  \mathbf{c}_t &= \sum_{i=1}^{T} \alpha_{t,i}\,\mathbf{h}_i.
  \label{eq:context-vector}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Attention Weights Form a Probability Distribution]
\label{thm:attention-prob}
For any $e_{t,1},\ldots,e_{t,T} \in \mathbb{R}$, the weights
$\{\alpha_{t,i}\}_{i=1}^T$ in~\eqref{eq:attention-weights}
satisfy $\alpha_{t,i} > 0$ for all $i$ and $\sum_{i=1}^{T} \alpha_{t,i} = 1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Positivity: $\exp(\cdot) > 0$.
Summation: $\sum_i \alpha_{t,i} =
\sum_i \exp(e_{t,i}) / \sum_j \exp(e_{t,j}) = 1$. \qedhere
\end{proof}

The context vector $\mathbf{c}_t$ is a convex combination of encoder states.
In a financial summariser, the decoder generating a sentence about net income assigns
high $\alpha_{t,i}$ to tokens where income figures appear in the source.

Applying attention within a \emph{single} sequence --- each token attending to all
others --- yields \emph{self-attention}, the core primitive of the Transformer
(Section~\ref{sec:transformer}).
The dot-product attention of \citet{vaswani2017attention} % [CITE: Vaswani et al. 2017, Attention Is All You Need]
replaces the additive score~\eqref{eq:alignment-score} with a scaled inner product,
reducing cost from $O(Td_a)$ to $O(Td_k)$.

\end{deepdive}

\section{The Transformer Architecture}
\label{sec:transformer}

\begin{deepdive}

The Transformer, introduced by \citet{vaswani2017attention}, % [CITE: Vaswani et al. 2017, Attention Is All You Need]
dispenses with recurrence and relies exclusively on attention to model dependencies.
Three major advantages motivate it for large-scale financial NLP\@:
(i) all token pairs are connected in $O(1)$ layers, eliminating the
vanishing-gradient bottleneck;
(ii) the attention computation is highly parallelisable;
and (iii) learned attention patterns are interpretable.

% ---
\subsection{Self-Attention (Mathematics)}
\label{sec:transformer-selfattn}

\begin{definition}[Scaled Dot-Product Attention]
\label{def:scaled-dot-product-attn}
Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ be the $n$-token input matrix.
Given projection matrices
$W^Q, W^K \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_k}$ and
$W^V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_v}$,
define
\begin{equation}
  Q = X W^Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k},\quad
  K = X W^K \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k},\quad
  V = X W^V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_v}.
  \label{eq:qkv}
\end{equation}
The \emph{scaled dot-product attention} output is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Attention}(Q, K, V)
  = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V
  \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_v},
  \label{eq:scaled-dot-product}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{softmax}$ is applied row-wise.
\end{definition}

$QK^{\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$; entry $(i,j)$ is
$\mathbf{q}_i^{\top}\mathbf{k}_j$.
Row-wise softmax gives the attention matrix
$A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ with $A_{i,j} > 0$ and $\sum_j A_{i,j} = 1$
(Proposition~\ref{thm:attention-prob}).
The output $AV$ represents token $i$ as $\sum_j A_{i,j}\mathbf{v}_j$, a convex
combination of value vectors.

\begin{proposition}[$\sqrt{d_k}$ Scaling Stabilises Softmax Gradients]
\label{thm:sqrt-dk-scaling}
Suppose query and key components are i.i.d.\ mean-zero unit-variance.
Then
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{q}_i^{\top} \mathbf{k}_j) = d_k,
  \label{eq:dot-product-var}
\end{equation}
so dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ normalises the pre-softmax variance to one.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\mathbf{q}_i^{\top}\mathbf{k}_j = \sum_{\ell=1}^{d_k} q_\ell k_\ell$
with components mutually independent, $\mathrm{Var}(q_\ell) = \mathrm{Var}(k_\ell) = 1$.
Since $\mathbb{E}[q_\ell k_\ell] = 0$ and
$\mathbb{E}[(q_\ell k_\ell)^2] = \mathbb{E}[q_\ell^2]\mathbb{E}[k_\ell^2] = 1$,
each term has unit variance, so
$\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{q}_i^{\top}\mathbf{k}_j) = d_k$.
For large $d_k$ the logits concentrate near $\pm\sqrt{d_k}$, pushing softmax into
its saturated regime where
$\partial\sigma(z)/\partial z = \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z)) \approx 0$.
Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ normalises variance to $1$, restoring gradient flow.
\end{proof}

In decoder-only models, token $i$ must not attend to $j > i$.
This is enforced by
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{S}_{ij} = \begin{cases}
    \mathbf{q}_i^{\top}\mathbf{k}_j/\sqrt{d_k} & j \leq i,\\
    -\infty & j > i,
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:causal-mask}
\end{equation}
so that $\mathrm{softmax}(\tilde{S}_{i,:})$ assigns zero weight to future positions.

% ---
\subsection{Multi-Head Attention}
\label{sec:transformer-mha}

\begin{definition}[Multi-Head Attention]
\label{def:mha}
Let $W^Q_i, W^K_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_k}$,
$W^V_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_v}$ for $i = 1,\ldots,h$,
and $W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{h d_v \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$.
Define $\mathrm{head}_i = \mathrm{Attention}(XW^Q_i, XW^K_i, XW^V_i)$ and
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MultiHead}(X)
  = \mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\ldots,\mathrm{head}_h)\,W^O
  \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}.
  \label{eq:multihead}
\end{equation}
In the original Transformer: $h = 8$, $d_{\mathrm{model}} = 512$, $d_k = d_v = 64$.
\end{definition}

Summing $O(n^2 d_k)$ per head over $h$ heads gives $O(n^2 d_{\mathrm{model}})$.
For $n = 4{,}096$ tokens, the attention matrix requires $\approx 134\,\mathrm{M}$
values per layer, motivating efficient variants such as FlashAttention
\cite{dao2022flashattention}. % [CITE: Dao et al. 2022, FlashAttention: Fast and Memory-Efficient Exact Attention with IO-Awareness]

Probing studies \cite{clark2019bert} % [CITE: Clark et al. 2019, What Does BERT Look at? An Analysis of BERT's Attention]
show lower-layer heads capture syntactic dependency relations (subject--verb
agreement) and deeper heads capture semantic associations (co-reference, entity
types). In financial models, heads specialise in temporal relations
(``last quarter'' $\to$ figures), entity linking, and negation scope.

\begin{remark}[Connection to the Kernel Trick]
\label{rem:kernel-trick}
The attention output for token $i$ can be written
$\sum_j k(\mathbf{q}_i,\mathbf{k}_j)\mathbf{v}_j / Z_i$,
where $k(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{k}) = \exp(\mathbf{q}^\top\mathbf{k}/\sqrt{d_k})$
is an exponential kernel and $Z_i$ is the partition function.
The kernel analogy \cite{tsai2019transformer} % [CITE: Tsai et al. 2019, Transformer Dissection: A Unified Understanding of Transformer's Attention via the Lens of Kernel]
motivates random-feature approximations (Performers, Random Feature Attention) that
reduce the $O(n^2)$ complexity to $O(n)$.
\end{remark}

% ---
\subsection{Positional Encoding}
\label{sec:transformer-pos}

The attention operation~\eqref{eq:scaled-dot-product} is permutation-equivariant.
Positional information must therefore be injected explicitly.

\begin{definition}[Sinusoidal Positional Encoding]
\label{def:positional-encoding}
For position $\mathrm{pos} \in \{0,\ldots,n-1\}$ and index
$i \in \{0,\ldots,d_{\mathrm{model}}/2 - 1\}$:
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},\,2i)}
    &= \sin\!\left(\tfrac{\mathrm{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_{\mathrm{model}}}}\right),
  \label{eq:pe-sin}\\
  \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},\,2i+1)}
    &= \cos\!\left(\tfrac{\mathrm{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_{\mathrm{model}}}}\right).
  \label{eq:pe-cos}
\end{align}
The positionally-augmented input is
$\tilde{X}_{\mathrm{pos},:} = X_{\mathrm{pos},:} + \mathrm{PE}_{\mathrm{pos},:}$.
\end{definition}

Let $\omega_i = 10000^{-2i/d_{\mathrm{model}}}$.
For small $i$, $\omega_i \approx 1$ and the encoding oscillates rapidly (fine
position); for large $i$, $\omega_i \approx 0$ (coarse position).
This multi-scale design is analogous to a Fourier basis.

\begin{proposition}[Relative-Position Linearity of Sinusoidal Encoding]
\label{thm:pe-relative}
For any fixed offset $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ and index $i$,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos}+k,\,2i)} \\
    \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos}+k,\,2i+1)}
  \end{pmatrix}
  = R(\omega_i k)
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},\,2i)} \\
    \mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},\,2i+1)}
  \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:pe-rotation}
\end{equation}
where $R(\theta) = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta
& \cos\theta \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ depends only on $k$, not on $\mathrm{pos}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Set $s = \sin(\omega_i\,\mathrm{pos})$, $c = \cos(\omega_i\,\mathrm{pos})$.
By the angle-addition identities:
\begin{align*}
  \sin(\omega_i(\mathrm{pos}+k)) &= s\cos(\omega_i k) + c\sin(\omega_i k),\\
  \cos(\omega_i(\mathrm{pos}+k)) &= c\cos(\omega_i k) - s\sin(\omega_i k).
\end{align*}
In matrix form this is
$R(\omega_i k)[\mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},2i)},\mathrm{PE}_{(\mathrm{pos},2i+1)}]^\top$,
where $R(\omega_i k)$ depends only on $k$ and $\omega_i$, not $\mathrm{pos}$.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{thm:pe-relative} implies
$\mathrm{PE}_{\mathrm{pos}}^\top\mathrm{PE}_{\mathrm{pos}'}$ depends only on
$|\mathrm{pos}-\mathrm{pos}'|$, making relative position linearly decodable.
This motivated relative positional encodings \cite{shaw2018relative} % [CITE: Shaw et al. 2018, Self-Attention with Relative Position Representations]
and Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) \cite{su2024rope}, % [CITE: Su et al. 2024, RoFormer: Enhanced Transformer with Rotary Position Embedding]
which embed the rotation directly into query--key dot products.

\subsection{Encoder--Decoder Structure}
\label{sec:transformer-encdec}

The encoder layer transforms its input through self-attention followed by a feed-forward network. Let $X^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ be the layer-$\ell$ input.
With residual connections \cite{he2016resnet} % [CITE: He et al. 2016, Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition]
and Layer Normalisation \cite{ba2016layernorm}: % [CITE: Ba et al. 2016, Layer Normalization]
\begin{align}
  Z^{(\ell)}   &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
    X^{(\ell)} +
    \mathrm{MultiHead}(X^{(\ell)}, X^{(\ell)}, X^{(\ell)})\right),
  \label{eq:enc-layer-z}\\
  X^{(\ell+1)} &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
    Z^{(\ell)} + \mathrm{FFN}(Z^{(\ell)})\right),
  \label{eq:enc-layer-out}
\end{align}
with
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{FFN}(\mathbf{z})
  = \max(0,\, \mathbf{z} W_1 + \mathbf{b}_1)\,W_2 + \mathbf{b}_2,
  \label{eq:ffn}
\end{equation}
where $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times d_{\mathrm{ff}}}$,
$W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{ff}} \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$,
$d_{\mathrm{ff}} = 4\,d_{\mathrm{model}}$.
The $\max(0,\cdot)$ is ReLU; later variants use GELU \cite{hendrycks2016gelu}. % [CITE: Hendrycks & Gimpel 2016, Gaussian Error Linear Units]

Residual connections ensure direct gradient pathways and LayerNorm stabilises
activations across the $d_{\mathrm{model}}$ dimension.

The decoder layer consists of three sub-layers: (i) masked self-attention
(mask~\eqref{eq:causal-mask}), (ii) \emph{cross-attention} with encoder output, and
(iii) FFN.
With $Y^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ and
$H = X^{(L_{\mathrm{enc}})}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CrossAttn}(Y^{(\ell)}, H)
  = \mathrm{MultiHead}\!\left(Y^{(\ell)} W^Q,\; H W^K,\; H W^V\right),
  \label{eq:cross-attn}
\end{equation}
where $Y^{(\ell)}$ provides queries and $H$ provides keys and values.
This is the direct parallel of the Bahdanau context vector $\mathbf{c}_t$
from~\eqref{eq:context-vector}, computed in a single matrix operation.

\begin{remark}
The Transformer encoder--decoder consists of: (i) a shared token embedding and
positional encoding (Figure~\ref{fig:ch02-illustration} illustrates the sinusoidal
encoding); (ii) $N$ stacked encoder layers, each with multi-head self-attention
and a position-wise feed-forward network; (iii) $N$ stacked decoder layers, each
with masked self-attention, cross-attention over the encoder output, and a
feed-forward network; and (iv) a linear projection to vocabulary logits followed
by softmax.  The base model uses $L = 6$ layers; the large model uses $L = 24$.
Modern LLMs use $L = 32$--$96$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Pre-training, Fine-tuning, and BERT vs.\ GPT}
\label{sec:transformer-pretrain}

\begin{definition}[Pre-training]
\label{def:pretraining}
\emph{Pre-training} is the first phase: training a language model on a large
unlabelled corpus using a self-supervised objective whose labels are derived from
the data itself (predicting masked tokens, or predicting the next token).
The pre-trained weights provide initialisation and inductive bias for fine-tuning.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Fine-tuning]
\label{def:finetuning}
\emph{Fine-tuning} is the second phase: updating pre-trained parameters on a smaller
labelled dataset $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{task}}$ for a specific downstream task.
Pre-training substantially reduces labelled-data and compute requirements.
\end{definition}

\subsubsection{Masked Language Modelling: BERT}

BERT \cite{devlin2019} % [CITE: Devlin et al. 2019, BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding]
(Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) is an encoder-only
Transformer trained on two self-supervised objectives.

The Masked Language Model (MLM) objective selects $15\%$ of input tokens; $80\%$ replaced with \texttt{[MASK]},
$10\%$ with a random token, and $10\%$ left unchanged.
The model predicts original tokens at masked positions:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MLM}}(\theta)
  = -\sum_{i \in \mathcal{M}}
    \log p_\theta\!\left(w_i \mid \mathbf{x}_{\backslash \mathcal{M}}\right),
  \label{eq:mlm-loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{M}$ is the set of masked positions and
$\mathbf{x}_{\backslash\mathcal{M}}$ the unmasked context.
Bidirectional attention means both left and right context inform each prediction.

The Next Sentence Prediction (NSP) objective uses a \texttt{[CLS]} binary head to predict whether sentence $B$ follows sentence $A$.
\citet{liu2019roberta} % [CITE: Liu et al. 2019, RoBERTa: A Robustly Optimized BERT Pretraining Approach]
later showed NSP is unhelpful and removed it in RoBERTa.

\subsubsection{Causal Language Modelling: GPT}

GPT \cite{radford2018gpt} % [CITE: Radford et al. 2018, Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training]
and successors (GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4) are decoder-only models trained on the causal
language modelling (CLM) objective:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CLM}}(\theta)
  = -\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(w_t \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{t-1}).
  \label{eq:clm-loss}
\end{equation}
The causal mask~\eqref{eq:causal-mask} makes this a proper autoregressive density
model, naturally supporting text generation.

\subsubsection{Encoder-Only vs.\ Decoder-Only: Task Suitability}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Encoder-only models (BERT-family) use bidirectional attention
        conditioned on the full input, making them ideal for
        classification (sentiment, credit rating), token-level
        extraction (NER, financial metric extraction), and semantic
        similarity.

  \item Decoder-only models (GPT-family) are the natural generative
        engine for summarisation, report drafting, and instruction
        following \cite{ouyang2022training}. % [CITE: Ouyang et al. 2022, Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback]
        Unidirectional attention is suboptimal for tasks requiring
        full-context understanding.

  \item Encoder--decoder models (T5, BART) are well suited to
        sequence-to-sequence tasks: abstractive summarisation of
        earnings calls and multi-document QA \cite{lewis2020bart}. % [CITE: Lewis et al. 2020, BART: Denoising Sequence-to-Sequence Pre-training for Natural Language Generation, Translation, and Comprehension]
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Domain-Adapted Financial Models}

General-purpose models under-perform on financial text due to distinctive vocabulary
(EBITDA, contango, covenants), regulatory legalese, and distribution shift from
web-crawl pre-training data.

\begin{example}[FinBERT: Two Distinct Domain-Adapted Models]
\label{ex:finbert}
The name ``FinBERT'' designates two related but independently trained models.
\citet{araci2019finbert} % [CITE: Araci 2019, FinBERT: Financial Sentiment Analysis with Pre-trained Language Models]
released the first variant: a BERT-base model further pre-trained on Reuters/Bloomberg
news, 10-K filings, and earnings call transcripts.
On the Financial PhraseBank dataset \cite{malo2014phrasebank} % [CITE: Malo et al. 2014, Good Debt or Bad Debt: Detecting Semantic Orientations in Economic Texts]
(all-agree split), this model achieves over $97\%$ accuracy on financial phrase
sentiment classification, outperforming general BERT-base by approximately 8
percentage points.  A second, independently trained model by \citet{huang2023finbert}
fine-tunes BERT on analyst report sentences labelled by domain experts, achieving
state-of-the-art performance on three financial sentiment benchmarks and demonstrating
that contextual embeddings substantially outperform the Loughran--McDonald dictionary
and classical machine-learning classifiers on sentences that those methods
systematically mislabel as neutral.  These two models share the FinBERT name but
differ in pre-training corpus, fine-tuning labels, and intended use case, and should
not be conflated when reporting benchmark results.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[BloombergGPT]
\label{ex:bloomberggpt}
\citet{wu2023bloomberggpt} % [CITE: Wu et al. 2023, BloombergGPT: A Large Language Model for Finance]
trained BloombergGPT, a 50-billion-parameter decoder-only model, on $363$ billion
tokens from Bloomberg financial archives plus $345$ billion general tokens.
The mixed strategy outperformed purely financial and purely general baselines on
ConvFinQA (numerical reasoning over tables) and FiQA (opinion-based QA).
BloombergGPT illustrates that domain adaptation at scale requires both domain
knowledge and general linguistic competence.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
Continued pre-training of an open-source base model (Llama, Mistral) on
$10$--$50$ billion domain tokens, followed by instruction fine-tuning on curated
financial datasets, is currently the most cost-effective domain-adaptation strategy
for practitioners.
\end{remark}

\section{The Modern LLM Landscape}
\label{sec:landscape}

\begin{context}

In November 2022, a chatbot launched with almost no fanfare.  Within sixty days
it had a hundred million users---the fastest consumer adoption of any product in
history.  ChatGPT did not introduce any fundamentally new technology; the
Transformer architecture had existed since 2017, GPT-3 since 2020.  What it
introduced was \emph{interface}: a fluent, patient, conversational layer over a
raw language model that made the underlying capability legible to people who had
never written a line of code.  For finance, the question was not whether this was
impressive.  The question was more specific and more urgent: what, exactly, can
these models do, and what will they get confidently, catastrophically wrong?  The
answer begins with understanding what is actually being deployed.

\subsection{Generative Models vs.\ Reasoning Models}
\label{sec:landscape-genreason}

\begin{definition}[Autoregressive Generative Language Model]
\label{def:autoregressive}
An \emph{autoregressive language model} is a probability distribution over sequences
of tokens.  Given a vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$, the model factorises the joint
probability of a sequence $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ as a product of
conditional probabilities:
\begin{equation}
  P(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) = \prod_{t=1}^{n} P\!\left(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1}\right).
  \label{eq:autoregressive}
\end{equation}
Generation proceeds by sampling one token at a time, conditioning on all previously
generated tokens.  Each forward pass through the network produces a probability
distribution over $\mathcal{V}$; the next token is obtained by drawing from this
distribution (or selecting its mode, as discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:api-concepts}).
\end{definition}

This factorisation is not merely a modelling convenience: it imposes a \emph{causal}
(left-to-right) structure that corresponds naturally to how language and, indeed, how
financial disclosures are read.  An earnings call transcript, a research report, or a
regulatory filing all unfold sequentially, and the meaning of a phrase frequently
depends on all that precedes it.

Standard autoregressive generation produces output token by token in a \emph{single
chain of forward passes}: given a prompt of $L$ tokens, the model runs $L$ attention
computations in parallel (using a KV-cache for efficiency) to generate each successive
output token.  For many financial NLP tasks---sentiment labelling, named-entity
recognition, brief summarisation---this single-pass regime is entirely adequate.

However, it is ill-suited to tasks requiring multi-step deliberation: valuing a firm
under a discounted-cash-flow (DCF) framework from a noisy earnings call, reasoning
about whether a merger satisfies regulatory criteria across three jurisdictions, or
constructing a quantitative argument from a numerical table embedded in a 10-K filing.
Such tasks require the model to \emph{plan}, backtrack, and verify intermediate
results before committing to a final answer.  This motivated a second paradigm.

\begin{definition}[Reasoning (Extended-Thinking) Language Model]
\label{def:reasoning}
A \emph{reasoning model} is an autoregressive language model trained (typically via
reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards) to generate a structured
\emph{chain-of-thought} (CoT) before producing its final answer.  Formally, the
model samples a latent reasoning trace $\mathbf{r} = (r_1, \ldots, r_m)$ prior to
the answer tokens $\mathbf{a}$:
\begin{equation}
  P(\mathbf{a} \mid \mathbf{x})
  = \sum_{\mathbf{r}} P(\mathbf{a} \mid \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{r})\,
    P(\mathbf{r} \mid \mathbf{x}).
  \label{eq:cot}
\end{equation}
The trace $\mathbf{r}$ may be exposed to the user (as in OpenAI o1/o3 and
DeepSeek-R1) or kept internal (as in the \emph{extended thinking} mode of
Claude 3.7/4.x).  In either case, the effective compute invested per query is
substantially larger than in standard autoregressive generation.

\emph{Note on marginalisation.}  Equation~\eqref{eq:cot} shows \emph{exact}
marginalisation over all possible reasoning traces.  In practice the model
draws a single sample $\hat{\mathbf{r}} \sim P(\mathbf{r} \mid \mathbf{x})$,
so inference corresponds to a Monte Carlo approximation
$P(\mathbf{a} \mid \mathbf{x}) \approx P(\mathbf{a} \mid \mathbf{x}, \hat{\mathbf{r}})$
with one sample.  Self-consistency decoding \citep{wang2022selfconsistency} uses
multiple samples and majority voting to approximate the full marginal.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The distinction in Definition~\ref{def:reasoning} is one of \emph{training
objective} and \emph{inference regime}, not of architecture.  Both paradigms use the
same decoder-only Transformer building block.  What differs is that reasoning models
are rewarded for producing correct final answers, with the chain-of-thought providing
a scratch-pad that can represent thousands of intermediate tokens.  This has been
shown to dramatically improve performance on mathematical proofs, competitive
programming, and, increasingly, on quantitative finance tasks such as numerical
reasoning over financial statements.  It does not, however, eliminate hallucination
(see Section~\ref{sec:hallucinations}).
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Merger Valuation from an Earnings Call]
\label{ex:merger-valuation}
Consider the following excerpt from an earnings call (paraphrased for illustration):

\begin{quote}
``We are acquiring TechCo for \$4.2 billion.  TechCo's trailing twelve-month EBITDA
is \$310 million, and we expect \$150 million in synergies within three years.  Our
current weighted average cost of capital is 8.5\%.''
\end{quote}

A standard generative model (GPT-4o, temperature $= 0$) reads the excerpt and
directly outputs a one-paragraph assessment, typically noting that the EV/EBITDA
multiple is approximately $13.5\times$ and characterising the deal as
\emph{``strategically sound given the synergy guidance.''}  This response is often
plausible but does not verify whether the synergy NPV actually justifies the purchase
price premium.

A reasoning model (o3, DeepSeek-R1) approaches the same input differently.  It first
computes the implied enterprise value, then discounts the synergy stream at 8.5\% over
three years (approximated as a growing annuity), compares this to the purchase premium
over a benchmarked stand-alone valuation, and checks internal consistency before
concluding that the deal is modestly value-accretive under the stated assumptions but
sensitive to the synergy realisation timeline.  The reasoning trace makes each step
auditable.

This contrast recurs throughout the book.  For tasks with a verifiable numerical or
logical answer, reasoning models offer a meaningful and measurable advantage.  For
primarily generative tasks (summarisation, translation, drafting), the additional
latency and cost of extended thinking may not be justified.
\end{example}

The most capable models (GPT-4o, Claude 4, Gemini 1.5 Pro) are proprietary: their

This raises important issues for financial practitioners.  First, there is a
\emph{data-privacy} concern: sending confidential earnings call transcripts, trading

compliance policies, GDPR obligations, or NDA terms.  Second, there is an

Open-weight models such as Llama~3 \citep{touvron2023llama} (Meta, released under a community licence) address
both concerns: they can be deployed on-premises, fine-tuned on proprietary data
without data leaving the organisation, and their inference behaviour can be exactly
reproduced given the same random seed.  The trade-off is that the largest open models
require substantial GPU infrastructure (a 405-billion-parameter model at 16-bit
precision requires approximately 810~GB of GPU memory).

BloombergGPT~\cite{wu2023bloomberggpt} % [CITE: wu2023bloomberggpt Wu et al. 2023]
was trained on 363 billion financial tokens (news, filings, analyst reports) alongside
345 billion general-domain tokens, and represented the first large-scale demonstration
that domain-specific pre-training on financial text was feasible at scale.

Subsequent benchmarks revealed a nuanced picture.  On tasks requiring deep financial
\emph{knowledge} (entity recognition in financial news, financial sentiment on
FinancialPhraseBank), BloombergGPT outperforms same-size general-purpose baselines.
But general-purpose models at significantly larger scale---GPT-4 or Claude 3---often
match or exceed BloombergGPT on the same benchmarks, because scale compensates for
domain specificity.  This is an instance of the \emph{emergent capability} effect:
beyond a threshold of model scale and training compute, models acquire domain knowledge
implicitly, even without targeted domain training \citep{wei2022emergent}.
The practical implication is that \emph{task-specific fine-tuning of a general-purpose
model} often outperforms a domain-pre-trained model of the same size, and
\emph{prompt engineering} applied to a large general model often outperforms
both---a theme developed extensively in Chapter~6.
\citet{niszczota2023gpt} provide an independent calibration of this point: in a
standardised financial literacy test, GPT-4 achieves a near-perfect score of 99\%
against a population of college students, confirming that frontier LLMs have absorbed
substantial financial domain knowledge from their pre-training corpus, without any
task-specific fine-tuning.

Raw pre-trained models (\emph{base} models) are not directly usable as assistants.
\emph{Instruction tuning} fine-tunes a base model on curated (instruction, response)
pairs, teaching the model to follow natural-language directives.
\emph{Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback} (RLHF) \cite{ouyang2022training}
% [CITE: ouyang2022training Ouyang et al. 2022, InstructGPT]
further aligns the model with human preferences by training a reward model on pairwise
human rankings and optimising the language model's policy via proximal policy
optimisation (PPO).  The result is the \emph{chat} or \emph{instruct} variant
familiar to practitioners.  For financial NLP, instruction tuning is especially
important: it makes models reliably follow structured output formats (JSON, Markdown
tables) that downstream data pipelines depend on.

\section{Temperature, Sampling, and Controlled Generation}
\label{sec:sampling}

\begin{deepdive}

Autoregressive generation produces text one token at a time.  At each step $t$,
the model computes a vector of \emph{logits}
$\mathbf{z}^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$ and converts them to a
probability distribution from which the next token is drawn.  The mapping from
logits to a distribution, and the sampling rule applied to that distribution,
jointly determine the character of the output.  For financial applications this
choice is not cosmetic: it determines whether the model produces a reliable,
deterministic extraction of numerical facts or a plausible-but-fictitious
narrative.

\subsection{Temperature Scaling}
\label{sec:sampling-temperature}

\begin{definition}[Temperature-Scaled Softmax]
\label{def:temperature}
Let $\mathbf{z} = (z_1,\ldots,z_{|\mathcal{V}|}) \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$
be the logit vector at a given generation step and $\tau > 0$ a scalar
\emph{temperature}.  The temperature-scaled probability distribution is
\begin{equation}
  p_i(\tau) = \frac{\exp(z_i / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^{|\mathcal{V}|} \exp(z_j / \tau)},
  \quad i = 1,\ldots,|\mathcal{V}|.
  \label{eq:temperature-softmax}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Dividing the logits by $\tau$ before the softmax scales the sharpness of the
resulting distribution.  As $\tau \to 0^+$, the distribution concentrates all
mass on the highest-logit token---equivalently, the model selects the single most
probable token at every step.  This limit is called \emph{greedy decoding} and
is maximally deterministic.  At $\tau = 1$ the distribution equals the standard
softmax, representing the model's unmodified beliefs.  As $\tau \to \infty$ the
distribution approaches the discrete uniform: all tokens become equally likely,
and generation becomes essentially random.

The effect on information content is captured precisely by the Shannon entropy of
the distribution.  Let $H(\tau) = -\sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{V}|} p_i(\tau) \log
p_i(\tau)$ denote the entropy at temperature $\tau$.  One can show that $H$ is
strictly increasing in $\tau$ whenever the logits are not all equal: raising
temperature always increases uncertainty.  For practitioners, this means that
lowering $\tau$ below $1$ biases the model toward its most confident completions,
which is desirable for structured data extraction, while raising $\tau$ above $1$
widens the distribution and produces more diverse, sometimes more creative, outputs.

In financial practice, $\tau$ is the primary control knob for the trade-off between
reliability and diversity.  Earnings extraction from an SEC filing benefits from
$\tau \approx 0$ or $\tau \to 0$ (greedy); scenario generation for stress-testing
benefits from $\tau \in [0.7, 1.0]$ so that the model explores plausible but
non-obvious futures.

\subsection{Top-$k$ and Nucleus (Top-$p$) Sampling}
\label{sec:sampling-topk-topp}

Even at moderate temperatures, sampling uniformly from the full vocabulary at each
step can assign meaningful probability mass to tokens that are contextually
incoherent---an artefact of the finite training corpus that occasionally leaves
very low-probability tokens with non-negligible logits.  \emph{Truncation sampling}
methods address this by restricting the sampling pool before each draw.

\begin{definition}[Top-$k$ Sampling]
\label{def:topk}
Given a temperature-scaled distribution $p(\tau)$, top-$k$ sampling retains only
the $k$ tokens with the highest probabilities, renormalises, and samples from the
resulting distribution:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{p}_i(\tau) \propto p_i(\tau) \cdot \mathbf{1}[i \in \mathcal{V}_k],
  \label{eq:topk}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{V}_k$ is the index set of the $k$ largest entries of $p(\tau)$.
\end{definition}

Top-$k$ sampling is simple but has a known weakness: the appropriate value of $k$
varies wildly across contexts.  When the model is highly confident, setting $k=50$
may include many near-zero probability tokens; when the model is genuinely uncertain,
$k=50$ may exclude reasonable alternatives.

\begin{definition}[Nucleus (Top-$p$) Sampling]
\label{def:nucleus}
Nucleus sampling, introduced by \citet{holtzman2020curious}, selects the smallest
set of tokens whose cumulative probability meets or exceeds a threshold $p \in (0,1]$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{V}_p = \arg\min_{S \subseteq \mathcal{V}} \bigl\{ |S| :
    \textstyle\sum_{i \in S} p_i(\tau) \geq p \bigr\},
  \label{eq:topp}
\end{equation}
where the tokens in $S$ are ordered by decreasing probability.
The model then samples from $\mathcal{V}_p$ proportionally to the renormalised original probabilities.
\end{definition}

Nucleus sampling adapts the vocabulary size to the model's confidence at each step:
a sharp distribution yields a small $\mathcal{V}_p$, while a flat distribution
yields a large one.  In practice, $p = 0.9$ or $p = 0.95$ is a common default for
open-ended generation tasks.

\subsection{Beam Search}
\label{sec:sampling-beam}

Greedy decoding is fast but myopic: it selects the locally optimal token at each step
without considering how that choice affects future tokens.  \emph{Beam search} keeps
the $B$ most probable partial sequences at each step and expands each of them, retaining
the $B$ best sequences by total log-probability.

\begin{definition}[Beam Search]
\label{def:beam-search}
Let $B \in \mathbb{N}$ be the \emph{beam width}.  At step $t$, let
$\mathcal{H}_t = \{h_1^{(t)}, \ldots, h_B^{(t)}\}$ be the set of $B$ current
\emph{hypotheses}, where each $h_b^{(t)} = (x_1,\ldots,x_t)$ is a partial sequence
and $\text{score}(h_b^{(t)}) = \sum_{s=1}^t \log p(x_s \mid x_{<s})$ is its
accumulated log-probability.  Beam search expands each hypothesis over all tokens
$v \in \mathcal{V}$, forming $B \cdot |\mathcal{V}|$ candidate continuations, and
retains the top $B$ by score:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H}_{t+1} = \operatorname*{top-}B_{h \in \mathcal{H}_t,\, v \in \mathcal{V}}
    \bigl[ \text{score}(h) + \log p(v \mid h) \bigr].
  \label{eq:beam-search}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Beam search was the dominant decoding strategy for neural machine translation and
abstractive summarisation throughout the late 2010s, and is still used for tasks
where output quality is evaluated by a deterministic metric (ROUGE, BLEU) rather than
human preference.  However, for open-ended generation it tends to produce repetitive,
generic text---a phenomenon known as \emph{beam search degeneration}---and has been
largely replaced by nucleus sampling for conversational and creative tasks.

\end{deepdive}

\subsection{Sampling Strategy Selection for Financial Tasks}
\label{sec:sampling-finance}

\begin{context}

A risk analyst asks an LLM to summarise a credit agreement.  She runs the same
prompt twice and gets two different answers.  Not wrong answers---two reasonable
answers, each emphasising different clauses.  This is not a bug; it is a feature
she did not ask for.  Temperature is the dial that governs how much the model
wanders from its most probable completion.  Set it too high and the model
fabricates; set it too low and it becomes a repetition engine, parroting training
data verbatim.  The right setting is not a universal constant: it depends on what
you need the model to do, and on the consequences if it gets it wrong.
Table~\ref{tab:sampling-strategies} translates this principle into concrete
recommendations for five common financial NLP tasks.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Recommended sampling strategies for financial NLP tasks.}
\label{tab:sampling-strategies}
\begin{tabular}{lllp{5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Task} & \textbf{Temperature} & \textbf{Sampling} & \textbf{Rationale} \\
\midrule
Structured data extraction
  (earnings figures, covenants)
  & $\tau = 0$ (greedy)
  & ---
  & Determinism is essential; any deviation from the source document is an error. \\[4pt]
Sentiment labelling
  & $\tau \approx 0.1$
  & greedy or top-$k$ ($k=5$)
  & Low variance; multiple samples for self-consistency if needed. \\[4pt]

  & $\tau \approx 0.3$--$0.5$
  & top-$p = 0.9$
  & Some lexical diversity is desirable; factual accuracy still critical. \\[4pt]

  & $\tau \approx 0.1$

  & Low temperature + retrieved context minimises hallucination risk. \\[4pt]

A pragmatic rule of thumb: if the output will be compared to ground-truth values
or fed into a downstream system that expects a specific schema, use $\tau = 0$ and
add retrieval grounding (see Section~\ref{sec:rag}).  If the output will be read
by a human analyst who values novelty and breadth, use $\tau \in [0.5, 1.0]$ with
nucleus sampling.  In either case, running multiple samples and aggregating via
self-consistency (Section~\ref{sec:hallucinations-mitigation}) improves reliability
at modest additional cost.

\end{context}

\section{Structured Generation}
\label{sec:structured-gen}

\begin{context}

A bank's model risk team has a problem.  Their junior analysts are using an LLM
to extract covenants from loan agreements, and the model keeps producing prose---
long, fluent, accurate-sounding prose---when what the downstream system needs is
a JSON object with three fields.  The analysts copy-paste.  The pipeline breaks.
Escalation follows.  The answer is not a better prompt.  The answer is
\emph{constrained decoding}: making it structurally impossible for the model to
output anything that does not conform to a predefined schema.  Financial
applications rarely need the model to be creative.  They need it to be
\emph{precise}.

\subsection{Constrained Decoding}
\label{sec:structured-gen-constrained}

The central idea of constrained decoding is to apply a \emph{validity mask} at each
generation step, blocking any token that would render the partial output inconsistent
with the target schema.  This mask is computed from the grammar or schema together
with the tokens generated so far, and is applied before the softmax so that invalid
tokens receive $-\infty$ log-probability and contribute zero probability mass.

\begin{definition}[Token-Level Validity Mask]
\label{def:validity-mask}
Given a target grammar $\mathcal{G}$ and partial output $x_{<t}$, the
\emph{validity mask} $M_t \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$ is defined by
\begin{equation}
  M_t[i] = \mathbf{1}\bigl[\text{token } i \text{ can extend } x_{<t}
            \text{ to a string in } \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{G})\bigr],
  \label{eq:validity-mask}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{G})$ is the language defined by $\mathcal{G}$.
The masked logits are $\tilde{z}_i = z_i + \log M_t[i]$, so invalid tokens
receive $-\infty$ before softmax.
\end{definition}

This is not a post-hoc filter.  Applying the mask pre-softmax means that the model's
sampling distribution is renormalised over valid tokens only, so it always produces
a syntactically valid continuation in a single forward pass.  Parsing the partial
output against the grammar to compute $M_t$ efficiently is the core implementation
challenge; in practice this is done with incremental parsers (e.g.\ Earley or
pushdown automata) that maintain parse state across tokens in $O(|\mathcal{V}|)$
per step.

\subsection{JSON Mode and Tool Use}
\label{sec:structured-gen-json}

Modern LLM APIs expose structured generation without requiring the user to manage
grammars explicitly.  Anthropic's \emph{tool use} (also called function calling)
instructs the model to produce a JSON object conforming to a JSON Schema, and the
API guarantees that the returned object is valid.  Internally, this is implemented
using constrained decoding against the schema-derived grammar.  The approach
substantially simplifies application code: rather than parsing free-text and hoping
the model produced the right fields, the developer declares a Pydantic model, converts
it to a JSON Schema, passes that schema as a tool definition, and receives a typed
Python object in return.

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{python}
import anthropic
from pydantic import BaseModel
from typing import Optional

class EarningsReport(BaseModel):
    revenue_bn: Optional[float] = None
    net_income_bn: Optional[float] = None
    eps: Optional[float] = None
    guidance_bn: Optional[float] = None

client = anthropic.Anthropic()
message = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-sonnet-4-6",
    max_tokens=256,
    tools=[{
        "name": "record_earnings",
        "description": "Record structured earnings figures from text.",
        "input_schema": EarningsReport.model_json_schema(),
    }],
    tool_choice={"type": "tool", "name": "record_earnings"},
    messages=[{"role": "user",
               "content": ("Extract earnings: Revenue $4.2B, net income $820M, "
                           "EPS $1.34, guidance raised to $5.1B.")}],
)
result = EarningsReport(**message.content[0].input)
print(result)
# EarningsReport(revenue_bn=4.2, net_income_bn=0.82, eps=1.34, guidance_bn=5.1)
\end{minted}
\caption{Structured earnings extraction with the Anthropic API and Pydantic.}
\end{listing}

Note the \texttt{tool\_choice} parameter: setting it to a specific tool name forces the
model to invoke that tool rather than choosing between text response and tool call.
This is essential in production pipelines where the absence of a structured response
should be an error, not a silent degradation to free text.

\subsection{Grammar-Based Generation}
\label{sec:structured-gen-grammar}

For outputs that do not map cleanly to flat JSON---nested financial hierarchies,
XBRL-like taxonomies, or domain-specific interchange formats---grammar-based generation
offers more expressive control.  The \texttt{outlines} library \citep{willard2023efficient}
implements constrained decoding against Extended Backus--Naur Form (EBNF) grammars
for any Hugging Face model.  The GBNF format supported by \texttt{llama.cpp} extends
this to locally deployed quantised models.

In financial reporting, SEC EDGAR's inline XBRL specification and MiFID~II's
transaction reporting format both impose schemas far richer than flat JSON.  A
constrained decoder that encodes those schemas as grammars can serve as a zero-shot
structured extraction layer without any task-specific fine-tuning.

The listing below demonstrates EBNF-constrained generation with \texttt{outlines}
for a minimal financial reporting record containing a company ticker, a numeric
earnings figure, and a currency code.

\begin{listing}[htbp]
\begin{minted}{python}
import outlines
import outlines.models as models
import outlines.samplers as samplers

# EBNF grammar for a minimal earnings record
EARNINGS_GRAMMAR = r"""
start: "{" ticker "," eps "," currency "}"
ticker : "\"" /[A-Z]{1,5}/ "\""
eps    : /[-]?[0-9]+(\.[0-9]{1,4})?/
currency : "\"" ("USD" | "EUR" | "GBP" | "JPY") "\""
"""

model = models.transformers("mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1", device="cuda")
generator = outlines.generate.cfg(model, EARNINGS_GRAMMAR,
                                   sampler=samplers.greedy())

prompt = ("Extract: 'Apple reported Q3 EPS of $1.26 (USD).'\n"
          "JSON record:")
result = generator(prompt)
# result is guaranteed to match the grammar, e.g. {"AAPL",1.26,"USD"}
print(result)
\end{minted}
\caption{Grammar-constrained generation for financial data extraction using
\texttt{outlines} \citep{willard2023efficient}.  The EBNF grammar restricts
the decoder to outputs that match the schema exactly; no post-processing or
JSON repair is needed.}
\label{lst:ebnf-grammar}
\end{listing}

\subsection{Why Structured Generation Is Critical in Finance}
\label{sec:structured-gen-finance}

Three failure modes of unstructured LLM output are particularly consequential for
financial applications.

The first is numerical hallucination.  When asked to extract earnings figures, an
unconstrained model may output values that are internally consistent with its training
distribution but factually wrong.  A number that looks like a plausible EPS figure
is far more dangerous than an obviously malformed output, because downstream systems
may ingest it without detecting the error.  Constrained decoding does not guarantee
factual accuracy---the model can still produce an incorrect number if the number
tokens are all syntactically valid---but pairing it with temperature $\tau = 0$ and
a retrieval-grounded prompt (Section~\ref{sec:rag}) dramatically reduces the incidence
of fabricated figures.

The second failure mode is silent field omission.  A free-text model asked to extract
a dozen fields from a 10-K will occasionally omit a field without any signal that the
field is missing.  A structured schema with required and optional fields, enforced at
the token level, forces the model to produce a null-valued field rather than silently
skipping it, making the absence detectable and auditable.

The third is unit confusion.  Financial text uses millions (M), billions (B), and
trillions (T) inconsistently, and some documents mix currencies without explicit labels.
A Pydantic model with a canonical unit (e.g.\ all monetary fields in billions of USD)
and explicit validation logic can detect and reject outputs where the scale is
implausible, triggering a re-query or escalation to human review rather than propagating
a scale error into a portfolio management system.

Taken together, these considerations make structured generation not an optional
optimisation but an architectural requirement for any production financial NLP pipeline.

\end{context}

\section{Working with LLMs via API}
\label{sec:api}

\begin{context}

Every LLM workflow that matters in production goes through an API.  The model you
choose, the temperature you set, the number of tokens you send---these are not
academic parameters.  They are engineering decisions with real cost, latency, and
risk consequences.  A 10,000-token prompt sent to a frontier model costs roughly
twenty cents.  Run that prompt a thousand times a day and you have spent
\$73,000 in a year on a single workflow.  This section covers the concepts and
Python code you need to build that workflow correctly from the start.

\subsection{Core Concepts: Tokens, Context Windows, and Sampling}
\label{sec:api-concepts}

\begin{definition}[Token]
\label{def:token}
A \emph{token} is the atomic unit of input and output processed by a language model.
Tokens are produced by a \emph{tokeniser}, a deterministic mapping from strings to
integer sequences.  Modern LLMs use sub-word tokenisation schemes such as Byte-Pair
Encoding (BPE) \cite{sennrich2016bpe} % [CITE: sennrich2016bpe Sennrich et al. 2016]
or WordPiece \cite{wu2016wordpiece}, % [CITE: wu2016wordpiece Wu et al. 2016]
in which the vocabulary consists neither of individual characters nor of whole words,
but of frequently occurring sub-word fragments.
\end{definition}

Byte-Pair Encoding builds the vocabulary iteratively:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Initialise the vocabulary with the set of all individual Unicode characters
    (or bytes) appearing in the training corpus.
  \item Count the frequency of all adjacent symbol pairs in the current tokenised
    corpus.
  \item Merge the most frequent pair into a single new symbol, updating the corpus.
  \item Repeat steps 2--3 until the vocabulary reaches a target size $|V|$
    (typically 32,000--100,000 for LLMs).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{example}[BPE Tokenisation of a Financial Term]
\label{ex:bpe}
Consider the word \texttt{"earnings"}.  A BPE tokeniser with vocabulary size 50,000
(as used by GPT-2 and GPT-3) would likely tokenise this as two sub-words:
\texttt{["earn", "ings"]}.  Rarer financial terms may receive more fragmented
tokenisations; for example, \texttt{"EBITDA"} might tokenise as
\texttt{["EB",\,"IT",\,"DA"]}, and \texttt{"Berkshire"} as
\texttt{["Berk",\,"shire"]}.  As a rule of thumb, one English word corresponds to
approximately 1.3 tokens on average; a typical earnings call transcript of 5,000 words
therefore contains roughly 6,500 tokens.  This conversion factor is used in the cost
analysis in Section~\ref{sec:api-practical}.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Context Window]
\label{def:context-window}
The \emph{context window} (or \emph{context length}) of a language model is the
maximum number of tokens $L_{\max}$ that the model can process in a single forward
pass.  Formally, the attention mechanism in a Transformer of context length $L_{\max}$
can only attend to positions $j \leq \min(t, L_{\max})$ when computing the
representation of position $t$.  Any input exceeding $L_{\max}$ tokens must be
truncated or split across multiple calls.
\end{definition}

Context windows grew rapidly in the 2022--2024 period: GPT-3 offered 4,096 tokens;
GPT-4-turbo extended this to 128,000 tokens; Gemini 1.5 Pro supports 1,000,000
tokens; Claude 3/4 supports up to 200,000 tokens with plans to expand further.  For
financial NLP, the context window determines whether an entire 10-K filing (which may
exceed 100,000 tokens) can be processed in a single call---a question with significant
implications for retrieval-augmented generation architectures (Chapter~5).

The three principal sampling hyperparameters are temperature $\tau$, nucleus threshold
$p$, and top-$k$ cutoff.  Their mathematical definitions and finance-specific selection
guidance appear in Section~\ref{sec:sampling}; here it suffices to note that most API
providers expose temperature and top-$p$ as named parameters, while top-$k$ is less
universally supported.  For financial tasks where reproducibility is paramount---
extracting numerical data from filings, generating compliance summaries---greedy
decoding ($\tau = 0$) is the recommended default, as it is the only deterministic
regime across identical inputs (modulo floating-point non-determinism on GPU hardware).

To make this concrete, suppose a compliance team needs to process $N = 10{,}000$ earnings call transcripts,
each approximately 5,000 words.  At 1.3 tokens per word (see
Example~\ref{ex:bpe}), each transcript is $\approx 6{,}500$ input tokens.  Adding a
500-token system prompt and $\approx 400$ output tokens, and applying
\eqref{eq:cost-model}:

\begin{align}
  \text{cost}_{\text{GPT-4o}}
  &= 10{,}000 \times \left(\frac{7{,}000}{10^6} \times 5.00
    + \frac{400}{10^6} \times 15.00 \right) \nonumber \\
  &= 10{,}000 \times (0.035 + 0.006) \;=\; \$410.
  \label{eq:cost-example}
\end{align}

The same workload on Claude 3 Haiku costs approximately \$20---a twenty-fold
reduction.  This illustrates the core cost-optimisation decision in financial NLP
pipelines: a small, cheap model handles straightforward extraction tasks, while a
large model is reserved for complex reasoning steps.

Several engineering considerations govern the cost-latency trade-off in production pipelines.
\begin{itemize}
  \item For offline workloads (historical transcript processing,
    regulatory filing analysis), maximum parallelism reduces wall-clock
    time without increasing cost.  OpenAI's Batch API offers a 50\%
    price discount in exchange for up to 24 hours of processing
    latency---appropriate for end-of-day compliance runs but unsuitable
    for real-time applications.
  \item For real-time applications (live earnings call summarisation,
    intraday news monitoring), latency is a binding constraint.
    Time-to-first-token for GPT-4o is typically 1--3 seconds; for
    Claude 3 Haiku under 500 ms.  Locally-hosted Llama 3 (8B, single
    A100 GPU) can achieve sub-100 ms first-token latency.
  \item A common two-stage financial NLP pipeline uses a cheap, fast
    model (FinBERT, Claude Haiku) to filter and classify large volumes
    of text, then routes only the high-signal subset to a large model
    for deep analysis.  This can reduce total API cost by 80--95\% on
    typical workloads.
\end{itemize}

\begin{remark}[Prompt Caching]
\label{rem:prompt-caching}
Anthropic's \emph{prompt caching} API allows users to designate a prefix of the
system prompt as cacheable.  Subsequent requests that share this prefix incur a
reduced input-token price (approximately 10\% of the standard rate for cache hits),
reducing costs by up to 90\% in applications with a fixed, long system prompt---such
as a financial analysis assistant with fixed instructions and a reference document.
OpenAI offers analogous automatic prompt caching for context windows exceeding 1,024
tokens.  When designing pipelines that issue many similar requests (e.g., processing
all filings from a given company under the same analytical framework), caching the
shared system prompt is among the highest-ROI optimisations available.
\end{remark}

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:rag}

\begin{context}

An asset manager wants an LLM that can answer questions about their portfolio
companies' recent filings.  They try prompting a frontier model directly.  The
model knows Apple and Microsoft---their 10-Ks are woven into its training
data---but their portfolio includes thirty mid-cap industrials the model has
never encountered.  Every answer about those companies is a confident
hallucination, decorated with plausible-looking numbers.  The solution is not a
bigger model or a better prompt.  The solution is \emph{retrieval-augmented
generation}: instead of asking the model what it remembers, you hand it the
document and ask it to read.

\subsection{The RAG Pipeline}
\label{sec:rag-pipeline}

\begin{definition}[Retrieval-Augmented Generation]
\label{def:rag}
Let $\mathcal{D} = \{d_1,\ldots,d_N\}$ be a corpus of documents (or passages),
$q$ a user query, and $k \in \mathbb{N}$ the number of retrieved passages.
A RAG system operates in three stages:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Retrieval:} compute a relevance score $s(q, d_i)$ for each
        $d_i \in \mathcal{D}$ and return the top-$k$ passages
        $\mathcal{R}(q) = \{d_{(1)},\ldots,d_{(k)}\}$.
  \item \emph{Augmentation:} construct the augmented prompt
        $\tilde{q} = [d_{(1)},\ldots,d_{(k)},\, q]$.
  \item \emph{Generation:} produce the answer $a \sim P_\theta(\,\cdot \mid \tilde{q})$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

RAG transfers the burden of factual accuracy from the model's parameters to the
retrieval index.  This has two important consequences for financial applications.
First, the knowledge base can be updated---by adding new filings to the index---without
any model retraining.  Second, every factual claim in the model's output can be
traced back to a retrieved passage, enabling auditors to verify provenance and detect
hallucination.  For these reasons, RAG has become the dominant architecture for
document question-answering and compliance search in production financial systems.

\subsection{Dense Retrieval and Vector Databases}
\label{sec:rag-dense}

The most widely used relevance function in RAG is cosine similarity between dense
vector representations of the query and each passage.  A bi-encoder model (typically
a fine-tuned transformer such as SBERT, \citet{reimers2019}) maps both
query and passage into the same embedding space; the relevance score between query
$q$ and passage $d_i$ is
\begin{equation}
  s_{\text{dense}}(q, d_i) = \frac{\phi(q) \cdot \phi(d_i)}{\|\phi(q)\| \|\phi(d_i)\|},
  \label{eq:cosine-sim}
\end{equation}
where $\phi : \text{text} \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is the encoder.

Computing \eqref{eq:cosine-sim} exhaustively over a corpus of millions of passages
is infeasible at query time.  In practice, approximate nearest-neighbour (ANN)
search is used.  The FAISS library \citep{johnson2019billion} provides efficient
ANN search via the inverted file index (IVF) and hierarchical navigable small-world
(HNSW) graph algorithms.  Managed vector databases such as Pinecone, Chroma, and
Weaviate build FAISS-like indices behind a REST API and add features such as
metadata filtering, versioning, and access control.

For financial documents, a critical preprocessing decision is how to \emph{chunk}
the source text.  Long filings must be divided into passages of manageable size before
embedding, because an embedding of a full 10-K loses all local context.  A standard
heuristic is to split on paragraph or section boundaries, capping passages at 512
tokens with a 50-token overlap between adjacent chunks so that sentences near a
boundary appear in at least one complete passage.

\subsection{Sparse Retrieval and Hybrid Methods}
\label{sec:rag-sparse}

Dense retrieval excels at semantic similarity but can fail on exact-match queries
that are common in finance: a specific CUSIP number, a precise clause reference such
as ``Section 6.4(b) of the Indenture'', or an exact ticker symbol.  For these cases,
sparse \emph{lexical} retrieval methods outperform dense approaches because they
match on exact token overlap rather than learned semantic proximity.

BM25 \citep{robertson2009probabilistic} is the standard sparse retrieval baseline.
For a query $q = \{t_1, \ldots, t_{|q|}\}$ and document $d$, the BM25 score is
\begin{equation}
  \text{BM25}(q, d) = \sum_{t \in q} \text{IDF}(t) \cdot
  \frac{f(t, d)\,(k_1 + 1)}{f(t, d) + k_1\!\left(1 - b + b\,\frac{|d|}{\text{avgdl}}\right)},
  \label{eq:bm25}
\end{equation}
where $f(t, d)$ is the term frequency of $t$ in $d$, $|d|$ is the document length,
$\text{avgdl}$ is the average document length in the corpus, and $k_1 \approx 1.5$,
$b \approx 0.75$ are smoothing parameters.  The IDF weight penalises terms that
appear in many documents.

Hybrid retrieval combines dense and sparse scores.  A simple but effective fusion
strategy is \emph{reciprocal rank fusion} \citep{cormack2009reciprocal}:
\begin{equation}
  \text{RRF}(d) = \sum_{r \in \{\text{dense},\,\text{sparse}\}}
    \frac{1}{k_{\text{rrf}} + \text{rank}_r(d)},
  \label{eq:rrf}
\end{equation}
where $k_{\text{rrf}} = 60$ is the standard constant and $\text{rank}_r(d)$ is the
position of $d$ in the ranking produced by retriever $r$.  RRF is robust to score
scale differences between the two retrievers and requires no learned weighting parameter.

\subsection{Re-Ranking}
\label{sec:rag-reranking}

A two-stage pipeline that first retrieves a large candidate set and then re-ranks it
with a more expensive model often achieves better precision than a single-stage
retriever.  The re-ranker is a \emph{cross-encoder}: it takes a query-passage pair
as a single input and outputs a scalar relevance score, allowing richer interactions
between query and passage tokens than the bi-encoder architecture.

A standard production setup retrieves the top-100 passages with a fast BM25 or
ANN-based retriever, then re-ranks them with a cross-encoder to obtain the final top-5
context passages.  The ColBERT \citep{khattab2020colbert} late-interaction model
offers a middle ground: it computes token-level embeddings for both query and passage,
scores via a MaxSim operator, and achieves cross-encoder-level accuracy at
bi-encoder-level latency.

\subsection{RAG for Financial Documents}
\label{sec:rag-finance}

The following listing implements a minimal but production-relevant RAG pipeline for
answering questions about a 10-K filing.  It uses FAISS for dense retrieval and
the Anthropic API for generation.  Citation of passage numbers in the system prompt
enables answer verification.

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{python}
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer
import faiss, numpy as np, anthropic

def build_index(passages: list[str]):
    model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")
    embeddings = model.encode(passages, normalize_embeddings=True)
    index = faiss.IndexFlatIP(embeddings.shape[1])
    index.add(embeddings.astype("float32"))
    return index, model

def rag_query(query: str, passages: list[str],
              index, embed_model, k: int = 5) -> str:
    q_emb = embed_model.encode([query], normalize_embeddings=True)
    _, ids = index.search(q_emb.astype("float32"), k)
    context = "\n\n".join(
        f"[{i+1}] {passages[ids[0][i]]}" for i in range(k))
    client = anthropic.Anthropic()
    msg = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-sonnet-4-6",
        max_tokens=512,
        system=("Answer using only the provided passages. "
                "Cite passage numbers as [N]."),
        messages=[{"role": "user",
                   "content": f"{context}\n\nQuestion: {query}"}])
    return msg.content[0].text
\end{minted}
\caption{Minimal RAG pipeline for a 10-K filing.}
\end{listing}

Three features of this design merit emphasis.  First, normalising the embeddings
before adding them to the FAISS \texttt{IndexFlatIP} (inner product) index converts
the inner product to cosine similarity, so the retrieval score is \eqref{eq:cosine-sim}
exactly.  Second, the system prompt constrains the model to answer only from the
provided passages; pairing this with low temperature ($\tau = 0$) makes hallucination
outside the retrieved context extremely unlikely.  Third, requiring the model to cite
passage numbers creates an audit trail that allows a downstream verification step to
check each factual claim against the source passage---a key step in the SelfCheckGPT
and FActScore pipelines described in Section~\ref{sec:hallucinations-detection}.

The difficulty of the financial QA task even with retrieval is highlighted by
\citet{zhang2024financebench}, who introduce FinanceBench, a benchmark of over 10,000
questions grounded in SEC filings.  They find that GPT-4-Turbo with a retrieval system
\emph{incorrectly answers or refuses} 81\% of questions, demonstrating that na\"ive
RAG pipelines are insufficient for high-stakes financial document QA and that more
sophisticated chunking, re-ranking, and verification strategies are necessary.

\end{context}

\section{Knowledge Distillation and Model Compression}
\label{sec:distillation}

\begin{deepdive}

Deploying a 70-billion-parameter model in a latency-sensitive trading system is
impractical.  A single forward pass for a 70B model requires roughly 140~GB of GPU
memory for 16-bit weights alone, and inference latency measured in seconds is
incompatible with real-time order routing or risk monitoring.  \emph{Knowledge
distillation} and its companion techniques---parameter-efficient fine-tuning,
quantisation, and pruning---are the tools that bridge the gap between frontier
model quality and production deployment constraints.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}
\label{sec:distillation-kd}

The key insight of knowledge distillation \citep{hinton2015distilling} is that the
\emph{soft} probability distribution produced by a large \emph{teacher} model over
the full vocabulary contains substantially more information than the hard one-hot
label of the correct token.  Even the near-zero probabilities assigned to incorrect
tokens encode the teacher's implicit similarity structure: the teacher assigns more
probability to semantically related tokens than to unrelated ones, and this
information is invisible in the hard label.

\begin{definition}[Knowledge Distillation]
\label{def:kd}
Let $\mathcal{T}$ be a teacher model with parameters $\theta_T$ and
$\mathcal{S}$ a student model with parameters $\theta_S$, where
$|\theta_S| \ll |\theta_T|$.  Given a training set
$\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, knowledge distillation minimises
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} = \alpha \,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathbf{p}_S, y)
    + (1-\alpha)\, \tau^2 \,
      \mathrm{KL}\!\left(\mathbf{p}_T^{(\tau)} \,\big\|\, \mathbf{p}_S^{(\tau)}\right),
  \label{eq:kd-loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{p}_T^{(\tau)}$ and $\mathbf{p}_S^{(\tau)}$ are the temperature-scaled
softmax distributions (Definition~\ref{def:temperature}) of the teacher and student,
$\alpha \in [0,1]$ balances the hard-label cross-entropy and the soft-label KL
divergence, and $\tau^2$ re-scales the gradient magnitude (since temperature scaling
reduces gradient magnitudes by $1/\tau^2$, the factor restores them to the
scale of the cross-entropy term).
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Forward versus Reverse KL in Distillation]
\label{rem:kd-kl-direction}
The distillation loss uses the \emph{forward} KL, $\mathrm{KL}(p_T^{(\tau)} \| p_S^{(\tau)})$,
rather than the reverse KL, $\mathrm{KL}(p_S^{(\tau)} \| p_T^{(\tau)})$.  Since
$\mathrm{KL}(p_T \| p_S) = H(p_T, p_S) - H(p_T)$ and $H(p_T)$ does not depend
on the student parameters $\theta_S$, minimising the forward KL with respect to
$\theta_S$ is equivalent to minimising the cross-entropy $H(p_T, p_S)$.  The
forward KL is \emph{mean-seeking}: the student is penalised for assigning low
probability to any token the teacher assigns non-negligible probability, encouraging
broad coverage of the teacher's distribution.  The reverse KL is \emph{mode-seeking}
and would collapse the student toward the teacher's dominant mode, an undesirable
property when the teacher assigns meaningful probability mass to multiple plausible
next tokens.
\end{remark}

In the financial domain, a common distillation scenario is the following: a large
general-purpose model ($\text{e.g.}$, 70B parameters) is used as teacher on a curated
dataset of annotated financial documents; a smaller student model (7B or 13B
parameters) is trained on the same data with the additional KL term in
\eqref{eq:kd-loss}.  The student inherits the teacher's implicit knowledge of
financial language without requiring the teacher's computational footprint at
inference time.

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning: LoRA}
\label{sec:distillation-lora}

Full fine-tuning updates all $|\theta|$ parameters of a model on task-specific data.
For a 7B-parameter model this requires several hundred gigabytes of optimizer state,
which is infeasible on most academic or practitioner hardware.
\emph{Low-rank adaptation} (LoRA) \citep{hu2022lora} offers a solution:
instead of updating the full weight matrix, it injects a pair of low-rank matrices
whose product approximates the update.

\begin{definition}[Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)]
\label{def:lora}
For a pre-trained weight matrix $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$, LoRA
parameterises the weight update as
\begin{equation}
  W = W_0 + \Delta W = W_0 + BA,
  \label{eq:lora}
\end{equation}
where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$, $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$,
and $r \ll \min(d, k)$ is the \emph{rank}.  During training, $W_0$ is frozen
and only $A$ and $B$ are updated.  The number of trainable parameters
reduces from $dk$ to $r(d + k)$.
\end{definition}

For a typical attention layer with $d = k = 4096$ and $r = 16$, LoRA reduces the
trainable parameters from $16.8\text{M}$ to $131\text{K}$---a reduction by a factor
of $128$.  Because $W_0$ is frozen, its weights do not need to be stored in optimizer
state; only the adapter matrices $A$ and $B$ accumulate gradients.  This makes
fine-tuning feasible on a single consumer GPU.

QLoRA \citep{dettmers2023qlora} extends this further: the frozen weights $W_0$ are
stored in 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) quantisation, reducing memory for a 7B model from
roughly 14~GB (BF16) to approximately 4~GB, while the LoRA adapters are kept in
BF16 for training stability.  A finance team with a single A100~GPU can fine-tune a
7B model on a proprietary corpus of 10-K filings in under a day using QLoRA.

\subsection{Quantisation}
\label{sec:distillation-quant}

Quantisation reduces the numerical precision of model weights from 32- or 16-bit
floating point to lower-bit integer formats.  Post-training quantisation (PTQ) applies
quantisation after training without any gradient updates, making it applicable to
models one does not own (e.g., weights downloaded from a model hub).

GPTQ \citep{frantar2022gptq} is a layer-wise second-order PTQ method.  It quantises
one layer at a time by solving a weighted least-squares problem that minimises the
quantisation error propagated through subsequent layers.  In practice, GPTQ produces
INT4 models that match FP16 perplexity on most tasks with negligible accuracy loss.

AWQ (Activation-aware Weight Quantisation, \citet{lin2023awq}) improves on GPTQ
by identifying the 1\% of weight channels that correspond to high-activation inputs
and protecting them from aggressive quantisation.  The intuition is that quantisation
error matters most when the activation magnitude is large, so protecting those
channels at higher precision yields a better accuracy-compression trade-off.

The practical implication for financial practitioners is that a 70B frontier model
can be deployed at INT4 on two consumer GPUs (80~GB combined VRAM) with negligible
accuracy degradation on financial NLP benchmarks.  This changes the deployment
economics significantly: tasks that previously required cloud API calls---with
associated data-privacy and latency concerns---can be run on-premises on standard
data-centre hardware.

\subsection{Model Pruning}
\label{sec:distillation-pruning}

Pruning removes weights or structural components from a trained model.
\emph{Unstructured} pruning zeroes individual weights below a magnitude threshold;
it achieves high compression ratios but requires specialised sparse-matrix hardware
to realise the theoretical speedup.  \emph{Structured} pruning removes entire
attention heads, feed-forward layers, or even full transformer blocks, yielding dense
models that run efficiently on standard hardware.

The \emph{lottery ticket hypothesis} \citep{frankle2019lottery} conjectures that
dense networks contain sparse subnetworks (``winning tickets'') that can be trained in
isolation to full accuracy.  While this finding motivates the study of pruning, in
practice lottery-ticket-based methods require costly iterative magnitude pruning
schedules and have not yet scaled reliably to models of the size relevant for financial
NLP.  For most practitioners, quantisation and LoRA offer a better trade-off between
compression and implementation complexity.

\end{deepdive}

\section{Hallucinations: Detection and Mitigation}
\label{sec:hallucinations}

\begin{context}

In 2023, a New York attorney submitted a legal brief citing six case precedents
that did not exist.  ChatGPT had generated them, complete with plausible docket
numbers and verbatim quotations.  The attorney was sanctioned and the incident
made international headlines.  But the more instructive story is quieter: the
hundreds of financial analysts who asked LLMs about earnings figures, covenant
terms, and regulatory thresholds, received confident wrong answers, and did not
catch them.  A language model \emph{hallucination} is an output that is fluent,
confident, and wrong.  In general text generation this is a nuisance.  In finance
it is a liability.  This section classifies hallucinations by origin, surveys
modern detection methods, and presents the mitigation techniques that should be
deployed before any LLM touches a production financial system.

\subsection{A Taxonomy of Hallucinations}
\label{sec:hallucinations-taxonomy}

\begin{definition}[Hallucination]
\label{def:hallucination}
A language model \emph{hallucination} is an output that is (a) presented with apparent
confidence, yet (b) is not supported by---or actively contradicts---the model's input
or the external world.  Following \citet{maynez2020faithfulness},
hallucinations are classified as:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Intrinsic hallucinations:} the output contradicts information
    explicitly present in the input document.
  \item \emph{Extrinsic hallucinations:} the output introduces information not
    present in the input that cannot be verified from any accessible source.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

Within financial NLP, three sub-types deserve particular attention.
Numerical hallucination occurs when the model outputs a figure---a ratio, a revenue
number, an interest rate---that differs from the value stated in the source document.
Such errors are especially dangerous because they are indistinguishable in format from
correct figures; a downstream system or non-expert reader has no visual signal that
anything is wrong.  Entity hallucination substitutes one company, person, or
regulatory body for another: a common failure mode when two organisations have similar
names or when the model is uncertain about a rare entity.  Citation hallucination
fabricates a regulatory reference, court ruling, or standard that does not exist---a
failure mode documented in multiple legal and financial AI deployments \citep{ji2023survey}.

\begin{example}[Hallucination in Financial Analysis]
\label{ex:hallucination}
A financial analyst uses an LLM to summarise a 10-K filing.  The filing states that
the company's debt-to-equity ratio is 1.8.  The LLM summarises this as ``a
debt-to-equity ratio of 0.8, indicating a conservatively leveraged balance sheet.''
This is an intrinsic numerical hallucination: a direct transcription error that
inverts the true risk profile.  Separately, the LLM notes that ``the company's
accounting practices comply with IFRS~16 as confirmed by the PCAOB in their 2023
inspection report.''  No such report is mentioned in the filing; this is an extrinsic
citation hallucination.  Both errors could materially mislead an investor relying on
the LLM output without cross-checking the source document.
\end{example}

The root cause is not a simple bug but an intrinsic property of autoregressive
generation: the model is trained to produce plausible text, and plausibility
correlates with, but is not identical to, factual accuracy \citep{ji2023survey}.
Reducing temperature to zero eliminates stochasticity but does not eliminate
hallucination, because the model's most probable completion may still be factually
incorrect.  \citet{kang2023hallucination} provide the first systematic empirical
examination of hallucination specifically in financial tasks, showing that off-the-shelf
LLMs exhibit severe hallucination when asked to recall historical stock prices or
explain domain-specific financial concepts, and that retrieval augmentation (RAG)
is the most effective of four mitigation strategies they evaluate.

\subsection{Detection}
\label{sec:hallucinations-detection}

Detecting hallucinations without access to ground-truth labels requires methods that
measure \emph{internal consistency} rather than external verification.

SelfCheckGPT \citep{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} is a zero-resource detection method
that exploits the observation that hallucinated claims are less consistently reproduced
across independent samples than factual claims.  For a generated statement $s$, it
draws $N$ additional samples from the model (with temperature $\tau > 0$) and measures
the average probability of $s$ under each sample using a lightweight natural-language
inference (NLI) model:
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}_{\text{SelfCheck}}(s)
    = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \bigl(1 - P_{\text{NLI}}(s \mid \text{sample}_n)\bigr).
  \label{eq:selfcheckgpt}
\end{equation}
A high score indicates that $s$ is inconsistent with the independently sampled
passages and is therefore likely hallucinated.  The method requires no external
knowledge base and works with any black-box API that returns text.

FActScore \citep{min2023factscore} decomposes a long-form answer into atomic factual
claims, then verifies each claim against a retrieval-backed knowledge source.  The
overall \emph{factual precision} score is the fraction of atomic claims that are
supported.  For financial document Q\&A, FActScore can be adapted to use the source
filing as the knowledge base, providing a fine-grained sentence-level audit of model
outputs.

A complementary approach exploits the model's own token-level probability estimates.
\citet{chen2024uncertainty} construct an entropy-based \emph{inner confidence} measure
from the conditional probability distributions of autoregressive LLMs, and show that
in financial news classification, high-confidence predictions are systematically more
accurate: a portfolio built from high-inner-confidence LLM signals achieves a Sharpe
ratio approximately 20\% above the unconditional benchmark, while low-confidence
predictions offer no excess returns---a result that motivates confidence-gating as a
practical hallucination-mitigation strategy in production pipelines.

\subsection{State-of-the-Art Mitigation Techniques}
\label{sec:hallucinations-mitigation}

The mitigation landscape has matured considerably since 2022.  The following techniques
represent the current practitioner consensus; they are complementary and may be
combined in a production pipeline.

Retrieval-augmented generation (Section~\ref{sec:rag}) is the single most effective
mitigation for extrinsic hallucination.  By grounding the model's context in retrieved
passages from verified sources, RAG replaces the model's implicit parametric memory
with an explicit, auditable document store.  A model prompted with the relevant passage
and instructed to answer only from provided context will almost never fabricate figures
that appear nowhere in the retrieved text.

Self-consistency sampling \citep{wang2022selfconsistency} draws $K$ independent
reasoning chains from the model at temperature $\tau > 0$ and takes the majority vote
over final answers:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{a} = \arg\max_{a} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \mathbf{1}[a_k = a].
  \label{eq:self-consistency}
\end{equation}
This is particularly effective for numerical reasoning tasks such as discounted cash
flow calculations, ratio analysis, and multi-step financial arithmetic, where a single
chain may make an error at any intermediate step but the correct answer is more likely
to appear in the majority of independent traces.

Chain-of-thought prompting \citep{wei2022chain} instructs the model to produce explicit
intermediate reasoning steps before its final answer.  For financial applications, this
means the model must write out the formula it is applying, the values it is substituting,
and the arithmetic it is performing.  Each intermediate step is independently
verifiable, so errors can be caught by a downstream checking step or by the analyst
before the final answer is accepted.  Reasoning models (Section~\ref{sec:landscape-genreason})
implement a form of chain-of-thought natively via their extended thinking mechanism.

Claim decomposition and verification, formalised in the FActScore framework
\citep{min2023factscore}, breaks the model's output into atomic claims and verifies
each against a structured source (a database, a specific document section, or an
API endpoint).  In a production compliance-checking pipeline, this approach allows
each factual claim in a generated report to be tagged with its verification status---
supported, unsupported, or contradicted---before the report reaches a human analyst.

Constitutional AI \citep{bai2022constitutional} is a \emph{training-time} alignment
technique in which a model is fine-tuned to critique and revise its own outputs
against a set of explicit principles; it requires access to model weights and
retraining, and is not directly applicable as an inference-time mitigation by
practitioners using closed API models.  The inference-time analogue is
\emph{prompted self-critique}: a second pass in which the model is instructed to
verify each claim against the source document and retract any assertion it cannot
locate.  For financial systems, domain-specific principles guiding this revision
include rules such as ``all numerical figures must be drawn from the provided source
document'' or ``do not attribute regulatory positions to agencies not mentioned in
the input.''  This revision step substantially reduces the incidence of fabricated
numerical and citation hallucinations, at the cost of an additional inference pass.

Confidence elicitation and abstention asks the model to express its uncertainty and
to refuse or flag answers where it is below a confidence threshold.  A prompt fragment
such as ``if you are not certain about a figure, say so and cite the passage you would
need to answer'' shifts the model toward epistemic humility at the cost of reduced
answer completeness.  Calibration curves---plotting accuracy against elicited
confidence---can be used to set thresholds that balance recall and reliability for a
given application.

\begin{remark}
Reasoning models exhibit reduced hallucination rates on tasks with verifiable
intermediate steps because the chain-of-thought trace provides a built-in
self-checking mechanism.  However, they are not immune: extrinsic hallucinations
persist on obscure regulatory facts or firm-specific historical data not well
represented in the training corpus.  The mitigation strategies above remain
necessary regardless of model type.
\end{remark}

\end{context}

\section{Limitations and Responsible Use}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{context}

In 2024, the EU's AI Act came into force, classifying certain AI applications in
finance---credit scoring, insurance pricing, certain forms of algorithmic
trading---as high-risk.  The regulation does not ban these applications.  It
requires documentation, explainability, human oversight, and ongoing monitoring.
These requirements are not obstacles to using LLMs in finance; they are the
engineering specification.  An LLM deployed without explainability
infrastructure is not a regulatory problem waiting to happen.  It is a model
risk problem that has already happened, quietly, on every inference.  This
section provides the vocabulary and framework for thinking about those risks
rigorously.

\subsection{Regulatory and Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations-ethics}

\begin{remark}
Nothing in this book constitutes legal, regulatory, or investment advice.  The
discussion below describes the regulatory landscape for educational purposes only.
Practitioners deploying AI systems in financial contexts must obtain qualified legal
counsel and ensure compliance with all applicable laws and regulations in their
jurisdiction.
\end{remark}

The European Union's AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689), which entered into force in
August 2024, establishes a risk-based classification for AI systems \citep{euaiact2024}.
AI systems used for credit scoring, insurance risk assessment, pricing of life
insurance products, and AI-assisted securities pricing fall into the \emph{high-risk}
category (Annex III).  High-risk systems are subject to mandatory conformity
assessments, post-market monitoring, transparency obligations towards regulators, and
human oversight requirements.  An LLM deployed as part of an algorithmic trading
system or a loan-approval pipeline would likely qualify as high-risk under the Act,
requiring documentation of the model's training data, performance metrics, and the
mechanisms ensuring human oversight before market deployment.

In the United States, the Securities and Exchange Commission has issued staff guidance indicating that
investment advisers using AI-generated research must demonstrate that reasonable care
was exercised in evaluating the AI's output, and that AI-generated recommendations are
consistent with the adviser's fiduciary duty to clients \citep{sec2023ai}.
The \emph{fiduciary duty} question is particularly acute: whether an LLM's output
constitutes a ``recommendation'' under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940 has not
been definitively resolved by case law, but the SEC's enforcement posture has been to
hold advisers fully responsible for AI-generated outputs used in client-facing
communications.

A subtler risk in financial back-testing applications is \emph{look-ahead bias} induced
by the LLM's training data.  \citet{didisheim2025memory} demonstrate that LLMs exhibit
\emph{predictable memory}: when queried about aggregate financial time series or
low-frequency economic indicators that were part of their training corpus, models can
reconstruct historical values from parametric memory, producing spurious return
predictability in any back-test that does not account for this leakage.  The bias is
most pronounced for widely reported aggregate indices and diminishes for granular
firm-level or high-frequency data, which are under-represented in pre-training corpora.
Practitioners using LLMs in historical signal generation must therefore verify that
any information the model might have memorised is treated as contaminated.

Fine-tuning or retrieval-augmented generation on proprietary client data---transaction
histories, portfolio compositions, know-your-customer (KYC) files---raises significant
obligations under the General Data Protection Regulation \citep{gdpr2016} (GDPR, EU 2016/679).
Key issues include: (a) whether training on personal data requires explicit consent;
(b) the right to erasure and whether a model trained on a data subject's information
can comply with an erasure request without full retraining; (c) data-transfer
restrictions if API calls route personal data to non-EU servers.  Chapter~6
(Fine-tuning) and Chapter~9 (Risk and Compliance) discuss technical mitigations
including federated learning, differential privacy, and on-premises deployment.

General-purpose LLMs are trained predominantly on English-language data from North
American and Western European sources.  Financial training data is similarly skewed:
the largest available corpora (SEC filings, English-language Bloomberg terminals,
Reuters news) disproportionately represent US-listed equities and USD-denominated
instruments.  As a consequence:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Sentiment models calibrated on US earnings calls may systematically
    mis-classify sentiment in Japanese, Chinese, or Indian financial contexts.
  \item Named entity recognition models fine-tuned on EDGAR filings may fail on
    corporate structures common in family-controlled firms typical of Asian or Latin
    American markets.
  \item Regulatory knowledge embedded in LLM weights is heavily biased toward US/EU
    rules; extrapolation to other jurisdictions should be treated with extreme caution.
\end{itemize}
Chapter~13 quantifies these biases empirically using cross-lingual evaluation of
FLUE-equivalent tasks in French, German, and Chinese.

The deepest ethical question for financial LLM applications is whether a system that
generates investment recommendations---even as decision-support rather than autonomous
execution---triggers fiduciary obligations and, if so, who bears them.  Current legal
consensus is that the \emph{human adviser} (or firm) deploying the AI retains full
fiduciary responsibility for its outputs, making thorough testing, documentation, and
human oversight essential rather than optional.  Chapter~7 (AI Agents in Finance) and
Chapter~8 (Multi-Agent Systems) return to this question in the context of agentic
systems that take actions---executing trades, sending communications---rather than
merely producing text.

\end{context}
