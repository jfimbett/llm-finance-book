Text as financial data

Where the words come from, and the measurement pitfalls hiding in raw text.
The premise

Why treat language as data at all?

Text is a data source with its own measurement challenges — exactly like prices and volume, just with different defects.

Every data source has flaws
  • Price data has bid–ask bounce
  • Volume data has reporting lags
  • Text has selection, duplication, and timestamp pitfalls

Step zero

From raw text to tokens the model can read

Every pipeline normalizes, deduplicates, and time-aligns before any modelling. Skip a step and you bias the sentiment measure downstream.

Tokenization & normalization
  • Subword tokenizers: BPE (GPT) / WordPiece (BERT)
  • Case folding for lexicons; preserve case for cased transformers
  • Number handling ([NUM]) — but EPS \(\$\)2.40 vs \(\$\)0.24 carries real signal
  • Cashtag/ticker harmonisation (\(\$\)AAPL); boilerplate removal (XBRL, exhibits)
Normalisation, deduplication, and temporal alignment are the three steps that must precede modelling. Each one, if skipped, introduces a systematic bias in the downstream sentiment estimate — not random noise that averages out, but a directional distortion.
The two cleaning steps people skip

Duplicates and timestamps quietly break backtests

Deduplication — most important, most neglected
  • Wires reprint verbatim; retweets propagate identical text
  • Duplicates bias sentiment and inflate sample size → spuriously narrow confidence intervals
  • MinHash / LSH; mark dupes when trigram Jaccard > 0.6–0.8
temporal alignment Avoid look-ahead bias — post-close news cannot explain the same-day return — and stale timestamps. Store publication and retrieval times separately.

Three ways to score sentiment

Dictionaries, fine-tuned classifiers, and LLMs — coexisting, not competing.
No method wins outright

Three generations live side by side

Lexicons are fast and interpretable; fine-tuned classifiers are strong in-domain; LLMs are flexible and need no labels. A competent practitioner uses all three and knows when each fits.

MethodLabels?StrengthWeakness
Lexicon (LM)nonetransparent, cheapno negation / context

LLM zero/few-shotnoneflexible, nuancedcost & latency
the bigger picture The three rows are a cost–flexibility frontier, not a ranking. The "best" choice depends on your labels, your latency budget, and how unusual your text is.
Generation one

Counting words — but with a finance dictionary

The simplest score: count positive and negative words and net them out — as long as your word list was built from financial language, not everyday English.

Harvard's General Inquirer (Stone et al. 1966) mislabels tax, liability, cost, foreign, crude, cancer as negative — they're neutral in 10-Ks. On filings, GI "negativity" tracks legal boilerplate, not performance.

For a document \(d\), Loughran–McDonald sentiment is \[ S_{\text{LM}}(d) = \frac{N_{\text{pos}}(d) - N_{\text{neg}}(d)}{N_{\text{words}}(d)}. \]
Worked example

Scoring one MD&A paragraph by hand

MD&A excerpt
"Revenue declined 12%, reflecting challenging conditions and competitive pressure. The Company remains cautious but is confident in its long-term position."
  • Negative hits: declined, challenging, pressure, cautious = 4
  • Positive hits: confident = 1; total non-trivial words ≈ 35
  • \(S_{\text{LM}} = (1-4)/35 \approx \mathbf{-0.086}\) — a bottom-quartile MD&A
  • A General-Inquirer classifier also flags competitive → ≈ −0.17: a distortion
With \(N_{\text{pos}}=1\), \(N_{\text{neg}}=4\), \(N_{\text{words}}\approx 35\), \[ S_{\text{LM}} = \frac{1-4}{35} \approx -0.086. \]
known limits No negation ("not confident" scores positive); context-blind; domain shift (misses "headwinds", "softness"). Still widely used: transparent, cheap, auditable.
Generation two

Fine-tuned classifiers read the whole sentence

Take a pre-trained language model, attach a small classification head, and train it on labelled finance text. Because it sees the entire sequence, it handles negation and hedging that dictionaries miss.

Take a pre-trained encoder, attach a head, fine-tune. For the [CLS] embedding \(\mathbf{h}\in\mathbb{R}^{d_h}\), \[ P(y=c\mid d)=\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{W}_c\mathbf{h}+\mathbf{b}_c), \quad c\in\{\text{pos},\text{neu},\text{neg}\}, \] minimising cross-entropy; predict \(\hat{y}=\arg\max_c P(y=c\mid d)\).
FinBERT (Araci 2019; Huang 2023)
  • Further-pretrain BERT on financial news + filings
  • Fine-tune on PhraseBank + analyst + call data
  • Beats GI, LM, and generic BERT
SEC-BERT (Loukas 2022)
  • Pre-trained only on EDGAR (10-K/Q/8-K)
  • Vocabulary tuned to legal-financial text
  • Wins in-domain on risk-factor / event tasks

Edge over lexicons: \(\mathbf{h}\) sees the whole sequence ⇒ handles negation, hedging, and entity-specific qualifications implicitly.

Where the labels come from

The Financial PhraseBank — and why neutral matters

Fine-tuning needs labelled examples. The standard benchmark is a hand-labelled set of news sentences (Malo et al. 2014).

  • 4,840 news sentences, labelled pos / neu / neg
  • 16 finance-trained annotators; majority label used
  • At 75% agreement: ≈ 3,400 high-confidence sentences; inter-annotator agreement ≈ 75–80%
  • Neutral ≈ half the corpus — which is why we report macro-F1, not accuracy
caveat News-sentence style ≠ filings / call language. Models tuned on PhraseBank may transfer poorly to long, syntactically complex documents — supplement with domain-matched labels.
Generation three

LLMs classify with no training set at all

GPT-4, Claude, and LLaMA-3 classify sentiment from a task description alone — no fine-tuning, no labelled set (Brown et al. 2020). That makes rapid prototyping on a new domain almost free.

Zero-shot prompt sketch
"You are a financial analyst. Classify the sentiment … from the perspective of a stock investor." → "Neutral", with a rationale (revenue growth offset by margin compression).
the bigger picture The model brings its own world knowledge to the task, so it can reason about why a sentence is neutral instead of just matching words — the leap that older, smaller models could not make.
Does it actually work?

LLMs match fine-tuned models — at a price

  • Ko & Lee 2024: GPT-4 zero-shot matches or exceeds FinBERT on PhraseBank, FiQA (Maia et al. 2018), and a proprietary call dataset — with no labels.
  • López-Lira & Tang 2023: GPT headline scores positively predict next-day returns, beating lexicons.
  • Kirtac & Germano 2024: OPT (GPT-3 family) yields a markedly higher long–short Sharpe than the LM dictionary.
  • Fatemi & Hu 2023: fine-tuned small LLMs hit SOTA; extra few-shot examples beyond a small set add no accuracy.
cost is the catch Roughly \(\$\)100–\(\$\)500 per million short sentences ⇒ distil the LLM into a small model for production.

Social media

From retail mood to a bank run on Twitter

The noisiest source is also the one that can move markets in real time, not just reflect them.

  • Bollen 2011: "calm" Twitter mood Granger-causes DJIA (10-week sample; mixed replications)
  • Cookson & Niessner 2020: StockTwits disagreement predicts volume and spreads
  • Gil-Bazo et al. 2025: 1.6M fund-family posts — tone and volume predict fund inflows
  • Cookson et al. 2026: SVB collapse — high-Twitter-exposure banks lost more value; social media can catalyse bank runs (a streaming use case)

WSB / GameStop (Jan 2021): coordinated WSB sentiment led GME/AMC by 1–2 days; mechanism via call demand → gamma-hedging amplification.

methodological hazards Bots and coordinated inauthentic behaviour; severe selection; reverse causality (returns → posting).

Beyond sentiment — readability

When managers hide bad news in dense prose

Tone isn't the only signal in text. How hard a document is to read is itself informative — harder MD&A tends to accompany worse, more volatile performance.

\[ \text{Fog} = 0.4\!\left(\frac{N_{\text{words}}}{N_{\text{sentences}}} + 100\cdot\frac{N_{\text{complex}}}{N_{\text{words}}}\right) \] where \(N_{\text{complex}}\) counts words with \(\geq 3\) syllables (excluding proper nouns, compound modifiers, simple verb inflections).
  • Fog = 12 ⇔ US high-school reading level
  • 10-K MD&A typically 18–22 — much harder than a newspaper
  • Li 2008: higher Fog → lower current earnings, higher earnings volatility (managers obscure when performance is poor)
  • Lehavy 2011: less readable 10-Ks → fewer, less accurate analysts
04

From sentiment to a tradeable signal

A score per document is not yet alpha. Aggregate, standardize, then test for economic content.
Mind the gap

A raw score is not yet a signal

A sentiment number on one document can't be traded. It needs aggregation, denoising, and statistical transformation before it becomes a position.

Document → entity aggregation (all docs on firm \(i\), day \(t\))
  • Volume-weighted by reach / circulation
  • Recency-weighted by exponential decay
  • Noisy sources (social) need weighting; curated sources (filings) do not
The baseline aggregate over the \(n_t\) documents on firm \(i\) at day \(t\) is \[ \bar S_t = \frac{1}{n_t}\sum_i S_i, \] then refined with volume- or recency-weights.
the bigger picture Two empirical frameworks test whether the aggregate has economic content: event studies and predictive regressions.
Making scores comparable

Standardize within industry — and check for drift

A defence firm and a software firm use different language. Z-scoring within each industry-year strips out that sector "accent" so the numbers are comparable.

Within industry \(j(i)\) and year \(t\), \[ \tilde S_{i,t} = \frac{S_{i,t}-\mu_{j(i),t}}{\sigma_{j(i),t}}, \] removing sector language fixed effects so firms across industries become comparable.
Stationarity
  • Long sentiment series drift (style change, universe change, regime shift)
  • Apply ADF / KPSS; first-difference or detrend non-stationary series
sentiment inflation Re-scoring old text with a newer model changes measured sentiment. Use one fixed model vintage across all periods — or document the vintages.
Testing for content

Does the text beat the earnings number?

The sharp test: after you already know the earnings surprise, does sentiment still predict the drift? If yes, the text carries information the number doesn't.

The event study framework (Fama 1970) tests whether a class of events carries information by measuring the average abnormal return in a window around the event date. Abnormal return \(AR_{i,\tau}=R_{i,\tau}-\hat R_{i,\tau}\) against a benchmark (e.g. CAPM); cumulated over \([a,b]\), \[ \mathrm{CAR}_i[a,b]=\sum_{\tau=a}^{b}AR_{i,\tau},\qquad \overline{\mathrm{CAR}}[a,b]=\tfrac1N\sum_i \mathrm{CAR}_i[a,b]. \]
Condition the post-event drift on the standardized earnings surprise and sentiment: \[ \mathrm{CAR}_i[1,60]=\alpha+\beta_1\,\mathrm{SUE}_{i,0}+\beta_2\,\tilde S_{i,0}+\varepsilon_{i,0}, \] with \(\mathrm{SUE}=(\mathrm{EPS}-\widehat{\mathrm{EPS}})/\hat\sigma\). Incremental text content ⇒ \(\hat\beta_2>0\).

High-frequency FOMC: a 30-minute window around announcements (Nakamura 2018) demands sentence-level scoring inside the window → low-latency inference.

Evaluation, part 1

How much do human labellers even agree?

Before trusting a classifier, ask how reliable the labels are. Krippendorff's alpha (Krippendorff 1970) measures agreement beyond chance — finance sentences are genuinely ambiguous, so that ceiling matters.

Agreement beyond chance: \[ \alpha = 1-\frac{D_o}{D_e}, \] \(D_o\) = observed, \(D_e\) = expected disagreement. For nominal \(k\)-class data, \(D_e = 1-\sum_c (n_c/n)^2\). \(\alpha=0\): chance; \(\alpha=1\): perfect.

Thresholds: \(\alpha\geq 0.667\) reliable; \(\geq 0.800\) high confidence. Financial benchmarks land at 0.65–0.78 — the genuine ambiguity of finance sentences.

Evaluation, part 2

Report macro-F1 — and never confuse it with alpha

Accuracy lies when one class dominates. With neutral at half the corpus, an "always neutral" model looks great on accuracy and is useless for trading.

Per class \(c\), \[ \text{P}_c=\frac{TP_c}{TP_c+FP_c},\; \text{R}_c=\frac{TP_c}{TP_c+FN_c},\; F1_c=\frac{2\,\text{P}_c\text{R}_c}{\text{P}_c+\text{R}_c}. \] Report macro-F1 — the unweighted mean over classes.
why macro, not accuracy Neutral ≈ half of financial sentences. An accuracy-maximiser that over-predicts neutral scores high while failing on the economically important positive / negative classes.
economic validity ≠ statistical metrics Macro-F1 = 0.75 can still have zero alpha if the test set is unrepresentative or labels are misaligned. Best practice: regress aggregate sentiment on forward returns / earnings surprises in a strictly held-out period.

Appendix · inference at scale

Batch for throughput, streaming for latency

Batch (throughput-bound)
  • FinBERT (110M), 512 tokens, single A100: hundreds of docs/sec; a multi-million archive in hours
  • Optimisations: dynamic padding/batching; FP16/BF16; distillation (110M → 33M, <5% accuracy loss); data/CPU parallelism
Streaming (latency-bound)
  • Target <100 ms for FOMC; near-real-time for live call transcription
  • Kafka/Kinesis → ONNX inference workers → downstream aggregation
  • onnxruntime + quantised weights: <100 ms/sentence on modern CPUs
Appendix · ordinal agreement

A stricter agreement measure for ordered labels

Sentiment labels are ordered (pos > neu > neg), so disagreeing pos-vs-neg should count worse than pos-vs-neu. Ordinal \(\alpha\) penalises by the number of scale steps.

The nominal metric is replaced by a weighted disagreement, \[ d^2(c,c') = \Bigl(\sum_{g=c}^{c'} n_g\Bigr)^2, \] penalising disagreements by the number of scale steps between labels (\(n_g\) = frequency of category \(g\)). Ordinal \(\alpha\) is more conservative than nominal \(\alpha\) for sentiment corpora — a stricter lower bound on agreement.
Appendix · research frontiers

Text as a macro risk gauge: NVIX, factors, ESG

  • News-Implied Volatility (Manela & Moreira 2017): project WSJ front-page text onto realized VIX variation; the measure extends back to 1890, capturing disaster-risk eras (Depression, WWII, 2008–09) far beyond VIX's 1990 start.
  • News in factor models (Ke et al. 2019): supervised extraction of price-relevant news features adds predictive content, especially at short horizons; intraday alpha captured by HFT, longer-horizon signals lower-Sharpe but accessible.
  • Climate / ESG (Engle et al. 2020): a climate-news index builds a hedge portfolio; high climate-news exposure → lower returns on high-news days. Generalises to pandemic / geopolitical / supply-chain risk indices.
