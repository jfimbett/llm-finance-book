\section{Calibration and Overconfidence}
\label{sec:ch13-calibration-overconfidence}

A language model that answers a financial question with apparent certainty is not
necessarily correct. The model's expressed confidence---whether surfaced as a stated
probability, a hedging phrase, or the structure of a chain-of-thought response---may
diverge substantially from its actual empirical accuracy. This divergence is the
calibration problem, and it has direct consequences in finance, where the downstream
cost of a confidently wrong forecast can cascade through portfolios, loan books, and
regulatory submissions.

This section establishes the formal machinery of probabilistic calibration, explains
why large pre-trained models are systematically overconfident, and surveys the practical
tools for measuring and correcting miscalibration in financial applications.

\subsection{What Calibration Means: Reliability Diagrams and ECE}
\label{sec:ch13-calibration-reliability-diagrams}

Calibration was introduced for credit default probabilities in \Cref{ch:credit-risk};
this chapter treats it as a general criterion for evaluating any probabilistic LLM output.
A classifier is \emph{perfectly calibrated} if, among all predictions assigned
confidence $p$, exactly a fraction $p$ of them are correct. Formally, let $\hat{p}$ be
the predicted probability for the positive class and $Y \in \{0,1\}$ be the true label.

\begin{definition}[Perfect Calibration]
\label{def:ch13-perfect-calibration}
A model is perfectly calibrated if
\begin{equation}
  \Pr(Y = 1 \mid \hat{p} = p) = p \quad \text{for all } p \in [0, 1].
  \label{eq:ch13-perfect-calibration}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The reliability diagram (also called a calibration curve) provides a graphical
assessment of~\eqref{eq:ch13-perfect-calibration}. Predictions are binned by
confidence into $M$ equal-width intervals $B_1, \ldots, B_M$ (typically $M = 10$).
For each bin $B_m$, the average confidence $\overline{p}_m$ and the fraction of
positives $\text{acc}(B_m)$ are plotted. A perfectly calibrated model produces points
along the $45^\circ$ diagonal.

The Expected Calibration Error (ECE) provides a scalar summary \cite{guo2017calibration}:

\begin{equation}
  \text{ECE} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \frac{|B_m|}{n} \left|\, \text{acc}(B_m) -
  \overline{p}(B_m) \,\right|,
  \label{eq:ch13-ece}
\end{equation}

where $n = \sum_m |B_m|$ is the total number of predictions and $|B_m|$ is the count
in bin $m$. The ECE is a weighted average of calibration gaps, with each bin's
contribution proportional to the fraction of predictions it contains.

A related measure, the Maximum Calibration Error (MCE), reports the worst-case bin gap:

\begin{equation}
  \text{MCE} = \max_{m \in \{1,\ldots,M\}} \left|\, \text{acc}(B_m) -
  \overline{p}(B_m) \,\right|.
  \label{eq:ch13-mce}
\end{equation}

MCE is more relevant in high-stakes financial contexts where a single catastrophically
miscalibrated confidence region---say, 90--100\% confidence predictions that are only
40\% accurate---is unacceptable even if the average calibration is reasonable.

\begin{remark}
The ECE as defined in~\eqref{eq:ch13-ece} is sensitive to the choice of bin boundaries.
Adaptive binning---where bins are defined by equal mass rather than equal width---is
more reliable when confidence scores are clustered near 0 or 1, as they typically are
for overconfident models \cite{niculescu2005predicting}.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Computing ECE for an LLM Credit-Risk Classifier]
\label{ex:ch13-ece-credit}
An LLM is fine-tuned to predict whether a borrower will default within twelve months,
and its output token probabilities are mapped to a default probability $\hat{p}$. On a
hold-out set of 1{,}000 predictions, the model produces the following calibration
statistics (using 5 equal-width bins of width 0.2):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccrcc}
\hline
Bin & Confidence interval & $|B_m|$ & $\overline{p}(B_m)$ & $\text{acc}(B_m)$ \\
\hline
1 & [0.0, 0.2) & 120 & 0.08 & 0.11 \\
2 & [0.2, 0.4) & 180 & 0.31 & 0.28 \\
3 & [0.4, 0.6) & 200 & 0.50 & 0.47 \\
4 & [0.6, 0.8) & 300 & 0.71 & 0.55 \\
5 & [0.8, 1.0] & 200 & 0.88 & 0.62 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The ECE computes as:
\begin{align}
  \text{ECE} &= \frac{120}{1000}\cdot|0.08-0.11| + \frac{180}{1000}\cdot|0.31-0.28|
               + \frac{200}{1000}\cdot|0.50-0.47| \notag \\
             &\quad + \frac{300}{1000}\cdot|0.71-0.55| + \frac{200}{1000}\cdot|0.88-0.62|
               \notag \\
             &= 0.12 \cdot 0.03 + 0.18 \cdot 0.03 + 0.20 \cdot 0.03 + 0.30 \cdot 0.16
               + 0.20 \cdot 0.26 \notag \\
             &= 0.0036 + 0.0054 + 0.0060 + 0.0480 + 0.0520 = 0.115.
\end{align}

An ECE of $11.5\%$ is substantial. The model is well-calibrated at low confidence levels
but severely overconfident in the high-confidence regime: predictions issued with
80--100\% confidence are only correct 62\% of the time. A credit officer relying on
the model's stated confidence to triage manual review would misallocate effort
systematically toward cases where the model is least reliable.
\end{example}

\subsection{Overconfidence in LLM Financial Outputs}
\label{sec:ch13-overconfidence-financial-outputs}

Neural networks have been known to be poorly calibrated since at least the work of
\citet{niculescu2005predicting}, but the problem intensified with the shift to deep
architectures. \citet{guo2017calibration} demonstrate that modern deep neural networks
are systematically overconfident: their confidence distributions are right-skewed, with
many predictions clustered near certainty even when accuracy is modest. This
miscalibration is typically absent in the training data distribution but emerges on
held-out data, and it worsens as model depth and width increase.

Large language models inherit and amplify this pathology for several interconnected
reasons.

The first is \emph{RLHF-induced overconfidence}. Reinforcement Learning from Human
Feedback (RLHF) trains models to produce responses that humans rate as high quality
\cite{ouyang2022training}. Human raters consistently prefer confident, fluent responses
to hedged, uncertain ones---even when the hedged response is more epistemically
appropriate. The reward signal therefore pushes the model toward confident expression
regardless of actual knowledge, a form of training-time miscalibration.

The second is \emph{token-level versus claim-level confidence}. The probabilities
produced by an autoregressive LLM are token-level quantities: the model assigns
probability to the next token given the context. These are not directly equivalent to
claim-level probabilities. Even a model that is perfectly calibrated at the token level
can be profoundly miscalibrated at the level of the factual claim it is expressing,
because a claim spans many tokens and the token-level probabilities compound
multiplicatively.

The third mechanism is specific to finance: \emph{distributional shift from
training to deployment}. Financial data is non-stationary. A model trained on
pre-2022 text has a knowledge boundary; applied to 2024 market conditions, its
expressed confidence on topics that have structurally changed is not anchored by
recent evidence. The model does not know that it does not know, so it continues to
produce confident outputs on stale information \cite{zhao2025frontiers}.

In practice, overconfidence manifests in financial LLM outputs as:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Earnings per share estimates stated to two decimal places without confidence
        intervals, despite being based on qualitative analysis of management commentary.
  \item Credit-worthiness assessments that read as definitive even when the underlying
        borrower is near a classification boundary.
  \item Market-direction forecasts accompanied by confident-sounding rationales that
        cherry-pick supporting evidence from training data \cite{cfa2025llmguide}.
  \item Regulatory compliance assessments that state a firm is ``in compliance'' or
        ``not in compliance'' without flagging the jurisdictional ambiguities that make
        the question genuinely hard.
\end{itemize}

\citet{bang2023multitask} conducted a systematic evaluation of ChatGPT on factual
tasks and found that the model showed consistent overconfidence---asserting incorrect
facts with high lexical confidence markers such as ``certainly'' and ``definitely.''
In financial contexts this behaviour is particularly dangerous because the audience
includes practitioners who may take model outputs at face value. Going further,
\citet{ChenGreenGulenZhou2024} document that LLM stock return forecasts systematically
over-extrapolate recent price trends and display optimistic bias, exhibiting miscalibration
analogous to well-known human behavioral biases---extrapolation and over-optimism---that
persist even after prompt engineering, providing direct evidence of calibration failure
in a high-stakes financial forecasting setting.

\subsection{Measuring and Improving Calibration}
\label{sec:ch13-measuring-improving-calibration}

Measuring calibration requires access to model confidence scores. For autoregressive
LLMs accessed via API, this requires requesting log-probabilities from the completion
endpoint and aggregating them appropriately. For models accessed only through text
outputs, calibration must be inferred indirectly---either through self-consistency
sampling (generating $k$ responses and computing the fraction that agree) or through
verbalized confidence elicitation (prompting the model to express its confidence as a
percentage).

\citet{wang2023survey} show that verbalized confidence elicitation is unstable:
the same model on the same question may report 70\% confidence under one prompt
template and 95\% under a different one, undermining its utility as a calibration signal.

Three post-hoc recalibration techniques are well-established in the broader machine
learning literature and applicable to LLM outputs.

\emph{Temperature scaling} is the simplest \cite{guo2017calibration}. Given a logit
vector $\mathbf{z}$, the calibrated probability is $\operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{z}/T)$, where $T > 1$
spreads the softmax distribution (reducing overconfidence) and $T < 1$ sharpens it.
The temperature $T$ is a single scalar learned on a held-out calibration set by
minimising the negative log-likelihood. Temperature scaling preserves the ordering of
predictions, so it does not alter \emph{discrimination}---the model's ability to rank
defaulters above non-defaulters, measured by the area under the ROC curve
(AUROC)---while improving calibration.

\emph{Isotonic regression} is a non-parametric post-hoc method that fits a
non-decreasing step function mapping predicted probabilities to empirical frequencies
\cite{zadrozny2002transforming}. It is more flexible than temperature scaling but
requires a larger calibration set (typically several hundred to a few thousand
examples) and can overfit on small sets.

\emph{Platt scaling} fits a logistic regression on the model's raw scores to produce
calibrated probabilities \cite{platt1999probabilistic}. It is slightly less flexible
than isotonic regression but more robust on small calibration sets.

For financial applications, a complementary approach to calibration is
\emph{conformal prediction} \cite{angelopoulos2022gentle}, which produces prediction
sets with guaranteed coverage under mild exchangeability assumptions. Rather than
asserting a point estimate with a miscalibrated confidence, a conformal predictor
outputs a set of possible outcomes $\mathcal{C}(\mathbf{x})$ such that
$\Pr(Y \in \mathcal{C}(\mathbf{x})) \geq 1-\alpha$ with $\alpha$ chosen by the user
(e.g.\ $\alpha = 0.1$ for 90\% coverage). In a credit context, the output might be
``\{default, non-default\}'' (the uncertain region) rather than a single label, which
is more informative than a miscalibrated probability.

\begin{remark}
Calibration is a property of model outputs on a specific distribution. A model
recalibrated on data from 2020--2022 will not be calibrated on data from 2024 if the
underlying distribution has shifted. Financial practitioners should re-estimate
calibration parameters at least quarterly, treating them as part of the model monitoring
workflow, consistent with the model validation principles in SR~11-7 guidance \cite{sr117}.
\end{remark}

\section{Temporal Leakage and Look-Ahead Bias}
\label{sec:ch13-temporal-leakage}

Evaluating an LLM on financial tasks is not simply a matter of asking it questions and
measuring correctness. The model may have encountered the answers---or even the specific
test instances---during pretraining. If the training corpus includes financial news
articles, company filings, analyst reports, and web forum discussions, and if the
evaluation benchmark draws from the same universe of documents, then the model's
performance on the benchmark may reflect memorisation rather than generalisation. This
is the temporal leakage problem, and it is arguably the most underappreciated
methodological failure mode in current LLM finance research.

\subsection{How Pretrained Models Can Memorize Future Events}
\label{sec:ch13-memorize-future-events}

Consider a canonical financial prediction task: given a company's earnings call
transcript from Q2 2021, predict whether the stock price rose or fell in the following
month. An LLM trained on a corpus with a cutoff of January 2022 will have seen, in its
training data, not only the transcript but also the news articles discussing the
subsequent price movement, analyst reactions, and social media commentary on the
earnings call. When the model is asked the prediction question, it is not reasoning
about an unknown future from known past information---it is pattern-matching against
memorised associations.

This is not merely a theoretical concern. \citet{ziemke2024temporal} provide a
systematic analysis of temporal leakage in LLM-based financial forecasting, identifying
three distinct channels:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Direct memorisation}: The model has seen the exact document or a near-verbatim
        version in training. This is most common for widely-circulated news items and
        SEC filings.
  \item \emph{Indirect memorisation via outcome narratives}: The model has seen subsequent
        commentary that frames past events through the lens of their outcomes. An LLM
        trained on 2022 data evaluating a 2020 investment decision has seen the 2021
        ``how the trade played out'' retrospectives.
  \item \emph{Statistical bias via imbalanced corpora}: The training corpus
        over-represents narratives about companies and assets that subsequently became
        notable (a form of survivorship bias in textual data). The model's priors
        implicitly favour the kinds of situations that attracted commentary---typically
        extreme outcomes.
\end{enumerate}

The consequences are severe for empirical evaluation. A model that scores 65\% accuracy
on a stock direction prediction task may appear to outperform chance, but if 10
percentage points of that accuracy derive from temporal leakage, the true
out-of-distribution performance may be barely above random.

The severity of the memorisation problem has been confirmed empirically at the numerical
level. \citet{LopezLiraTangZhu2025} demonstrate that LLMs have memorised exact numerical
values of key economic and financial variables from within their training window, making
it impossible to distinguish genuine out-of-sample forecasting from data recall---an
observation that directly establishes memorisation as a fundamental threat to evaluation
validity rather than a theoretical nuisance. From a practitioner's perspective,
\citet{NoguerAlonso2024} provides a comprehensive taxonomy of look-ahead bias sources in
LLM-based financial applications, covering training cutoffs, entity embeddings, and
backtesting methodology, and proposes mitigation strategies that inform the contamination-resistant
design presented in Section~\ref{sec:ch13-contamination-resistant-splits}.

\subsection{Point-in-Time Data Requirements and Anonymization}
\label{sec:ch13-point-in-time-data}

The concept of \emph{point-in-time} (PIT) data originates in quantitative finance,
where it refers to the data that was actually available to a decision-maker at a
specific historical moment, as opposed to the revised or restated values that would
appear in a later database query. Compustat's CRSP/Compustat Merged database, for
example, maintains historical snapshots of financial statements precisely because
accounting restatements, benchmark revisions, and corrections introduce look-ahead
bias if analysts use the most recent version of historical data.

For LLM evaluation, the PIT requirement extends beyond structured data to text. A
rigorous evaluation protocol demands:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Hard temporal cutoffs}: All evaluation instances are drawn from a period
        that post-dates the model's confirmed knowledge cutoff by a safety margin of at
        least three months, to account for the lag between real-world events and their
        appearance in training corpora.
  \item \emph{Document-level timestamps}: Each evaluation document must carry a verified
        publication timestamp. Documents with uncertain provenance or retroactive dating
        are excluded.
  \item \emph{Contextual anonymisation}: In tasks involving specific companies or
        securities, company names, ticker symbols, and prominent executive names are
        replaced with synthetic identifiers. This prevents the model from retrieving
        memorised associations based on entity identity rather than genuine reasoning
        over the provided text.
\end{enumerate}

Contextual anonymisation is a particularly important control because LLM performance on
named entities can be driven entirely by memorised facts about those entities. An LLM
asked ``Will Apple report revenue above \$100 billion in Q4 2022?'' is effectively
being asked a factual recall question, not a forecasting question. Replacing ``Apple''
with ``Company X, a consumer electronics firm with annual revenue of approximately \$380
billion'' forces the model to reason from context rather than retrieve memorised outcomes.

\subsection{Designing Contamination-Resistant Evaluation Splits}
\label{sec:ch13-contamination-resistant-splits}

Standard train-test splits based on random sampling are inadequate for temporal data.
The correct methodology is \emph{temporal splitting}: the training set ends at time
$T_1$, the validation set spans $[T_1, T_2]$, and the test set begins at $T_2$, with
a \emph{gap period} $[T_1 - \Delta, T_1]$ and $[T_2 - \Delta, T_2]$ excluded from
both splits to prevent contamination through near-boundary examples.

\begin{definition}[Contamination-Resistant Temporal Split]
\label{def:ch13-temporal-split}
Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_t, y_t)\}_{t=1}^T$ be a dataset ordered by event time $t$.
Choose cutoff times $T_1 < T_2$ and gap $\Delta > 0$. Define:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}} &= \{(x_t, y_t) : t \leq T_1 - \Delta\}, \notag \\
  \mathcal{D}_{\text{valid}} &= \{(x_t, y_t) : T_1 \leq t \leq T_2 - \Delta\}, \notag \\
  \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}} &= \{(x_t, y_t) : t \geq T_2\}.
  \label{eq:ch13-temporal-split}
\end{align}
The gap $\Delta$ should be chosen to be at least as long as the prediction horizon $h$ (e.g.\
one month for monthly return prediction) plus an additional buffer for publication lag.
\end{definition}

Beyond temporal splits, three additional controls strengthen contamination resistance:

\emph{N-gram deduplication} computes hash fingerprints of $n$-gram windows (e.g.\ 13-grams)
from both the LLM's known training corpus and the evaluation set, then removes evaluation
instances with high overlap. Tools such as the MinHash LSH algorithm can scale this to
trillion-token corpora.

\emph{Factual probing} tests whether the model can answer factual questions about the
evaluation instances that a non-leaked model could not answer. If a model correctly
states the quarterly earnings of a fictional company without being given that information,
contamination is suspected.

\emph{Date-blinding ablations} compare model performance on original instances versus
versions with all temporal markers removed. A significant performance drop upon date
blinding suggests that performance was partly driven by temporal pattern retrieval
rather than semantic reasoning.

The quantitative stakes of contamination are confirmed by two complementary studies.
\citet{RahimikiaDrinkall2024} eliminate look-ahead bias by pre-training year-specific
LLMs for each year from 2007 to 2023, and show that properly time-bounded models
outperform much larger general-purpose models on trading tasks---demonstrating that
benchmark results obtained from off-the-shelf LLMs substantially overstate true
predictive performance. \citet{GaoJiangYan2025} introduce the Lookahead Propensity
(LAP), a statistical measure of training-data contamination, and use it to show that
a positive correlation between LAP and forecast accuracy on stock-return and
capital-expenditure tasks confirms that apparent LLM predictive skill is
substantially driven by lookahead bias rather than genuine reasoning.

\begin{example}[A Contamination Audit for an LLM Earnings-Surprise Predictor]
\label{ex:ch13-contamination-audit}
A research team constructs an evaluation dataset of 500 earnings calls from 2022--2023
and asks an LLM (training cutoff: December 2023) to predict whether the reported EPS
will beat or miss consensus. To audit for contamination:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item They extract the ten most distinctive 13-gram sequences from each transcript
        and check membership in the Common Crawl dumps that constitute the model's
        training data. 34 of 500 transcripts show $\geq$3 matching 13-grams: these are
        flagged and removed.
  \item They apply contextual anonymisation to the remaining 466 transcripts, replacing
        company names, tickers, and executive names with synthetic tokens.
  \item They compare the model's accuracy on original versus anonymised transcripts.
        Accuracy falls from 62\% to 54\% upon anonymisation, suggesting that
        approximately 8 percentage points of the original performance was driven by
        entity-level memorisation.
\end{enumerate}

After contamination controls, the residual 54\% accuracy is still above the 50\%
random baseline, but the magnitude of the apparent edge has shrunk substantially. The
finding illustrates that contamination auditing is not cosmetic---it changes conclusions.
\end{example}

\section{Stock Movement Prediction: A Cautionary Tale}
\label{sec:ch13-stock-movement-prediction}

No application of LLMs to finance has attracted more optimistic claims---and more
methodological scrutiny---than stock price movement prediction. The basic premise is
appealing: financial markets are driven in part by information, and LLMs can process
information at scale. If LLMs can extract sentiment, digest news, and reason about
earnings calls faster and more comprehensively than human analysts, perhaps they can
identify price-relevant signals that are not yet reflected in market prices.

This section examines the theoretical limits imposed by market efficiency, the empirical
evidence on LLM prediction accuracy, and the conditions under which simpler models
can outperform LLMs.

\subsection{The Theoretical Case: Efficient Markets and the Information Barrier}
\label{sec:ch13-efficient-markets-information-barrier}

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) \cite{fama1970efficient, fama1991efficient}
asserts that asset prices reflect all available information. Under the semi-strong form,
prices adjust instantaneously and without bias to all publicly available information.
Under the strong form, even private information is reflected.

The EMH creates a fundamental challenge for any LLM-based forecasting strategy: if the
model is trained on publicly available text (news, filings, earnings transcripts), and
if markets are semi-strong efficient, then the information content of that text is
already incorporated in prices before the model can act on it. The LLM is not extracting
a novel signal; it is reconstructing information that arbitrageurs have already traded on.

The Grossman-Stiglitz impossibility theorem \cite{grossman1980impossibility} sharpens
this argument. If markets were perfectly informationally efficient, rational agents would
have no incentive to acquire costly information, since they cannot profit from it.
Markets therefore cannot be perfectly efficient: there must be enough mispricing to
compensate informed traders for their information-acquisition costs. The relevant
question is whether LLMs can access that residual informational edge before it
is arbitraged away.

For publicly available NLP signals, the evidence suggests the answer is increasingly
no. The information from earnings call sentiment, news sentiment indices, and social
media mood has been studied and traded systematically since at least 2010
\cite{tetlock2007giving, bollen2011twitter}. Many practitioners believe the alpha from simple text-based signals has declined as these strategies became more widely known and deployed~\citep{harvey2016cross}.
An LLM processing the same publicly available text is unlikely to extract signals that
have not already been identified and traded by dedicated quant shops with faster
execution infrastructure.

The informational barrier is not absolute. Three channels remain where LLMs might
contribute genuine value:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Synthesis at scale}: LLMs can process and synthesise far more documents
        than a human analyst team, potentially identifying diffuse signals spread across
        thousands of low-attention documents.
  \item \emph{Novel reasoning chains}: LLMs trained on diverse knowledge can make
        cross-domain connections (e.g.\ linking supply chain dynamics to earnings risk)
        that domain-specific factor models might miss.
  \item \emph{Private or proprietary text}: If an institution has access to non-public
        information (client data, proprietary research) that is not in the model's
        training corpus and is not reflected in market prices, an LLM could extract
        value from it---though this raises distinct regulatory questions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Empirical Evidence: LLMs Achieve 51--65\% Accuracy on 250 Stocks}
\label{sec:ch13-empirical-evidence-llm-accuracy}

A substantial empirical literature has tested whether LLMs can predict the direction
of stock price movements. The results present a consistent pattern: LLMs achieve
directional accuracy modestly above the 50\% chance level on daily or weekly prediction
tasks, with average performance across studies clustering only a few points above chance
\cite{xu2024stock}.

\citet{LopezLiraTang2023} provided one of the first systematic evaluations, testing
ChatGPT on headline sentiment and showing that a sentiment-based trading strategy
generated positive returns out-of-sample, with the LLM achieving statistically
significant directional accuracy on short-horizon price movements; they document that this predictive accuracy scales with model
complexity, establishing the benchmark against which subsequent methodological critiques
are directed. However, their evaluation period coincided with a period of unusually high
market volatility, and the returns were concentrated in specific news categories.

\citet{VidalSSRN2024} provides an independent empirical benchmark, evaluating four
leading LLMs on predicting 30-day directional price movements for a sample of S\&P~500
firms and reporting an average accuracy only marginally above chance---a result that
falls well below the threshold required for economically
meaningful trading after transaction costs.

A meta-analysis~\cite{zhao2025frontiers} finds that this modest-edge result is
ubiquitous across different LLMs, different markets, and
different time periods, but that there is enormous heterogeneity around the mean.
Studies that achieve accuracy above 62\% almost uniformly exhibit one or more of the
following methodological vulnerabilities:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Insufficient temporal gap between LLM training cutoff and evaluation period.
  \item No contextual anonymisation of company names and tickers.
  \item Use of overlapping training and evaluation windows.
  \item Reporting of gross accuracy without transaction cost adjustment.
\end{itemize}

After controlling for these biases, the residual predictive accuracy is close to 51--54\%---
statistically above 50\% in some studies but economically negligible after trading costs.

The CFA Institute practical guide on AI in investment management \cite{cfa2025llmguide}
reaches a similarly cautious conclusion: LLM-based signals are unlikely to generate
durable alpha in liquid, well-covered markets, but may provide value in less-efficient
segments such as small-cap equities, emerging markets, or in the synthesis of
idiosyncratic private information.

\subsection{ARIMA, LSTM, and When Simpler Models Outperform LLMs}
\label{sec:ch13-arima-lstm-simpler-models}

The discourse around LLMs in finance has sometimes implied a strict hierarchy: LLMs
are more sophisticated than deep learning, which is more sophisticated than classical
econometrics. This hierarchy does not translate into predictive accuracy.

ARIMA models \cite{box1970distribution}, despite their vintage, impose a principled
structure on time-series dynamics---autoregressive dependence, differencing for
stationarity, and moving-average error terms---that is well-matched to the statistical
properties of financial returns. Return series are typically covariance-stationary (after
differencing), have limited autocorrelation, and exhibit variance clustering that
GARCH extensions handle well. An ARIMA-GARCH model requires far fewer parameters than
an LLM, is faster to fit, and does not require a training corpus that might be
temporally contaminated.

LSTM networks \cite{hochreiter1997long} occupy an intermediate position. They are
capable of learning longer-horizon dependencies and have been extensively applied to
financial time series. In studies that compare LSTMs with LLMs on pure price-sequence
prediction (without text input), LSTMs frequently match or outperform LLMs
\cite{zhao2025frontiers}. The advantage of LLMs in financial forecasting is therefore
contingent on the value added by text processing---and as argued above, that value is
limited in liquid markets where publicly available text signals are rapidly arbitraged.

Three conditions systematically favour simpler models over LLMs in financial prediction:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Text irrelevance}: When the prediction target is determined primarily by
        price and volume dynamics with limited text-driven fundamentals, ARIMA and GARCH
        models have lower parameter count, lower overfitting risk, and faster iteration.
  \item \emph{Short evaluation history}: LLM fine-tuning requires substantial labelled
        data. For instruments with limited trading history---newly issued bonds, recently
        listed equities, ESG-rated securities with short track records---there is
        insufficient data to justify fine-tuning, and in-context LLMs are outperformed
        by parsimonious time-series models.
  \item \emph{Regime changes}: Financial markets undergo structural breaks---
        regime changes in volatility, liquidity, or correlation structure---that render
        historical training data non-representative of the current environment. The
        parameters of an ARIMA model can be re-estimated rapidly on a rolling basis;
        re-training or re-fine-tuning an LLM on new data is an order of magnitude
        more expensive.
\end{enumerate}

The broader lesson is methodological: evaluating LLMs against a random walk baseline
is insufficient. Any proposed LLM application should be benchmarked against the best
available classical alternative for that specific task, using the same evaluation
period and the same information set.

\section{Evaluation Beyond Classification Accuracy}
\label{sec:ch13-evaluation-beyond-accuracy}

Classification accuracy is a necessary but not sufficient metric for evaluating
financial AI models. A model that achieves 53\% directional accuracy on a stock
prediction task may be economically worthless if it incurs sufficient transaction costs,
generates returns with a poor risk-adjusted profile, or cannot be implemented at scale.
Conversely, a model that achieves only 51\% directional accuracy but with high
confidence on the most extreme predictions can be highly valuable if those confident
predictions are concentrated on large price moves. This section presents the evaluation
framework appropriate for financial applications.

\subsection{Economic Value Metrics: Sharpe Ratio, Alpha Generation, Information Ratio}
\label{sec:ch13-economic-value-metrics}

The most fundamental transformation required in financial AI evaluation is from
statistical metrics (accuracy, F1, AUROC) to economic metrics (returns, risk-adjusted
performance, alpha).

The \emph{Sharpe ratio} \cite{sharpe1994sharpe} is the canonical measure of
risk-adjusted return:

\begin{equation}
  \text{SR} = \frac{\overline{R}_p - R_f}{\sigma_p},
  \label{eq:ch13-sharpe-ratio}
\end{equation}

where $\overline{R}_p$ is the portfolio's average excess return over the evaluation
period, $R_f$ is the risk-free rate, and $\sigma_p$ is the standard deviation of excess
returns. A strategy with positive expected returns but high volatility is less valuable
than one with similar returns and lower volatility. The Sharpe ratio is typically
annualised by multiplying by $\sqrt{252}$ for daily returns or $\sqrt{12}$ for monthly.

The \emph{information ratio} (IR) generalises the Sharpe ratio to active management
\cite{grinold1999active}:

\begin{equation}
  \text{IR} = \frac{\alpha}{\text{TE}},
  \label{eq:ch13-information-ratio}
\end{equation}

where $\alpha$ is the annualised active return (portfolio return minus benchmark return)
and $\text{TE}$ is the tracking error (standard deviation of active returns). The IR
measures how efficiently a strategy converts informational advantage into
risk-adjusted alpha. Rules of thumb from active management hold that IR $> 0.5$
represents strong skill and IR $> 1.0$ represents exceptional skill.

The fundamental theorem of active management \cite{grinold1999active} relates the IR
to the strategy's breadth and skill:

\begin{equation}
  \text{IR} \approx \text{IC} \cdot \sqrt{N},
  \label{eq:ch13-fundamental-law}
\end{equation}

where IC is the Information Coefficient (the cross-sectional rank correlation between
predicted and realised returns) and $N$ is the number of independent bets per year.
This decomposition is particularly useful for evaluating LLM strategies because it
makes explicit the two sources of value: the quality of the signal (IC) and its
breadth (N, driven by the number of securities covered and the prediction frequency).

For \emph{alpha} estimation, the standard approach regresses the strategy's daily
excess returns $r_{p,t}$ on a factor model:

\begin{equation}
  r_{p,t} = \alpha + \beta_{\text{MKT}} r_{\text{MKT},t} + \beta_{\text{SMB}}
            r_{\text{SMB},t} + \beta_{\text{HML}} r_{\text{HML},t} + \varepsilon_t,
  \label{eq:ch13-factor-alpha}
\end{equation}

where $r_{\text{MKT}}, r_{\text{SMB}}, r_{\text{HML}}$ are the Fama-French
\cite{fama1993common} market, size, and value factors. A statistically significant
$\alpha$ indicates the strategy delivers returns not explained by passive factor
exposures. Many LLM trading strategies, when subjected to factor regression, produce
$\alpha$ that is statistically indistinguishable from zero.

\subsection{Walk-Forward Tests and Expanding-Window Validation}
\label{sec:ch13-walk-forward-tests}

Standard cross-validation is inappropriate for time-series data because future
observations ``leak'' into training folds when instances are shuffled randomly. The
two correct alternatives are walk-forward validation and expanding-window (purged
cross-validation) methods.

In \emph{walk-forward validation}, the model is trained on data up to time $t$,
evaluated on the window $[t, t + h]$, and then the training window advances to
$t + h$ for the next iteration. This procedure is repeated across the full evaluation
horizon, producing a sequence of out-of-sample predictions. The final performance
metric is computed by aggregating across all out-of-sample windows.

\emph{Expanding-window validation} (also called anchored walk-forward) fixes the
start of the training window and expands it through time, re-estimating model
parameters at each step. This is appropriate when the model benefits from
accumulating data (as with fine-tuning) and is the standard protocol in the
financial machine learning literature \cite{lopezdeprado2018advances}.

Both protocols require a \emph{purge period}: a gap between the end of the training
window and the beginning of the evaluation window, equivalent to the gap $\Delta$ in
Definition~\ref{def:ch13-temporal-split}. The purge period must be at least as long
as the prediction horizon to prevent any individual observation from appearing in both
training and evaluation windows through overlapping label windows.

\begin{definition}[Walk-Forward Evaluation with Purge]
\label{def:ch13-walk-forward}
Let $h$ be the prediction horizon and $\Delta_{\text{purge}}$ be the purge gap.
At iteration $k$, the training set is $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}^{(k)} =
\{(x_t, y_t) : t \leq kh - \Delta_{\text{purge}}\}$ and the evaluation set is
$\mathcal{D}_{\text{eval}}^{(k)} = \{(x_t, y_t) : kh \leq t \leq (k+1)h - 1\}$.
The model is retrained from scratch (or adapted via PEFT (parameter-efficient fine-tuning, e.g., LoRA; see \Cref{ch:llm-training})) at each iteration $k$.
\end{definition}

The walk-forward protocol makes an implicit assumption about model persistence: the
model fit on data through time $t$ is used to predict at time $t + \Delta$ to
$t + h$. If model parameters are expensive to re-estimate (as for full LLM fine-tuning),
practitioners may opt for longer evaluation windows $h$ to amortise the re-estimation
cost, at the expense of evaluating a potentially stale model in the later part of
the window.

\subsection{Trading-Grade Evaluation: Transaction Costs, Capacity, Drawdown}
\label{sec:ch13-trading-grade-evaluation}

A strategy that performs well in academic evaluation can fail to be tradeable in
practice for three reasons: transaction costs erode returns, position sizing saturates
available liquidity, and drawdown periods are psychologically and operationally
intolerable.

\emph{Transaction cost adjustment} is the minimum bar for any published trading
strategy. For a strategy that turns over the portfolio at rate $\tau$ (fraction of
portfolio replaced per period), the round-trip transaction cost per period is:

\begin{equation}
  c_{\text{round-trip}} = 2 \tau \cdot (\text{half-spread} + \text{market impact}),
  \label{eq:ch13-transaction-cost}
\end{equation}

where the half-spread is the bid-ask spread divided by two and the market impact
is the price movement caused by the strategy's own trading. For an LLM strategy
trading liquid US large-cap equities, half-spreads are typically 1--3 basis points
(bps), but market impact for meaningful position sizes can easily add 5--20 bps per
trade. A strategy requiring daily rebalancing based on news sentiment can incur 10--50
bps per day in transaction costs, eliminating any statistical edge.

\emph{Capacity analysis} asks how much capital the strategy can absorb before market
impact destroys its edge. The Almgren-Chriss framework \citep{almgren2001optimal} provides a principled model:
the linear temporary impact of trading $q$ shares in a stock with daily volume $V$
and price $P$ is approximately $\eta \cdot (q / V)$ per share, where $\eta$ is an
empirically estimated market-impact coefficient, $q$ the number of shares traded,
$V$ the daily volume, and $P$ the price. A strategy that is profitable at
\$10M in assets may be unprofitable at \$1B if it trades in less liquid securities.

\emph{Drawdown analysis} measures the maximum cumulative loss from peak to trough.
For financial institutions subject to value-at-risk constraints or regulatory capital
requirements, a strategy's maximum drawdown is as important as its average return.
The maximum drawdown (MDD) is:

\begin{equation}
  \text{MDD} = \max_{t_1 < t_2} \left( \frac{P_{t_1} - P_{t_2}}{P_{t_1}} \right),
  \label{eq:ch13-max-drawdown}
\end{equation}

where $P_t$ is the portfolio value at time $t$. The Calmar ratio, defined as the
annualised return divided by the MDD, is frequently used alongside the Sharpe ratio
in practitioner evaluation.

A complete trading-grade evaluation for an LLM signal should report:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Gross and net (after costs) Sharpe ratio.
  \item Information ratio against a relevant benchmark.
  \item Maximum drawdown and Calmar ratio.
  \item Turnover rate and estimated capacity.
  \item Performance decomposition by factor loadings (equation~\eqref{eq:ch13-factor-alpha}).
  \item Statistical significance of alpha using heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors
        and multiple-testing corrections.
\end{itemize}

\section{Hallucination in Financial Contexts}
\label{sec:ch13-hallucination-financial-contexts}

Hallucination---the generation of text that is fluent and confident but factually
incorrect---is among the most consequential failure modes of LLMs deployed in finance.
Unlike errors of imprecision or incompleteness, hallucinations are undetectable by a
reader who lacks prior knowledge of the correct answer. A junior analyst receiving a
hallucinated financial analysis has no automatic signal that the document contains
fabricated facts; the prose reads as plausibly as accurate content.

\subsection{Fabricated Citations, Incorrect Numerical Facts, Phantom Companies}
\label{sec:ch13-fabricated-citations-phantom-companies}

Financial hallucinations appear in several distinct categories, each with its own
profile of risk.

\emph{Fabricated citations and sources.} LLMs asked to support financial claims with
references frequently generate plausible-sounding but nonexistent citations: journals
that do not publish the attributed paper, SSRN working papers with correct-looking
identifiers but wrong titles or authors, and regulatory guidance documents with
fabricated section numbers. \citet{bang2023multitask} document this systematically,
finding that hallucinated citations are stylistically indistinguishable from real ones
and can pass casual verification. In legal and compliance contexts, a fabricated
regulatory citation used in a filing or advice document carries serious liability.

\emph{Incorrect numerical facts.} Financial analysis is numerically intensive: balance
sheet figures, interest rates, credit ratings, option Greeks, return histories. LLMs
hallucinate numbers with disturbing frequency. \citet{kang2023hallucination} construct
a dedicated benchmark of numerical queries (revenue, EPS, EBITDA, credit spreads) from
public filings and report that, on their benchmark, GPT-4 class models answered a non-trivial fraction of queries incorrectly, with no reliable indicator distinguishing correct from
incorrect answers in the model's lexical output. The model does not say ``I am not
sure''; it produces a number with the same fluency whether that number is correct or
fabricated.

\emph{Phantom companies and executives.} Asked to describe the competitive landscape
of a niche financial sector, LLMs frequently generate companies, funds, and executives
that do not exist. These phantom entities are constructed by analogy from real entities
the model has encountered: they have plausible names, consistent histories, and realistic
attributes---but they are not real. In due diligence, counterparty verification, and
regulatory compliance workflows, acting on phantom entity information is a material
operational risk.

\emph{Anachronistic facts.} A model with a training cutoff of mid-2024 answering
questions in 2026 may confidently state information that was accurate at training
time but is now stale: interest rates, credit ratings, executive leadership, ownership
structures, and regulatory status change. The model has no awareness of its own
obsolescence and will not spontaneously flag uncertainty on time-sensitive facts
\cite{ji2023survey}.

\subsection{Grounding Strategies: RAG, Retrieval Verification, Tool Use}
\label{sec:ch13-grounding-strategies}

The fundamental challenge of hallucination is that it arises from the model's
parametric memory---its internal representation of facts encoded during pretraining.
Grounding strategies mitigate hallucination by reducing the model's reliance on
parametric memory and anchoring its responses to verified external sources.

\emph{Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)} \cite{lewis2020rag} is the most widely
deployed grounding strategy. The model retrieves a set of relevant documents from a
curated knowledge base, and these documents are included in the prompt context before
generation. If the knowledge base consists of verified, current, and numerically
accurate financial documents (current 10-K filings, real-time pricing feeds, verified
regulatory guidance), the model can generate factually grounded responses without
relying on its parametric memory.

The effectiveness of RAG depends critically on retrieval quality. If the retriever
returns documents that are topically relevant but factually tangential to the specific
claim being made, the model may still hallucinate while appearing to cite the retrieved
source. The model must be prompted to base its response exclusively on the retrieved
context, and the retrieved context must contain the specific information needed.

\emph{Retrieval verification} extends RAG by adding a post-generation step that checks
whether each claim in the generated response can be directly grounded in a retrieved
document. \citet{min2023factscore} introduce FActScore, which decomposes a generated
text into atomic facts and verifies each against a retrieval system, reporting
the fraction of facts that are grounded. In financial applications, retrieval
verification can be implemented with a purpose-built claim checker that queries
an authoritative data source (EDGAR for filings, Bloomberg for pricing, a regulatory
database for compliance guidance) for each numerical or entity claim in the generated
text.

\emph{Tool use and function calling} \cite{schick2023toolformer} allows the model to
invoke external APIs during generation, fetching current data rather than relying on
potentially stale parametric memory. A financial LLM equipped with tools to query a
live financial data API, execute database queries, and search regulatory filings can
produce grounded numerical outputs with high accuracy. The model's role shifts from
knowledge store to reasoning engine: it knows how to ask for information rather than
needing to remember it.

\emph{Self-consistency sampling} \cite{wang2022selfconsistency} generates multiple
independent responses to the same query and uses majority vote or variance analysis
as a hallucination signal. \citet{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} show that facts that appear
consistently across independent samples are more likely to be correct, while facts that
vary across samples are flagged for human review. In a financial workflow, self-consistency
sampling can automatically triage responses that require verification before use.

\begin{remark}
Grounding strategies reduce but do not eliminate hallucination. RAG can fail if the
retrieved documents themselves contain errors; tool use can return stale data if the
underlying data source has a lag; self-consistency can consistently produce the same
incorrect fact if it is strongly represented in the model's parametric memory. A
complete anti-hallucination workflow combines grounding with human-in-the-loop review
for high-stakes claims \cite{cfa2025llmguide}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Hallucination Benchmarks for Financial Applications}
\label{sec:ch13-hallucination-benchmarks}

Several benchmarks have been developed specifically to assess LLM factuality in
financial settings.

\emph{FinanceBench} \cite{zhang2024financebench} tests question answering over real
SEC filings, with questions that require precise numerical extraction and multi-step
calculation. It distinguishes between questions that can be answered by retrieval from
the provided document and those that require synthesis across documents or external
knowledge. Hallucination rates on FinanceBench are high even for frontier models: the
benchmark finds that models struggle substantially with multi-step numerical questions~\cite{zhang2024financebench}.

\emph{FinQA} \cite{chen2021finqa} requires numerical reasoning over financial tables
and text, with evaluation via program execution rather than string match. This execution
grounding catches numerical hallucinations that would pass a surface-level text match.

\citet{kang2023hallucination} construct a dedicated hallucination benchmark drawing on
public company filings from EDGAR, testing whether models correctly state revenue,
earnings, and balance sheet figures. Their evaluation protocol includes controlled
perturbations: the model is asked the same question about both real and fictitious
companies, with the expectation that it should express uncertainty for the latter.
Many models instead confidently answer questions about fictitious companies with
invented but plausible figures.

The gap between benchmark performance and real-world reliability is substantial. Models
that perform well on structured benchmarks such as FinQA can still hallucinate in
open-domain financial workflows where the information is not pre-retrieved. The benchmark
ecosystem is developing rapidly, and practitioners evaluating a model for deployment
should assess performance on the specific task type, information source, and output
format relevant to their use case, rather than relying on aggregate benchmark scores.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:ch13-summary}

This chapter has examined five interconnected sources of fragility in the application
of LLMs to finance, each requiring a specific methodological response.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Calibration}: LLMs are systematically overconfident, with ECE values
        that can exceed 10\% on financial classification tasks. Confidence scores must
        be post-processed with temperature scaling, isotonic regression, or Platt
        scaling before they are used to drive decisions. Conformal prediction sets
        provide coverage guarantees under mild assumptions and should be considered for
        high-stakes outputs.

  \item \emph{Temporal leakage}: Evaluating LLMs on financial tasks that fall within
        or close to their training cutoff produces inflated performance estimates.
        Robust evaluation requires hard temporal splits, purge periods, contextual
        anonymisation, and n-gram deduplication against known training corpora. The
        contamination audit in Example~\ref{ex:ch13-contamination-audit} illustrates
        that published accuracy figures can be reduced by 8 percentage points or more
        after contamination controls.

  \item \emph{Stock prediction realism}: The preponderance of evidence places
        LLM directional accuracy on stock price prediction in the 51--65\% range, with
        the upper end driven by methodological artefacts. After controlling for temporal
        leakage, transaction costs, and factor exposures, the residual alpha from
        LLM-based signals in liquid markets is economically modest. Simpler models
        (ARIMA, LSTM) frequently outperform LLMs when text is not the dominant
        information source.

  \item \emph{Economic evaluation}: Financial model evaluation must extend beyond
        classification accuracy to economic metrics: Sharpe ratio, information ratio,
        factor alpha, and trading-grade metrics including transaction costs, capacity,
        and maximum drawdown. A complete evaluation protocol is defined in
        Section~\ref{sec:ch13-trading-grade-evaluation}.

  \item \emph{Hallucination}: LLMs fabricate citations, numerical facts, and entities
        in financial contexts, with no reliable lexical signal distinguishing correct from
        hallucinated content. Grounding strategies---RAG, retrieval verification, tool
        use, and self-consistency sampling---reduce but do not eliminate hallucination.
        Human review remains necessary for high-stakes financial outputs.
\end{enumerate}

Taken together, these limitations do not preclude the use of LLMs in finance, but they
do require a disciplined evaluation framework that treats LLM outputs as inputs to a
decision process, not as end products. The standards developed in this chapter---calibration
measurement, contamination-resistant evaluation design, economic metrics, and grounding
workflows---provide the methodological foundation for responsible LLM deployment in
financial practice.

\section{Further Reading}
\label{sec:ch13-further-reading}

\emph{Calibration.} The canonical reference on calibration of modern neural networks
is \citet{guo2017calibration}, which introduces temperature scaling and provides a
comprehensive empirical analysis. \citet{niculescu2005predicting} covers Platt scaling
and isotonic regression. \citet{angelopoulos2022gentle} provides an accessible
introduction to conformal prediction and its financial applications are surveyed in
\citet{chen2024uncertainty}.

\emph{Temporal leakage and evaluation design.} \citet{ziemke2024temporal} is the
primary reference on temporal leakage in LLM financial evaluation. \citet{LopezLiraTangZhu2025}
provides the definitive empirical evidence that LLMs memorise exact numerical financial
variables from their training corpora. \citet{NoguerAlonso2024} offers a practitioner-oriented
taxonomy of look-ahead bias sources and mitigation strategies. \citet{RahimikiaDrinkall2024}
demonstrates the practical magnitude of the problem by training year-specific LLMs and
showing that properly time-bounded models outperform larger off-the-shelf alternatives.
\citet{GaoJiangYan2025} introduces the Lookahead Propensity (LAP) measure as a
statistical test for contamination. \citet{lopezdeprado2018advances} covers purged
cross-validation and walk-forward evaluation in the financial machine learning context.
The importance of point-in-time data is treated extensively in the quantitative finance
literature; see \citet{lewellen2010empirical} for the asset-pricing dimension.

\emph{Stock prediction and market efficiency.} \citet{fama1970efficient} and
\citet{fama1991efficient} are the foundational texts on market efficiency.
\citet{grossman1980impossibility} provides the theoretical limit on informational
efficiency. \citet{LopezLiraTang2023} establish the
benchmark accuracy findings for ChatGPT-based headline trading strategies.
\citet{VidalSSRN2024} reports an average directional accuracy only marginally above chance
across several LLMs and a sample of S\&P~500 firms, providing an independent empirical baseline. \citet{ChenGreenGulenZhou2024}
documents the extrapolation bias and over-optimism present in LLM stock forecasts.
\citet{xu2024stock} surveys the broader accuracy literature, and the meta-analysis in
\citet{zhao2025frontiers} surveys the broader accuracy landscape across a large set of heterogeneous LLM evaluation studies.

\emph{Economic evaluation.} \citet{sharpe1994sharpe} defines the Sharpe ratio.
\citet{grinold1999active} develops the information ratio and the fundamental law of
active management. \Cref{ch:portfolio-quant-trading} of this book covers the
practical implementation of walk-forward backtesting and transaction cost modelling.

\emph{Hallucination.} \citet{ji2023survey} provides a comprehensive survey of
hallucination in NLG. \citet{kang2023hallucination} is the primary reference for
LLM hallucination in financial contexts. \citet{min2023factscore} introduces the
FActScore methodology for fine-grained factual evaluation. \citet{manakul2023selfcheckgpt}
covers self-consistency as a hallucination detection method. For grounding via RAG,
see \citet{lewis2020rag} and the applied financial RAG system described in
\citet{wang2025finsage}.
