1Executive Summary

NVIDIA closed FY2026 with $215.9B revenue (+65% YoY) and $120.1B net income (+65%), having compounded revenue at a 68% CAGR and net income at an 87% CAGR over FY2022–FY2026 — a scale-up with virtually no precedent for a company of this size. The Data Center franchise ($193.7B, ~90% of revenue) plus the CUDA software moat remain the engine, and the balance sheet is fortress-grade: net cash, ~0.05x debt/equity, $62.6B of cash and securities. Cash generation is real — $96.7B free cash flow at a 45% FCF margin.

The two genuine watch-items are gross-margin normalization (71.1%, down ~390bp from 75.0%, partly a $4.5B H20 charge and the Blackwell system-mix transition) and extreme concentration (one customer at 22% of revenue, top two at 36%, Data Center ~90%). The $95.2B of inventory-purchase and supply/capacity commitments is both a demand-confidence signal and the single largest obsolescence risk on a one-year product cadence. The debate is not the quality of the business but the durability of hyperscaler AI capex and how much is already priced in.

Networking grew +142% (vs. Compute +59%), validating the rack-scale "AI factory" strategy (NVLink, Spectrum-X, InfiniBand) and lifting dollar content per deployment. Non-DC segments (~10% combined) are now optionality, not thesis.

Margin debate. A 60% operating margin on $216B is the headline of the analysis, but the gross-margin step-down from 75.0% to 71.1% is the key question.

Drivers of the ~390bp gross-margin decline

(a) Mix shift from Hopper HGX systems to Blackwell full-scale datacenter solutions — more system/networking content dilutes percentage margin even as gross-profit dollars grow; (b) the $4.5B H20 charge. Inventory & excess-commitment provisions were $7.2B in FY2026 vs. $3.7B in FY2025 (a 2.6% unfavorable gross-margin hit). The open question: is ~71% the new floor for a richer-mix system franchise, or the start of normalization as AMD/ASICs and hyperscaler in-housing erode pricing? A break into the 60s without a clear mix explanation is the worry signal.

Operating leverage: R&D rose 43% to $18.5B yet intensity fell to 8.6% of revenue (from 27.2% in FY2023) — revenue outrunning a still-growing R&D base, not under-investment. SBC of $6.4B is ~3% of revenue and was more than offset by buybacks (diluted share count fell to 24.51B).

4Growth Trajectory & Sustainability

The trajectory is staggering but decelerating off a vast base: +126% (FY24) → +114% (FY25) → +65% (FY26). At this valuation the second derivative is what the market trades. Supportive: a stated one-year product cadence (Hopper → Blackwell → Blackwell Ultra/GB300 shipping since Q2 FY26 → Rubin, production 2H FY2027 with a claimed ~10x cost-per-token reduction) — a genuine competitive weapon. Unresolved: the durability of hyperscaler capex, whether end-customer AI ROI materializes, and the company's own caveat that energy/power availability is a "complex, multi-year" constraint. Visibility beyond ~12 months is thin: contracted remaining performance obligations over one year are only $2.3B — the real "backlog" lives in purchase commitments and informal allocation, not RPO. The honest read: growth is real and supply-led today, but forward sustainability is a judgment about a capex cycle, not a fact in the filings.

5Balance Sheet, Cash Flow & Capital Returns

Fortress balance sheet. FY2026: $62.6B cash + marketable securities, total long-term debt of just $8.5B, debt/equity 0.05x, equity $157.3B, +$22.6B net cash (understates strength — excludes ~$22.3B of non-current equity stakes & securities per data notes). Liquidity comfortable at 3.9x current ratio. The watch-items are working-capital, not solvency: receivables nearly doubled to $38.5B (concentrating counterparty risk in a few buyers) and inventory more than doubled to $21.4B (obsolescence exposure on annual product turns).

Cash conversion is excellent. Operating cash flow $102.7B (0.86x net income); FCF $96.7B at a 45% margin against modest fabless capex of $6.0B. One caveat: investing outflows jumped to $52.2B, partly equity-investment purchases (a disclosed Intel common-stock stake, a Groq license) — NVIDIA increasingly funds its own ecosystem (Neocloud builders, AI labs): strategically rational, but introduces circular-financing optics and mark-to-market volatility in "Other income."

Capital returns (FY2026)

$40.1B buybacks + $1.0B dividends = $41.1B returned. Fresh $60B repurchase authorization (Aug 2025) leaves $58.5B outstanding. The $0.04/sh dividend is a rounding error — buybacks are the return vehicle and shrank the share count, compounding per-share value.

Valuation framing — no live price feed

Diluted EPS $4.90 (89% EPS CAGR FY22–FY26); FCF/share ≈ $3.94 ($96.7B / 24.51B shares). P/E, EV/EBITDA and FCF yield are not computed (no market price). Framing for the PM: at a 45% FCF margin on a net-cash balance sheet the quality side of growth-at-a-price is unimpeachable; the risk is entirely on the durability and multiple side. NVDA earns its premium only if growth persists; per-share value compounds further from buybacks.

6Key Risks

  • HighCustomer concentration. One direct customer = 22% of revenue, a second = 14% (top two = 36%); additional indirect customers each >10%. One AI lab drives a "meaningful amount" of revenue via cloud customers. A single-buyer pullback is materially impactful.
  • High · partly realizedChina / export controls. China (incl. Hong Kong) fell to $19.7B / 9.1% of revenue (from $25.0B / 19.2%). NVIDIA took a $4.5B H20 charge and booked only ~$60M licensed H20 revenue; the filing states it is "effectively foreclosed" from much of the market — structural, not transient.
  • HighInventory & purchase-commitment obsolescence. $95.2B of inventory-purchase and long-term supply/capacity obligations vs. $21.4B on-hand (~4.4x). A confidence signal (prepaid TSMC/CoWoS/HBM capacity), but on a one-year cycle any demand air-pocket converts commitments into write-downs. Auditor flagged inventory valuation as a critical audit matter.
  • Med-HighHyperscaler in-sourcing & competition. The same concentrated buyers build alternatives (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, custom ASICs, AMD). CUDA lock-in and the annual cadence are the defenses; share erosion would show first in gross margin.
  • MedMargin normalization. Already underway (75.0% → 71.1%) from system mix plus provisions. Track whether ~71% holds.
  • MedSupply / power constraints. Asia-concentrated supply chain; energy availability cited as a multi-year limiter on customer deployment scale.

7Outlook & Recommendation

Constructive on the business, disciplined on the entry. NVIDIA is a genuinely exceptional franchise — 60% operating margins, 45% FCF margin, net cash, a widening software/system moat, and an annual product cadence (Rubin in 2H FY2027) competitors struggle to match. The financials show no demand exhaustion: +65% revenue, +142% networking, and a $95.2B forward supply commitment. The case against complacency lives in the same filing: ~90% one segment, 36% two customers, gross margin already rolled over ~390bp, a structurally impaired China, and a double-edged $95.2B of commitments — and the H20 write-down plus China decline are realized examples of those tails materializing.

For the portfolio manager: treat NVDA as a high-quality, high-beta expression of the AI-infrastructure capex cycle rather than a defensive compounder. The fundamentals justify a premium; position size should reflect that the thesis is a bet on the durability of customer capex with concentration and policy risk that can move fast. Monitorables: sequential Data Center growth and forward guidance, gross margin holding ~71%+, China policy, top-customer percentages in subsequent filings, and inventory/purchase-commitments vs. realized demand. A sustained sequential Data Center deceleration combined with a sub-70% gross margin would be the signal to reduce.
