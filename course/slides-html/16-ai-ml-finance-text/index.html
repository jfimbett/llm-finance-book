Artificial intelligence: definitions and scope

From a 1956 coinage to a working hierarchy — and from hand-coded rules to learning from data.
The moving target

What do we even mean by "artificial intelligence"?

John McCarthy coined "AI" in 1956: making machines do tasks that would require intelligence if a human did them. The trouble is, the goalposts keep moving.

the AI effect The boundary of "AI" is socially constructed: once a machine does something reliably, we shrug and call it "mere computation." The field is forever chasing a target that retreats as it succeeds.
Our working hierarchy for the book
  • AI — the broad enterprise of building systems that perform cognitive tasks.
  • Machine learning — the dominant contemporary methodology.
  • Deep learning / LLMs — particular implementations of that methodology.

A historical pivot

Why we stopped writing rules and started learning from data

1956–1980s · Expert systems
  • Hand-crafted "if–then" rules encoding domain knowledge
  • A 1980s credit system: hundreds of rules for "high-risk applicant"
  • strength transparent — every decision traces to a rule
  • weakness brittle — rules proliferate, fail on the unanticipated
Late 1980s on · Induced from data
  • Intelligence learned from data, not encoded by hand
  • Bigger datasets + faster hardware made empirical evaluation feasible
  • By the mid-2000s, data-driven methods overtook symbolic ones wherever labeled data existed: speech, vision, spam, eventually NLP
the shift in one sentence The brittleness of rule-based systems pushed the field toward knowledge that is induced from data rather than encoded by hand.
Two ways to build a mind

Symbolic vs. statistical AI — and why finance uses both

Symbolic AI
Represents the world through discrete, human-readable symbols; reasons by applying explicit rules. Knowledge is encoded by experts before deployment and stays static as new data arrive.
Home: logic programming, semantic networks, knowledge graphs.
Statistical AI
Represents the world through numerical parameters estimated from data; predicts by evaluating a learned function. Knowledge is implicit in the parameters and changes on retraining.
not mutually exclusive — the finance hybrid A statistical model flags an anomalous transaction; a deterministic compliance rule decides the regulatory report. We lean statistical (markets are data-rich) but return to symbolic methods for interpretability and regulatory constraints.
02

The AI–ML–deep-learning hierarchy

Nesting the boxes: learning from data, three supervisory regimes, depth, and LLMs as a special case.
The core idea

Machine learning is getting better at a task through experience

ML = algorithms that improve at a task by being exposed to data. The whole point is generalization: perform well not just on data you've seen, but on new inputs drawn from the same world.

Supervised learning. Given a training set \(\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n\), learn \(f_\theta:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Y}\) by minimizing \[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ell\big(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i), y_i\big), \] with per-sample loss \(\ell\) (cross-entropy, squared error). This covers classification and regression — the two workhorses of quant finance.
the bigger picture Don't memorise the training set — learn the pattern that survives outside it. Generalization is the one yardstick every method in this book is judged against.
Three flavours of supervision

The same goal under three different learning regimes

The generalization goal recurs in three forms, each mapping to a different class of financial problem.

  • Supervised — credit scoring, default prediction, factor estimation: learn from labeled \((\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\) pairs.
  • Unsupervised — clustering trades into market regimes: find structure without labels.
  • Reinforcement — trading, rebalancing: maximise cumulative reward, learning from interaction.
The catch

The price of learning from data: distribution shift

A model only knows the world it was trained on. When that world changes, the model can be confidently, dangerously wrong.

why the learning paradigm matters in finance A supervised credit model needs labeled historical defaults and is locked to its training period. Fit on a benign credit cycle, it can badly underestimate tail risk in a crisis — a distribution shift with practical and regulatory consequences.
Practical implications threaded through the book
  • Generalization challenges pervade financial ML
  • They motivate careful cross-validation (purging look-ahead bias)
  • Reference: Advances in Financial Machine Learning (Lopez de Prado, 2018)
When the network gets deep

Deep learning: let the model invent its own features

Deep learning is ML with neural networks deep enough that the model learns intermediate representations automatically — nobody hand-specifies the features.

Universal approximation: hierarchical compositions of simple nonlinear functions can represent arbitrarily complex mappings given enough width/depth. Depth confers a specific advantage:
  • Lower layers: general, transferable features (edges, phonemes, collocations).
  • Higher layers: task-specific combinations.
This is why transfer learning is efficient: pre-train broadly, then fine-tune on a narrow task with far fewer labeled examples.
An analogy worth keeping

A deep network aggregates information the way a market does

deep nets ↔ markets Both aggregate information hierarchically across many interacting units / layers, and both are hard to interpret from the outside — which is exactly what motivates the explainability methods later in the book.
Why depth matters in finance
The same hierarchy that makes deep nets powerful — transferable low-level features composed into task-specific representations — is what makes a single pre-trained backbone reusable across many financial text tasks, the theme of the pre-training and transfer-learning slides ahead.
The hierarchy's newest box

An LLM is just a very large next-word predictor

Strip away the hype and a language model is a network that, given the words so far, predicts the next one — trained at enormous scale until that simple skill becomes surprisingly general.

A token is the atomic unit of text; the vocabulary \(\mathcal{V}\) is the finite set of such units. An LLM is a network with billions of parameters, trained on hundreds of billions of tokens, that assigns probabilities to token sequences. The canonical objective is next-token prediction: \[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{LM}} = -\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log P_\theta\big(x_t \mid x_1,\dots,x_{t-1}\big). \]
Why this box is different

What actually makes LLMs special: emergence

Scale + architecture buys more than smoother predictions — at some size, brand-new abilities appear that weren't there before (Wei et al., 2022).

  • Multi-step reasoning and instruction following.
  • In-context learning — adapt to a task from a few prompt examples, with no parameter update.
consequence for finance A single LLM becomes a flexible substrate for earnings-call summarization, regulatory parsing, and strategy explanation — e.g. BloombergGPT (Wu et al., 2023).
03

AI applications in finance

Beliefs and decisions, the reflexivity twist, forecasting / risk / alpha, and the compliance frontier.
What finance actually is

Finance is decision-making under uncertainty — AI helps at two levels

Form beliefs about future states, then act subject to constraints. AI sharpens both halves of that sentence.

Quality of beliefs
Better forecasts; richer models of uncertainty.
Quality of decisions
Portfolio optimization, dynamic programming, reinforcement learning.
reflexivity — finance is not medical imaging The decision procedure can reshape the market and invalidate the forecast that produced it. AI systems in finance are actors within the market, not mere observers — a strategic dimension with no analogue in most prediction problems.
Reflexivity, made concrete

The market maker that changes the market it trades in

A deep-RL market maker
An agent quotes bid/ask at high frequency, earning the spread while managing inventory risk. A deep reinforcement learning agent (a neural-network policy maximising cumulative reward) trains across millions of simulated episodes, learning to adapt to changing liquidity.

The catch is a feedback loop the agent is part of:

\[ \text{agent's actions} \longrightarrow \text{order book} \longrightarrow \text{signals the agent conditions on} \longrightarrow \dots \]
design lesson The loop must be modeled explicitly in the training environment — otherwise the policy looks great in simulation but destabilizes in live markets.
The classic quant trio

Forecasting, risk, and alpha — all transformed, all fragile

The common enemy is a brutal signal-to-noise ratio: returns are near-unpredictable short-horizon, risk models extrapolate into rare tails, and alpha erodes once discovered.

ProblemML contributionCare required
Return forecastingHigh-dim feature spaces; no pre-specified interactionsPurge look-ahead bias; control multiple comparisons
Risk estimationRicher distributions; regime switching; cross-asset learningTails rarely observed; stress periods break Gaussian VaR
Alpha / signalsText-based signals add info absent from price & volumeGenuine alpha erodes fast once public
where text comes in Variance–covariance VaR assumes Gaussian returns and stable correlations — assumptions that fail precisely under stress. Language models parse narrative risk disclosures, turning qualitative uncertainty into quantitative signals.
Beyond the trading desk

Where AI guards the firm: compliance and fraud

AML, sanctions screening, KYC, real-time fraud — all the same problem: spot rare, anomalous patterns in high-volume streams, a natural fit for classifiers trained on labeled historical incidents.

Why language models add particular value here
Much of the relevant information is textual: suspicious-activity reports, correspondence, filings, analyst opinions. An LLM can surface unusual phrasing, flag narrative-vs-quantitative discrepancies, or help interpret new guidance.
regulation constrains design The EU AI Act and GDPR's "right to explanation" can make opaque models inadmissible for certain decisions regardless of accuracy — the recurring performance-vs-interpretability tension.
04

The textual nature of financial data

Structured numbers are backward-looking; the edge increasingly hides in unread language.
Two kinds of data

Structured vs. unstructured — and why the numbers aren't enough

Structured data
Rows & columns; well-defined types; each row a known entity at a time. Prices, accounting items, volumes. Served well by regression, factor models, and time series since the 1960s.
Unstructured data
No pre-defined schema — text, audio, images, video. Needs a pipeline to turn raw content into statistical features.

Limits of structured data: backward-looking; it captures outcomes (prices, earnings) not the reasoning or sentiment behind them; and everyone observes it simultaneously, so the informational edge is limited.

the processing bottleneck A large institution may see tens of thousands of documents per day — too voluminous to read systematically. That bottleneck is a persistent opportunity for ML methods that scale.
Where the words live

Three sources of financial text — and the evidence they carry

SourceContent & evidence
Corporate filingsSEC EDGAR: 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K — narrative MD&A, Risk Factors, forward-looking statements. Loughran–McDonald (2011): 10-K sentiment (finance lexicon) predicts post-filing returns.
News & wiresHigh-frequency public information. Tetlock (2007): pessimistic WSJ "Abreast of the Market" language predicts downward pressure, then mean-reversion.
Earnings callsLive: scripted remarks plus unscripted Q&A. Tone, question types, hedging, even speech pace carry information beyond the reported numbers.
signal hidden in text EPS \(\$\)1.20 beats the \(\$\)1.15 consensus — looks positive. But Risk Factors grew 30% YoY, three new risks concern a politically sensitive supply chain, and the CEO said "challenging" eleven times — a record. The headline reader misses the signal.
Not just unread — genuinely hard

Why financial text resists naive reading

  1. Ambiguous. Meaning depends on author, time, context. "Uncertainty" in a Fed statement ≠ in a founder's letter; "positive outlook" is bullish in equity research but cautious in credit ratings. About 3/4 of Harvard-Inquirer "negative" words are neutral/positive in filings (Loughran–McDonald) — hence a domain lexicon.
  2. High-dimensional & sparse. A 50k-word vocabulary gives a document-term matrix with far more dimensions than observations — needs dimensionality reduction (topic models, embeddings, LM encoders).
  3. Strategically produced. Disclosures are written by lawyers and comms teams aware of liability and market reaction. Taking text at face value gets you systematically misled.
context is a first-class variable Bullish vs. bearish, compliant vs. misleading, consistent vs. contradictory — all need a rich representation of entity, time, regime, and genre. This long-range contextual reasoning is exactly what transformers are built to do.
05

From bag-of-words to large language models

The arc of financial NLP: counting words, then topics, then the attention mechanism that changed everything.
The starting point

Early NLP in finance: counting words and looking them up

Bag-of-Words (BoW) + tf-idf
A document becomes a vector of word counts; all order and grammar are discarded. tf-idf up-weights rare, informative words. Applied to filings since the 1990s for tone and readability (Kearney & Liu, 2014).
Dictionary sentiment
Map words to sentiment scores; net sentiment = (positive − negative) frequency. The Loughran–McDonald lexicon — about 2,700 words in six categories (negative, positive, uncertainty, litigious, strong/weak modal) — is still a benchmark.
One step deeper, same ceiling

Topic models go further — but hit the same wall

Topic models (LSA / LDA)
Learn representations from co-occurrence — each document is a mixture over latent themes (e.g. operational efficiency vs. regulatory risk).
the shared limitation Shallow semantics: words are atomic, meanings are context-independent, and word order carries no information at all.
The turning point

2017: "Attention Is All You Need" rewrote NLP

Vaswani et al. (2017) triggered the largest transition in NLP history. The key move: self-attention lets every position look directly at every other position.

the bigger picture Self-attention resolves long-range dependencies — a pronoun to its antecedent, a Risk Factor to a financial-statement figure — that recurrent nets, plagued by vanishing gradients over long contexts, could not handle reliably.
The mechanism itself

Scaled dot-product attention, in one equation

For each word, attention builds a weighted blend of all the other words — the weights say "how much should I listen to you?" — and a scaling factor keeps the math well-behaved.

For each position \(i\), a weighted sum of value vectors: \[ \mathrm{Attention}(\mathbf{Q},\mathbf{K},\mathbf{V}) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}, \] with \(\mathbf{Q},\mathbf{K}\) the query/key projections (dimension \(d_k\)) and \(\mathbf{V}\) the value projection. The \(\sqrt{d_k}\) scaling keeps dot products from pushing the softmax into a saturated, vanishing-gradient regime. Multiple heads attend to different aspects (syntax, coreference) in parallel.
The economics of pre-training

Pre-train once, fine-tune cheaply many times

Pre-trained transformers — BERT, GPT, RoBERTa and successors — combine self-attention with unsupervised pre-training on very large corpora.

The transfer-learning economics
Rich, context-sensitive representations mean you can fine-tune on downstream tasks with relatively small labeled datasets. Tasks that once needed thousands of labeled examples now need hundreds — lowering the barrier for financial applications where labels are expensive.
  • Lower layers learn transferable structure; higher layers specialise.
  • The same backbone serves sentiment, NER, similarity, summarization, and Q&A.
  • Full attention derivation & financial NLP implications: the LLM-foundations chapter.
Why this matters for finance specifically

Four reasons LLMs fit financial text — and one warning

  1. Long-document reasoning. Context windows of tens-to-hundreds of thousands of tokens process a whole 10-K or call transcript in one pass — no information loss from chunking.
  2. In-context learning. Supply three examples of suspicious patterns, then evaluate a new transaction — few-shot, valuable where labels are scarce.
  3. World knowledge. Geopolitics, supply chains, governance norms let the model contextualise a disclosure. BloombergGPT shows finance pre-training helps, but general frontier models already do well.
  4. Natural-language interface. "Summarize the key risks in this 10-K and flag changes from last year" — no code, democratising text analysis.
LLMs are not a panacea They hallucinate (confident but wrong), are prompt-sensitive, reason opaquely, and may be poorly calibrated on rare, high-stakes events. Deploy carefully — evaluation, monitoring, human oversight.

Appendix · context

The AI effect: why the definition keeps shifting

The AI effect (a ratchet)
Tasks once seen as hallmarks of intelligence — chess, diagnosis, translation — get reclassified as "mere computation" once machines do them reliably. So the boundary of AI is socially constructed as much as it is technically defined, and the field perpetually chases a moving target.

Our pragmatic convention for the book: AI = building systems that perform cognitive tasks; ML = the dominant methodology; DL and LLMs = particular implementations. It avoids endless definitional debate while staying precise enough to work.

Appendix · reference

Formal definitions, collected

Input space \(\mathcal{X}\), output space \(\mathcal{Y}\), parameters \(\theta\in\Theta\), per-sample loss \(\ell:\mathcal{Y}\times\mathcal{Y}\to\mathbb{R}_{\geq0}\). Minimise \(\mathcal{L}(\theta)=\frac1n\sum_i \ell(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i),y_i)\); the goal is generalization to unseen inputs.
A network with billions of parameters, trained on hundreds of billions of tokens, assigning probabilities to token sequences; objective \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{LM}}=-\sum_t \log P_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t})\).
Structured vs. unstructured
Structured = relational rows/columns, typed fields, a known entity-at-time. Unstructured = no schema (text, audio, image, video); needs a feature pipeline.
Appendix · derivation

Why divide by \(\sqrt{d_k}\)? And what multi-head buys

Why scale by \(\sqrt{d_k}\)? If query/key components are \(\sim(0,1)\) i.i.d., then \(\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{q}^\top\mathbf{k})=d_k\). Large logits push the softmax into a saturated regime where gradients vanish; dividing by \(\sqrt{d_k}\) normalises the variance back to \(1\).

Multi-head: run \(h\) heads in parallel subspaces, then concatenate and project. One head may track syntactic agreement while another tracks coreference. In the standard single-head presentation \(d_v=d_k\); multi-head implementations may differ.

Standard configuration (base Transformer)
\(h=8\) heads, \(d_k=d_v=64\), \(d_{\text{model}}=512\), \(L=6\) layers. Modern LLMs: \(L=32\text{–}96\), \(d_{\text{model}}=4{,}096\text{–}8{,}192\).
