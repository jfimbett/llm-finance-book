Recap — the three tools you'll use today

Shapley attribution, faithfulness metrics, and the constrained explanation pipeline.
Recap · attribution

Shapley values: fair credit, and a built-in correctness check

A Shapley value answers "how much did this feature contribute to the decision?" by averaging its marginal effect over every order the features could arrive in (Lundberg & Lee, 2017).

The check you can never skip — efficiency
The attributions must add up to the gap between the actual prediction and the average prediction. If they don't, you computed something wrong.
\[ \phi_i(v) = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|!\,(n-|S|-1)!}{n!}\bigl[v(S \cup \{i\}) - v(S)\bigr]. \] For \(n=2\), each feature has exactly two orderings, so \[ \phi_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[v(\{1\})-v(\emptyset)\bigr] + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[v(\{1,2\})-v(\{2\})\bigr]. \] Efficiency: \[ \phi_1 + \phi_2 = v(\{1,2\}) - v(\emptyset) = f(x) - \mathbb{E}[f(X)]. \]
why it matters for compliance In an adverse-action notice, \(|\phi_i|\) ranks the reasons — the biggest driver is listed first.
Recap · faithfulness & pipeline

Does the explanation actually describe the model?

Two ablation tests turn "this looks like a good explanation" into a number. Sufficiency: keep only the explained words — does the prediction survive? Comprehensiveness: remove them — does the prediction collapse?

Sufficiency (keep only the explained set \(e\)): \[ \text{Suff}(e,f,x) = 1 - |f(x) - f(x_{e(x)})| \quad (\to 1 \text{ is best}). \] Comprehensiveness (remove the explained set): \[ \text{Comp}(e,f,x) = |f(x) - f(x_{\bar{e}(x)})| \quad (\text{high is good}). \]
The constrained explanation pipeline (for Problem 3)
Decide → attribute (SHAP / IG) → constrained LLM translation → compliance filter → human review. The generator may refer only to the attribution feed; it must not invent factors or reference protected characteristics (ECOA).

Problem 1 · 12 min

Shapley values for a two-feature credit game

A credit model scores an applicant on two features: \(x_1=\) debt-to-income, \(x_2=\) revenue trend. The value function \(v(S)\) is the model's \(P(\text{creditworthy})\) when only the features in \(S\) are known.

Coalition \(S\)\(v(S)\)
\(\emptyset\) (baseline)0.50
\(\{1\}\) (DTI only)0.32
\(\{2\}\) (revenue only)0.41
\(\{1,2\}\) (both)0.14
Your tasks
  • A. Compute \(\phi_1\) and \(\phi_2\) from the \(n=2\) formula.
  • B. Verify efficiency: \(\phi_1+\phi_2 = v(\{1,2\})-v(\emptyset)\).
  • C. Which feature drives the rejection? In an ECOA notice, which reason is listed first?
Problem 2 · 12 min

Is this explanation faithful — or just plausible?

A text classifier outputs \(f(x)=0.14\) for a loan application (baseline 0.50). An XAI method claims the set \(e(x)=\{\)"struggled", "declining", "down 30%"\(\}\) accounts for the decision. You run two ablations:

  • Keep only \(e(x)\) (mask the rest): \(f(x_{e(x)}) = 0.19\).
  • Remove only \(e(x)\) (keep the rest): \(f(x_{\bar{e}(x)}) = 0.44\).
Tasks A–C
  • A. Compute sufficiency \(\text{Suff}(e,f,x)\).
  • B. Compute comprehensiveness \(\text{Comp}(e,f,x)\).
  • C. Is it faithful? What would a low sufficiency and a low comprehensiveness have told you?
Task D — the attention trap The attention map for the same input highlights "restaurant" and "market" instead. After ablating those two tokens, \(f\) is unchanged at 0.14. What does that imply about the attention explanation? (Jain & Wallace, 2019)
Problem 3 · 8 min

Audit an LLM-drafted denial letter

An LLM was given the attribution feed (DTI \(-0.18\), revenue \(-0.12\), cash reserve \(-0.07\)) and produced the draft below. Find every compliance defect against ECOA / Reg. B.

draft denial letter "Dear Applicant, your application was declined. Your overall credit profile did not meet our criteria. We also note that applicants in your area and of your demographic tend to default more often, and your business outlook seemed weak. We wish you the best."
Task — flag which the draft violates and why
  1. Vagueness (specificity requirement — Reg. B requires 4–5 principal reasons, stated specifically).
  2. Introduction of a factor not in the attribution feed (hallucinated by the generator).
  3. Reference to a protected characteristic (ECOA prohibition).
  4. Missing required disclosures (statement-of-reasons / consumer-report rights).
Then rewrite the first sentence to be specific and compliant using the feed.

Case study · diagnostic questions

Run it, then interrogate the output

  1. Q1. What is the printed sum of the seven shown tokens? Compare to the full gap \(0.14 - 0.50 = -0.36\). Why is the sum of the shown tokens not exactly \(-0.36\)? (Hint: completeness.)
  2. Q2. Compute the SHAP completeness ratio \(\sum_{i\in e}\phi_i / (f(x)-\mathbb{E}[f(X)])\) for the seven tokens. What fraction of the decision do they explain?

Discussion — when a plausible explanation is dangerous

Decide whether each explanation is safe to ship, and what extra check you'd require.
Discussion · 8 min

Three explanations — which would you ship?

  1. Fluent but unanchored. A retail chatbot writes a polished paragraph explaining a loan rejection — but it was not given the upstream SHAP feed.
  2. Plausible but not faithful. An attention heat-map highlights tokens an expert agrees are relevant, yet ablating them does not change the prediction (Jain & Wallace, 2019).
  3. An invented number. A robo-adviser's suitability narrative cites a 9% expected return that does not appear in the product's Key Information Document (violates MiFID II suitability requirements, ESMA 2018).
Frame your answer around
faithfulness vs. plausibility (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020) · the separation-of-concerns pipeline · protected characteristics (ECOA) · reproducibility for audit.

Solutions

Work the problems first — then check your numbers and reasoning against these.
Solution · Problem 1

Debt-to-income is the stronger driver

Both features push the score down; DTI pushes harder, so it heads the ECOA notice.

Task A. \[ \phi_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}[v(\{1\})-v(\emptyset)] + \tfrac{1}{2}[v(\{1,2\})-v(\{2\})] = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.32-0.50) + \tfrac{1}{2}(0.14-0.41) \] \[ = \tfrac{1}{2}(-0.18) + \tfrac{1}{2}(-0.27) = -0.090 - 0.135 = \mathbf{-0.225}. \] \[ \phi_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.41-0.50) + \tfrac{1}{2}(0.14-0.32) = \tfrac{1}{2}(-0.09) + \tfrac{1}{2}(-0.18) = \mathbf{-0.135}. \] Task B (efficiency). \( \phi_1 + \phi_2 = -0.225 - 0.135 = -0.360 = v(\{1,2\}) - v(\emptyset) = 0.14-0.50.\ \checkmark \)
Task C \(|\phi_1| > |\phi_2|\): debt-to-income is the stronger driver \(\Rightarrow\) listed first in the ECOA notice.
Solution · Problem 2

Faithful by both tests — attention fails one

Tasks A & B

Sufficiency: \(\text{Suff} = 1 - |0.14 - 0.19| = 1 - 0.05 = \mathbf{0.95}\) — high: keeping only \(e(x)\) nearly reproduces the prediction.

Comprehensiveness: \(\text{Comp} = |0.14 - 0.44| = \mathbf{0.30}\) — large: removing \(e(x)\) moves the prediction most of the way back toward the 0.50 baseline.

Task C High sufficiency and high comprehensiveness \(\Rightarrow\) the explanation is faithful: the three tokens both suffice for and are necessary to the decision. Low sufficiency would mean \(e(x)\) omits real drivers; low comprehensiveness would mean the model routes around \(e(x)\).
Task D The attention explanation fails comprehensiveness (ablating its tokens leaves \(f=0.14\) unchanged). It is plausible but not faithful — not audit-grade (Jain & Wallace, 2019).
Solution · Problem 3

Four defects — and a compliant rewrite

Defects in the draft
  1. Vagueness: "credit profile did not meet our criteria" — no specific, quantified reasons. Violates Reg. B specificity.
  2. Unlisted factor: "business outlook seemed weak" is not in the feed — the generator hallucinated it.
  3. Protected characteristic: "applicants … of your demographic" — prohibited basis under ECOA. Severe.
  4. Missing disclosures: no statement-of-reasons right, no free-consumer-report-copy notice.
compliant rewrite of the first sentence "Your application was declined primarily because your debt-to-income ratio of 54% exceeded our maximum of 45%, your revenue declined 28% year-on-year, and your cash reserves covered under one month of operating expenses."

Specific, quantified, drawn only from the feed, no protected factors.

Wrap-up

Four lessons to carry out the door

  • Problem 1: Shapley values are marginal contributions averaged over orderings; efficiency is your built-in correctness check, and \(|\phi_i|\) orders the ECOA reasons.
  • Problem 2: Faithfulness = sufficiency and comprehensiveness. Attention can be plausible yet fail comprehensiveness — not audit-grade (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020).
  • Problem 3: The dangerous failures are hallucinated factors and protected-characteristic leakage. Constrain the generator + add a compliance filter + human review.
  • Case study: Deterministic, seeded explanations are reproducible — the property audits demand and live LIME / KernelSHAP runs do not guarantee.

Appendix · stretch problem

Counterfactual search: the smallest change that flips the decision

A scoring model uses two structured features, DTI \(d\) and cash-reserve months \(c\). Approve iff score \(\geq 0.5\), where \(\text{score} = 0.5 - 1.2(d - 0.45) + 0.1(c - 3)\) and \(d\) is a fraction. An applicant has \(d=0.54\), \(c=0.8\) (matching the chapter example).

Tasks
  1. Compute the current score. Is the applicant approved?
  2. Find the minimal single-feature change in \(d\) (holding \(c\) fixed) that flips the decision. State it as an actionable counterfactual (Wachter et al., 2017).
  3. Repeat for \(c\). Which counterfactual is the smaller relative change?
  4. Why might the \(d\)-counterfactual be preferred even if the \(c\)-change is numerically smaller? (Hint: feasibility & cost-reflecting distance.)
\[ \text{score} = 0.5 - 1.2(0.09) + 0.1(-2.2) = 0.5 - 0.108 - 0.22 = 0.172 < 0.5 \Rightarrow \text{declined}. \] Approval needs the linear term \(\geq 0\): e.g. \[ d \leq 0.45 - \frac{0.1(c-3)}{1.2}. \] Articulate the resulting threshold as a plain counterfactual the applicant could act on.
