01

Recap — the four formulas you will use

Mean-variance, Black–Litterman, out-of-sample Sharpe, and turnover cost.
Recap · keep these four close

Four formulas, in plain words first

  • Mean-variance — a portfolio is a trade-off between expected return and risk; "best Sharpe" tilts toward high-return, low-risk assets.
  • Black–Litterman — start from the market's equilibrium view, then nudge it with your views, weighted by how confident you are.
  • Out-of-sample Sharpe — return per unit of risk, measured only on data the model never saw.
  • Turnover cost — every time you move the weights you pay; trading is not free.
Mean-variance: portfolio mean and variance, and the tangency (max-Sharpe) weights: \[ \mu_p=\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{w},\quad \sigma_p^2=\mathbf{w}^\top\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}, \quad \mathbf{w}^{\text{SR}}\propto\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}. \] Black–Litterman posterior mean (view $q_i=\alpha s_i+\Pi_i$): \[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}= \bigl[(\tau\boldsymbol{\Sigma})^{-1}+\mathbf{P}^\top\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1}\mathbf{P}\bigr]^{-1} \bigl[(\tau\boldsymbol{\Sigma})^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Pi}+\mathbf{P}^\top\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1}\mathbf{q}\bigr]. \] Out-of-sample Sharpe (annualised) and turnover cost: \[ \text{SR}^{\text{OOS}}=\frac{\bar r_p^{\text{OOS}}}{\hat\sigma_p^{\text{OOS}}}\sqrt{252}, \qquad \text{TC}_t=c\sum_i|\Delta w_{i,t}|,\;\; \Delta w_{i,t}=w_{i,t}-w_{i,t-1}. \]

Problems — from signal to position

Three short numerical exercises on trust, views, and costs.
Problem 1 · 12 min

How much should you trust the LLM's returns?

An LLM reads each firm's news flow and outputs estimated monthly excess returns for two assets. The covariance comes from price history. Question: how aggressively should you size on those estimates?

The data
\[ \boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{LLM}}=\begin{pmatrix}0.8\%\\0.4\%\end{pmatrix},\qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}=\begin{pmatrix}0.0036 & 0.0009\\ 0.0009 & 0.0016\end{pmatrix}. \] ($\sigma_1=6\%$, $\sigma_2=4\%$, $\rho=0.375$.)
  • A. Min-variance — the "trust the LLM nothing" baseline, which ignores $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ entirely: \(w^\star=\dfrac{\sigma_2^2-\sigma_{12}}{\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2-2\sigma_{12}}\).
  • B. Tangency — "fully trust the LLM": \(\mathbf{w}^{\text{SR}}\propto\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{LLM}}\), normalised.
  • C. Signal reliability — suppose the LLM over-states asset 1 by 50% ($\mu_1:0.8\%\to0.4\%$). Recompute tangency. How far do the weights move? What does that say about sizing on a noisy LLM signal vs. shrinking toward $1/N$ (DeMiguel et al., 2009)?
Problem 2 · 12 min

From an LLM score to a Black–Litterman view

A raw sentiment score is not yet a forecast. Black–Litterman wants two things from you: a view value (where you think return goes) and a view uncertainty (how sure you are).

Setup
LLM scores firm $i$'s earnings call: $s_i=+0.6$ (bullish). Equilibrium prior $\Pi_i=0.30\%$ monthly. Calibration constant $\alpha=0.8\%$; view-noise scale $\sigma_q^2=(0.5\%)^2$.
  • A. Compute the view value $q_i=\alpha s_i+\Pi_i$.
  • B. Compute the view uncertainty $\Omega_{ii}=\sigma_q^2(1-|s_i|)$.
  • C. A second firm scores $s_j=+0.05$ (near-neutral). Compute $q_j,\ \Omega_{jj}$. Which view moves the posterior more, and why?
  • D. Conceptual — you accidentally calibrate $\alpha$ on the same window you evaluate on. Name the bias and the fix.
hint Posterior pull toward a view scales with view precision $\Omega_{ii}^{-1}$. Strong signal $\Rightarrow$ small $\Omega_{ii}\Rightarrow$ large precision.
Problem 3 · 10 min

Does the signal survive trading costs?

A backtest's headline return is gross. The honest number is what's left after you pay to trade — and high turnover can quietly eat a whole strategy.

Setup — a daily long-short sentiment strategy
  • Gross (pre-cost) annualised return $4.0\%$, volatility $8.0\%$.
  • Average one-way daily turnover $20\%$; cost $c=0.04\%$ per unit turnover.
  • 252 trading days.
  • A. Annualised transaction cost $\approx \text{turnover}\times252\times c$.
  • B. Net annualised return $=$ gross \(-\) cost. Net Sharpe (assume vol unchanged).
  • C. By how much must one-way turnover fall to keep net return $\ge 2\%$, holding $c$ fixed?
  • D. Capacity — the strategy wants \(\$\)10M in a stock with \(\$\)50M ADV. What participation rate is that, and does it breach the $5$–$10\%$ guideline?
03

Case study — a walk-forward sentiment backtest

Run a leakage-free backtest of a language factor and read off the cost-adjusted OOS Sharpe.
Case study · the plan

The minimal honest backtest

A backtest is only believable if the model never peeks at the future. Walk-forward enforces that: fit on the past, evaluate strictly on the next, untouched window.

What you will do
  1. Build quintile language-factor returns from per-firm sentiment scores.
  2. Slide the walk-forward window; fit calibration on train, evaluate on test.
  3. Subtract turnover costs; compute $\text{SR}^{\text{OOS}}$.
  4. Answer three diagnostics on leakage and decay.

Notebook: code/notebooks/10-portfolio-quant-trading/demo.ipynb

Frontier figure: gen_efficient_frontier.py (deterministic, no network).

discipline Any parameter ($\alpha$, $\lambda$, $W_{\max}$) touched by the test window invalidates the result.
Case study · the code

Two functions: the factor and the walk-forward

import numpy as np, pandas as pd

def language_factor(scores, fwd_ret):     # firms x dates, scores in [-1,1]
    """Top-minus-bottom quintile return from demeaned sentiment."""
    s = scores.sub(scores.mean(axis=0), axis=1)   # cross-sectional demean
    lf = {}
    for t in s.columns:
        q = s[t].rank(pct=True)
        top, bot = q >= 0.8, q <= 0.2
        lf[t] = fwd_ret.loc[top, t].mean() - fwd_ret.loc[bot, t].mean()
    return pd.Series(lf)

def walk_forward_sharpe(lf, train=252, step=21, turnover=0.15, c=5e-4):
    oos = []
    for m in range(0, len(lf) - train - step, step):
        test = lf.iloc[m + train : m + train + step]  # never seen in fit
        oos.append(test - turnover * c)               # cost-adjust
    r = pd.concat(oos)
    return float(r.mean() / r.std() * np.sqrt(252))
# sharpe = walk_forward_sharpe(language_factor(scores, fwd_ret))
Case study · diagnostics

Three questions to ask after you run it

  • Q1 (leakage). The demean step uses scores.mean(axis=0) — a cross-sectional (per-date) mean. Why is that safe, whereas a time-series mean over all dates would leak?
  • Q2 (decay). Replace raw scores with EWMA-decayed scores ($\lambda\in\{0.1,0.3,0.5\}$). How does $\text{SR}^{\text{OOS}}$ move? Relate to the 1–7 day half-life rule.
  • Q3 (costs). Sweep $c\in\{0,\,5,\,10\}$ bps. At what cost does the Sharpe cross below the $1.5$ paper-trading threshold? Connect to the capacity constraint.
optional extension Feed the per-firm view $q_i=\alpha s_i+\Pi_i$ into a Black–Litterman optimizer instead of the raw quintile sort. Does BL's prior shrinkage raise or lower turnover? Why?

Solutions

Worked answers to Problems 1–3.
Solution · Problem 1

One 50% error swings the book by ~37 points

Fully trusting the LLM looks fine — until you nudge one input. A single noisy estimate collapses the "optimal" tangency portfolio all the way back to the no-trust baseline.

  • A. Min-variance: \(w^\star=\dfrac{0.0016-0.0009}{0.0034}\approx 0.21\Rightarrow \mathbf{w}_{\min}\approx(0.21,\,0.79)\).
  • B. Tangency: \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}\propto(9.2,\,6.7)\times10^{-6}\Rightarrow \mathbf{w}^{\text{SR}}\approx(0.58,\,0.42)\).
  • C. Reliability: with $\mu_1$ corrected to $0.4\%$, \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}\propto(2.8,\,10.8)\times10^{-6}\Rightarrow \mathbf{w}^{\text{SR}}\approx(0.21,\,0.79)\) — back to min-variance.
the takeaway A single 50% error in expected return swings the allocation by ~37 points. Position sizing is brutally sensitive to LLM signal noise — which is exactly why you shrink toward $1/N$ or use Black–Litterman rather than plug raw LLM $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ into mean-variance (DeMiguel et al., 2009).
Solution · Problem 2

Confident view pulls the posterior; neutral view defers

Firm $i$ ($s_i=0.6$): \[ q_i=0.8\%\times0.6+0.30\%=\mathbf{0.78\%},\quad \Omega_{ii}=(0.5\%)^2(1-0.6)=\mathbf{1.0\times10^{-5}}. \] Firm $j$ ($s_j=0.05$): \[ q_j=0.8\%\times0.05+0.30\%=\mathbf{0.34\%},\quad \Omega_{jj}=(0.5\%)^2(1-0.05)=\mathbf{2.375\times10^{-5}}. \]
  • C. View $i$ moves the posterior more: bigger deviation from the prior ($0.78\%$ vs. $0.34\%$) and higher precision ($\Omega_{ii}^{-1}$ is $\approx2.4\times$ larger). The near-neutral signal $j$ lets the prior dominate — exactly the intent of $\Omega_{ii}=\sigma_q^2(1-|s_i|)$.
  • D. Calibrating $\alpha$ on the evaluation window is model-selection look-ahead leakage. Fix: estimate $\alpha$ only on data predating each test window (walk-forward).
Solution · Problem 3

Costs halve the Sharpe; the strategy is cost-bound

A. Annualised cost: \[ 0.20\times252\times0.04\%=\mathbf{2.016\%}\ \text{p.a.} \] B. Net return / Sharpe: \[ \text{net}=4.0\%-2.016\%\approx\mathbf{1.98\%},\quad \text{net SR}=\frac{1.98\%}{8.0\%}\approx\mathbf{0.25}\ \text{(vs. gross }0.50). \] C. Turnover for net $\ge2\%$: \[ \text{turnover}\times252\times0.0004\le0.02\Rightarrow \text{turnover}\le\frac{0.02}{0.1008}\approx\mathbf{19.8\%}. \]
  • A–B. Cost $\approx 2.0\%$ p.a.; net return $\approx 1.98\%$; net Sharpe $\approx 0.25$ — costs halve the Sharpe, nearly destroying a marginal signal.
  • C. Turnover must fall below $\approx19.8\%$ — barely under the current $20\%$. No slack: the strategy is cost-bound.
  • D. Capacity: \(\$\)10M / \(\$\)50M ADV $=\mathbf{20\%}$ participation — double the $5$–$10\%$ guideline. Expect material price impact; scale the book down.
Wrap-up

Four takeaways — and where Practical 11 goes

  • P1: On two assets, $1/N$ already sits near tangency — the optimization prize is small once $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ is estimated.
  • P2: $q_i=\alpha s_i+\Pi_i$, $\Omega_{ii}=\sigma_q^2(1-|s_i|)$ turns a raw LLM score into a confidence-weighted BL view; neutral signals defer to the prior.
  • P3: Costs and turnover are first-order — a 4% gross strategy can net 2% and lose half its Sharpe; watch ADV participation.
  • Case study: walk-forward + cross-sectional demean + cost adjustment is the minimal honest backtest; leakage is the silent killer.

Appendix · stretch problem A

A CVaR budget under a textual risk spike

VaR tells you a threshold loss; CVaR tells you the average loss beyond it. A textual risk signal can feed straight into the risk model that sets the position cap.

Setup
Daily returns are fat-tailed with $\text{VaR}_{0.05}=-3.0\%$ and expected shortfall (CVaR) $=-4.5\%$ in the worst 5%.
  • A. Interpret both numbers in one sentence each.
  • B. A textual risk signal $v_{t-1}$ spikes; the GARCH update \(\sigma_t^2=\omega+\varphi_1\varepsilon_{t-1}^2+\beta_1\sigma_{t-1}^2+\kappa v_{t-1}\) with \(\kappa>0\) raises \(\sigma_t\) by 20%. Qualitatively, what happens to \(\text{CVaR}_{0.05}\) and to the CVaR-constrained weight on this position?
  • C. Why is CVaR preferred to VaR for optimization? (Hint: coherence; convex / LP-representable.)
Appendix · stretch problem B

Can you trust the LLM strategy's track record?

Before allocating, ask how long a record you would need to believe a reported Sharpe. Fat left tails — common in text-driven strategies — inflate that requirement sharply.

Your LLM news-sentiment strategy reports a daily $\text{SR}^*=0.08$. The minimum track record length: \[ T^*=1+\Bigl(1-\gamma_1\text{SR}^*+\tfrac{\gamma_2-1}{4}(\text{SR}^*)^2\Bigr)\frac{Z_{1-\alpha}^2}{(\text{SR}^*)^2}, \] with skewness $\gamma_1=-0.5$, kurtosis $\gamma_2=6$, $Z_{0.95}=1.645$.
  • A. Compute $T^*$ in trading days, then in years ($\approx252$/yr).
  • B. Repeat for a symmetric, normal benchmark ($\gamma_1=0,\gamma_2=3$). How much does the fat left tail inflate the required record?
  • C. The strategy has only 18 months of genuine out-of-sample data (the model was pre-trained on overlapping earlier text). Given your $T^*$, is that enough to deploy? Connect to temporal leakage for text-trained signals.
