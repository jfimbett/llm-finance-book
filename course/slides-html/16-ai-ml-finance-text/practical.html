01

Recap: the ideas the problems lean on

Where text sits in the AI hierarchy, and the three formulas you will use with your pencil.
The map

Why statistics, not hand-coded rules, runs the desk

the bigger picture AI \(\supset\) ML \(\supset\) DL \(\supset\) LLM. Statistical AI — learn the parameters from data — dominates finance over symbolic AI — hand-coded rules — because markets are data-rich. But symbolic rules come back for compliance and interpretability.

Carry one thing into every problem today: text is hard for three specific reasons.

  • Ambiguous — about \(3/4\) of general "negative" words are neutral or positive inside filings \(\Rightarrow\) use a finance lexicon.
  • Sparse — a 50k-word vocabulary dwarfs the number of observations you actually have.
  • Strategic — disclosures are written by lawyers, not handed to you as unbiased signals.
The toolbox

Three formulas you will use today

A sentiment score is a fraction; a bag-of-words vector is an average; attention is a weighted average where the weights come from how well words match.

Dictionary sentiment
Net polarity = how positive minus how negative, per word.
Mean BoW embedding
Average the word vectors — order is thrown away by construction.
Scaled dot-product attention
Match queries to keys, soften into weights, average the values.
Dictionary sentiment of a document: \[ \mathrm{Sentiment} = \frac{\#\text{positive words} - \#\text{negative words}}{\#\text{total words}}. \] Mean bag-of-words embedding (order-blind by construction): \[ \bar{\varphi}(d) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\varphi(w_i). \] Scaled dot-product attention: \[ \mathrm{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V, \qquad \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{x})_i = \frac{e^{x_i}}{\sum_j e^{x_j}}. \] Tip: subtract \(\max(\mathbf{x})\) before exponentiating for numerical stability.

Problem 1 · 12 min

Dictionary sentiment, and why domain matters

Snippet — 10-K risk factors, stylised
"Demand remained positive, but litigation exposure and macroeconomic uncertainty could adversely affect results; management believes the outlook is strong."

Three tasks — classify the bold words, expose the general-dictionary trap, then score it.

  • Task A. Using Loughran–McDonald's six categories (negative, positive, uncertainty, litigious, strong/weak modal), classify the four bold words. Which category do "litigation" and "uncertainty" fall into?
  • Task B. A general (Harvard Inquirer) dictionary tags "litigation," "liability," and "tax" as negative — yet about \(3/4\) of Harvard negatives are neutral or positive in filings. What does that imply for a naive negative-word count on legal-heavy filings?
  • Task C. Compute net sentiment \((\#\text{pos}-\#\text{neg})/\#\text{total}\) for the snippet (10 words after dropping stop-words), where only the positive and negative categories count toward polarity.
Problem 2 · 10 min

Mean pooling is order-blind

Two sentences, same words, opposite meaning. Watch a bag-of-words representation collapse them into one identical vector.

The pair
  • \(A\): "Revenue rose, costs fell."
  • \(B\): "Costs rose, revenue fell."
  • Task A. The sentences share the same multiset of words. Show that the mean BoW embedding \(\bar\varphi(A)=\bar\varphi(B)\) exactly. Why?
  • Task B. A long-only signal goes long on positive-sentiment filings. If your representation can't tell \(A\) from \(B\), what failure mode appears when the subject of "rose" vs. "fell" matters?
  • Task C. Name the architectural ingredient (from the lecture) that restores order sensitivity, and say in one line why it can connect "revenue" to "rose" across a long sentence where an RNN struggles.
Problem 3 · 8 min

A \(3\times3\) self-attention trace

Sequence: "risk", "increased", "materially". Head dimension \(d_k = 2\). Run the attention formula by hand.

The matrices
\[ Q = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix},\quad K = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix},\quad V = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \]
  • Task A. Compute \(S = QK^\top\), entry \((i,j)=\mathbf{q}_i^\top\mathbf{k}_j\).
  • Task B. Scale: \(\tilde S = S/\sqrt{2}\) (two decimals).
  • Task C. Softmax row-wise \(\to A\).
  • Task D. Output \(O = AV\).
discussion Which token does "risk" attend to most? If we set \(d_k=64\) with the same raw dot products, are the weights sharper or flatter?

Case study · the code (1/2)

Data and the dictionary baseline

Three snippets — one clearly positive, one legal-heavy negative, one order-sensitive — and a five-word positive/negative list to score them.

from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer, util

SNIPPETS = [
    "Revenue rose 12% and margins expanded.",          # clearly positive
    "Litigation exposure and uncertainty increased.",  # negative, legal-heavy
    "Costs fell, revenue fell, outlook unchanged.",    # order-sensitive
]
POS = {"rose", "expanded", "growth", "strong", "gain"}
NEG = {"fell", "litigation", "uncertainty", "loss", "decline"}

def dict_sentiment(text):
    w = text.lower().replace(",", " ").replace(".", " ").split()
    return (sum(t in POS for t in w) - sum(t in NEG for t in w)) / len(w)
Case study · the code (2/2)

Embedding similarity to a "good news" anchor

Encode each snippet, encode an explicitly bullish anchor sentence, and measure cosine similarity — the embedding's version of "how positive is this?"

model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")
anchor = model.encode("This is good news for shareholders.",
                      normalize_embeddings=True)
for s in SNIPPETS:
    d = dict_sentiment(s)
    emb = model.encode(s, normalize_embeddings=True)
    sim = float(util.cos_sim(emb, anchor))
    print(f"dict={d:+.3f}  embed_sim={sim:+.3f}  | {s}")
what to watch for The dictionary reads isolated words; the embedding reads the whole sentence in context. The gap between them is the whole point of the exercise.

Solutions

Worked answers to the three problems — surface intuition first, the arithmetic one click away.
Solution · Problem 1

The general dictionary gets the sign wrong

  • Task A — categories. "positive" \(\to\) positive; "strong" \(\to\) strong modal (positive-leaning); "litigation" \(\to\) litigious; "uncertainty" \(\to\) uncertainty. Litigious and uncertainty are not polarity-negative per se — they are their own categories.
  • Task B. A general dictionary tags "litigation" as negative, so a naive negative count systematically over-penalises legal-heavy filings — exactly the Loughran–McDonald finding (about \(3/4\) of Harvard negatives are neutral/positive in filings).
With only positive/negative counting toward polarity: positives \(=\{\text{positive, strong}\}=2\), negatives \(=0\) (litigious and uncertainty excluded). \[ \text{Net} = \frac{2-0}{10} = \mathbf{+0.20}. \] A general dictionary would instead score this snippet negative — the wrong sign.
Solution · Problem 2

Same words in, same vector out — that's the bug

  • Task A. Mean pooling sums per-word embeddings then divides by \(n\). Addition is commutative, so any permutation of the same words gives the identical mean. \(A\) and \(B\) share the multiset \(\{\text{revenue, rose, costs, fell}\}\Rightarrow \bar\varphi(A)=\bar\varphi(B)\) exactly.
  • Task B. A sentiment-driven long-only signal can't tell "revenue rose" (bullish) from "revenue fell" (bearish): it takes the same position on opposite fundamentals — a sign error no amount of training fixes if the representation discards order.
  • Task C. Self-attention (with positional information) restores order sensitivity. It can directly connect "revenue" to "rose" because every position attends to every other in one step — no sequential path to decay, unlike an RNN whose gradient vanishes over long spans.
Solution · Problem 3

"Risk" attends to itself and "increased"

Because \(K=Q\) here, the score matrix is symmetric and the diagonal is the largest entry per row — each token first looks at itself.

With \(K=Q\), \(S=QQ^\top\): \[ S = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \tilde S = S/\sqrt{2} \approx \begin{pmatrix} 0.71 & 0.71 & 0.00 \\ 0.71 & 1.41 & 0.71 \\ 0.00 & 0.71 & 0.71 \end{pmatrix}. \] Row-wise softmax \(\to A\), then output \(O = AV\) with \(V=\big[(2,0),(0,2),(1,1)\big]^\top\): \[ A \approx \begin{pmatrix} 0.422 & 0.422 & 0.156 \\ 0.270 & 0.461 & 0.270 \\ 0.156 & 0.422 & 0.422 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad O \approx \begin{pmatrix} 1.00 & 1.00 \\ 0.81 & 1.19 \\ 0.73 & 1.27 \end{pmatrix}. \]
the discussion answer "risk" attends most to itself and "increased" (tied at \(0.422\)). Larger logits make softmax sharper — which is exactly why the \(\sqrt{d_k}\) scaling exists: to counter that saturation as \(d_k\) grows.
Wrap-up

Four takeaways — and where the next practical goes

  • Problem 1. A domain lexicon avoids the systematic negative bias a general dictionary inflicts on legal-heavy filings — the canonical motivation for finance-specific NLP.
  • Problem 2. Bag-of-words mean pooling is provably order-blind; opposite-meaning sentences collapse to one vector. Self-attention is the fix.
  • Problem 3. Attention is a weighted average of value vectors; weights are softmaxed scaled dot products; \(\sqrt{d_k}\) keeps softmax out of its saturated, vanishing-gradient regime.
  • Case study. Dictionary and embedding scores disagree exactly where context matters ("costs fell" is good; "positive" is cautious in ratings) — the case for climbing to encoders and LLMs.

tf-idf by hand

Three one-sentence "documents"
  • \(d_1\): "revenue revenue growth"
  • \(d_2\): "revenue decline"

With \(\mathrm{tf}\) = raw count and \(\mathrm{idf}(w)=\log\frac{N}{df(w)}\) (\(N=3\)), compute the tf-idf weight of "revenue" in \(d_1\) and of "growth" in \(d_3\). Which term is up-weighted, and why does tf-idf down-weight a word appearing in every document?

the point tf-idf rewards rare, document-specific words — the bag-of-words refinement that made 1990s filing analysis workable before embeddings existed.
Appendix · stretch B

Reflexivity in a backtest

A text-based "negative-sentiment \(\Rightarrow\) short" signal backtests with a Sharpe of 1.4 over 2015–2019. Will it survive contact with the live market?

  • Task A. Name two reasons the live Sharpe may be far lower: (i) the signal becomes public; (ii) the strategy's own trades move prices (reflexivity).
  • Task B. LLMs can reconstruct historical series from parametric memory, producing spurious predictability. If your sentiment model was pre-trained on data overlapping the backtest window, what bias contaminates the result, and how would you guard against it?
the point AI systems in finance are actors within the market, not observers — look-ahead and reflexivity are first-order design concerns, not footnotes.
