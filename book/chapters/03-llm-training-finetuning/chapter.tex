\section{Data for LLMs: Sources and Preprocessing}
\label{sec:data-sources}

\begin{context}
Every capability of a large language model --- its grammar, its factual knowledge,
its ability to reason about numbers, and its stylistic range --- is a function of
what it has read.  Unlike classical econometric models, which are fit on
carefully curated datasets with known provenance, LLMs absorb their priors from
corpora that are measured not in rows and columns but in petabytes of raw text.
The composition of that corpus determines what the model can and cannot do.

For financial applications the stakes are unusually high.  A model trained
primarily on Reddit discussions will handle casual investment commentary gracefully
but may hallucinate terminology from earnings releases.  A model trained on SEC
filings and central-bank communiqu\'{e}s will parse regulatory prose with
precision but may struggle with the elliptical shorthand of trading desks.
Understanding how training corpora are constructed --- and how to influence that
construction --- is therefore a practical skill, not a theoretical luxury.

The data pipeline for a large language model has four sequential stages:
\emph{sourcing} (where does the text come from?), \emph{tokenisation}
(how is text converted to integers?), \emph{quality filtering}
(which documents survive and in what form?), and \emph{mixing} (in what
proportions are sources blended?).  The stages interact: small decisions at
the sourcing stage propagate to the final model in ways that are difficult
to measure after the fact.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Text Corpora: Web Crawls, Books, Wikipedia, and Code}
\label{subsec:text-corpora}

The dominant source of pre-training text is the World Wide Web.  Common Crawl,
a non-profit organisation that has been crawling the public web since 2008,
makes its data freely available.  A single monthly snapshot contains
approximately 200--400 terabytes of compressed HTML, representing tens of
billions of distinct web pages.  The scale is astonishing: Common Crawl dwarfs
every other publicly available text corpus.  But raw web data is also noisy:
it contains boilerplate navigation text, duplicate content across mirror sites,
spam, adult material, and machine-generated filler.

The Pile \cite{gao2020pile} was an early attempt to move beyond raw Common Crawl.
Its 800\,GB of text were drawn from 22 curated sub-sources: web crawls,
books, academic papers (PubMed, arXiv), code repositories (GitHub), and
encyclopaedic content (Wikipedia, DM Mathematics).  The curation strategy
tested a hypothesis: that diversity of register and subject matter improves
generalisation.  \citet{penedo2023refinedweb} later challenged this.  They
showed that a single high-quality web source, filtered aggressively, matches
the performance of much more heterogeneous mixtures.  Their RefinedWeb corpus
applied a strict deduplication and quality-filtering pipeline to CommonCrawl
and achieved strong benchmark performance with less data than The Pile.

Beyond web text, several domain-specific sub-corpora have become standard
ingredients:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Books}: Project Gutenberg (public-domain literature) and
        Books3 (a larger, legally contested scrape) provide long-form prose
        with complex narrative structure and broad vocabulary.

  \item \emph{Wikipedia}: The English-language Wikipedia contains
        approximately six million articles covering factual knowledge in a
        consistent encyclopaedic register.  Its structured citation network
        also makes it useful for factual grounding.

  \item \emph{Code}: GitHub repositories are included in most contemporary
        pre-training corpora.  Code training improves not only code generation
        but also structured reasoning in non-code tasks, presumably because
        programs have unambiguous semantics and explicit control flow.

  \item \emph{Scientific literature}: Papers from arXiv, Semantic Scholar,
        and PubMed provide technical vocabulary, quantitative reasoning, and
        formal mathematical notation.  For financial LLMs, economics and
        finance preprints are a natural inclusion.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Financial Data: Filings, Earnings Calls, News, and Alternative Data}
\label{subsec:financial-data}

General-purpose corpora contain remarkably little financial text.  Finance-specific
text --- earnings transcripts, research reports, regulatory filings, wire news,
and market commentary --- constitutes fewer than 0.5\% of the tokens in a typical
web-crawled corpus \cite{wu2023bloomberggpt}.  This proportion is far
smaller than finance's economic weight, which explains why general-purpose LLMs
perform poorly on specialised financial tasks without domain adaptation.

The major sources of financial training text fall into four categories:

\emph{Regulatory filings.}  The U.S.~Securities and Exchange Commission
requires public companies to file annual reports (Form 10-K), quarterly reports
(Form 10-Q), and material event disclosures (Form 8-K) through its
EDGAR\footnote{Electronic Data Gathering, Analysis, and Retrieval.} system.
These filings are rich in legal and accounting language, contain numerical
tables, and are accompanied by management discussion sections in natural
English.  The full EDGAR archive dates to 1993 and contains tens of millions
of documents.  European equivalents include filings with the FCA (UK),
the AMF (France), and documents submitted under the Transparency Directive.

\emph{Earnings call transcripts.}  Conference calls with analysts and investors,
transcribed to text, capture real-time management commentary and Q\&A in a
conversational register that differs markedly from formal filings.  Services
such as Refinitiv, FactSet, and Seeking Alpha provide historical transcript
archives.  BloombergGPT \cite{wu2023bloomberggpt} used a corpus of approximately
708 billion tokens (363\,B finance-specific from Bloomberg's archives plus
345\,B general-purpose tokens), described further in
Section~\ref{subsec:bloomberggpt}.  The cost and data requirements
of such from-scratch training motivated open-source alternatives: FinGPT
\cite{yang2023fingpt_lora} democratises access by applying LoRA fine-tuning
to publicly available foundation models, reducing training costs to under
\$300 while achieving competitive performance on standard financial NLP tasks.

\emph{Financial news and wire services.}  Reuters, Dow Jones Newswire, and
Bloomberg News produce high-frequency text tightly coupled to market events.
News text is time-stamped, which creates opportunities for temporal alignment
with price data, but also introduces survivorship and look-ahead bias if the
corpus is not handled carefully.

\emph{Alternative data.}  Social media (Twitter/X, Reddit's r/wallstreetbets,
StockTwits), satellite imagery captions, patent filings, and supply-chain
data increasingly supplement traditional text.  These sources require careful
normalisation: social media in particular contains abbreviations, hashtags,
emojis, and sarcasm that confuse standard tokenisers trained on edited prose.

\begin{remark}[Temporal alignment and look-ahead bias]
\label{rem:temporal-alignment}
Financial text is inherently temporal: a 10-K filed on 2 March 2021 reflects
information available as of that date.  When assembling a training corpus,
it is essential to record document timestamps and ensure that evaluation
examples do not appear in the training set.  This is the text-data analogue of
the look-ahead bias familiar from quantitative finance.  Models trained on
data that includes future information will show inflated performance on
in-sample evaluations and fail catastrophically out-of-sample.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Tokenisation and Vocabulary Construction}
\label{subsec:tokenisation}

A language model operates over a discrete vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$.  The
tokeniser is the function that maps a Unicode string to a sequence of integers
$t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_T$ where each $t_i \in \{0, 1, \ldots, |\mathcal{V}|-1\}$,
and its inverse maps the sequence back to a string.

A \emph{corpus} is a finite collection of text documents used as training data.

\begin{definition}[Byte-Pair Encoding]
\label{def:bpe}
Let $\Sigma$ be the set of bytes (values 0--255).  The Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)
algorithm \cite{sennrich2016bpe} initialises the vocabulary as $\mathcal{V}_0 = \Sigma$
and iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent pair of tokens in the training
corpus:
\begin{equation}
  (a^*, b^*) = \argmax_{(a, b)} \; \text{count}(a, b \mid \text{corpus}),
  \label{eq:bpe-merge}
\end{equation}
replacing every occurrence of the pair $(a, b)$ in the corpus with a new token
$[ab]$, and setting $\mathcal{V}_{k+1} = \mathcal{V}_k \cup \{[ab]\}$.
The process repeats until the vocabulary reaches a target size $|\mathcal{V}|$,
typically 32{,}000--100{,}000 tokens.
\end{definition}

BPE has several attractive properties.  It handles out-of-vocabulary words
gracefully because any Unicode string can be represented as a sequence of bytes,
and the byte-level fallback is always available.  It produces shorter token
sequences for frequent words (which become single tokens) and longer sequences
for rare words (which are broken into sub-word pieces).  For financial text,
this matters: tickers like \texttt{AAPL} and legal terms like
\textit{securitisation} may receive quite different segmentations depending on
their frequency in the training corpus.

An alternative to BPE is the \emph{unigram language model} tokeniser
implemented in SentencePiece \cite{kudo2018sentencepiece}.  Instead of
building a vocabulary by merging, the unigram approach starts with a large
candidate vocabulary and prunes tokens that contribute least to the training
data likelihood under a unigram language model.  This produces a stochastic
tokeniser that samples from a distribution over segmentations, which has
regularisation benefits during training.

The fertility of a tokeniser is its average number of tokens per word.
For English prose, BPE-50k achieves a fertility of roughly 1.3, meaning
that the average English word maps to 1.3 tokens.  For financial text,
fertility tends to be higher: specialised terms, alphanumeric codes (CUSIP,
ISIN, SEDOL), and accounting line items may split into multiple sub-word
pieces.  A ticker like \texttt{MSFT} might tokenise as three tokens
(\texttt{MS}, \texttt{FT} and a suffix) in a general-purpose vocabulary,
whereas a finance-trained vocabulary would represent it as a single token.
This asymmetry affects both input length (and therefore compute cost) and the
quality of downstream representations.

A domain-specific vocabulary trained on financial text will have strictly
lower fertility on that domain than a general-purpose vocabulary, reducing
context-window consumption and enabling longer documents to fit within the
model's attention window.

\subsection{Data Quality: Deduplication, Filtering, and Cleaning}
\label{subsec:data-quality}

Raw web data is approximately 90\% noise by weight.  Transforming it into
useful training material requires a sequential pipeline; each step introduces
its own failure modes.

\emph{Language identification.}  A fastText or n-gram classifier scores each
document for its most likely language.  Documents below a confidence threshold
are discarded.  For financial corpora, multilingual documents are common:
a company report may contain French, English, and German sections.  Discarding
such documents loses useful code-switching data.

\emph{Heuristic quality filters.}  RefinedWeb \cite{penedo2023refinedweb} applies
a cascade of rule-based filters: minimum document length, maximum repetition
fraction (documents where the most common 5-gram appears in more than 30\% of
lines are likely machine-generated), and URL blacklists for known low-quality
domains.  These filters are cheap to apply and remove the bulk of spam.

\emph{Perplexity filtering.}  A small reference language model, pre-trained on
high-quality text, assigns a perplexity score to each document.  Documents with
perplexity above a threshold are discarded as incoherent.  This filter is
computationally more expensive but catches noise that heuristics miss.

\emph{Deduplication.}  Perhaps the most impactful single step is deduplication.
Training on duplicate documents causes the model to over-fit to repeated content,
inflating apparent memorisation and degrading generalisation.  MinHash LSH
computes locality-sensitive hash fingerprints of each document's shingles (overlapping
n-gram sequences) and groups near-duplicates for removal.  Exact deduplication
handles byte-identical copies.

A \emph{$k$-shingle} (or $k$-gram) of a document is a contiguous sequence of $k$ tokens; the shingle set $\mathcal{S}_k(d)$ is the set of all such sequences in document $d$.

\begin{definition}[MinHash similarity]
\label{def:minhash}
Let $A$ and $B$ be two documents represented as sets of $k$-shingles.
The Jaccard similarity is $J(A, B) = |A \cap B| / |A \cup B|$.
For a random hash function $h$ drawn from a min-wise independent family (a family of hash functions such that $\Pr[\min_h h(A) = h(x)] = 1/|A|$ for any set $A$ and element $x \in A$),
\begin{equation}
  \Pr\!\left[\min_{x \in A} h(x) = \min_{x \in B} h(x)\right] = J(A, B).
  \label{eq:minhash}
\end{equation}
By drawing $m$ such hash functions and comparing their minima, one obtains
an unbiased estimator of $J(A, B)$ with variance $J(1-J)/m$.
\end{definition}

In practice, 128--256 hash functions are used, giving a standard error on
the Jaccard estimate of roughly 0.04 at $J = 0.5$.  Documents with
estimated Jaccard similarity above a threshold (commonly 0.8) are treated
as near-duplicates and only one is retained.

\emph{PII scrubbing.}  Personally identifiable information --- names, email
addresses, phone numbers, social security numbers --- should be removed from
training data both for privacy reasons and to reduce memorisation of sensitive
content.  Regular-expression patterns and named-entity recognition models are
applied in combination.

\subsection{Data Mixing and Curriculum Strategies}
\label{subsec:data-mixing}

Quality filtering produces a corpus of multiple sub-sources with different
characteristics.  The final design decision is how to mix them during training.
The simplest approach is \emph{proportional mixing}: sample each batch with
probability proportional to the token count of each sub-source.  Proportional
mixing favours large sources (Common Crawl) over small but high-quality ones
(Wikipedia, books), which motivates alternatives.

\emph{Up-sampling} addresses this by assigning each source a sampling weight
proportional to its token fraction raised to a power $\alpha \in (0,1)$:
compressing the distribution boosts small, high-quality sources relative to
large ones.  The GPT-3 mixture used a schedule of this kind.

\emph{Curriculum learning} goes further: the mixing weights change over the
course of training.  Early in training, the model benefits from easy, clean,
high-quality text.  Later, harder or more specialised text can be introduced
as the model's representations become more stable.  For a financial LLM,
a natural curriculum might begin with general English text, then progressively
increase the proportion of financial filings and news as training progresses,
ensuring that general linguistic competence is established before domain
specialisation begins.

\begin{remark}[Replay buffers for continual learning]
\label{rem:replay}
When fine-tuning a general-purpose model on financial text, there is a risk of
\emph{catastrophic forgetting}: the model forgets general knowledge in favour of
domain-specific patterns.  A \emph{replay buffer} mixes a small fraction of
general pre-training data (typically 1--5\%) into every fine-tuning batch,
acting as a regulariser that preserves general capabilities.  This technique
is standard in continual-learning research and is directly applicable to
domain-adaptive pre-training for finance.
\end{remark}

\section{Pre-training Objectives and Scaling}
\label{sec:pretraining}

\begin{deepdive}
In the spring of 2022, a team at DeepMind published a short report that would
change how the entire field thought about training large language models.
The team had trained over 400 models at different scales, carefully recording
the loss achieved at each compute budget, and they found something surprising:
the models that dominated AI benchmarks at the time were, almost universally,
too large and trained on too few tokens.  The conventional wisdom had been to
make models as big as possible.  The new result, which became known as the
Chinchilla scaling laws, said that for a fixed compute budget the optimal
strategy is to train a \emph{smaller} model on \emph{more} data.  The practical
implications reverberated through both academia and industry: it justified
training 7-billion-parameter models to state-of-the-art performance, brought
down inference costs dramatically, and made powerful open-source models viable.

\subsection{Causal Language Modelling vs Masked Language Modelling}
\label{subsec:lm-objectives}

Two pre-training objectives dominate the LLM landscape: causal language
modelling (CLM) and masked language modelling (MLM).

\begin{definition}[Causal Language Modelling]
\label{def:clm}
Given a sequence of tokens $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T)$, the causal language
modelling objective maximises the log-likelihood under the autoregressive
factorisation:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CLM}}(\theta) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1}).
  \label{eq:clm}
\end{equation}
The model predicts the next token given all previous tokens; no future tokens
are visible during training.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Masked Language Modelling]
\label{def:mlm}
Let $\mathcal{M} \subset \{1, \ldots, T\}$ be a randomly chosen mask set,
with each position masked independently with probability $p$ (typically 0.15).
The masked language modelling objective maximises the conditional log-likelihood
of the masked tokens given the unmasked context:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MLM}}(\theta) = \sum_{t \in \mathcal{M}} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{\{1,\ldots,T\} \setminus \mathcal{M}}).
  \label{eq:mlm}
\end{equation}
Unlike CLM, the model can attend to tokens both before and after each masked
position.
\end{definition}

These objectives produce architectures with fundamentally different inductive
biases.  CLM, used by the GPT family and most modern decoder-only models,
produces representations where every token is conditioned only on its past;
this is ideal for generation tasks.  MLM, used by BERT \cite{devlin2019}
and its financial variants, produces bidirectional representations that are
richer for classification and extraction tasks but cannot generate text
autoregressively without modification.

A third objective, \emph{span corruption} (used by T5 and related encoder-decoder
models), generalises MLM by masking contiguous spans of tokens and training the
decoder to reconstruct them.  This bridges the generation-understanding divide
and is well suited to tasks framed as sequence-to-sequence transformation,
such as summarising an earnings call or translating a regulatory filing into
plain English.

For financial applications, the choice of objective follows from the downstream task:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Sentiment classification, NER, relation extraction:} MLM-pretrained
        encoder (FinBERT, RoBERTa-financial).
  \item \emph{Text generation, summarisation, Q\&A:} CLM-pretrained decoder
        (GPT, LLaMA, BloombergGPT).
  \item \emph{Structured prediction with explicit input--output:} encoder-decoder
        (T5-finance, FLAN-T5).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Compute--Data Scaling: The Chinchilla Laws}
\label{subsec:chinchilla}

The central question of scaling is: given a compute budget $C$ (measured in
floating-point operations), how should one allocate it between model parameters
$N$ and training tokens $D$?

\citet{kaplan2020scaling} established empirically that loss decreases as a power
law in both $N$ and $D$ independently:
\begin{equation}
  L(N, D) \approx \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta} + L_\infty,
  \label{eq:kaplan-loss}
\end{equation}
where $L_\infty$ is the irreducible entropy of the data, and $A$, $B$, $\alpha$,
$\beta$ are empirical constants estimated by fitting to many training runs.
Kaplan et al.\ found $\alpha \approx 0.076$ and $\beta \approx 0.095$, suggesting
that scaling model size is slightly more efficient than scaling data.

\citet{hoffmann2022chinchilla} (the Chinchilla paper) challenged this conclusion.
Their key observation was that the Kaplan et al.\ estimates were obtained by varying
$N$ and $D$ non-orthogonally, conflating the two effects.  Running a more careful
experimental design --- 400 models, each fully trained, spanning model sizes from
70 million to 16 billion parameters and data sizes from 1 billion to 500 billion
tokens --- they obtained revised estimates and derived the compute-optimal scaling
law.

\begin{theorem}[Chinchilla compute-optimal allocation]
\label{thm:chinchilla}
Under the Hoffmann et al.\ (2022) scaling model, the training loss satisfies
\begin{equation}
  L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta},
  \label{eq:chinchilla-loss}
\end{equation}
with fitted constants $E \approx 1.69$, $A \approx 406.4$, $B \approx 410.7$,
$\alpha \approx 0.34$, $\beta \approx 0.28$.  Given the constraint $C \approx 6ND$
(the FLOPs required for one forward--backward pass through an $N$-parameter
Transformer on $D$ tokens), the compute-optimal model size and token budget scale
as power laws in compute:
\begin{align}
  N^*(C) &\propto C^{\,\beta/(\alpha+\beta)}, \label{eq:chinchilla-Nstar-scale} \\
  D^*(C) &\propto C^{\,\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)}. \label{eq:chinchilla-Dstar-scale}
\end{align}
Substituting the fitted Chinchilla constants, the \emph{ratio} of optimal tokens
to optimal parameters at a reference compute budget of $C_0 = 10^{23}$ FLOPs
satisfies $D^*/N^* \approx 78$ under the parametric model.  The widely cited
``20 tokens per parameter'' heuristic derives from Hoffmann et al.'s IsoFLOP
approach (Approach~1 in the original paper), which uses a different estimation
method and yields a lower ratio at comparable compute scales; both approaches
approximate the same underlying compute-optimal frontier.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We minimise $L(N, D)$ subject to $C = 6ND$.  Substituting $D = C/(6N)$:
\begin{equation}
  \ell(N) = E + \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B(6N)^\beta}{C^\beta}.
  \label{eq:chinchilla-substituted}
\end{equation}
Setting $d\ell/dN = 0$:
\begin{equation}
  -\frac{\alpha A}{N^{\alpha+1}} + \frac{\beta B \cdot 6^\beta \, N^{\beta-1}}{C^\beta} = 0.
  \label{eq:chinchilla-foc}
\end{equation}
Solving for $N$:
\begin{equation}
  N^{\alpha+\beta} = \frac{\alpha A \, C^\beta}{\beta B \cdot 6^\beta},
  \quad\text{so}\quad
  N^*(C) = \left(\frac{\alpha A}{\beta B \cdot 6^\beta}\right)^{1/(\alpha+\beta)}
            C^{\,\beta/(\alpha+\beta)}.
  \label{eq:chinchilla-Nstar}
\end{equation}
The optimal token count follows from $D^*(C) = C/(6N^*(C))$, giving
$D^*(C) \propto C^{\,\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)}$.
Inserting the Chinchilla constants ($\alpha = 0.34$, $\beta = 0.28$,
$A = 406.4$, $B = 410.7$) and evaluating at $C_0 = 10^{23}$ FLOPs gives
$N^*(C_0) \approx 1.46 \times 10^{10}$ and $D^*(C_0) \approx 1.14 \times 10^{12}$,
a ratio $D^*/N^* \approx 78$.  Because $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are close in
magnitude, this ratio is slowly varying in $C$ near the empirically relevant
compute range.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Compute-optimal allocation for a financial LLM]
\label{ex:chinchilla-finance}
Suppose an organisation can afford a training run of $C = 10^{23}$ FLOPs
(approximately the budget for a 7B-parameter model trained for two weeks on
256 A100s).  Applying equation~\eqref{eq:chinchilla-Nstar} with the Chinchilla
constants ($\alpha = 0.34$, $\beta = 0.28$, $A = 406.4$, $B = 410.7$):
\begin{align}
  N^*(C) &= \left(\frac{0.34 \times 406.4}{0.28 \times 410.7 \times 6^{0.28}}\right)^{1/0.62}
           \times (10^{23})^{0.28/0.62}
           \approx 1.46 \times 10^{10} \text{ parameters}, \label{eq:ex-nstar} \\
  D^*(C) &= \frac{C}{6 N^*} = \frac{10^{23}}{6 \times 1.46 \times 10^{10}}
           \approx 1.14 \times 10^{12} \text{ tokens}. \label{eq:ex-dstar}
\end{align}
This is a 14.6-billion-parameter model trained on 1.14 trillion tokens, giving a
ratio $D^*/N^* \approx 78$ under the parametric Chinchilla model.  The widely
cited ``20 tokens per parameter'' heuristic (Hoffmann et al., Approach~1) is
derived from IsoFLOP curves using a different estimation strategy; the two
methods bound the same compute-optimal frontier.  If the available
financial corpus contains only 100 billion high-quality tokens, the organisation
faces a data constraint: they should reduce model size or augment the corpus,
since running more compute steps on fewer unique tokens risks overfitting.
\end{example}

The Chinchilla paper had three immediate practical consequences.  First, it
validated the 7B and 13B model families (LLaMA, Mistral) as compute-efficient
inference targets.  Second, it shifted the bottleneck in many organisations
from compute to data, increasing the premium on data curation.  Third, it
raised the possibility that even the largest deployed models (GPT-3 at 175B,
PaLM at 540B) were under-trained relative to their parameter count.

\subsection{Distributed Training: Data, Model, and Pipeline Parallelism}
\label{subsec:distributed-training}

A single A100 GPU has 80\,GB of HBM2e memory.  A 7-billion-parameter model in
32-bit floating point requires $7 \times 10^9 \times 4 = 28$\,GB for parameters
alone.  Gradients require another 28\,GB, and Adam optimizer states (two moments
per parameter) add 56\,GB.  Total memory for a 7B model under full-precision
training is roughly 112\,GB --- more than any single GPU holds.  Three
complementary distributed-training strategies address this constraint.

\emph{Data parallelism} replicates the model across $K$ devices and partitions
the batch so each device processes $B/K$ examples.  After the forward and
backward passes, gradients are all-reduced across devices using the ring-AllReduce
algorithm, which communicates $2(K-1)/K$ times the gradient tensor size.
Data parallelism is communication-efficient when the gradient tensor is small
relative to the compute per batch, but does nothing to alleviate the memory
pressure from storing the model itself.

\emph{Model (tensor) parallelism}, introduced for Transformers by Megatron-LM
\cite{shoeybi2019megatron}, partitions the weight matrices of each layer across
devices.  For a linear layer $Y = XW$ where $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d'}$,
column-wise partitioning gives $W = [W_1 \mid W_2 \mid \cdots \mid W_K]$ where
$W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times (d'/K)}$ lives on device $k$.  Each device
computes $Y_k = X W_k$ in parallel, and an all-gather operation reassembles $Y$.
This reduces peak memory per device by a factor of $K$ at the cost of
synchronisation at every layer.

\emph{Pipeline parallelism} assigns consecutive Transformer layers to consecutive
devices.  Device 1 processes layers 1--4, device 2 processes layers 5--8, and so
on.  Micro-batches flow through the pipeline like assembly-line parts.  The
key challenge is the \emph{pipeline bubble}: when a micro-batch reaches the
last device and gradients flow backward, earlier devices are idle.  The GPipe
and PipeDream schedules reduce the bubble fraction, but it remains non-zero
for finite pipeline depth.

The ZeRO (Zero Redundancy Optimizer) strategy \cite{rajbhandari2020zero}
addresses the memory inefficiency of vanilla data parallelism by sharding
optimizer states, gradients, and parameters across devices rather than
replicating them.  ZeRO defines three stages (reductions assume Adam in fp32 and a large number
of devices $K$; baseline = 16 bytes/parameter: 4 params + 4 gradients +
8 optimizer states):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{ZeRO-1}: Shard optimizer states (${\sim}2\times$ memory reduction
        per device: removing 8 bytes from 16 leaves ${\approx}8$ bytes/param
        for large $K$).
  \item \emph{ZeRO-2}: Shard gradients as well (${\sim}4\times$ reduction:
        removes optimizer states + gradients, leaving ${\approx}4$ bytes/param
        for large $K$).
  \item \emph{ZeRO-3}: Shard parameters, gradients, and optimizer states
        ($K$-fold reduction where $K$ is the number of devices).
\end{itemize}
ZeRO-3 enables training models whose total parameter memory exceeds the
memory of any individual device, at the cost of additional communication
to gather parameter shards before each forward pass.  In practice, ZeRO-2
(available as DeepSpeed ZeRO stage 2) provides most of the memory benefit
with lower communication overhead.

The three parallelism strategies are complementary and are combined in
\emph{3D parallelism}: data $\times$ tensor $\times$ pipeline.  A 175B-parameter
model trained on 1{,}024 A100s might use 64-way data parallelism, 8-way tensor
parallelism, and 2-way pipeline parallelism, filling a three-dimensional
device mesh.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Mixed-Precision Training and Gradient Checkpointing}
\label{subsec:mixed-precision}

Beyond parallelism, two engineering techniques are universally applied in
large-scale training: mixed-precision arithmetic and gradient checkpointing.

\emph{Mixed-precision training} maintains a master copy of parameters in 32-bit
float (\texttt{float32}) but performs forward and backward passes in 16-bit
float (\texttt{float16} or \texttt{bfloat16}).  The \texttt{bfloat16} format,
which uses the same 8-bit exponent as \texttt{float32} but only 7 mantissa bits
(versus 10 for \texttt{float16}), is preferred in modern hardware because it
preserves the dynamic range needed to represent very small gradients without
overflow.  The memory savings are approximately $2\times$ for activations and
reduce communication costs in all-reduce operations.

Formally, a \emph{loss scaling} factor $s > 1$ is applied to the loss before
the backward pass, multiplying all gradients by $s$, then unscaled before the
optimizer step.  This prevents underflow of small gradient values in 16-bit
arithmetic.  Dynamic loss scaling adjusts $s$ automatically: it increases $s$
by a fixed factor every $N$ steps and decreases it whenever a gradient overflow
(NaN or Inf) is detected.

\emph{Gradient checkpointing} (also called activation recomputation) trades
compute for memory.  During the forward pass, intermediate activations are
discarded rather than stored.  During the backward pass, the forward pass is
recomputed in segments to recover the activations needed for gradient computation.
This reduces activation memory from $O(L \cdot T \cdot d)$ to $O(\sqrt{L} \cdot T \cdot d)$
for an $L$-layer model with sequence length $T$ and hidden dimension $d$,
at the cost of approximately 33\% additional FLOPs.%
\footnote{The $O(\sqrt{L})$ bound assumes optimal checkpoint placement at layer
granularity; checkpointing at finer granularity can reduce memory further at
proportionally higher recomputation cost.}

\begin{remark}[Flash Attention]
\label{rem:flash-attention}
A complementary technique for reducing attention memory is FlashAttention, which
computes the scaled dot-product attention in tiles that fit in the on-chip SRAM
(shared memory) of the GPU, avoiding the materialisation of the full
$T \times T$ attention matrix in HBM.  For $T = 4{,}096$, the attention matrix
requires $4{,}096^2 \times 2$ bytes $\approx 32$\,MB per head, which accumulates
across heads and layers; FlashAttention reduces this to negligible.
FlashAttention is now standard in every major training framework and enables
sequence lengths of 32{,}000--100{,}000 tokens that would otherwise be
memory-prohibitive.
\end{remark}

\section{Transfer Learning and Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:finetuning}

\begin{context}
Pre-training a 7-billion-parameter model from scratch on two trillion tokens
requires millions of dollars in compute.  Fine-tuning that same model on a
curated financial corpus requires a fraction of a percent of that budget.
This asymmetry is the economic engine of modern applied NLP: the field has
converged on a paradigm where a small number of well-resourced organisations
train foundation models, and a much larger number of practitioners adapt those
models to specific tasks using far more modest resources.  For financial
applications --- where proprietary data is valuable, compute budgets are finite,
and regulatory requirements demand model stability --- the fine-tuning paradigm
is not merely convenient; it is often the only viable option.
\end{context}

\begin{deepdive}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Adaptation Spectrum: Zero-Shot to Full Fine-Tuning}
\label{subsec:adaptation-spectrum}

The strategies for adapting a pre-trained model to a new task form a spectrum
ordered by the number of parameters modified and the amount of task-specific
data required.

At one extreme, \emph{zero-shot prompting} modifies no parameters: the
pre-trained model is queried with a natural-language instruction (a prompt)
and its output is taken as the task prediction.  No gradient descent is
performed.  Zero-shot performance is determined entirely by the alignment
between the pre-training distribution and the task.  For financial sentiment
classification, a well-constructed prompt such as

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
Classify the sentiment of the following earnings call excerpt as
Positive, Neutral, or Negative. Respond with a single word.

Text: "Revenue grew 12% year-over-year, driven by strong demand
       in our cloud segment, while margins remained under pressure."

Sentiment:
\end{minted}
\caption{Zero-shot sentiment prompt for earnings text.}
\label{lst:zero-shot-prompt}
\end{listing}

\noindent achieves surprisingly competitive performance on encoder-based
benchmarks, though it falls short of fine-tuned models on complex tasks.

\emph{Few-shot prompting} includes a handful of labelled examples (typically
1--32) directly in the prompt.  This is also called \emph{in-context learning}:
no gradient update is performed, but the model conditions its predictions on
the examples.  The mechanism is not fully understood theoretically
\citep{xie2022explanation,akyurek2022learning}, but
empirically it improves performance substantially on tasks with idiosyncratic
label conventions.

\emph{Adapter-based methods} (discussed in detail in
Section~\ref{subsec:peft}) insert small trainable modules into the frozen
pre-trained model.  Only the adapter parameters are updated during fine-tuning;
the original weights remain fixed.

\emph{Full fine-tuning} updates all $N$ parameters of the pre-trained model
on the task-specific dataset.  This achieves the lowest training loss but
risks catastrophic forgetting of pre-training knowledge, requires the most
memory (optimizer states for all parameters), and is most expensive to run.
For large models, full fine-tuning is often impractical.

\begin{remark}[The adaptation tax]
\label{rem:adaptation-tax}
Full fine-tuning on a small domain dataset can degrade performance on tasks
outside that domain --- a phenomenon called catastrophic forgetting.  The
\emph{adaptation tax} refers to the loss of general capability per unit of
domain specialisation gained.  Parameter-efficient methods (Section~\ref{subsec:peft})
mitigate this tax by keeping the pre-trained weights frozen, thereby preserving
the representations learned during pre-training.
\end{remark}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Methods: LoRA, Prefix Tuning, and Adapters}
\label{subsec:peft}

Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods update a small subset of
parameters while freezing the rest.  All three major families ---  LoRA,
prefix tuning, and adapters --- are in active use for financial LLM deployment.

\subsubsection*{Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)}

\begin{definition}[LoRA decomposition]
\label{def:lora}
Let $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$ be a pre-trained weight matrix (frozen).
LoRA \cite{hu2022lora} parameterises the update as a scaled low-rank decomposition:
\begin{equation}
  W = W_0 + \Delta W = W_0 + \frac{\alpha}{r} B A,
  \label{eq:lora}
\end{equation}
where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$ and $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$
with rank $r \ll \min(d, k)$, and $\alpha > 0$ is a scaling hyperparameter.
At initialisation, $A$ is drawn from $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$
and $B = 0$, so $\Delta W = 0$ and training begins from the pre-trained weights.
The trainable parameter count for this layer is $r(d + k)$ rather than $dk$,
a reduction by a factor of $\min(d, k)/r$.
\end{definition}

The forward pass with LoRA adds negligible latency:
$h = W_0 x + \tfrac{\alpha}{r}(BA)x = W_0 x + \tfrac{\alpha}{r}B(Ax)$.
At inference time, $\Delta W$ can be merged into $W_0$, so LoRA incurs zero
inference overhead.  This is a key practical advantage over adapter modules,
which add sequential computation.

The rank $r$ is the primary hyperparameter.  For instruction-following tasks,
$r = 8$ or $r = 16$ suffices.  For domain adaptation where the distribution
shift is large (e.g., adapting a general English model to Chinese regulatory
documents), $r = 64$ may be needed.  The optimal $r$ can be found by sweeping
on a held-out validation set and monitoring the training loss curve.

\begin{example}[LoRA for sentiment fine-tuning]
\label{ex:lora-sentiment}
Suppose we fine-tune a 7B-parameter LLaMA model for financial sentiment
classification.  The model has 32 Transformer layers, each containing query,
key, value, and output projection matrices of dimension $d = 4{,}096$.
Applying LoRA with $r = 8$ to all four projection matrices in every layer
adds $32 \times 4 \times r(d + k)$ parameters.  Since all projection matrices
are square ($d = k = 4{,}096$), each LoRA pair contributes $r(d+k) = 8 \times 8{,}192 = 65{,}536$
parameters, for a total of $32 \times 4 \times 65{,}536 = 8{,}388{,}608$ trainable
parameters, roughly 0.12\% of the 7B total.  Training with a learning rate
of $3 \times 10^{-4}$ and a batch of 64 sequences for 2{,}000 steps on a
10{,}000-document financial sentiment dataset takes approximately 90 minutes on a
single A100, compared with 40 hours for full fine-tuning.
\end{example}

\subsubsection*{Prefix Tuning}

Prefix tuning \cite{li2021prefix} prepends a sequence of $L_p$ trainable
continuous vectors (the \emph{prefix}) to the key and value sequences of every
attention layer.  The trainable prefix matrices $P_K, P_V \in \mathbb{R}^{L_p \times d_h}$
are concatenated with the original key and value matrices computed from the input,
so that queries attend over the prefix tokens in addition to the input tokens,
effectively prepending $L_p$ soft tokens to every layer's context.

Prefix tuning was proposed for generation tasks (table-to-text, summarisation)
and achieves competitive performance with fine-tuning on 1\% of the parameters.
Its disadvantage is that the prefix consumes context window: $L_p$ tokens of
the attention budget are reserved for the prefix at every layer, reducing the
effective sequence length available for input.

\subsubsection*{Adapter Modules}

Adapter modules \cite{houlsby2019adapters} insert small two-layer networks into
each Transformer block.  After the attention sub-layer (and after the feed-forward
sub-layer), an adapter computes:
\begin{equation}
  h \leftarrow h + W_{\mathrm{up}} \, \sigma(W_{\mathrm{down}} \, h),
  \label{eq:adapter}
\end{equation}
where $W_{\mathrm{down}} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}$ projects down to a
bottleneck of dimension $r$, $\sigma$ is a non-linearity (typically ReLU or
GELU), and $W_{\mathrm{up}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ projects back up.
The residual connection ensures that at initialisation (with $W_{\mathrm{up}} = 0$)
the adapter is an identity map.  Each adapter adds $2rd$ parameters; with two
adapters per layer and $L$ layers the total is $4rdL$.

\begin{remark}[Comparing PEFT methods]
\label{rem:peft-comparison}
LoRA, prefix tuning, and adapters achieve comparable task performance at similar
parameter counts.  LoRA is preferred in practice for three reasons: (i) zero
inference overhead after merging; (ii) compatibility with quantised weights
(QLoRA combines LoRA with 4-bit quantisation, enabling 7B fine-tuning on a
24\,GB consumer GPU); (iii) simpler implementation via the PEFT library.
Prefix tuning is advantageous when task switching is needed at inference
time (different prefixes can be loaded without reloading the base model).
Adapters are well suited to multi-task learning scenarios where many adapters
are trained and composed.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Instruction Tuning and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback}
\label{subsec:instruction-tuning}

A pre-trained language model is a probabilistic model of text.  It does not
inherently follow instructions.  Instruction tuning and RLHF are the two
dominant approaches to transforming a pre-trained model into an assistant
that responds helpfully and safely to user requests.

\subsubsection*{Instruction Tuning}

Instruction tuning \cite{wei2022finetuned} fine-tunes a pre-trained model on
a dataset of (instruction, response) pairs, formatted consistently so that the
model learns the correspondence between a natural-language request and an
appropriate completion.  The FLAN models demonstrated that instruction tuning
on a diverse collection of tasks, formatted as instructions, dramatically
improves zero-shot performance on unseen tasks.

The key insight is that \emph{task format diversity} is as important as task
diversity: exposing the model to many different ways of phrasing the same
underlying operation (``classify,'' ``what is the sentiment,''
``positive or negative?'') improves its ability to generalise to novel phrasings.
This principle has been applied directly to financial LLMs.
\citet{yang2023investlm} demonstrate that fine-tuning LLaMA-65B with a carefully
curated but small financial instruction dataset --- spanning CFA exam questions,
SEC filings, and quantitative finance discussion threads --- yields responses
rated by financial experts as comparable to GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, supporting
the \emph{Superficial Alignment Hypothesis} that instruction quality matters
more than raw quantity.  Similarly, \citet{xie2023pixiu} introduce PIXIU and
its instruction-tuned model FinMA, constructed from 136{,}000 financial
instruction samples across five task types and nine datasets, providing both
a fine-tuned model and an open evaluation benchmark for the community.

\subsubsection*{Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback}

InstructGPT \cite{ouyang2022training} extended instruction tuning with a
three-stage pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Supervised fine-tuning (SFT)}: Fine-tune the pre-trained model
        on a high-quality set of human-written demonstrations.
  \item \emph{Reward model training}: Train a reward model $r_\phi(x, y)$
        to predict human preference scores.  Specifically, given pairs of
        model outputs $(y_1, y_2)$ for the same prompt $x$, humans label which
        output is preferred.  The Bradley-Terry model assigns probability $P(y_w \succ y_l) = \sigma(r(y_w) - r(y_l))$ to output $y_w$ being preferred over $y_l$, where $\sigma(z)=1/(1+e^{-z})$ is the sigmoid and $r(\cdot)$ is a scalar reward.  The reward model is trained with the
        Bradley-Terry objective:
        \begin{equation}
          \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{RM}}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l) \sim \mathcal{D}}
            \left[\log \sigma\!\left(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\right)\right],
          \label{eq:reward-model}
        \end{equation}
        where $y_w$ is the preferred (``winner'') output, $y_l$ is the less
        preferred (``loser'') output, and $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function.
  \item \emph{PPO fine-tuning}: We frame the language model as a \emph{policy} $\pi_\theta(y \mid x)$: a conditional distribution over output sequences $y$ given input $x$, parameterised by $\theta$. The environment is the reward model, and the goal is to find $\theta$ maximising expected reward while staying close to the supervised baseline $\pi_{\mathrm{SFT}}$.  Optimise the SFT model using Proximal Policy
        Optimisation (PPO) to maximise the expected reward, regularised by a
        KL penalty toward the SFT model.  Here $\mathrm{KL}(\pi \| \pi') = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi}[\log(\pi(y)/\pi'(y))]$ is the KL divergence.
        \begin{equation}
          \mathcal{J}_{\mathrm{PPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)}
            \!\left[r_\phi(x, y) - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{SFT}}(y \mid x)}\right],
          \label{eq:ppo-objective}
        \end{equation}
        where $\pi_\theta$ is the policy (the language model), $\pi_{\mathrm{SFT}}$
        is the reference SFT policy, and $\beta > 0$ controls the strength of
        the KL regularisation.  $\mathcal{J}$ denotes an objective to be
        \emph{maximised}.
\end{enumerate}

The KL penalty in equation~\eqref{eq:ppo-objective} is essential: without it,
the policy collapses to reward hacking (generating degenerate strings that
achieve high reward without being useful).  The parameter $\beta$ represents
a trade-off between reward maximisation and staying close to the pre-trained
distribution.

\emph{Direct Preference Optimisation (DPO)} \cite{rafailov2023direct} eliminates
the separate reward model by reparameterising the RLHF objective directly in
terms of the policy.  Under the Bradley-Terry model, the optimal policy
$\pi^*$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
  r^*(x, y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y \mid x)} + \beta \log Z(x),
  \label{eq:dpo-reward}
\end{equation}
where $Z(x) = \sum_y \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y \mid x) \exp(r(x,y)/\beta)$ is
the partition function.  Substituting into the Bradley-Terry loss and noting
that $Z(x)$ cancels in the preference comparison, the DPO loss simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DPO}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l) \sim \mathcal{D}}
    \!\left[\log \sigma\!\left(
      \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_w \mid x)}
      - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_l \mid x)}
    \right)\right].
  \label{eq:dpo}
\end{equation}
DPO requires only supervised learning on preference data, avoiding the
instability and complexity of PPO.  For financial LLM deployment, DPO is
increasingly preferred: it is simpler to implement, more stable to train, and
produces models that are easier to audit, which matters for regulatory compliance.
An important practical caution is that RLHF-aligned models may still exhibit
\emph{information overload}: \citet{BaloghDidisheim2025} show that providing
LLMs with more context in financial analysis tasks follows an inverted-U
relationship, where accuracy first rises then falls as the volume of input
exceeds the model's effective capacity --- a phenomenon directly relevant to
the design of financial RAG and prompt engineering pipelines.

\subsection{Domain Adaptation for Finance}
\label{subsec:domain-adaptation}

Domain adaptation transfers a general-purpose pre-trained model to the
financial domain through two sequential stages.

In the first stage, \emph{continued pre-training} (also called domain-adaptive
pre-training, DAPT), the model is trained on a large financial corpus using the
same CLM or MLM objective as pre-training, but with a smaller learning rate
and for fewer steps.  The goal is to shift the model's representations toward
financial vocabulary and discourse patterns without catastrophic forgetting.
A learning rate of $10^{-5}$--$10^{-4}$ and a cosine schedule with warmup
are typical; the replay buffer technique (Section~\ref{subsec:data-mixing})
mixes in general text to preserve breadth.

In the second stage, \emph{task-specific fine-tuning} trains the model on
labelled examples for the target task: sentiment labels from analyst reports,
named entities from 10-K filings, or numerical answers from earnings Q\&A.
This stage uses a much smaller dataset (often 1{,}000--100{,}000 examples) and
a very small learning rate ($10^{-5}$ with LoRA or $5 \times 10^{-6}$ with
full fine-tuning).

\begin{example}[Two-stage adaptation for earnings sentiment]
\label{ex:two-stage-adaptation}
Consider adapting LLaMA-2 (7B) for earnings call sentiment analysis.

\emph{Stage 1.} Continued pre-training on 50 billion tokens of financial text
(SEC EDGAR filings, Bloomberg News, earnings transcripts), using LoRA rank 32,
learning rate $5 \times 10^{-5}$, for 5 epochs.

\emph{Stage 2.} Fine-tuning on 15{,}000 labelled sentences from the Financial
PhraseBank dataset \cite{malo2014financial}, with three-class labels (Positive,
Neutral, Negative).  Training for 10 epochs with learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$
and LoRA rank 8.

For illustration, suppose the two-stage approach achieves accuracy of approximately 94\% on a held-out Financial PhraseBank test set, compared with roughly 89\% for Stage~2 fine-tuning alone and 79\% for zero-shot prompting (exact figures vary with corpus, hardware, and random seed).
\end{example}

\end{deepdive}

\section{Pre-Trained Models for Finance Tasks}
\label{sec:finance-models}

\begin{context}
The applied NLP landscape in finance has evolved rapidly.  Before 2019,
practitioners built task-specific pipelines on top of classical representations
or fine-tuned GloVe embeddings on small labelled datasets.  FinBERT in 2019
demonstrated that a Transformer pre-trained on financial text could be fine-tuned
to state-of-the-art performance across multiple tasks simultaneously.
BloombergGPT in 2023 showed that the same logic applied to generative models
at scale: a 50-billion-parameter model pre-trained on a proprietary financial
corpus outperformed general-purpose GPT models on almost every financial NLP
benchmark, often by large margins.  These two milestones define the current
landscape, in which domain-specific models coexist with general-purpose models
and the choice between them is a function of task type, data availability,
and compute budget.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{FinBERT and Encoder-Based Models}
\label{subsec:finbert}

FinBERT \cite{yang2020finbert} is a BERT-base model (110 million parameters)
that was further pre-trained on three financial corpora: (i) TRC2 financial
news from Reuters (1.8\,GB); (ii) Financial PhraseBank \cite{malo2014financial},
a collection of financial sentences with sentiment labels; and (iii) a corpus
of financial analyst reports.  The continued pre-training used the MLM objective
with the original WordPiece vocabulary unchanged; gains arise from weight updates
that shift representations toward financial vocabulary and discourse, not from
vocabulary expansion.

On the \emph{Financial PhraseBank} sentiment task, \citet{yang2020finbert} report accuracy of 88.5\% for FinBERT versus 80.7\% for a general BERT baseline. Performance on other financial classification tasks---such as ESG category labelling or forward-looking statement detection---varies by corpus and annotation scheme; typical FinBERT gains over general BERT are in the 5--15 percentage-point range on in-domain data.

These gains arise from two mechanisms.  First, continued pre-training on
financial text produces richer contextual representations of financial
terminology within the unchanged WordPiece vocabulary, effectively teaching
the model how financial tokens co-occur and relate.  Second, the pre-training
distribution is closer to the fine-tuning distribution, so fewer gradient
steps are needed to reach a given loss level.

A parallel line of work by \citet{huang2023finbert} pre-trained FinBERT on a
much larger corpus of 4.9 billion financial tokens drawn from corporate filings,
analyst reports, and earnings call transcripts, publishing the study in
\emph{Contemporary Accounting Research}; they demonstrate that the resulting
representations substantially outperform the dictionary-based Loughran--McDonald
lexicon and classical machine-learning approaches on sentiment classification,
and they apply the model to predict stock returns and corporate fraud.

The FinBERT architecture follows BERT-base precisely: 12 Transformer layers,
hidden dimension $d = 768$, 12 attention heads, feed-forward dimension 3{,}072.
For a classification task, the representation of the \texttt{[CLS]} token at
the final layer is passed through a linear head:
\begin{equation}
  p(y \mid x) = \mathrm{softmax}(W_{\mathrm{cls}} \, h_{\texttt{[CLS]}} + b),
  \label{eq:finbert-cls}
\end{equation}
where $h_{\texttt{[CLS]}} \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$, $W_{\mathrm{cls}} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times 768}$
for $C$ output classes, and the cross-entropy loss is minimised over labelled examples.

\begin{remark}[Alternatives to FinBERT]
\label{rem:finbert-alternatives}
Several variants have built on FinBERT.  RoBERTa-financial replaces BERT's
next-sentence-prediction objective with longer training and larger batches.
FLANG (Financial Language model for NLP in finance, 2022) covers five financial
domains.  For non-English markets, models pre-trained on German or French
regulatory text show similar gains relative to multilingual BERT.  The general
principle --- that domain-adaptive pre-training of encoder models improves
financial NLP --- is now well established.
\end{remark}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{BloombergGPT and Domain-Specific Pre-Training}
\label{subsec:bloomberggpt}

BloombergGPT \cite{wu2023bloomberggpt} is a 50-billion-parameter, decoder-only
language model trained on an approximately 708-billion-token corpus assembled
from Bloomberg's proprietary data archive.  The corpus consists of two
components: (i) a finance-specific portion of 363\,B tokens from Bloomberg's
news archive, company filings, press releases, and web crawl of financial
domains; and (ii) a general-purpose portion of 345\,B tokens from The Pile.
The training mixture gave 51\% weight to financial text and 49\% to general
text, targeting a model that is strong in finance without sacrificing general
language capabilities.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{BloombergGPT training corpus composition.}
  \label{tab:bloomberggpt-corpus}
  \begin{tabular}{lrr}
    \toprule
    Source & Tokens (B) & Mix weight \\
    \midrule
    Bloomberg News     & 105 & 14.5\% \\
    Bloomberg Web      &  88 & 12.2\% \\
    Bloomberg Filings  &  88 & 12.2\% \\
    Bloomberg Press    &  63 &  8.7\% \\
    Bloomberg Social   &  19 &  2.6\% \\
    The Pile (general) & 345 & 49.9\% \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The Chinchilla compute-optimal prescription (Section~\ref{subsec:chinchilla})
would recommend training a 50B-parameter model on approximately 1 trillion tokens.
BloombergGPT's 708B-token corpus is under-trained by this standard.  The authors
justify this by noting that the finance-specific corpus is exhausted at roughly
363B tokens; adding more general text beyond this point would dilute the financial
content below the 50\% threshold, defeating the purpose of domain-specific
pre-training.  This highlights a general tension in domain-specific pre-training:
the Chinchilla optimum assumes unlimited data, but domain-specific corpora are
inherently bounded.

On financial benchmarks (FPB sentiment, FiQA-SA, Headline, NER, ConvFinQA),
BloombergGPT outperforms GPT-NeoX-20B (a general-purpose open-source model)
by 10--20\% on average~\citep{wu2023bloomberggpt}, demonstrating the value of in-domain pre-training.
On general NLP benchmarks (MMLU, BIG-bench), BloombergGPT performs comparably
to GPT-NeoX-20B, confirming that the general-text component of the corpus
preserved broad capabilities.

BloombergGPT adopts architectural choices from the BLOOM model family --- specifically
ALiBi positional encodings rather than learned position embeddings --- rather than
building on the BLOOM weights directly.
ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases) adds a position-dependent bias to attention
logits:
\begin{equation}
  a_{ij} = \frac{q_i^\top k_j}{\sqrt{d_k}} - m \cdot (i - j),
  \label{eq:alibi}
\end{equation}
where $m > 0$ is a per-head slope and $i - j \geq 0$ is the distance from
token $j$ to token $i$.  The bias penalises long-distance attention linearly,
acting as an implicit positional prior.  ALiBi generalises better to
sequence lengths longer than those seen during training, which matters for
financial filings that can run to hundreds of pages.

\subsection{Task-Specific Fine-Tuning: Sentiment, NER, and Summarisation}
\label{subsec:task-finetuning}

Beyond sentiment analysis, financial NLP encompasses a range of tasks that
require different architectural choices and training strategies.

\emph{Named Entity Recognition (NER).}  Financial NER identifies entities
such as company names, person names, financial instruments, and monetary
amounts in unstructured text.  The standard approach adds a token-level
classification head to an encoder model: for each token position $t$, the
contextual representation $h_t$ is projected through a linear layer with
softmax to predict a BIO (Begin--Inside--Outside) tag $y_t$.
The CoNLL-2003 NER scheme extends naturally
to financial entities: B-ORG for company name beginnings, I-ORG for continuations,
B-MON for monetary amounts, and so on.

\emph{Numerical reasoning.}  Tasks such as FinQA require the model to parse
tables of financial data and perform arithmetic over them.  Pure language model
approaches are insufficient here; instead, \citet{chen2021finqa} propose a
``program executor'' architecture where the model generates a symbolic program
(a sequence of arithmetic operations) that is evaluated by an external calculator,
separating the language understanding task from the arithmetic computation.

\emph{Summarisation.}  Generating concise summaries of earnings calls or
research reports is a natural application for decoder or encoder-decoder models.
Standard fine-tuning on (document, summary) pairs with the CLM or span-corruption
objective achieves reasonable ROUGE scores (Section~\ref{subsec:task-metrics}),
but financial summaries require factual fidelity --- a model that summarises
``revenue grew 12\%'' as ``revenue grew 2\%'' is worse than no summary at all.
Factually-consistent summarisation is an active research area; retrieval-augmented
generation (RAG) is one practical mitigation.

\emph{LLM-based financial analytics via fine-tuned open models.}
\citet{zhang2023finllamallora} demonstrate that LoRA fine-tuning of
Llama~2 for multi-task financial news analytics --- covering sentiment, key-point
extraction, summarisation, and named-entity recognition simultaneously --- achieves
competitive accuracy at a fraction of the cost of fine-tuning larger proprietary
models, confirming that PEFT methods are now viable for end-to-end financial NLP
pipelines in resource-constrained settings.

\end{context}

\section{Evaluation and Benchmarking}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\begin{context}
The history of AI is littered with evaluations that turned out to measure
something other than what was intended.  Leaderboard performance on a benchmark
often reflects training data contamination, prompt engineering, or
hardware-specific optimisations rather than genuine linguistic capability.
In finance, the stakes of misplaced confidence are material: a model deployed
to summarise analyst research and then found to hallucinate financial figures
can generate incorrect investment advice, trigger regulatory scrutiny, and
destroy institutional credibility.  Rigorous evaluation is not a bureaucratic
formality; it is a professional obligation.

Evaluation of financial LLMs operates at three levels.  \emph{Intrinsic
evaluation} measures the model itself --- perplexity on a held-out financial
corpus captures how well the model has absorbed domain language, but says
little about task performance.  \emph{Downstream task evaluation} measures
performance on specific financial NLP tasks --- sentiment classification,
named entity recognition, numerical QA --- using standard metrics (accuracy,
F1, ROUGE) on established benchmarks.  \emph{Real-world evaluation} measures
behaviour in deployment: does the model's output lead to better analyst
productivity, fewer compliance incidents, or more accurate risk assessments?
Each level addresses different concerns and requires different methodology.
The sections below cover intrinsic and downstream metrics; real-world evaluation
is addressed in the governance discussion of Section~\ref{sec:safety}.
\end{context}

\begin{deepdive}

\subsection{Perplexity and Cross-Entropy}
\label{subsec:perplexity}

Perplexity is the canonical intrinsic evaluation metric for language models.
It measures how surprised the model is by a held-out text corpus, with lower
perplexity indicating better fit.

\begin{definition}[Perplexity]
\label{def:perplexity}
Let $p_\theta$ be a language model and $(x_1, \ldots, x_T)$ a test sequence.
The cross-entropy loss on this sequence is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log_2 p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}),
  \label{eq:cross-entropy}
\end{equation}
measured in bits per token (using $\log_2$) or nats per token (using $\ln$).
Perplexity is the exponentiated cross-entropy:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{PPL}(\theta) = 2^{\mathcal{H}(\theta)}
                       = \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})^{-1/T}.
  \label{eq:perplexity}
\end{equation}
A perplexity of $K$ corresponds to the model being, on average, as uncertain
as a uniform distribution over $K$ alternatives at each token.
\end{definition}

The relationship between cross-entropy and perplexity is immediate from the
definitions: perplexity is the geometric mean of the per-token surprisals
$1/p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})$.  For a model that assigns uniform probability
over a vocabulary of size $|\mathcal{V}|$, perplexity equals $|\mathcal{V}|$.
For a perfect model (zero cross-entropy), perplexity is 1.

Perplexity has three well-known limitations.  First, it is not comparable
across models with different vocabulary sizes: a model with 100{,}000 tokens
reports lower perplexity than an equivalent model with 50{,}000 tokens simply
because it partitions probability mass more finely.  Second, perplexity
measures marginal token-level prediction, not the coherent discourse-level
understanding required for financial reasoning.  Third, in-domain perplexity
does not predict zero-shot performance on out-of-domain tasks.  Perplexity is
therefore best used as a diagnostic during training --- to detect overfitting or
data contamination --- rather than as a deployment metric.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Downstream Task Metrics: F1, ROUGE, and Accuracy}
\label{subsec:task-metrics}

For classification tasks (sentiment, NER, intent detection), standard metrics
apply:

For class $c$, let $\mathrm{TP}^{(c)}, \mathrm{FP}^{(c)}, \mathrm{FN}^{(c)}$ denote true positives, false positives, and false negatives respectively. Then $\mathrm{Precision}^{(c)} = \mathrm{TP}^{(c)}/(\mathrm{TP}^{(c)}+\mathrm{FP}^{(c)})$ and $\mathrm{Recall}^{(c)} = \mathrm{TP}^{(c)}/(\mathrm{TP}^{(c)}+\mathrm{FN}^{(c)})$.

\begin{definition}[Macro-F1]
\label{def:macro-f1}
For a $C$-class classification problem, macro-F1 is the unweighted average of
the per-class F1 scores:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Macro-F1} = \frac{1}{C} \sum_{c=1}^{C} F_1^{(c)},
  \quad \text{where} \quad
  F_1^{(c)} = \frac{2 \, \text{Precision}^{(c)} \cdot \text{Recall}^{(c)}}
                   {\text{Precision}^{(c)} + \text{Recall}^{(c)}}.
  \label{eq:macro-f1}
\end{equation}
Macro-F1 treats all classes equally regardless of frequency, making it
appropriate for imbalanced financial datasets where rare events (e.g., fraud
alerts, covenant violations) are as important as common ones.
\end{definition}

For generation tasks (summarisation, question answering), ROUGE is the
standard automatic metric.  An \emph{$N$-gram} is a contiguous sequence of $N$ tokens; $\mathcal{G}_N(y)$ denotes the multiset of all such subsequences in sequence $y$.  ROUGE-N measures n-gram overlap between a generated
summary $\hat{y}$ and a reference summary $y$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{ROUGE}\text{-}N = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{g \in \mathcal{G}_N(y)} \mathrm{count}_{\mathrm{match}}(g,\, \hat{y},\, y)}
                                  {\displaystyle\sum_{g \in \mathcal{G}_N(y)} \mathrm{count}(g,\, y)},
  \label{eq:rouge}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{G}_N(y)$ is the multiset of all $N$-grams in the reference $y$,
$\mathrm{count}(g, y)$ is the frequency of $N$-gram $g$ in $y$, and
$\mathrm{count}_{\mathrm{match}}(g, \hat{y}, y) = \min(\mathrm{count}(g, \hat{y}),\, \mathrm{count}(g, y))$
clips each match to the reference frequency.
ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L (longest common
subsequence) are typically reported together.

ROUGE is a recall-oriented metric: it rewards covering the reference material
but does not penalise hallucinated content that does not appear in the reference.
For financial summarisation, factual precision is paramount.  Practitioners
supplement ROUGE with \emph{factuality scores} computed by a secondary model
(e.g., a natural language inference model that checks whether every claim in
the summary is entailed by the source document).

\subsection{Financial Benchmarks: FinQA, FiQA, and SEC-EDGAR Tasks}
\label{subsec:financial-benchmarks}

Several benchmarks have emerged specifically for financial NLP.  They test
capabilities not covered by general-purpose benchmarks such as MMLU or BIG-bench.

\emph{FinBen} \cite{xie2024finben} is the most comprehensive open-source evaluation
benchmark for financial LLMs, covering 42 datasets across 24 tasks including
information extraction, textual analysis, question answering, text generation,
risk management, forecasting, and decision-making.  Its evaluation of 21 representative
LLMs (GPT-4, ChatGPT, Gemini, open-source models) reveals that LLMs excel at
extraction and textual analysis but struggle with advanced numerical forecasting
and decision-making tasks --- a finding that calibrates realistic expectations
for financial AI deployment.

\emph{FinQA} \cite{chen2021finqa} is a dataset of 8{,}281 question-answer pairs
requiring multi-step numerical reasoning over earnings reports.  Each example
consists of a financial document (containing both text and tables), a question,
a gold numerical answer, and a gold reasoning program (sequence of arithmetic
operations).  FinQA tests three skills simultaneously: information extraction
from heterogeneous sources, multi-hop reasoning, and arithmetic.  At the time of the benchmark's introduction, \citet{chen2021finqa} reported human execution accuracy of 91\%; top neural systems at that time achieved roughly 68--75\%. Current leaderboard figures should be consulted directly, as the field advances rapidly.

\emph{FiQA-SA} \cite{maia2018fiqa} (Financial Question Answering -- Sentiment
Analysis) consists of financial microblogs and news headlines annotated with
sentiment scores in $[-1, 1]$ and aspect-level targets (company, economy,
product).  The task is to predict both the sentiment polarity and the target.
FiQA-SA is challenging because sentiment is expressed implicitly through market
jargon (``the stock is overbought,'' ``the company is burning cash'') that
requires domain knowledge to interpret correctly.

\emph{SEC-EDGAR tasks} encompass a family of evaluations based on the EDGAR
archive: document classification by form type, clause extraction from 10-K
risk-factor sections, and forward-looking statement detection.  These tasks
are particularly relevant because EDGAR filings are subject to legal scrutiny;
a model that misclassifies a material risk factor could contribute to a
compliance failure.

\subsection{Red-Teaming and Human Evaluation}
\label{subsec:red-teaming}

Automated metrics measure average-case performance on curated benchmarks.
They do not reveal tail risks: the rare but consequential failures that occur
when a model is prompted adversarially or encounters out-of-distribution inputs.
\emph{Red-teaming} is a structured adversarial evaluation that probes for these
failures by systematically attempting to elicit harmful, incorrect, or
misleading outputs.

In a financial context, red-teaming targets several failure modes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Hallucinated financial figures:} prompting the model to state
        specific revenue numbers, earnings dates, or regulatory limits and
        checking whether the stated figures are accurate.
  \item \emph{Regulatory advice:} prompting the model to provide specific
        investment advice, tax guidance, or legal opinions and checking
        whether it does so inappropriately.
  \item \emph{Market manipulation:} prompting the model to generate text
        designed to influence market sentiment (e.g., false rumours about
        a company's financial health).
  \item \emph{Information barrier violations:} prompting the model to reveal
        or infer material non-public information from patterns in its training data.
\end{itemize}

Red-teaming exercises should be conducted by specialists familiar with both
the model's capabilities and the relevant regulatory environment (MiFID II,
the EU AI Act, SEC Rule 17a-4).  Their findings feed directly into the
governance framework discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:governance}.

\emph{Human evaluation} complements red-teaming by measuring subjective quality
attributes: factual accuracy, relevance, coherence, and appropriateness.
A panel of domain experts (analysts, compliance officers, portfolio managers)
rates model outputs on these dimensions, providing signal that no automated
metric can capture.  Human evaluation is expensive and slow, which limits its
scale; but for high-stakes deployment decisions it is indispensable.

A notable example of rigorous out-of-sample evaluation is \citet{LopezLiraTang2023},
who evaluate ChatGPT's zero-shot ability to forecast next-day stock returns from
news headlines for 4{,}123 US common stocks.  They find that the sentiment
scores generated by GPT-4 predict both the initial market reaction and the
subsequent price drift, achieving annualised Sharpe ratios above 6 in 2021 and
declining toward 1 by 2024 as the market absorbed the strategy --- a natural
experiment illustrating how benchmark performance can degrade under real trading
conditions once an LLM-based signal becomes publicly known.

\end{context}

\section{Safety, Bias, and Responsible AI in Finance}
\label{sec:safety}

\begin{context}
The deployment of large language models in financial services sits at the
intersection of two demanding regulatory traditions: the algorithmic accountability
requirements of financial regulation (MiFID II, SR 11-7, OSFI E-23) and the
emerging AI governance frameworks (the EU AI Act, the NIST AI Risk Management
Framework, and the UK AI Safety Institute's evaluation protocols).  In no other
industry are the governance requirements more stringent or the potential for harm
more immediate.  A language model deployed in a trading system can move markets.
A model used to screen loan applications can perpetuate discrimination at scale.
A model used to generate research notes can violate research independence rules
under Article~37 of Commission Delegated Directive (EU) 2017/593~\citep{esma2018mifid2}, which implements MiFID~II research independence requirements.  Understanding these risks --- and the mitigations
available --- is as fundamental to the financial NLP practitioner as understanding
the training objectives and architectures described in earlier sections.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sources of Bias in Financial Corpora}
\label{subsec:bias}

\citet{bender2021stochastic} argue that large language models are ``stochastic
parrots'': systems that reproduce statistical patterns from their training data
without understanding the content.  In financial corpora, this creates several
specific bias risks.

\emph{Survivorship bias.}  A training corpus assembled from news archives and
SEC filings over-represents companies that were large, publicly listed, and
active throughout the sample period.  Bankrupt firms, delisted companies, and
private businesses contribute little text.  A model trained on such a corpus
will have systematically more optimistic priors about corporate performance:
it has read far more descriptions of growing, profitable companies than of
distressed or failed ones.

\emph{Temporal bias.}  Financial language is time-varying.  The phrase ``CDO''
meant one thing in 2006 (a sophisticated structured product) and another in
2009 (a toxic instrument associated with the financial crisis).  A model
pre-trained on a corpus spanning multiple decades will conflate these meanings
unless temporal context is provided explicitly.

\emph{Geographic and cultural bias.}  Most financial text corpora are dominated
by English-language sources from the United States and United Kingdom.  This
creates biases in industry classification (US SIC codes rather than NACE
codes), accounting convention (GAAP rather than IFRS), and regulatory
terminology.  A model deployed in a European context may produce outputs
calibrated to US rather than European regulatory standards.

\emph{Analyst consensus bias.}  Earnings call transcripts and analyst reports
are written by professionals who share common priors, vocabulary, and
interpretive frameworks.  A model trained heavily on such text learns to
replicate analyst consensus rather than independent analysis.  This is
particularly problematic for applications that seek to identify contrarian
or early-warning signals.

\begin{remark}[Measuring bias in financial outputs]
\label{rem:bias-measurement}
Bias in language model outputs can be measured at several levels:
(i) \emph{lexical}: does the model use different vocabulary for equivalent
situations depending on firm size, geography, or industry?
(ii) \emph{sentiment}: does the model assign systematically different sentiment
scores to structurally identical sentences about different asset classes?
(iii) \emph{factual}: does the model's stated ``factual'' knowledge differ
systematically across subgroups?  Each type requires different measurement
methodologies (counterfactual probing, matched samples, or knowledge probes
respectively), and all three should be part of a deployment audit.
\end{remark}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Hallucination in Financial Contexts}
\label{subsec:hallucination}

\citet{ji2023survey} define hallucination in natural language generation as
the phenomenon whereby a model generates content that is factually incorrect,
unverifiable, or inconsistent with the source document, even while maintaining
a fluent and confident surface form.  In financial contexts, hallucination
poses risks that go beyond annoyance or embarrassment.

\emph{Factual hallucination} occurs when the model states incorrect financial
figures: reporting a company's revenue as \$12.3 billion when it was
\$1.23 billion, or citing an earnings date that has not occurred.  These errors
are particularly dangerous in automated workflows where model output is not
reviewed by a human before being acted upon.

\emph{Regulatory hallucination} occurs when the model cites non-existent
regulatory provisions, makes up article numbers, or attributes statements to
regulatory bodies that did not make them.  This type of hallucination is
difficult to detect without domain expertise and can mislead compliance teams.

\emph{Mitigating hallucination} requires a combination of architectural and
procedural interventions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):} Condition the model's
        generation on retrieved source documents, reducing reliance on
        parametric memory.  For financial applications, the retrieval index
        is typically built from a curated document store (EDGAR filings,
        verified news articles) rather than the open web.
  \item \emph{Confidence calibration:} Train the model to express uncertainty
        explicitly (``I am not certain of the exact figure'') when its internal
        confidence is low.  Calibrated models are less likely to hallucinate
        with false confidence.
  \item \emph{Output verification:} Pass model outputs through a secondary
        fact-checking model or rule-based system that cross-references stated
        figures against a structured database.
  \item \emph{Human-in-the-loop:} For high-stakes outputs (investment recommendations,
        risk assessments), require human review before the output is acted upon.
\end{itemize}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Regulatory Considerations: MiFID~II and Model Explainability}
\label{subsec:regulatory}

The Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID II), which governs
investment services in the European Union, imposes a duty of best execution,
suitability assessment, and research independence on financial intermediaries.
LLMs used in investment contexts must comply with these requirements, and
compliance creates specific technical obligations.

\emph{Research independence (Article~37 of Commission Delegated Directive (EU) 2017/593~\citep{esma2018mifid2}).}  Under MiFID~II, firms that produce
investment research must maintain independence from trading desks.  An LLM that
generates investment commentary and is simultaneously used to inform trading
decisions risks violating this independence principle if its training data
was compiled by a team with access to proprietary trading information.
Governance frameworks must trace the data provenance of any LLM used in a
research context.

\emph{Suitability and explainability.}  When an LLM is used to make or inform
suitability assessments (recommending investment products to retail clients),
MiFID II's suitability rules require that the firm be able to explain the
basis of the recommendation.  Transformer attention weights are not sufficient
for this purpose: they are not interpretable as causal importance scores and
cannot substitute for a documented decision-making process.  Firms must
implement auxiliary explanation mechanisms (LIME, SHAP applied to the model's
numeric features, or structured decision logs) alongside any LLM-based
recommendation system.

\emph{Model validation (SR 11-7).}  The US Federal Reserve's guidance on model
risk management (SR 11-7) requires that models used in banking be subject to
independent validation, including conceptual soundness review, ongoing monitoring,
and outcome analysis.  LLMs represent a challenge for SR 11-7 compliance
because their complexity makes conceptual soundness review non-trivial.
Industry practice is converging on a tiered approach: lower-risk LLM applications
(document summarisation, draft generation for human review) face lighter
validation requirements than higher-risk applications (credit scoring, automated
trading).

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Governance Frameworks for LLM Deployment}
\label{subsec:governance}

A governance framework for LLM deployment in financial services consists of
four interlocking components: risk classification, audit trails, monitoring,
and incident response.

\emph{Risk classification.}  Not all LLM applications carry the same risk.
A chatbot that answers FAQ queries about account services poses lower risk than
a model that generates credit assessments.  A useful classification scheme
assigns each application to one of three tiers:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Tier 1 (Low risk):} Human-reviewed outputs, advisory use only,
        no automated decision-making.  Example: summarisation of research notes
        for analyst review.
  \item \emph{Tier 2 (Medium risk):} Semi-automated outputs, human oversight
        required before action, limited scope.  Example: automated screening of
        loan applications with mandatory human review for borderline cases.
  \item \emph{Tier 3 (High risk):} Automated decision-making with material
        financial consequences.  Example: real-time trading signals from an LLM
        that executes without human approval.  These applications require
        full SR 11-7 validation and continuous monitoring.
\end{itemize}

\emph{Audit trails.}  Every inference call in a production LLM system should
be logged with: the input prompt, the model version, inference parameters
(temperature, top-$p$, maximum tokens), the output, and a timestamp.  For
Tier 3 applications, logs should be retained for at least seven years under
SEC Rule 17a-4.  Logs enable post-hoc investigation of model failures and
are essential for regulatory examinations.

\emph{Monitoring.}  Production LLMs must be monitored for distributional shift
(the input prompt distribution changes over time as users find new ways to
query the system), performance degradation (downstream task metrics decline
as the world changes and the model's knowledge becomes stale), and safety
violations (harmful outputs detected by a secondary classification model).
Monitoring should trigger automatic escalation when metric thresholds are
breached.

\emph{Incident response.}  Governance frameworks must specify procedures for
responding to model failures: who is notified, what information is logged,
whether the model is suspended, and how the incident is communicated to
regulators.  For firms subject to MiFID II, incidents involving material
model failures may require disclosure to the relevant national competent
authority.

\begin{remark}[The EU AI Act and high-risk AI systems]
\label{rem:eu-ai-act-highrisk}
Building on the Act's four-tier scheme (\Cref{rem:eu-ai-act}), we focus here on the
obligations most relevant to model training and governance. AI systems used in credit
scoring and insurance risk assessment are classified as ``high-risk'' under Annex III.
High-risk systems must meet requirements
for technical documentation, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, and
cybersecurity before deployment.  General-purpose AI models (including
large language models) with systemic risk are classified as ``GPAI models
with systemic risk'' and face additional obligations including adversarial
testing (red-teaming) and incident reporting to the European AI Office.
Financial institutions deploying LLMs must map their applications to the
Act's classification scheme and implement the required controls before the
relevant provisions become applicable (August 2026 for high-risk systems
under Annex III).
\end{remark}

The themes of this chapter are unified by a single principle: every capability
of a deployed financial LLM --- from its fluency in regulatory language to its
resistance to hallucination --- is determined by decisions made at the stages
of pre-training, fine-tuning, evaluation, and governance.  Compute-optimal
pre-training (Section~\ref{sec:pretraining}) determines what the model knows;
parameter-efficient fine-tuning and RLHF (Section~\ref{sec:finetuning})
determine whether it applies that knowledge correctly; rigorous evaluation
(Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}) determines whether we can trust our assessment
of its performance; and governance frameworks (this section) determine whether
deployment is legally and institutionally appropriate.  Weakness at any stage
propagates forward: a biased pre-training corpus corrupts every downstream
application, and a governance framework that ignores model monitoring will
fail precisely when the model fails.  Financial practitioners who understand
this chain of dependencies are better positioned to deploy LLMs responsibly
and to identify where interventions will have the greatest impact.

\end{context}
